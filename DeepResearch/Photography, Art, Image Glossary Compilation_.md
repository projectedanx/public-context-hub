# **The Convergent Lens: An A-Z Glossary of Essential Photography, Art, and Image Generation Terminology**

## **Section 1: Introduction**

### **Setting the Stage: The Convergence of Creative Domains**

The contemporary creative landscape is characterized by an unprecedented convergence of disciplines. Traditional photography, the rich history and theory of the visual arts, and the rapidly evolving domain of digital and artificial intelligence (AI) image generation are no longer isolated fields but increasingly interconnected territories.1 Modern creators frequently navigate and draw inspiration from all three, utilizing digital post-processing techniques that blur the lines with digital art, employing AI tools grounded in photographic and artistic principles, and applying established art theory to critique outputs across all mediums.3 Photographers leverage sophisticated software, digital artists build upon photographic fundamentals, and AI image synthesis often relies on prompts steeped in the language of art history, composition, and lighting.6

This intermingling necessitates a shared vocabulary. Understanding the nuances of aperture and aspect ratio remains crucial, but today's creative professional may also need to grasp the implications of postmodernism, the mechanics of diffusion models, or the principles of color management.2 Fluency in the terminology of one domain alone is no longer sufficient for navigating the complexities of modern image creation, critique, and technological advancement. Effective communication, collaboration, and innovation depend on a common lexicon that bridges these converging fields. The historical weight of art theory, the technical precision of photography, and the cutting-edge concepts of AI generation all contribute to the rich, complex language of contemporary visual culture.11 This glossary arises from the recognition that a creator or critic limited to the vocabulary of a single discipline faces significant constraints in understanding, utilizing, and discussing the full spectrum of tools and ideas available today.

### **Purpose and Scope of the Glossary**

The primary objective of this glossary is to serve as a comprehensive, authoritative, single-source reference guide to the essential terminology spanning Photography, Art (including history, theory, criticism, styles, mediums, and techniques), and modern Image Generation (encompassing digital art and AI-driven synthesis). It aims to compile and define approximately 2500 key terms encountered in professional practice, academic discourse, critical analysis, and technical documentation within these interconnected fields \[User Query\].

The scope is intentionally broad, covering foundational concepts vital for beginners (such as *Aperture*, *Composition*, *Pixel*) alongside more advanced and specialized terminology required for deeper engagement (like *Generative Adversarial Networks*, *Phenomenology*, *Chine Collé*).14 The selection prioritizes terms that are frequently used, fundamentally important, or represent significant concepts across the three core domains.2 It acknowledges the dynamic nature of these fields, particularly AI, aiming to capture the established and emerging vocabulary critical for contemporary understanding.7

### **Navigating the Glossary**

This glossary is organized alphabetically from A to Z for straightforward access and ease of reference.9 Each entry follows a consistent format:

* **Term:** Presented in bold type.  
* **(Domain Context):** An optional qualifier (e.g., *Photography*, *Art Theory*, *AI*, *Printmaking*, *Digital Imaging*) appears in parentheses when a term possesses distinct meanings in different fields or requires contextual clarification to avoid ambiguity. This addresses the inherent nuance where terms like "Lithograph" 8 or "Noise" 20 carry different specific meanings for photographers versus printmakers or digital artists.  
* **Definition:** A clear, concise, and accurate definition is provided, synthesized from authoritative sources within the respective fields.5 The language aims for precision suitable for an expert-level resource while remaining accessible.25  
* **See also:** Optional cross-references point to related terms within the glossary, facilitating a deeper exploration of interconnected concepts (e.g., *Aperture* might include "See also: F-Stop, Depth of Field, Exposure Triangle").

This structure ensures users can quickly locate specific terms while also understanding their broader context and connections within the interwoven terminologies of photography, art, and image generation.

**Section 2: The A-Z Glossary of Essential Photography, Art, and Image Generation Terminology**

This section presents a representative selection of terms from the comprehensive glossary, organized alphabetically. It integrates terminology from Photography, Art (including History, Theory, Criticism, Mediums, Techniques, Styles), and Image Generation (including Digital Art and AI) to reflect the interdisciplinary nature of the resource.

**A**

* **A-D Converter (ADC):** (Photography/Digital Imaging) An electronic chip within digital cameras responsible for converting the analog electrical signals, generated when light photons strike the image sensor pixels, into digital data that forms the image file.59  
* **Abbaye de Créteil:** (Art History) A short-lived utopian community and artistic group founded in 1906 near Paris by French writers, artists, and composers, inspired by ideals of communal living and artistic collaboration.9  
* **Aberration:** (Photography) An optical imperfection or defect in a camera lens that causes light rays passing through it to deviate from converging at a single point, resulting in image flaws such as blurriness, distortion, or color fringing. Common types include spherical aberration and chromatic aberration.59 See also: Chromatic Aberration, Distortion.  
* **Absolute Resolution:** (Digital Imaging) The total number of pixels comprising a digital camera's image sensor, typically expressed as width x height dimensions (e.g., 6720 x 4480 pixels) or as a total megapixel count.59 See also: Megapixel, Resolution.  
* **Abstract Art:** (Art) Art that does not attempt to represent an accurate depiction of external reality but instead uses shapes, colors, forms, and gestural marks to achieve its effect. It explores the relationships of forms and colors, departing from realistic representation.18 See also: Abstraction, Non-Objective Art, Formalism.  
* **Abstract Expressionism:** (Art Movement) An influential American art movement dominant in the 1940s and 1950s, originating in New York City. Characterized by diverse styles but united by a commitment to abstract art expressing personal convictions and profound human values, often through bold, gestural abstraction (Action Painting) or large fields of color (Color Field Painting).18 See also: Action Painting, Color Field Painting, Modernism.  
* **Abstraction:** (Art) The process of simplifying, modifying, or distorting the appearance of objects or figures from the real world to emphasize certain qualities or concepts, or the resulting non-representational artwork itself.18 See also: Abstract Art.  
* **Academic Art:** (Art History) Art governed by the rules and conventions established by official academies, particularly the French Academy in the 17th-19th centuries. Often characterized by technical skill, idealized subjects, and adherence to classical traditions, the term can sometimes imply conservatism or lack of originality.33 See also: Academy, Neoclassicism.  
* **Academy:** (Art History) An institution for the training of artists and the promotion of art, originating in the Renaissance to elevate artists' status. Academies established standards for artistic practice and often held official exhibitions (Salons).9 See also: Academic Art, Salon.  
* **Accent Light:** (Photography Lighting) A light source used to highlight a specific area of the subject or scene, adding dimension or drawing attention.70 See also: Key Light, Fill Light, Rim Light.  
* **Accountability:** (AI Ethics) The principle that developers and deployers of AI systems have obligations and responsibilities to ensure their systems operate ethically, fairly, transparently, and compliantly, and that their actions and outcomes can be traced.71  
* **Accuracy:** (AI) The degree to which an AI system correctly performs its intended task or produces correct outputs based on input data; a key metric for evaluating AI model reliability.71  
* **Achromatic:** (Color Theory) Lacking hue; referring to neutral colors such as white, gray, and black.56 See also: Hue, Monochrome.  
* **Acrylic Paint:** (Art Medium) A fast-drying paint composed of pigment suspended in an acrylic polymer emulsion. It is water-based, versatile, and can be used to mimic watercolor or oil paint effects.18  
* **Action Painting:** (Art Movement/Technique) A style of abstract painting associated with Abstract Expressionism, characterized by energetic, gestural application of paint, often involving dripping, splashing, or smearing, emphasizing the physical act of painting itself. Coined by critic Harold Rosenberg.18 See also: Abstract Expressionism.  
* **Active Autofocus:** (Photography) An autofocus system where the camera emits a beam (often infrared or red light) to measure the distance to the subject and then adjusts the lens focus accordingly. Less common now than passive systems.15 See also: Autofocus.  
* **Active Learning:** (AI/Machine Learning) A machine learning approach where the algorithm actively selects the data points from which it learns, often by querying a user or oracle for labels on the most informative unlabeled data points, aiming to achieve higher accuracy with less labeled data.47  
* **Activist Art:** (Art) Art created with the specific intention of addressing or protesting social, political, environmental, or economic issues, aiming to provoke dialogue, raise awareness, or inspire change.38  
* **Actuality:** (Film) An early form of non-fiction film, typically short (1-2 minutes) and unedited, showing real events, places, or people. Precursor to the documentary genre.18  
* **Additive:** (Sculpture/Art) A sculptural process involving building up, adding, or joining materials (like clay, wax, or found objects) to create a form, often around an armature.34 Contrast with: Subtractive. See also: Assemblage, Modeling.  
* **Additive Color:** (Color Theory) A color model where different colors of light (typically Red, Green, and Blue \- RGB) are mixed together to create a spectrum of colors. Combining all three primaries produces white light. Used in digital displays (monitors, screens).10 Contrast with: Subtractive Color. See also: RGB, CMYK.  
* **Adobe:** (Software) An American multinational software company known for its multimedia and creativity software products, including Photoshop, Lightroom, Illustrator, Premiere Pro, and Acrobat.15  
* **Adobe Camera Raw (ACR):** (Software) A powerful plug-in for Adobe Photoshop and Lightroom used for processing and editing RAW image files from various digital cameras, offering extensive control over adjustments before conversion to other formats.15 See also: RAW, Post-Processing.  
* **Adobe RGB (1998):** (Color Management) An RGB color space developed by Adobe Systems in 1998\. It offers a wider gamut (range of colors) than sRGB, particularly in cyans and greens, making it suitable for print workflows, but requires color management for accurate display on the web or non-color-managed devices.10 See also: Color Space, sRGB, ProPhoto RGB, Gamut.  
* **Adversarial Attack:** (AI Security) An attempt to manipulate or deceive an AI model by introducing specifically crafted malicious input data (adversarial examples), causing the model to make incorrect predictions or classifications, or generate unintended outputs.47  
* **AE Lock (Autoexposure Lock):** (Photography) A camera function that allows the photographer to lock the current exposure settings (aperture and shutter speed) determined by the camera's meter, enabling recomposition without the exposure changing.14 See also: Exposure, Metering.  
* **AEB (Auto Exposure Bracketing):** (Photography) A camera feature that automatically captures a sequence of images of the same scene at different exposure levels (typically one correctly exposed, one underexposed, one overexposed). Useful in high-contrast situations or for creating HDR images.59 See also: Bracketing, HDR.  
* **Aesthetic Emotion:** (Art Theory \- Formalism) A term coined by Clive Bell to describe the unique feeling evoked by encountering "Significant Form" in art, distinct from everyday emotions and related solely to the appreciation of formal qualities.79 See also: Formalism, Significant Form.  
* **Aesthetics:** (Art Theory) The branch of philosophy concerned with the nature of beauty, art, taste, and the creation and appreciation of beauty. It examines judgments of sensory or emotional values, sometimes called judgments of sentiment or taste, and explores concepts like the sublime, aesthetic experience, and aesthetic value.11 See also: Art Criticism, Art Theory, Formalism.  
* **AF Lock (Autofocus Lock):** (Photography) A camera feature that allows the photographer to lock the focus on a specific point, enabling them to recompose the shot while maintaining focus on the original subject.15 See also: Autofocus, Back Button Focus.  
* **AF Servo:** (Photography) See Continuous Focus.59  
* **African Diaspora:** (Art History/Cultural Studies) Refers to the communities throughout the world descended from the historic movement (largely forced) of peoples from Africa, primarily to the Americas, Europe, Asia, and the Middle East. Art from the African diaspora often explores themes of identity, history, migration, and cultural hybridity.18  
* **Afrofuturism:** (Art Movement/Cultural Theory) A cultural aesthetic, philosophy of science, and philosophy of history that explores the developing intersection of African Diaspora culture with technology. It combines elements of science fiction, historical fiction, fantasy, Afrocentrism, and magic realism with non-Western cosmologies to critique the present-day dilemmas of Black people and to envision possible futures.18  
* **After:** (Art History) Used in cataloging to indicate a work created in the style of a known artist, but not by the artist themselves, and often produced at a later date.69  
* **Agent:** (AI) An autonomous entity, typically a software program or AI model, that perceives its environment through sensors and acts upon that environment through actuators to achieve specific goals.43  
* **Agentic AI:** (AI) A type of AI system capable of proactive, goal-directed behavior, making decisions, taking actions, and learning autonomously with limited human supervision.43  
* **AGI (Artificial General Intelligence):** (AI) A hypothetical future level of AI possessing cognitive abilities comparable or superior to human intelligence across a wide range of tasks, capable of understanding, learning, and applying knowledge in diverse contexts, unlike current "narrow AI" which specializes in specific tasks.7  
* **AI Alignment:** (AI Ethics) The field of research and engineering focused on ensuring that AI systems' goals and behaviors align with human values and intentions, aiming to prevent unintended or harmful outcomes as AI capabilities increase.44  
* **AI Audit:** (AI Governance) A systematic review and assessment of an AI system's algorithms, data, and operational processes to ensure fairness, transparency, accountability, compliance with regulations, and identification/mitigation of risks and biases.71  
* **AI Bias:** (AI Ethics) Systematic errors or prejudices in AI outputs that result from biased assumptions in the machine learning process or biased training data, potentially leading to unfair or discriminatory outcomes.7  
* **AI Ethics:** (AI Philosophy/Governance) A branch of ethics focusing on the moral principles and guidelines governing the design, development, deployment, and use of artificial intelligence technologies, addressing issues like bias, fairness, transparency, accountability, privacy, and societal impact.44  
* **AI Governance:** (AI Policy) The system of laws, policies, standards, frameworks, and practices established to guide the development, deployment, and use of AI technologies responsibly and ethically, managing associated risks and ensuring alignment with societal objectives.71  
* **AI Literacy:** (Education/AI) The set of competencies enabling individuals to critically evaluate, effectively use, understand, and communicate about AI technologies in various contexts.43  
* **AI Server:** (Technology/AI) A dedicated computer system, either local or cloud-based, optimized with high-performance hardware (GPUs, RAM, storage) specifically for running computationally intensive AI models, such as those used for image generation.45  
* **AI Upscaler:** (AI Software/Technique) Software utilizing AI algorithms (often deep learning models) to increase the resolution (pixel dimensions) of an image, aiming to add plausible detail and enhance sharpness beyond traditional interpolation methods.2 See also: Upscaling, Super-Resolution.  
* **AI Writer:** (AI Software) Software employing AI, particularly large language models, to generate written content (e.g., articles, summaries, creative text) based on prompts or input data.47  
* **Albumen Silver Print:** (Photography \- Historical Process) A dominant photographic printing process from the 1850s to the 1880s, using paper coated with a layer of egg white (albumen) containing salt, which was then sensitized with silver nitrate. Known for its sharp detail and rich tonal range, often toned with gold for permanence and a purplish hue.18  
* **Algorithm:** (AI/Digital) A finite sequence of well-defined, computer-implementable instructions, typically to solve a class of problems or to perform a computation. In AI, algorithms define how models learn from data and make predictions or generate outputs.1 See also: Artificial Intelligence, Machine Learning.  
* **Aliasing:** (Digital Imaging) Visual artifacts, often appearing as jagged or stair-stepped edges ("jaggies"), that occur when representing smooth curves or diagonal lines on a discrete grid of pixels, especially at lower resolutions.59 See also: Anti-aliasing.  
* **Alignment:** (Graphic Design) The arrangement of text or graphic elements along a common edge or center line (e.g., left-aligned, centered, right-aligned, justified) to create order, visual connection, and a clean appearance in a design.28  
* **Alkyd Enamel Paint:** (Art Medium) A type of oil-based paint using alkyd resins (chemically modified vegetable oils) as a binder, known for its durability, quick drying time, and hard, often glossy finish. Commonly used for commercial and household applications but sometimes employed by artists.18  
* **Alla Prima:** (Painting Technique) Italian for "at first attempt," a painting technique where the artwork is completed in a single session, with layers of wet paint applied onto previous wet layers, rather than waiting for layers to dry. Known for its spontaneity and fresh appearance.30 Also known as wet-on-wet.  
* **Allegory:** (Art/Literary Theory) A narrative or visual representation in which characters, objects, or events symbolize deeper moral, spiritual, or political meanings. The interpretation often requires understanding specific cultural or historical conventions.11 See also: Symbolism, Iconography.  
* **Alligator Skinning:** (Painting Defect) A surface texture defect in paint films characterized by a pattern resembling alligator skin, often caused by issues with drying times, incompatible layers, or excessive medium content.18  
* **Allover Painting:** (Art Style/Technique) An approach to painting, particularly associated with Abstract Expressionism (e.g., Jackson Pollock), where the entire surface of the canvas is treated with equal importance, lacking a traditional focal point, center, or hierarchical arrangement. The composition often extends to the edges of the canvas.18  
* **Alloy:** (Sculpture Material) A mixture composed primarily of two or more metals, or a metal and another element, combined to enhance properties like strength or corrosion resistance. Bronze (copper and tin) is a common alloy used in sculpture.61 See also: Bronze.  
* **Altermodern:** (Art Theory) A term coined by curator Nicolas Bourriaud suggesting a form of modernity reconfigured for the 21st century globalized era, characterized by translation, hybridization, and negotiation across cultural boundaries, responding to increased travel, communication, and migration.33  
* **Ambient Light:** (Photography Lighting) The existing light illuminating a scene or subject that is not added by the photographer (e.g., sunlight, moonlight, existing room lights). Also known as available light.14  
* **Ambrotype:** (Photography \- Historical Process) A photographic process popular in the mid-19th century, creating a positive image on glass using a variation of the wet-plate collodion process. It's essentially an underexposed collodion negative that appears positive when viewed against a dark background.18  
* **Analog:** (Photography/Technology) Relating to processes or technologies that represent data by means of physical quantities (like voltage or silver halide density) that vary continuously. In photography, refers to film-based processes as opposed to digital.95 Contrast with: Digital.  
* **Analogous Colors:** (Color Theory) Colors that are adjacent to each other on the color wheel (e.g., yellow, yellow-green, green). They typically share a common hue and create harmonious, low-contrast color schemes.34 See also: Color Wheel, Complementary Colors.  
* **Analytical Philosophy:** (Philosophy/Art Theory) A tradition of philosophy emphasizing clarity, logical rigor, and argumentation, often focusing on the analysis of language. In aesthetics, it interrogates the definitions, ontology, and language used to discuss art and aesthetic experience.11  
* **Anamorphic:** (Optics/Film) Relating to lenses or processes that distort an image by compressing or stretching it in one dimension. In cinema, anamorphic lenses squeeze a wide image onto standard film, which is then unsqueezed during projection. In art, refers to a distorted image that appears normal only when viewed from a specific angle or with a special device.69  
* **Ancestral Sampler:** (AI \- Diffusion Models) A type of sampler used in diffusion models (like Stable Diffusion) where each step in the denoising process depends directly on the previous step, introducing a degree of randomness at each stage. This can lead to more varied results but may require more steps for convergence compared to non-ancestral samplers.7 See also: Sampler, Diffusion Model.  
* **Anchorage:** (Semiotics) The function of text (like a caption) in limiting the possible interpretations (polysemy) of an image, guiding the viewer towards a preferred meaning. Conversely, an image can anchor the meaning of text.97  
* **Angle of View (AOV):** (Photography) The extent of the scene captured by a camera lens, measured in degrees. It is determined by the lens's focal length and the camera's sensor size. Wider angles capture more of the scene, while narrower angles capture less but magnify the subject.15 See also: Field of View, Focal Length.  
* **AnimateDiff:** (AI Software/Technique) A framework or extension, often used with Stable Diffusion, designed to generate short video clips or animations from text prompts by ensuring coherence between generated frames.7  
* **Animation:** (Art/Media) The technique of creating the illusion of movement by displaying a sequence of static images or drawings in rapid succession. Can be traditional (hand-drawn), stop-motion, computer-generated (CGI), etc..28  
* **Annotation:** (AI/Data) The process of labeling data (e.g., images, text) with relevant information or metadata to make it usable for training supervised machine learning models.47 See also: Labeling.  
* **Anthropomorphism:** (AI/Psychology) The attribution of human traits, emotions, or intentions to non-human entities, including animals, objects, or AI systems like chatbots.44  
* **Anti-aliasing:** (Digital Imaging) Techniques used to reduce the visual defects of aliasing (jagged edges) when representing high-resolution images or graphics at a lower resolution. It typically involves smoothing edges by blending pixel colors.59 See also: Aliasing.  
* **Aperture:** (Photography) The adjustable opening within a camera lens through which light passes to reach the image sensor or film. Its size, measured in f-stops (e.g., f/2.8, f/16), controls the amount of light entering the camera (exposure) and influences the depth of field. A smaller f-number indicates a larger opening.3 See also: F-Stop, Depth of Field, Exposure Triangle.  
* **Aperture Priority (A or Av):** (Photography) A semi-automatic camera exposure mode where the photographer sets the desired aperture (f-stop), and the camera automatically selects the appropriate shutter speed to achieve correct exposure based on the meter reading.3  
* **API (Application Programming Interface):** (Software) A set of definitions, protocols, and tools for building software and applications. It specifies how software components should interact, allowing different systems or services to communicate with each other.7  
* **Appropriation:** (Art) The intentional borrowing, copying, and alteration of pre-existing images, objects, ideas, or styles from other artists or sources, often with little transformation, to create a new artwork. A common strategy in postmodern art.11 See also: Postmodernism, Collage, Found Object.  
* **APS-C (Advanced Photo System type-C):** (Photography) A common digital camera sensor format smaller than full-frame (36x24mm), typically measuring around 22x15mm to 25x17mm (varies by manufacturer). Lenses used on APS-C cameras have a narrower effective field of view due to the crop factor (usually 1.5x or 1.6x).14 See also: Crop Factor, Full Frame, Sensor Size.  
* **APS-H (Advanced Photo System type-H):** (Photography) A digital sensor format larger than APS-C but smaller than full-frame, based on the APS film "High Definition" format. It has a crop factor of approximately 1.3x. Used less commonly, notably in some older Canon EOS models.15  
* **Aquapasto:** (Art Technique) A painting technique involving the use of thick, paste-like applications of watercolor or gouache, sometimes mixed with a medium to increase body, creating impasto-like textures.100  
* **Aquatint:** (Printmaking) An intaglio printmaking technique used to create tonal areas rather than lines. Powdered rosin is dusted onto a metal plate and heated to adhere, creating a porous ground. When etched in acid, the acid bites around the rosin particles, creating a textured surface that holds ink to produce wash-like tones. Often used with etching.9 See also: Etching, Intaglio, Printmaking.  
* **Aqueous Inks:** (Printing) Inks that use water as the main carrier liquid for the colorant (dye or pigment). Commonly used in inkjet printers.40  
* **Arbitrariness (of the sign):** (Semiotics) A key concept from Saussurean linguistics stating that there is no inherent, natural, or necessary connection between a signifier (the form of a sign, e.g., the word "dog") and its signified (the concept it represents). The relationship is based on convention and must be learned.97  
* **Archival:** (Photography/Art Conservation) Refers to materials and processes designed for long-term stability and permanence, minimizing deterioration over time. Used for prints, storage materials, and processing techniques intended to preserve photographs or artworks.8  
* **Architectural Drawing:** (Art/Architecture) A technical or illustrative drawing depicting a building or part of a building, used for design, planning, or presentation. Can include plans, elevations, sections, perspectives, or renderings.18  
* **Architectural Fragment:** (Art/Architecture) A piece or element formerly part of a building or structure, sometimes displayed or repurposed as an artwork or historical artifact.18  
* **Architectural Model:** (Art/Architecture) A scale model representing a building or architectural design in three dimensions, used for visualization, presentation, or study.18  
* **Architectural Sculpture:** (Sculpture) Sculpture that is integrated into the design of a building, serving a decorative or structural purpose (e.g., reliefs, friezes, gargoyles).77  
* **Architecture:** (Art/Design) The art and science of designing and constructing buildings and other physical structures. Also refers to the complex or carefully designed structure of something, including software or AI models.18  
* **Armature:** (Sculpture) An internal framework or skeleton, often made of wire, metal, or wood, used to support pliable sculpting materials like clay, wax, or papier-mâché during the modeling process.69  
* **Art Brut:** (Art Movement) French for "raw art," a term coined by Jean Dubuffet to describe art created outside the boundaries of official culture, particularly by those untrained in art, such as psychiatric patients, prisoners, and children. Characterized by spontaneity and disregard for conventional norms.33 See also: Outsider Art.  
* **Art Criticism:** (Art Theory) The analysis, interpretation, evaluation, and judgment of works of art. Critics assess formal qualities, content, context, meaning, and significance, often informed by art theory and history.11 See also: Aesthetics, Art Theory.  
* **Art Deco:** (Art Movement/Style) A decorative style popular in the 1920s and 1930s, originating in France. Characterized by rich ornamentation, geometric patterns, streamlined forms, stylized figures, and the use of modern materials like chrome and plastic. Influenced architecture, design, fashion, and visual arts.31  
* **Art History:** (Academic Discipline) The academic study of the history and development of visual arts, examining artworks within their historical, social, cultural, and stylistic contexts.9  
* **Art Informel:** (Art Movement) A term encompassing various abstract painting styles prevalent in Europe (especially France) during the 1940s and 1950s, parallel to Abstract Expressionism in the US. Characterized by spontaneous gestures, textured surfaces, and an emphasis on the expressive qualities of paint itself, often rejecting geometric abstraction.33 See also: Tachisme.  
* **Art Nouveau:** (Art Movement/Style) An international style of art, architecture, and applied arts popular at the turn of the 20th century (c. 1890–1910). Characterized by intricate linear designs, flowing curves based on natural forms (plants, flowers), and a synthesis of arts, aiming to create a unified aesthetic. Known as Jugendstil in Germany and Sezessionstil in Austria.18  
* **Art Theory:** (Academic Discipline) The body of ideas and concepts concerning the nature, interpretation, and evaluation of art. It encompasses various philosophical, critical, and historical frameworks for understanding art's purpose, meaning, and value.11 See also: Aesthetics, Art Criticism.  
* **Art World:** (Sociology of Art) The social, cultural, economic, and institutional network within which art is produced, distributed, exhibited, interpreted, and valued. Includes artists, critics, curators, dealers, collectors, museums, galleries, auction houses, and audiences.11  
* **Artifact:** (Digital Imaging) An undesirable visual element or defect in a digital image, often introduced during capture, compression, processing, or transmission (e.g., noise, banding, compression blocks, moiré patterns).14  
* **Artifact:** (Art/Archaeology) An object made or modified by human skill or craft, especially one of archaeological or historical interest.25

* **Chop / Chopmark:** (Printmaking) An embossed or stamped mark, often incorporating a symbol or initials, used by artists, printers, or publishers to identify their work, typically placed discreetly on the print margin.90 See also: Blindstamp.  
* **Chroma:** (Color Theory) An attribute of color perception describing its purity or intensity; the degree of difference from a gray of the same lightness. Also known as saturation.34 See also: Saturation, Hue, Value.  
* **Chromatic Aberration:** (Photography) An optical aberration where a lens fails to focus all wavelengths of color onto the same focal plane, resulting in color fringing (often purple or green) around high-contrast edges in an image.3 See also: Aberration, Longitudinal Chromatic Aberration.  
* **Chromogenic Print (C-print):** (Photography) The most common type of color photographic print, produced from a color negative or slide. The paper contains multiple layers of emulsion, each sensitive to a primary color (RGB), with dye couplers that form cyan, magenta, and yellow dyes during development to create the final color image.18  
* **Chromoxylography:** (Printmaking) A complex color wood engraving technique popular in the 19th century, using multiple blocks intricately carved with fine lines (often simulating hatching) to print different colors, achieving a wide range of tints and tones through layering.100 See also: Wood Engraving.  
* **CIE (Commission Internationale de l'Éclairage):** (Color Science) The International Commission on Illumination, an organization responsible for standardizing measurements and models related to light and color, including defining standard observers and color spaces like CIE XYZ and CIELAB.10  
* **CIELAB (or L\*a\*b\*):** (Color Science) A perceptually uniform color space defined by the CIE in 1976, representing color based on three coordinates: L\* (lightness), a\* (green-red axis), and b\* (blue-yellow axis). Widely used in color management as a device-independent reference space (Profile Connection Space).52 See also: Color Space, Profile Connection Space (PCS).  
* **CIEXYZ:** (Color Science) A device-independent color space defined by the CIE in 1931, based on the color-matching functions of a standard observer. It serves as a foundational reference space from which other color spaces (like CIELAB) are derived. Also used as a Profile Connection Space in ICC color management.54 See also: Color Space, Profile Connection Space (PCS).  
* **Ciment Fondu:** (Sculpture Material) A type of calcium aluminate cement used for making durable molds or outdoor sculptures, often reinforced with fiberglass.74  
* **Cinematographer:** (Film) The person responsible for the camera and lighting work on a film set, controlling the visual look and feel of the movie. Also known as Director of Photography (DP).18  
* **Cinematography:** (Film/Art) The art and technique of motion-picture photography, involving decisions about camera angles, movement, lighting, composition, and lens choices to create visual narratives and moods.38  
* **Cinématographe:** (Film History) An early motion picture device, invented by the Lumière brothers in 1895, that served as a camera, printer, and projector all in one. Its portability allowed for location shooting.18  
* **Cire-Perdue:** (Sculpture Technique) French term for the Lost-Wax Casting method.68 See also: Lost-Wax Casting.  
* **Circular Polarizer Filter (CPL):** (Photography) A type of polarizing filter commonly used in digital photography (especially with autofocus cameras) that reduces reflections from non-metallic surfaces (like water and glass), darkens blue skies, and increases color saturation. It consists of two layers that are rotated to adjust the effect.15 See also: Polarizing Filter.  
* **Civitai:** (AI Resource) A popular online platform for sharing and discovering AI models, particularly those for image generation (like Stable Diffusion checkpoints, LoRAs, embeddings), along with user-generated examples and resources.7 See also: Hugging Face.  
* **Clarity:** (Post-Processing) An image editing adjustment, common in software like Lightroom and ACR, that increases local contrast, particularly in the midtones, enhancing texture and perceived sharpness without affecting overall contrast as much as the standard contrast slider.95  
* **Class:** (AI/Machine Learning) In classification tasks, a category or label assigned to data points (e.g., classifying images as "cat," "dog," or "bird").47  
* **Classicism:** (Art Movement/Style) An approach in art and architecture that draws inspiration from the principles, styles, and aesthetics of ancient Greek and Roman art, emphasizing harmony, proportion, balance, order, and idealized forms.18 See also: Neoclassicism, Renaissance.  
* **Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG):** (AI \- Diffusion Models) See CFG Scale.45  
* **Clay:** (Art Medium/Sculpture) A natural earthy material that is plastic when wet, consisting essentially of hydrated silicates of aluminum, used for making pottery, tiles, bricks, and sculpture. Can be fired in a kiln to harden permanently, or used in its pliable state for modeling (often oil-based clays like plastilene are used for models not intended for firing).35 See also: Modeling, Ceramics, Plastilene.  
* **Clay Model:** (Sculpture) The original sculpture created by the artist in clay, which often serves as the basis for creating a mold for casting in a more permanent material like bronze or plaster.77  
* **CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training):** (AI Model) A neural network model developed by OpenAI trained on vast amounts of image and text pairs from the internet. It learns to associate visual concepts with natural language descriptions, enabling it to understand how well text prompts match images. Used as the text encoder in many diffusion models like Stable Diffusion.7 See also: Text Encoder, Stable Diffusion, OpenAI.  
* **Clipping:** (Photography/Digital Imaging) The loss of image detail in the brightest highlights (appearing as pure white) or darkest shadows (appearing as pure black) when the tonal range of the scene exceeds the camera's dynamic range or due to incorrect exposure or processing.14 See also: Blown-Out Highlights, Crushed Shadows, Dynamic Range, Histogram.  
* **Clipping Path:** (Graphic Design/Post-Processing) A vector path created in image editing software (like Photoshop) to isolate a subject from its background, allowing the background to be removed or replaced.122 See also: Masking, Deep-etching.  
* **Close-Up:** (Photography/Film) A shot taken with the camera close to the subject, or using a lens that magnifies the subject, typically showing detail like a person's face.122  
* **CMOS Sensor (Complementary Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor):** (Photography) The most common type of image sensor used in modern digital cameras. CMOS sensors integrate amplifiers and A/D converters onto the sensor chip itself, generally offering faster readout speeds, lower power consumption, and lower manufacturing costs compared to CCD sensors.15 See also: CCD Sensor, Image Sensor.  
* **CMYK (Cyan, Magenta, Yellow, Key/Black):** (Color Model/Printing) A subtractive color model used in color printing. Inks of Cyan, Magenta, Yellow, and Black are layered on a typically white substrate to absorb certain wavelengths of light and reflect others, creating the perception of different colors.10 Contrast with: Additive Color, RGB.  
* **Coating:** (Painting) A layer of varnish applied over a finished painting to protect the surface and unify its gloss level.18 See also: Varnish.  
* **CoBrA:** (Art Movement) An avant-garde art movement active 1948-1951, founded by artists from Copenhagen, Brussels, and Amsterdam (hence the name). Characterized by spontaneous expression, bold colors, and inspiration from children's art and folk art, reacting against geometric abstraction and Surrealism.31  
* **Code (Semiotics):** (Semiotics) A system of conventions or rules that assigns meaning to signs within a particular cultural or social context. Codes enable interpretation and communication (e.g., linguistic codes, visual codes, social codes).11 See also: Semiotics, Sign.  
* **Codification:** (Semiotics) The social and historical process by which the conventions of a code become established and widely understood within a community.97  
* **Cold Cast Bronze:** (Sculpture Technique) A method of casting where bronze powder is mixed with a resin binder (like polyester or epoxy) and poured into a mold. The resulting sculpture has a metallic surface appearance similar to traditional bronze but is lighter and less expensive.74  
* **Cold Shoe:** (Photography) A mounting bracket on a camera or accessory, similar to a hot shoe, but without electrical contacts. Used for attaching accessories like microphones or external viewfinders that don't require power or communication from the camera.15 See also: Hot Shoe.  
* **Collab (Collaboration/Collaborative Art):** (Art Practice) Art created through the joint effort of two or more artists or participants working together.25  
* **Collage:** (Art Technique) An artwork created by adhering various materials, such as paper, photographs, fabric, and found objects, onto a flat surface.34 See also: Assemblage, Photomontage, Decoupage.  
* **Collate:** (Printing/Binding) To assemble individual printed sheets or signatures in their correct sequence before binding.29  
* **Collagraph:** (Printmaking) A printmaking technique where the printing plate (matrix) is created by collaging various materials with different textures (e.g., cardboard, fabric, sand, glue) onto a rigid base. The plate can be inked and printed as either a relief or intaglio print, transferring the textures to the paper.90  
* **Collector:** (Art World) An individual or institution that acquires and owns works of art.37  
* **Colophon:** (Printmaking/Publishing) An inscription or note, typically placed at the end of a book or portfolio of prints, providing details about its production, such as the printer, publisher, place and date of printing, typeface used, and edition size.101  
* **Color (Colour):** (Art Element/Physics) The visual perceptual property corresponding in humans to the categories called red, blue, yellow, etc. Derived from the spectrum of light (distribution of light power versus wavelength) interacting in the eye with the spectral sensitivities of the light receptors. Defined by attributes like hue, saturation (intensity), and value (lightness).11 See also: Hue, Saturation, Value, Color Theory.  
* **Color Balance:** (Photography/Post-Processing) The adjustment of the intensities of colors (typically the primary colors red, green, and blue) in an image to render neutral colors correctly or achieve a specific creative effect. Related to white balance.56 See also: White Balance, Color Temperature.  
* **Color Calibration:** (Color Management) The process of adjusting a device (monitor, printer) to conform to a known color standard, ensuring accurate color representation.40 See also: Calibration.  
* **Color Correction:** (Post-Processing/Printing) The process of adjusting the colors in an image or print file to correct inaccuracies, achieve a desired look, or ensure consistency across different media.56 See also: Color Balance, White Balance.  
* **Color Depth:** (Digital Imaging) The number of bits used to represent the color of a single pixel in a digital image. Higher color depth allows for a greater number of distinct colors to be represented (e.g., 8-bit color \= 256 colors per channel, 24-bit color \= 16.7 million colors total).59 See also: Bit Depth.  
* **Color Field Painting:** (Art Movement) A style of abstract painting emerging in the 1940s and 1950s, associated with Abstract Expressionism, characterized by large fields of flat, solid color spread across the canvas, creating an unbroken surface and a flat picture plane. Focuses on the expressive power and emotional impact of color itself.31 See also: Abstract Expressionism.  
* **Color Fringing:** (Photography) See Chromatic Aberration.3  
* **Color Management:** (Digital Imaging/Printing) A system and process for controlling the conversion of colors between different devices (cameras, scanners, monitors, printers) to ensure consistency and accuracy throughout a digital workflow. Relies on device profiles (ICC profiles) and standard reference color spaces.10 See also: ICC Profile, Color Space, Gamut, Calibration.  
* **Color Management Module (CMM):** (Software/Color Management) The software engine within an operating system or application that performs the color transformations (conversions) between different color spaces based on ICC profiles.53  
* **Color Model:** (Color Theory) An abstract mathematical model describing the way colors can be represented as tuples of numbers (e.g., RGB, CMYK, HSL, Lab). Defines the components used to create colors.10 See also: RGB, CMYK, CIELAB, Color Space.  
* **Color Palette:** (Art/Design) The specific range or selection of colors used in a particular artwork, design, or by an artist.12  
* **Color Properties:** (Color Theory) The fundamental characteristics used to describe color: Hue (the pure color name, like red or blue), Value (lightness or darkness), and Saturation/Intensity (purity or vividness).35 See also: Hue, Value, Saturation.  
* **Color Scheme:** (Art/Design) A planned combination of colors used in an artwork or design, often based on relationships on the color wheel (e.g., complementary, analogous, monochromatic).34  
* **Color Space:** (Color Management/Digital Imaging) A specific, defined range (gamut) of colors. Standard color spaces (like sRGB, Adobe RGB, ProPhoto RGB) provide a reference for consistent color representation across devices. Device color spaces describe the gamut a specific device can capture or reproduce.10 See also: Color Model, Gamut, ICC Profile.  
* **Color Temperature:** (Physics/Photography) A characteristic of visible light, measured in Kelvins (K), that describes its hue along the yellow-blue axis. Lower temperatures (e.g., 2700K) appear warmer (yellow/red), while higher temperatures (e.g., 6500K) appear cooler (blue). Important for achieving accurate white balance.14 See also: White Balance, Kelvin.  
* **Color Wheel:** (Color Theory) A circular diagram organizing colors based on their relationships (primary, secondary, tertiary) and showing connections between complementary and analogous colors. Used as a tool for color mixing and creating color schemes.  
* **ComfyUI:** (AI Software) A node-based graphical user interface (UI) for Stable Diffusion and other AI models, allowing users to build complex image generation workflows by connecting different processing nodes visually. Favored by advanced users for its flexibility.7 See also: Stable Diffusion, Automatic1111, Node, Workflow.  
* **Commodity Codes:** (Semiotics) Codes related to the meanings associated with consumer goods, fashion, and brands within a culture.97  
* **Complementary Colors:** (Color Theory) Colors located directly opposite each other on the color wheel (e.g., red and green, blue and orange, yellow and violet). When placed next to each other, they create strong contrast; when mixed, they tend to neutralize each other.34 See also: Color Wheel, Analogous Colors.  
* **Composite / Composite Photograph:** (Photography/Digital Art) An image created by combining elements from two or more separate photographs or images into a single picture, often using layers and masking in post-processing software.15 See also: Blending, Collage, Photomontage.  
* **Composition:** (Art/Photography/Design) The arrangement and organization of visual elements (lines, shapes, colors, textures, space, subject matter) within the frame of an artwork or photograph according to principles of design to create a unified and effective whole.3 See also: Elements of Art, Principles of Design, Rule of Thirds.  
* **Compression:** (Digital Imaging) The process of reducing the file size of digital data (like an image). Lossy compression (e.g., JPEG) permanently discards some data to achieve smaller sizes. Lossless compression (e.g., PNG, TIFF LZW) reduces file size without losing any original data.3 See also: Lossy, Lossless, JPEG, PNG, TIFF.  
* **Computational Creativity:** (AI) A multidisciplinary field aiming to build software that exhibits behavior deemed creative, often combining AI, cognitive science, philosophy, and the arts.47  
* **Computational Photography:** (Photography/Computer Science) A field of digital image capture and processing that uses digital computation instead of (or in addition to) traditional optical processes. Techniques include HDR, focus stacking, panoramas, light field cameras, and AI-driven enhancements often found in smartphone cameras.15  
* **Computer Vision:** (AI) A field of AI that enables computers and systems to derive meaningful information from digital images, videos, and other visual inputs, and take actions or make recommendations based on that information. Tasks include image recognition, object detection, and scene understanding.43  
* **Conceptual Art:** (Art Movement) An art movement emerging in the 1960s where the concept(s) or idea(s) involved in the work take precedence over traditional aesthetic, technical, and material concerns. The execution is often secondary to the idea itself.25  
* **Conceptual Practice:** (Art) An artistic approach where the underlying idea or concept driving the work is paramount, influencing the materials, form, and process.25  
* **Concrete Art:** (Art Movement) A form of abstract art originating in the 1930s that emphasizes geometric abstraction and is devoid of any figurative or symbolic content. Artworks are intended to be self-contained realities, based solely on elements like line, color, and plane.18 See also: Arte Concreto.  
* **Condition:** (Art Conservation/Market) The physical state of an artwork, particularly a print or photograph, described using terms like Pristine (Mint), Excellent, Very Good, Good, Fair, often affecting its value.8  
* **Connotation:** (Semiotics) The secondary, associative, cultural, or emotional meanings suggested by or associated with a sign, beyond its literal or primary meaning (denotation).97 See also: Denotation, Semiotics.  
* **Constructivism:** (Art Movement) An artistic and architectural philosophy originating in Russia around 1913, rejecting autonomous art in favor of art as a practice for social purposes. Characterized by geometric abstraction, functionalism, and the use of industrial materials.31  
* **Contemporary Art:** (Art History) Art produced in the present period, typically considered to be from the late 20th century (c. 1970s onwards) to the present day. Encompasses a wide range of styles, mediums, and concepts, often engaging with current social, political, and technological issues.11  
* **Content:** (Art/Media) The subject matter, themes, ideas, or information conveyed by an artwork, text, or other media object.34  
* **Context:** (Art History/Criticism) The historical, cultural, social, political, biographical, or other circumstances surrounding the creation and interpretation of an artwork, which influence its meaning and significance.11  
* **Contextual Analysis:** (Art Criticism) An approach to interpreting art that emphasizes understanding the artwork within its original historical, social, cultural, and biographical context.11 Contrast with: Formalism.  
* **Contextual Bandit:** (AI/Machine Learning) An extension of the multi-armed bandit problem in reinforcement learning where the agent uses contextual information to make better decisions about which action (arm) to choose to maximize rewards.47  
* **Contextual Criticism:** (Art Criticism) See Contextual Analysis.12  
* **Context Window:** (AI/NLP) The maximum amount of text (measured in tokens) that a large language model can process and consider at one time when generating a response or prediction. Information outside this window may be ignored.43 See also: Token, Large Language Model.  
* **Contour:** (Art/Sculpture) The outline defining the edge or boundary of a shape, form, or figure.76  
* **Contrapposto:** (Sculpture/Art History) Italian for "counterpoise." A pose in figure sculpture and painting where the subject shifts their weight onto one leg, causing the hips and shoulders to angle in opposite directions, creating a sense of naturalism and relaxed movement. Characterized by an S-curve through the body.125  
* **Contrast:** (Art/Photography/Design Principle) The juxtaposition of different elements (e.g., light and dark values, rough and smooth textures, large and small shapes, warm and cool colors) to create visual interest, emphasis, or drama.15  
* **Contrast Detection Autofocus:** (Photography) An autofocus method where the camera system analyzes the contrast between adjacent pixels on the sensor to determine focus. It adjusts the lens until maximum contrast is detected. Generally slower than phase detection autofocus, especially in low light.15 See also: Autofocus, Phase Detection Autofocus.  
* **ControlNet:** (AI Software/Technique) A neural network architecture used in conjunction with diffusion models (like Stable Diffusion) to provide additional spatial conditioning or control over image generation, using inputs like edge maps, depth maps, segmentation maps, or human pose skeletons to guide the output.2  
* **Convergence:** (AI/Machine Learning) In model training, the state where the model's performance (e.g., measured by the loss function) ceases to improve significantly with further iterations, indicating that the training process has stabilized or reached an optimal point.7  
* **Conversion (Color):** (Color Management) The process of transforming color data from one color space representation to another (e.g., from Adobe RGB to sRGB, or from RGB to CMYK), typically using ICC profiles and a Profile Connection Space, aiming to preserve color appearance as accurately as possible within the destination gamut.10 See also: Color Space, ICC Profile, Profile Connection Space (PCS), Gamut Mapping.  
* **Convolutional Neural Network (CNN):** (AI/Machine Learning) A class of deep neural networks highly effective for analyzing visual imagery. CNNs use convolutional layers to automatically learn spatial hierarchies of features from input data, making them well-suited for tasks like image classification, object detection, and image segmentation.2  
* **Cool Colors:** (Color Theory) Colors typically associated with blue, green, and violet hues, often perceived as calming, receding, or associated with coolness (water, sky).35 Contrast with: Warm Colors.  
* **Copilot:** (AI Software) Microsoft's brand name for its AI assistant features integrated into various products like Windows, Microsoft 365, and Edge, often powered by large language models like OpenAI's GPT.44  
* **Copy:** (Graphic Design/Publishing) The written text content of an advertisement, publication, or website.29  
* **Copyright:** (Law/Intellectual Property) The legal right granted to the creator of original works of authorship, including literary, dramatic, musical, artistic, and certain other intellectual works. It gives the owner exclusive rights to reproduce, distribute, perform, display, and create derivative works.8  
* **Core:** (Sculpture \- Casting) The refractory material placed inside a mold (especially in lost-wax casting) to form the hollow interior of a cast metal sculpture, reducing weight and material cost.77  
* **Corpus:** (AI/Linguistics) A large and structured set of texts or data used for statistical analysis, language modeling, or training AI systems.47  
* **Craft:** (Art) Skill in making things by hand; or an activity involving skill in making things by hand (e.g., pottery, weaving, woodworking). Sometimes distinguished from "fine art," though the boundary is often blurred.33 See also: Arts and Crafts Movement.  
* **Craftsmanship:** (Art) The skill, quality of design, and execution demonstrated in an artwork or handmade object.73  
* **Craquelure:** (Painting) The network of fine cracks that develops on the surface of older paintings, typically due to the aging and shrinkage of paint layers, ground, or varnish.69  
* **Crayon:** (Art Medium) A stick of colored wax, charcoal, chalk, or other material used for writing or drawing. In printmaking (lithography), a greasy crayon is used to draw on the stone or plate.35  
* **Creative Brief:** (Design/Advertising) A document outlining the key objectives, target audience, message, constraints, and deliverables for a creative project, serving as a guide for the design team.39  
* **Creative Process:** (Art) The sequence of thoughts and actions leading to the creation of an artwork, often involving exploration, experimentation, problem-solving, and refinement.37  
* **Critic:** (Art World) An individual who specializes in analyzing, interpreting, evaluating, and writing about art.11 See also: Art Criticism.  
* **Critical Theory:** (Philosophy/Social Theory/Art Theory) A school of thought, originating with the Frankfurt School, that critiques society and culture by applying knowledge from the social sciences and humanities to reveal and challenge power structures, ideology, and domination. Applied to art, it examines art's relationship to social, political, and economic contexts.38 See also: Marxist Theory.  
* **Crop Factor:** (Photography) The ratio of the diagonal measurement of a camera's sensor to the diagonal of a standard 35mm full-frame sensor (approx. 43.3mm). It indicates how much the field of view of a lens will be cropped or narrowed when used on a smaller sensor camera (e.g., a 1.5x crop factor means a 50mm lens behaves like a 75mm lens in terms of field of view).3 See also: APS-C, Full Frame, Sensor Size, 35mm Equivalent.  
* **Cropping:** (Photography/Graphic Design) The process of removing unwanted outer areas of an image to improve framing, change the aspect ratio, or focus attention on the subject.5 See also: Aspect Ratio, Composition.  
* **Crop Marks:** (Printing/Graphic Design) Fine lines printed in the corners or edges of a document outside the final trim area, indicating where the paper should be cut after printing.39 Also known as trim marks.  
* **Crop Sensor:** (Photography) Any digital camera image sensor that is physically smaller than the standard 35mm full-frame size (36x24mm). Common examples include APS-C and Micro Four Thirds sensors.15 See also: APS-C, Micro Four Thirds, Full Frame, Crop Factor.  
* **Cross Processing:** (Photography \- Film) Intentionally processing photographic film in a chemical solution intended for a different type of film (e.g., slide film in negative chemicals, or vice versa), resulting in unpredictable shifts in color, contrast, and saturation.15  
* **Cross-Validation:** (AI/Machine Learning) A technique for assessing how the results of a statistical analysis (like a machine learning model) will generalize to an independent dataset. It involves partitioning the data into subsets, training the model on some subsets, and validating it on the remaining subset(s), often repeated multiple times.47  
* **Crosshatching:** (Drawing/Printmaking Technique) Creating tonal or shading effects by drawing closely spaced parallel lines, with additional sets of lines drawn at angles over the first set to achieve darker values.69 See also: Hatching.  
* **Crushed Impasto:** (Painting) Thickly applied paint (impasto) that has been flattened or compressed, creating a specific texture.69 See also: Impasto.  
* **Crushed Shadows:** (Photography/Digital Imaging) See Blocked Shadows.15  
* **Cubism:** (Art Movement) A revolutionary early 20th-century art movement, pioneered by Pablo Picasso and Georges Braque, that abandoned traditional perspective and depicted subjects from multiple viewpoints simultaneously, breaking them down into geometric forms and fragmented planes.30  
* **Culling:** (Photography Workflow) The process of reviewing a set of photographs from a shoot and selecting the best images to keep, edit, or deliver, while discarding the rejects.121  
* **Cultural Objects Name Authority (CONA):** (Art Information Standard) A vocabulary developed by the Getty Research Institute containing structured information (titles, creators, dates, etc.) about works of art, architecture, and cultural objects, intended for cataloging and research.22  
* **Cultural Traditions:** (Anthropology/Art History) The patterns of practices, beliefs, values, and customs shared and transmitted within a societal group, often influencing artistic expression.25  
* **Curate:** (Museum Practice/Art) To select, organize, interpret, and present artworks or artifacts for an exhibition or collection.25  
* **Curator:** (Museum Practice/Art World) A person responsible for overseeing, acquiring, caring for, studying, and interpreting collections or exhibitions in a museum, gallery, or archive.25  
* **Curves:** (Post-Processing) A powerful tool in image editing software (like Photoshop or Lightroom) for adjusting the tonal range (brightness and contrast) and color balance of an image. Represented as a graph (tone curve) where the user manipulates points along a diagonal line to lighten or darken specific tonal ranges (shadows, midtones, highlights).4 See also: Tone Curve, Histogram.  
* **Cusping:** (Painting Conservation) Scalloped distortions along the edges of a canvas caused by the tension applied when stretching and tacking it to a frame.69  
* **Cyanotype:** (Photography \- Historical Process/Printmaking) A photographic printing process producing cyan-blue prints, invented by Sir John Herschel in 1842\. Paper is coated with a solution of iron salts, exposed to UV light (like sunlight) through a negative or with objects placed on it (photogram), and then washed in water to develop the image.106  
* **CycleGAN:** (AI Model) A type of Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) used for image-to-image translation tasks without requiring paired training data (e.g., translating photos of horses to zebras, or summer scenes to winter scenes).47 See also: GAN, Image-to-Image Translation.
