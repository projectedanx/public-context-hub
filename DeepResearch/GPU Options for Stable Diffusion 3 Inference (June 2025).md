# GPU Options for Stable Diffusion 3 Inference (June 2025\)

## Performance and Requirements Overview

Stable Diffusion **3** is expected to be a resource-intensive image generation model, likely comparable to or larger than Stable Diffusion XL in complexity. Running an image generation service with up to **20 concurrent requests** and a strict P95 latency under **2 seconds per image** demands **high-performance GPUs** for inference. The goal is to maintain sub-2s latency even under peak load, which effectively requires very high throughput or parallel processing capacity. Modern cloud GPU providers such as **CoreWeave**, **Lambda Labs**, and **RunPod** offer cutting-edge NVIDIA GPUs suited for this task. We consider three top-tier NVIDIA GPU instance types as of mid-2025 for this use case: **NVIDIA L40S**, **NVIDIA H100**, and **NVIDIA B100** (Blackwell architecture). Each of these GPUs represents a different balance of raw performance, memory capacity, and cost:

- *NVIDIA L40S:* A datacenter GPU based on the Ada Lovelace architecture (48 GB VRAM) optimized for inference and graphics. It’s often marketed as a cost-effective alternative to flagship GPUs, with significantly lower hourly pricing than H100.  
- *NVIDIA H100:* A flagship Hopper-generation GPU (80 GB HBM3 memory) known for top-tier AI training and inference performance. It offers an order-of-magnitude leap in throughput over the previous A100 and is widely deployed for large AI workloads.  
- *NVIDIA B100:* The next-generation **Blackwell** GPU (with a massive 192 GB HBM3e memory) slated to launch in 2024–2025. NVIDIA has teased that B100 will provide **3–4×** the AI inference performance of H100 in certain benchmarks, thanks to a new architecture with 178 billion transistors and faster memory. It is just becoming available in 2025 and comes at a premium price due to limited supply and its cutting-edge capabilities.

Below, we compare these GPU instances across key attributes relevant to the Stable Diffusion 3 service: GPU memory (VRAM), on-demand cost per hour, the **estimated maximum concurrent image generation tasks** one GPU can handle under a 2-second latency budget, and the **cost per 1000 images** (a measure of generation cost efficiency). All costs are based on cloud providers in mid-2025 (e.g. CoreWeave, Lambda, RunPod), which offer much lower rates than traditional hyperscalers. Performance estimates are derived from public benchmarks on Stable Diffusion XL and related generative models.

## Analysis of Results

**Memory (VRAM):** The NVIDIA B100 offers an **unprecedented 192 GB** of HBM3e memory on a single card. This is 4× the L40S’s VRAM and 2.4× the H100’s. Such large memory can accommodate very large diffusion models or multiple model instances, and it allows batching many image generation requests together. For example, a B100 could potentially run *several* Stable Diffusion inference processes or a large batch of images concurrently without running out of memory. The H100’s 80 GB HBM3 is also ample for Stable Diffusion 3 (which likely requires on the order of 10–20 GB for a single generation at high resolution), and it provides significantly higher memory bandwidth (over 3 TB/s) than the L40S’s GDDR6 memory (864 GB/s). The L40S’s 48 GB is sufficient for a single SD3 inference and maybe a small batch, but it leaves less headroom for concurrent execution. In summary, **B100 \>\> H100 \> L40S** in VRAM capacity, which aligns with their target use cases: B100 is built for the largest models and highest throughput, H100 for high-end training/inference, and L40S for mid-range inference and graphics workloads.

**Inference Performance:** In terms of raw speed, the **H100 outperforms the L40S** significantly for AI generation tasks. Measured on Stable Diffusion XL, one H100 generated roughly **2952 images/hour** (\~1.2 sec per image), whereas one L40S achieved \~1296 images/hour (\~2.8 sec per image). This \~2.3× performance gap means that to achieve the same throughput as a single H100, you would need about **2–3 L40S GPUs**. NVIDIA’s own positioning calls L40S a “poor man’s H100” – it is faster than the last-gen A100, but still a tier below H100. The **B100**, on the other hand, is expected to dwarf both. Early info from NVIDIA shows H100 as a 11× speedup over A100 on GPT-3 inference, and B100 as a huge leap beyond that – **at least 3× the H100’s performance on that test**. Blackwell GPUs also introduce new lower-precision modes (FP8/FP4) and higher clock speeds, which could accelerate diffusion model inference substantially. While exact Stable Diffusion numbers for B100 are not yet public, a reasonable expectation is that **B100 might generate on the order of 6000–9000 images/hour** (extrapolating 2–3× H100 throughput). This suggests one B100 could potentially handle multiple simultaneous diffusion requests with \<2s latency each, something H100 can only do with 1–2 at a time. In practice, H100 can utilize techniques like **MIG partitioning** (up to 7 GPU instances) to run two SD inference jobs in parallel (each on half the GPU) and still stay near the 2s mark, whereas L40S really can only process one at a time without exceeding the latency target. B100’s sheer speed might allow it to serve \~4 concurrent jobs on one card (either via MIG or by batching multiple images per inference pass) and still finish around 2 seconds. Thus, in the context of **20 concurrent** image generations: an **H100-based setup** might need on the order of 10 GPUs (each handling \~2 jobs), an **L40S setup** would need nearly 20 GPUs (one per job), whereas a **B100 setup** might handle it with perhaps 5 GPUs. This highlights how performance translates directly into fewer GPUs necessary to meet the throughput/latency target.

**Cost Per Hour:** A major factor is the cloud instance pricing. The L40S is notably *cheaper per hour* than H100 on many clouds – **about 30–50% lower cost**. For example, RunPod (a GPU hosting provider) cut L40S prices to \~$0.99/hr in May 2025, whereas H100 80GB instances typically run around $1.90–$2.40/hr on community/cloud marketplaces. CoreWeave’s price list similarly shows L40S starting around ~~$0.86/hr, vs H100 at $1.47/hr (lowest rates). This aligns with NVIDIA’s positioning that L40S offers more affordable inference: approximately 40% cheaper than H100 PCIe per hour for the customer. **B100**, being brand-new and supply-constrained, commands the highest price. As of June 2025, few providers have public prices for B100 – one report puts **CoreWeave’s early B200/B100 instances at \~$10/hr per GPU**, and another source lists community pricing for similar Blackwell-based nodes around **$5–$6/hr+** (for B200). We can infer that a single B100 might be on the order of **2–3× the cost of an H100** initially. This high cost is partly due to the massive jump in hardware capabilities (the B100 is an expensive $35k+ piece of hardware). So in raw $/hour: \*\*L40S is cheapest ($1), H100 moderate (~~$2), B100 highest ($5+)\*\* among these.

**Cost per Image:** The more meaningful metric for our service, however, is cost per inference (per image). Here, the **higher raw performance of H100 can offset its higher hourly price**. In an apples-to-apples Stable Diffusion XL test, an H100 (at $3.80/hr in that scenario) generated 1000 images for about **$1.29**, whereas the L40S (at $1.96/hr) cost **$1.51** per 1000 images. In other words, even though the H100 was almost 2× the hourly cost in that test, it produced images so much faster that each image was slightly cheaper than on L40S. With more optimized cloud pricing (e.g. H100 at \~$2/hr and L40S at \~$1/hr as in the table above), the cost per 1000 images comes out to roughly **$0.7 on H100 vs $0.8 on L40S** (about a 15% advantage to H100). The L40S’s lower price per hour *nearly* makes up for its slower speed, but not quite – effectively, L40S ends up **a bit more expensive per generated image** and also requires twice the number of instances to handle a given throughput. This diminishes the appeal of L40S for a latency-critical, high-concurrency service: you’d be paying for 2× as many GPUs and actually paying slightly more per request in the end. The **B100**, if we assume a moderate \~$5/hr and \~3× H100 output, could potentially push cost per 1000 images down further (perhaps $0.5 or lower). Its **price-to-performance ratio** is expected to eventually be better than H100’s – NVIDIA touts vastly improved efficiency, claiming up to *25× better energy efficiency and 30× throughput* on Blackwell for AI inference in some use cases. However, in the immediate term, if B100 is priced at a large premium (say $10/hr), some of that cost efficiency would be negated (at $10/hr, even a 3× faster B100 would cost \~$1.3 per 1000 images, similar to or worse than H100). So the cost-per-image benefit of B100 will depend on its actual market price once availability improves. It’s likely to become more cost-efficient than H100 in the long run, but as of mid-2025 early adopters are paying a premium.

**Concurrent Capacity:** For our specific latency requirement (95th percentile \< 2 seconds), the practical concurrency per GPU is limited. The **L40S** can handle one image generation comfortably only if the model or resolution is small – in tests with SDXL, a single image took \~2.8s on L40S, which is already above our 2s target (meaning L40S might actually *fail* the latency requirement for a full-quality SD3 image, even with no concurrency). Some optimizations (like reduced diffusion steps or lower precision) might bring L40S closer to \~2s for a single request, but it has essentially no headroom for parallelism. The **H100**, by contrast, can generate an image in \~1.2s, leaving some slack within the 2s budget. This makes it feasible to serve **2 simultaneous requests** on one H100 – if two images are generated in parallel, each might slow down (sharing GPU resources) and complete in roughly \~2.4s, which is slightly above target. In practice one could stagger the requests or use **MIG** to partition the H100 into two 40GB halves, each running an independent SD instance at \~1.2s latency – that way two images can be produced in parallel with each still hitting \~1.2s (since each MIG slice handles one image). In effect, an H100 can be configured to serve 2 concurrent streams at \~1–1.5s latency each. Serving more than 2 on one H100 would likely push latencies beyond 2s (e.g. 3 concurrent might yield \~3.5s each). The **B100**, given its enormous cores and memory, can potentially be split into 3 or 4 MIG partitions (e.g. 4× 48GB slices) each capable of running a diffusion model quickly, or it could handle a batch of 4 images in one go. If one B100 is \~3× faster than H100, even dividing it 4 ways could give each roughly 0.75× H100 performance – enough to keep \~4 jobs around the ~~1.5–2s mark each. NVIDIA’s MIG supports up to 7 instances on B100 (because of 192GB VRAM split into 7×~~27GB slices), though running 7 heavy jobs would certainly slow each down. Realistically, **3–4 concurrent SD3 requests per B100** GPU seems feasible while meeting the latency target (this could be done by quartering the GPU, or by smart scheduling/batching of requests). Therefore, to achieve 20 concurrent *2s* requests: one would deploy roughly **4–5 B100s, \~10 H100s, or \~20 L40S**. This is a crucial consideration – using L40S would mean managing a cluster twice as large as with H100, with higher overall power consumption and complexity, whereas B100 could potentially cut the required number of GPUs in half (at the expense of each GPU being very costly).

## Recommendation and Justification

After weighing performance, cost, and scalability, **our recommendation is to use NVIDIA H100 instances** for the Stable Diffusion 3 service at this time (June 2025). The **H100 offers the best balance** of maturity, performance, and cost-effectiveness for meeting a 2-second image latency at high concurrency:

- **Proven Performance:** The H100 can comfortably deliver \~1.2 second single-image inference on Stable Diffusion-like workloads, which leaves room to handle two requests in parallel per GPU if needed. Its throughput is about 2–3× higher than L40S, drastically reducing the number of GPUs required to serve 20 users at once. In practice, \~10× H100 GPUs could handle 20 concurrent generations, whereas \~20× L40S would be needed for similar output. This means less infrastructure to manage and simpler scaling. Each H100 also has ample 80GB memory to load the SD3 model and even run minor concurrent tasks or larger batch sizes if the application ever needs it.  
    
- **Cost Efficiency:** Despite a higher hourly price tag, the H100’s superior speed makes it **cheaper per image** than the L40S. As shown in benchmarks, the cost per 1000 images on an H100 was slightly lower than on L40S. For our use case, fewer H100 instances can do the work of many L40S, resulting in a similar or lower total cloud bill. For example, if one H100 (\~$2/hr) replaces \~2 L40S (\~2×$1/hr), the hourly cost is a wash, but the H100 setup actually delivers images at lower latency and with extra capacity headroom. In essence, **L40S’s apparent cost savings vanish at scale** – you’d pay about the same total to get the required throughput, and possibly even a bit more per image. Therefore, it’s wiser to invest in the higher-tier H100 and benefit from its efficiency.  
    
- **Scalability and Reliability:** The H100 is widely available across GPU cloud providers by now, and its software support (drivers, frameworks like PyTorch with TensorRT, etc.) is very robust. Many inference optimizations (TensorRT engines, half-precision and FP8 modes) have been battle-tested on H100. By contrast, the **B100**, while extremely promising, is **bleeding-edge technology**. Its availability is limited (often requiring special reservation or “contact sales” on platforms), and the pricing is currently **very high**. Using B100 at this stage could mean paying a premium for performance we don’t strictly need, or encountering early-driver issues. Unless the service’s scale or model size truly demands the B100’s extra 192GB memory and \~3× speed, an H100-based deployment is a safer choice in mid-2025. We anticipate that B100 prices will normalize and its advantages will grow in the future, but for now, **H100 strikes the optimal balance**.  
    
- **Meeting the Latency Target:** With H100, we have more confidence to meet the P95 \< 2s requirement. Each H100 can serve one image well under 2 seconds, and even in worst-case concurrent load, we can allocate at most one generation per MIG slice (ensuring each stays under 2s). Achieving \<2s on L40S would be challenging even for a single request (as SDXL tests showed \~2.8s). It would likely require model pruning or reduced inference quality on L40S to hit 2s, which is not ideal. H100 gives us performance headroom, meaning even if Stable Diffusion 3 is a bit heavier than expected, the GPU can handle it within 2s (H100 was designed to handle “large scale production models” including image generation). This headroom is important for P95 latency – we don’t want our 95th percentile to be right at the edge of the GPU’s capability. H100’s stronger performance ensures that even tail-end requests finish on time, whereas L40S would be running at 100% just to try to meet the median latency.  
    
- **Future-proofing vs. Overkill:** The **B100** will no doubt eventually become the top choice for AI inference, especially as its cost comes down. Its huge memory could enable new features (like simultaneously running multiple diffusion models or larger next-gen Stable Diffusion versions), and its speed would easily support far beyond 20 concurrent requests in under 2 seconds with fewer nodes. If the service were expecting to scale to hundreds of concurrent requests or incorporate much larger generative models, we might lean toward B100 despite the cost, or wait for it to become more accessible. But given the current requirement (20 concurrent) and the timeline, H100 is **more than capable** of handling the load. B100’s extra horsepower would be nice, but it’s not strictly necessary to achieve our 2s goal – it would mostly provide capacity for future growth. Moreover, the premium one would pay for B100 now might not justify the relatively modest reduction in cluster size (e.g. using 5 B100s instead of 10 H100s). In terms of ROI, today’s H100 offers better value. We can reconsider **Blackwell GPUs** in late 2025 when they are mainstream and priced closer to H100, but as of June 2025 we recommend sticking with H100 for this service.

In conclusion, **NVIDIA H100** instances emerge as the recommended choice for running Stable Diffusion 3 in a cloud environment that needs to handle 20 concurrent image generations with low latency. The H100 strikes the best compromise between **cost and performance**, delivering high throughput per dollar and requiring fewer instances to meet the demand, all while being readily available and supported. The **NVIDIA L40S**, while lower in hourly cost, would require roughly double the number of GPUs and still struggle with the sub-2s latency target, ultimately making it a less efficient and riskier option. The **NVIDIA B100**, though incredibly powerful, is currently expensive and scarce; it represents the future of GPU inference, but its advantages would overshoot our present needs and budget. By deploying H100 GPUs for the image generation API, we can confidently achieve **P95 \< 2s latency** for concurrent requests, maintain cost-efficiency at scale, and ensure stability with a well-supported platform. This configuration will meet the service requirements today, and also allow some headroom for growth or slight model expansion, without the steep learning curve or cost of the very latest hardware.
