

# **Cultural Hegemony in Diffusion Outputs: Diagnosing and Dismantling Visual Flattening in Generative Image Systems**

## **Introduction: The Coloniality of the Prompt**

The rapid proliferation of text-to-image diffusion models represents a paradigm shift in visual production, yet these systems are far from the neutral, objective tools their creators often claim them to be. This report posits that contemporary generative image platforms, such as DALL·E and Midjourney, function as powerful engines of cultural hegemony, perpetuating a form of **Promptual Colonialism**. This term describes the systemic process by which these models, through their underlying architecture, biased training data, and the political economy of their deployment, impose a default Euro-American visuality that systematically erases, misrepresents, and flattens the rich diversity of global visual cultures. This phenomenon is not an accidental byproduct but a direct extension of what the semiotician Walter Mignolo identifies as the "colonial matrix of power"—the enduring structures of knowledge, authority, and economy established during colonialism that continue to shape modernity.1 In the digital sphere, this matrix is re-inscribed with every generated image, turning the prompt into a site of neocolonial encounter.

The default aesthetic of these models—often characterized by a glossy, hyper-realistic, or "cinematic" finish—is not a passive statistical average but an active imposition of a dominant cultural worldview.3 This is a direct consequence of what scholars like Mohamed, Png, and Isaac (2020) have termed the coloniality of AI, where a supposedly "universal" ethics and aesthetics, which are in fact rooted in Western norms, are encoded as the default.2 The very act of generating an image, therefore, becomes a political act, a site of what Shaw et al. describe as "social struggles over visual meaning".5 The seemingly innocuous request for "a beautiful woman" or "a successful leader" defaults to Western archetypes, not because of any inherent neutrality in the data, but because the data itself is a reflection of historical and ongoing power imbalances.6 This process perpetuates a hegemonic ideology that fails to capture the diversity of human experience and risks amplifying discriminatory practices.8

This report offers a rigorous methodology for diagnosing and ultimately dismantling this visual flattening. It is structured into four parts. Part I establishes the decolonial theoretical framework, arguing that the generative AI industry mirrors the structure of a colonial metropole, built on data extractivism and an imposed "coloniality of knowledge." Part II presents a detailed diagnostic analysis using a multi-lens framework to systematically identify the mechanisms of cultural hegemony at play in diffusion outputs. Part III examines the political and economic ecosystem—from prompt marketplaces to platform governance—that reinforces and profits from this visual homogenization. Finally, Part IV moves from diagnosis to praxis, proposing a suite of actionable, decolonial strategies for artists, researchers, and developers to reclaim visual sovereignty and work towards a more equitable and pluralistic generative future.

## **Part I: The Algorithmic Metropole — Unpacking the Coloniality of Generative Vision**

To comprehend the depth of visual flattening in generative AI, it is essential to first analyze the system's architecture through a decolonial lens. The structure of the generative AI industry does not merely contain bias; it is foundationally organized along lines that mirror historical colonial power dynamics, creating what can be termed an "algorithmic metropole" that extracts resources from a digital periphery to consolidate its own cultural and economic power.

### **Data Extractivism and the Digital Metropole**

The supply chain of generative AI operates as a modern form of colonial extractivism.9 The vast datasets that form the bedrock of models like Stable Diffusion and Midjourney, such as LAION-5B, are created by scraping billions of image-text pairs from the global internet.10 This process treats the entire digital world—the "periphery"—as a raw resource to be harvested, often without the consent, credit, or compensation of the original creators.12 This data is then processed, refined, and used to train powerful foundation models within the technological and economic centers of the Global North—the "metropole".1 This dynamic recapitulates the historical extraction of raw materials from colonized lands, which were then manufactured into finished goods in the imperial center and sold back to the colonies, reinforcing economic dependency.

This neocolonial structure is further evidenced by the hidden human labor that underpins these "automated" systems. To train models like ChatGPT to avoid producing toxic content, OpenAI outsourced the traumatic work of sifting through graphic material to low-paid data workers in Kenya.1 This "microwork"—repetitive, low-wage digital labor—is a critical component of the AI economy, yet it remains largely invisible, performed by a "ghost workforce" in the Global South that cleans and moderates the data extracted from their own digital ecosystems.15 This constitutes a clear form of algorithmic exploitation, where AI actors take advantage of people through unfair and unethical means, perpetuating a metropole-periphery dynamic that centralizes power and profit in the hands of a few corporations.14

### **The Coloniality of Knowledge and Epistemic Justice**

The colonial logic extends beyond data extraction into the very "mind" of the AI. Kush Varshney's concept of the "coloniality of knowledge" provides a crucial framework for understanding how AI alignment itself is a colonial practice.9 The process of teaching an AI "desired values" is dominated by Western philosophical traditions such as deontology and consequentialism. Metropole companies impose seemingly universal values like "helpfulness" and "harmlessness," which are in fact culturally specific and mask a moral absolutism that disregards context-dependent ethics.9 This imposition amounts to a form of "epistemicide"—the silencing and erasure of other ways of knowing.9

Generative AI, in its current state, actively diminishes what anthropologists recognize as Indigenous Knowledge Systems (IKS), which include unique perceptual and interpretative frameworks for understanding the world.1 By being trained on datasets that overwhelmingly represent Euro-American cultures and aligned according to Western ethical norms, these models reify and perpetuate a Eurocentric methodology, unconsciously imposing it on the vast diversity of human cultures.1 This is not a failure of the technology to be "neutral," but rather a successful implementation of a technology that was never designed to be pluralistic. The assumption that data is objective or that machines can rise above human prejudice is a dangerous myth that serves to obscure these deep-rooted inequities.1 True epistemic justice requires not just diversifying the data, but decolonizing the very frameworks of knowledge and value that govern the models.9

### **From Data Colonialism to Enunciative Colonialism**

The colonial logic embedded within generative AI represents a profound evolution from what has been previously termed "data colonialism".17 While the extraction of data is a foundational injustice, the core function of generative models introduces a deeper, more insidious form of appropriation. This can be understood by connecting the political economy of data with the semiotic concept of "enunciative praxis".18 Enunciation, in semiotics, is the very act of producing an utterance or statement; it is the act of speaking, writing, or, in this case, visualizing.19 Generative models are "enunciational machines" that learn from a vast archive of past cultural productions (the dataset) and then generate new visual utterances (images).18

When these models are trained on data extracted from the global periphery, the power to "enunciate"—to visually speak for and represent a culture—is seized by the algorithmic metropole. The problem transcends the mere theft of data; it becomes the theft of the enunciative function itself. The model does not simply *use* a culture's data; it positions itself as the new, authoritative storyteller for that culture, capable of generating endless variations of its visual language. This process, which can be termed **Enunciative Colonialism**, is a re-creation of culture stripped of its memory, context, and the consent of its community.20 An AI can be prompted to "draw a Siberian shaman" or "write a Ghanaian folktale" without any engagement with the people to whom these traditions belong, producing a hollow simulation that masquerades as authentic representation.21 This is a more profound form of algorithmic dispossession, moving from the appropriation of resources to the appropriation of the very act of cultural self-expression.15

## **Part II: A LENS-Based Diagnostic Framework for Visual Hegemony**

To systematically diagnose the mechanisms of cultural hegemony in generative AI, this report employs a multi-faceted analytical framework. Each "lens" provides a distinct angle for interrogating the models, moving from the dataset to the final output and exposing the specific points at which visual flattening and cultural erasure occur.

### **1\. The Typological Drift Lens: From Dataset to Default**

The journey from a diverse collection of global images to a homogenized AI output can be understood as a process of "typological drift." This aesthetic regression is not random but is structurally determined by the model's architecture and the ideological biases embedded in its training and filtering processes.

#### **Mapping Aesthetic Regression**

Drawing on the concept of "enunciative praxis," generative models function by deconstructing an archive of past images into statistical patterns and then reconfiguring them into new enunciations.18 This process inherently produces a "compressed" and "averaged" way of seeing, where the model learns the most probable visual associations for a given concept.22 Consequently, the output drifts towards the statistical mean of the training data. For concepts that are globally diverse but dominated by Western examples in the dataset—such as "wedding" or "leader"—the model's output will inevitably regress toward the hegemonic archetype (e.g., a white dress, a man in a suit).23 This drift can be visualized through semantic maps that trace the path from a diverse set of cultural artifacts, such as those found in a geographically balanced dataset like GeoDE 24, to the flattened, stereotypical outputs generated by a model like Midjourney.

#### **The Hegemony of the "Aesthetic" Score**

This regression is actively accelerated by the curation of training data. Datasets like LAION-Aesthetics, which are highly influential for training models to produce "beautiful" images, are not raw collections. They are subsets of larger datasets like LAION-5B that have been filtered using a predictive model trained to identify "aesthetic" quality.25 This act of filtering is a powerful ideological intervention. As research indicates, these aesthetic rating systems are themselves biased, reflecting the taste preferences of a narrow, predominantly Western demographic.26 The result is the reinforcement of a limited cultural capital, where a specific, Eurocentric definition of beauty is encoded as a technical objective. The pursuit of a high "aesthetic score" thus becomes a mechanism for purging visual diversity and enforcing a hegemonic taste regime at the dataset level.

#### **Over-represented Archetypes and Default Styles**

The outcome of this typological drift is the dominance of a few over-represented visual archetypes in the default outputs of major platforms. Styles such as "photorealism," "cinematic lighting," and "hyperrealism" are not neutral descriptors but are stylistic regimes deeply rooted in Western visual traditions, particularly Hollywood cinema and European art history.3 Studies confirm that models fine-tuned for specific artistic styles exhibit significant bias, often amplifying the dominant features of that style at the expense of others.27 A prompt for a "portrait" is far more likely to yield an image with chiaroscuro lighting reminiscent of Rembrandt than one reflecting the flat, vibrant planes of Mughal miniature painting, unless explicitly and laboriously specified. This default stylization ensures that the colonial gaze is not just an option but the path of least resistance.

### **2\. The Failure Stack Lens: An Archive of Epistemic Blind Spots**

The inaccuracies and failures of generative models in rendering non-Western cultural artifacts should not be dismissed as mere technical glitches. They are, in fact, a "failure stack"—a layered record of the model's epistemic limitations and cultural incompetence.28 Cataloging these failures systematically reveals the profound gaps in the model's "knowledge" of the world, gaps that are a direct result of a biased training corpus and a coloniality of knowledge that privileges Western epistemologies.1 These failures are the visible symptoms of a system that has been taught to see the world through a narrow and distorted lens.

#### **Case Studies in Misrepresentation**

* **Pan-Indigenization and Indigenous Erasure:** A recurring failure mode is what has been termed "pan-Indigenization," where models generate a synthetic, "global average" of Indigeneity that is detached from any specific, place-based culture.20 This is a form of digital dispossession that erases the sovereignty and particularity of distinct Indigenous nations. For instance, AI-generated "Aboriginal artwork" found on platforms like Adobe Stock features "culturally meaningless" markings and homogenizes the unique artistic styles of hundreds of different language groups across Australia, constituting a form of digital appropriation and exploitation.29 Similarly, the content policies of platforms like DALL-E have been criticized for over-correcting, completely blocking the depiction of Indigenous peoples even in respectful historical or artistic contexts, which results in total erasure rather than protection from harm.30  
* **Semiotic Collapse in Religious Iconography:** The models' failures are particularly egregious in the domain of religious symbolism. AI-generated images of Hindu deities consistently fail to adhere to established iconographic rules (*śilpa śāstras*). Deities are rendered with the wrong number of limbs or fingers, incorrect *vahanas* (animal vehicles), or holding generic objects instead of their prescribed *astras* (divine weapons) like the *shankha* (conch) or *chakra* (discus) for Vishnu.31 From a devotional perspective, these are not minor aesthetic errors; they are semiotic failures that render the image  
  *khandit* (broken, defiled) and disrespectful, stripping the symbols of their sacred meaning and collapsing them into generic fantasy tropes.31 Similar failures have been documented in attempts to render Islamic architecture, where nuanced historical elements are blended into generic, exoticized forms, demonstrating an inability to capture intangible cultural heritage.33  
* **Stereotyping in African and Southeast Asian Representation:** The models' training data, which is geographically biased towards North America and Europe, leads to poor performance and stereotyping for other regions.34 AI models frequently misrepresent African cultural expressions, decontextualizing textile patterns and traditional motifs into a generic "tribal" aesthetic.35 Artists from Southeast Asia have also reported significant challenges; for example, Filipino artist Patrick Cabral found that AI models consistently failed to understand regional concepts, producing inaccurate depictions of traditional clothing and architecture. This forced him to manually intervene by creating his own code to train the model on specific Filipino motifs, highlighting the immense labor required to counteract the system's default ignorance.36

### **3\. The Cognitive Friction & Prompt Burden Lens**

The rhetoric of user empowerment surrounding generative AI often obscures a significant and unequally distributed form of labor: the "prompt burden." This refers to the cognitive, linguistic, and creative effort required to elicit a desired image from a model. This burden is not uniform; it is significantly heavier for users attempting to generate imagery that falls outside the model's hegemonic, Eurocentric defaults.

#### **Quantifying the Labor of Representation**

While a simple prompt like "a beautiful woman" or "a powerful CEO" defaults to images of white individuals conforming to Western beauty and corporate standards 7, generating a culturally specific and accurate image requires immense effort. Users from non-Western cultures must engage in laborious over-specification, crafting highly detailed prompts that often include historical, architectural, and symbolic keywords.37 This process frequently involves extensive "negative prompting" (specifying what

*not* to include) to fight against the model's tendency to insert stereotypical or incorrect elements. This unequal distribution of labor constitutes a cognitive and creative tax, where marginalized users must work significantly harder to achieve a baseline level of cultural fidelity that is granted by default to users operating within the hegemonic norm.

#### **The Hegemony of Intention**

This dynamic exposes the flaw in the dominant industry paradigm of "see what you intend".5 This mantra presupposes a frictionless translation from user intent to machine output, a premise that only holds true when the user's intent aligns with the model's pre-existing biases. For a user whose cultural world is underrepresented in the training data, their "intention" is met with resistance from the model's latent space. The struggle to overcome this resistance—to force the model to render a reality it does not easily "know"—is a clear manifestation of cognitive friction. The burden of making oneself understood falls squarely on the marginalized user, while the system remains unaccountable for its own epistemic limitations.

#### **"Conscious Prompting" as a Double-Edged Sword**

In response to these biases, communities have developed practices like "Conscious Prompting," an approach that involves carefully crafting prompts to actively subvert and mitigate stereotypes.38 This can be a powerful act of resistance and creative reclamation, as seen in the work of artists who use prompting to decolonize the AI's gaze. However, relying on "Conscious Prompting" as the primary solution is problematic. It places the full responsibility for ethical image generation onto the individual user, effectively absolving the developers and platform owners of their duty to build more equitable systems. It frames bias as a user-side problem to be solved with better prompts, rather than a systemic issue rooted in the model's architecture and the political economy of its creation.

### **4\. The Cross-Linguistic Visual Divergence Lens**

The cultural hegemony of generative models is deeply intertwined with linguistic hegemony. The vast majority of large-scale image-text datasets, such as LAION, are overwhelmingly composed of English-language pairs scraped from the web.11 This creates a profound linguistic bias, where English acts as the de facto

*lingua franca* not just for prompting, but for the underlying conceptual structure of the model's visual world.8

#### **English as the Lingua Franca of Vision**

Even when models are multilingual and can accept prompts in various languages, their internal "understanding" is often mediated through an English-centric latent space. A prompt in Swahili or Tagalog may be translated or mapped to an English-dominated conceptual cluster before image generation begins. This means that the visual output is still tethered to an Anglophone worldview, regardless of the input language. Research has shown that while multilingual prompts can sometimes increase diversity, they often magnify existing gender and cultural stereotypes, and the results vary unpredictably between languages.41 The expectation that a concept should render similarly across languages is frequently unmet, revealing the model's uneven cultural and linguistic competence.41

#### **Auditing Divergence and Collapse**

A systematic audit is required to map the contours of this linguistic bias. By running prompts for the same core concept across a wide range of languages, we can reveal where the model's visual output diverges in culturally meaningful ways and where it collapses into a single, hegemonic stereotype. For example, does the prompt for "a spiritual temple" generate a mosque when prompted in Arabic, a Shinto shrine in Japanese, and a Hindu mandir in Hindi? Or does it default to a generic Greco-Roman or East Asian pastiche in all cases? This comparative analysis can make the model's hidden cultural defaults visible.

### **5\. The Symbolic Relativity & Semiotic Collapse Lens**

The process of generating an image from a text prompt is an act of intersemiotic translation—a translation between sign systems, from the verbal to the visual.22 A decolonial semiotic analysis reveals that in this translation process, generative models frequently cause a "semiotic collapse," where culturally specific symbols are stripped of their meaning and flattened into generic, decontextualized signifiers. This is not a neutral act of simplification but a violent process of misinterpretation that re-inscribes the colonial gaze.

#### **From Sign to Simulacrum**

In semiotics, a sign consists of a *signifier* (the form, e.g., the word "cross" or the image of a cross) and a *signified* (the concept, e.g., Christianity, sacrifice, intersection). The relationship between them is culturally determined.44 Generative models are adept at mimicking signifiers but are often ignorant of their signifieds. They learn statistical correlations between pixels and text labels without grasping the underlying cultural, historical, or spiritual context.45 When a model generates an image of a Hindu deity, it is not reproducing the deity's sacred form; it is simulating a cluster of visual characteristics statistically associated with the prompt.45 The result is a simulacrum: a copy without an original, an image detached from the reality and meaning it purports to represent. A sacred symbol is thus transformed from a sign, rich with meaning, into a hollow aesthetic object.

#### **The Flattening of Meaning**

This process leads to a catastrophic flattening of meaning. We see this when models misrepresent Indigenous art, reducing complex visual languages to "culturally meaningless" patterns.29 For example, the sacred markings of Tā Moko, which encode genealogy and status, are rendered as generic "tribal" swirls. The intricate patterns of Kente cloth, where each design has a specific proverbial meaning, are reduced to a colorful geometric texture. This is a form of decontextualization that treats culture as a style to be sampled, rather than a system of meaning to be respected. The model's output becomes a form of "exoticism," where the visual markers of a culture are used for their aesthetic appeal while their significance is erased. This is particularly evident in the representation of non-Western religions, where sacred figures are often transformed into generic "fantasy" or "demonic" characters, reflecting a profound lack of cultural and spiritual literacy embedded in the system.31

#### **The Colonial Gaze, Re-inscribed Algorithmically**

This semiotic collapse is not politically neutral; it is a digital recapitulation of the colonial gaze.46 Historically, colonial powers used visual media—from photography to ethnographic displays and Orientalist art—to construct the colonized as the "Other": primitive, exotic, sexualized, or inferior.46 This gaze objectified and controlled the representation of colonized peoples. Generative models, trained on a vast visual archive that is saturated with the legacy of this colonial gaze, learn to reproduce its logic. When a model generates a "pan-Indigenous" figure 20 or an exoticized pastiche of Islamic architecture 33, it is not inventing a new bias. It is amplifying a historical one, using the power of automated generation to perpetuate colonial stereotypes at an unprecedented scale and speed. The machine, in its supposed objectivity, becomes the perfect instrument for laundering and re-inscribing these centuries-old power dynamics into the visual fabric of the 21st century.

## **Part III: The Political Economy of the Prompt**

The cultural hegemony of generative AI is not merely a technical or data-driven phenomenon. It is actively produced and reinforced by a political and economic ecosystem that incentivizes visual homogenization. Analyzing this ecosystem through the lenses of platform governance and affective trust reveals how market forces and emotional impacts conspire to entrench the default aesthetic.

### **1\. Prompt Ecosystem & Platform Governance**

The emergence of prompt marketplaces like PromptBase, PromptHero, and FlowGPT has created a new economic layer in the generative AI stack.47 While these platforms ostensibly democratize prompt engineering by allowing creators to monetize their skills, their underlying business model inadvertently fosters and rewards the production of hegemonic aesthetics.

#### **The Marketplace of Ideas and the Commodification of Hegemony**

These platforms function as marketplaces where users can buy and sell prompt templates designed for models like Midjourney and DALL-E.50 An analysis of the most popular and top-selling prompts on these sites reveals a clear trend towards commercially viable styles that align with dominant Western media tropes: hyperrealism, photorealism, cyberpunk, fantasy art, and various forms of logo and 3D asset generation.51 This is not surprising; sellers are incentivized to create prompts that appeal to the broadest possible market, which is a market already conditioned by hegemonic visual culture.

This dynamic creates a powerful economic feedback loop. The market quantifies and rewards visual hegemony. It is not just that the models have a default bias; an entire economy has emerged to monetize and amplify that bias. The platform's own governance and quality control mechanisms can exacerbate this. For example, PromptBase's review process rejects prompts that are "too simple" or "overly complex," a standard that may unintentionally filter out highly specific and nuanced cultural prompts in favor of more generalizable, "template-style" prompts that are easier to adapt and sell.47 This structure actively disincentivizes the labor-intensive work of crafting culturally faithful prompts, as they are likely to be less profitable and may not even pass the platform's review. This transforms cultural flattening from a mere technical flaw into a structural economic imperative. Furthermore, this business model is fraught with security vulnerabilities like "prompt template stealing," where attackers can reverse-engineer proprietary prompts from public sample images, a threat that disproportionately harms the individual creators upon whose labor the platforms are built.54

### **2\. Affective Trust Lens: Cultural Affinity & Emotional Resonance**

The technical and economic analysis of visual flattening must be paired with a qualitative understanding of its human impact. The "Affective Trust Lens" examines the emotional and psychological consequences of cultural misrepresentation, revealing a profound dissonance that erodes trust and inflicts a unique form of harm.

#### **The Affective Dissonance of Misrepresentation**

For creators and community members from marginalized or underrepresented cultures, encountering AI-generated misrepresentations of their heritage can be a deeply alienating experience. This creates what can be termed "affective dissonance": a painful gap between their internal, lived cultural memory and the AI's shallow, often grotesque, interpretation.55 When an Indigenous artist sees sacred designs turned into meaningless "tribal" patterns, or a Hindu devotee sees a revered deity depicted with monstrous features, the response is not one of simple disagreement but often of anger, sadness, and a sense of violation.29 This emotional toll is a significant, yet often overlooked, consequence of algorithmic bias. It is a form of harm that transforms the technology from a potential creative partner into an agent of cultural violence and erasure.56

This experience is not universal. For a user operating within the hegemonic culture, the AI's outputs are more likely to feel familiar and "true," fostering a sense of affective trust. For the marginalized user, however, the constant failures and misrepresentations lead to an erosion of that trust, reinforcing their alienation from a technological ecosystem that does not see or value their world accurately.58

#### **The "Aesthetic Uncanny Valley" as Affective Harm**

The harm of misrepresentation is often most acute not when the AI fails completely, but when it gets *partially* correct. The model's ability to produce images that are recognizable yet deeply flawed creates a phenomenon that can be described as an **"Aesthetic Uncanny Valley."** In robotics, the uncanny valley describes the feeling of revulsion caused by a humanoid that is almost, but not quite, human. Similarly, when an AI generates an image of a cultural artifact or symbol that is close enough to be identified but contains specific, jarring errors—such as a deity with the wrong number of fingers or a sacred pattern rendered incorrectly—it evokes a powerful sense of unease and disrespect.31

The image is not just inaccurate; it feels like a grotesque parody. Its proximity to correctness makes the failure more offensive than a completely abstract or unrelated image would be. This "Aesthetic Uncanny Valley" is a specific form of affective harm that generates feelings of revulsion and emotional distress.55 It complicates the simple goal of "improving" model accuracy, suggesting that a partially accurate but contextually ignorant representation can be more damaging than no representation at all. It is a testament to the fact that cultural fluency is not a matter of statistical approximation but of deep, contextual understanding—a quality these models fundamentally lack.

#### **Decolonial AI Art as Affective Repair**

In the face of this affective harm, some artists are turning the tool against itself, using AI for what might be called "affective repair".59 The work of Brazilian artist Mayara Ferrão exemplifies this decolonial practice. Ferrão uses AI to revisit and reimagine colonial archives, which historically depicted Black and Indigenous women in roles of servitude and sorrow. By prompting the AI to create new images that envision moments of love, tenderness, and queer affection within these historical contexts, she actively re-scripts the colonial gaze embedded in the data.60 This work is not about historical revisionism but about expanding the collective imagination, creating a visual archive of joy and resilience where one was denied. In doing so, Ferrão's practice demonstrates how the technology's affective impact is not predetermined. In the hands of a politically conscious creator, the AI can become a tool for restoring affective trust and generating new, empowering narratives for communities that have been systematically erased from the visual record.60

## **Part IV: Strategic Interventions and Decolonial Futures**

Diagnosing the coloniality of generative AI is a critical first step, but it is insufficient without a corresponding praxis of resistance and reconstruction. This final section moves from analysis to action, outlining a dual strategy for dismantling visual hegemony. This involves both grassroots interventions by artists and communities to reclaim visual sovereignty, and a call for systemic change from developers and platform governors to re-engineer the technological stack for epistemic justice.

### **1\. Dismantling the Defaults: A Toolkit for Cultural Fidelity**

For artists and cultural practitioners from marginalized communities, the most immediate path to resisting visual flattening lies in a strategic migration away from closed, proprietary platforms towards an open-source, user-controlled tool stack. This approach shifts power from the corporate metropole to the creative periphery, transforming the user from a passive consumer of biased outputs into an active agent of cultural production.

#### **Open-Source Models and the Paradox of Decolonial Debt**

While foundation models like Stable Diffusion are trained on the same biased datasets as their closed-source counterparts, their open-source nature is a crucial political distinction.61 It allows for direct intervention, modification, and fine-tuning, creating a potential site of resistance. However, this potential comes at a cost. The turn to open-source solutions creates a form of

**decolonial debt**: it offloads the immense labor and computational cost of fixing the system's biases onto the very communities that have been marginalized by it. While this provides agency, it also risks reinforcing existing inequalities, as communities with fewer technical and financial resources will be less able to perform this corrective labor. True epistemic justice, therefore, requires not only open tools but also funded, community-led initiatives to support this essential work.

#### **Fine-Tuning with LoRA for Cultural Specificity**

Despite this paradox, technical interventions remain a powerful strategy. **Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA)** has emerged as a particularly potent decolonial tool.63 LoRA allows users to fine-tune a massive foundation model on a small, curated dataset of specific images with relatively low computational cost. Artists can train custom LoRA models on datasets of their own cultural heritage—such as the patterns of Yangshao pottery, the style of Kalighat paintings, or the motifs of blue calico textiles—to "teach" the base model a new, highly specific aesthetic vocabulary.64 This is a direct method for creating and activating a "Dataset of Lost Styles," enabling the generation of images with high cultural fidelity that would be impossible to achieve with the base model alone. This process allows for the digital preservation and creative reinterpretation of endangered cultural art forms.64

#### **Compositional Control with ControlNet**

Beyond style, achieving symbolic accuracy requires structural control. **ControlNet** is a neural network architecture that provides this by allowing users to condition the image generation process on a visual input, such as a line drawing, depth map, or human pose skeleton.67 This is a game-changing capability for cultural representation. An artist can use ControlNet to enforce the correct composition of a sacred space, the precise posture (

*mudra*) of a deity, or the specific structure of an architectural element, overriding the model's biased priors.70 By providing an explicit visual guide, ControlNet ensures that key symbolic and structural elements are rendered with fidelity, preventing the semiotic collapse that is common in unconstrained generation. The combination of LoRA for stylistic control and ControlNet for compositional control forms a powerful toolkit for preserving the integrity of both tangible and intangible cultural heritage.66

### **2\. Towards Epistemic Justice: Re-engineering the Stack**

While user-side interventions are vital, they cannot be the only solution. Lasting change requires a fundamental re-engineering of the generative AI stack, from data collection to model alignment and user interface design.

#### **Radical Data Diversity and Provenance**

The industry must move beyond the extractive and ethically fraught practice of indiscriminately scraping the web. The future of equitable AI depends on the creation of ethically sourced, geographically diverse, and community-governed datasets. This involves funding and supporting initiatives like GeoDE, which uses crowdsourcing to build a balanced dataset of objects from six world regions, free of personally identifiable information.24 Furthermore, rigorous data auditing must become standard practice. Frameworks like G-AUDIT, which can quantify dataset bias across multiple attributes

*before* model training, offer a path toward identifying and mitigating sources of bias at the earliest possible stage.72 Transparency is paramount; datasets must be accompanied by detailed datasheets that document their provenance, composition, and limitations, allowing for genuine accountability.73

#### **Decolonial AI Alignment and Agonistic Interfaces**

The process of AI alignment must be radically decolonized. This requires abandoning the pretense of universal ethics and embracing a pluriversal approach that respects a multiplicity of worldviews and value systems.2 Drawing on Varshney's work, a decolonial alignment framework would be built on three forms of openness: openness of models (allowing communities to fine-tune them), openness to society (co-creating values with affected communities), and openness to excluded knowledges (incorporating values from non-textual and non-Western epistemologies).9 This means designing systems that can accommodate context-specific notions of right and wrong, such as the Hindu concept of

*viseśa-dharma*, rather than imposing a single, absolute moral code.9

This philosophical shift must be reflected in the user interface. The current paradigm, which prioritizes actualizing user intention, often does so by opaquely "correcting" for bias, as seen in the Gemini controversy, a move that can feel inauthentic and paternalistic.74 An alternative, proposed by Shaw et al., is the

**agonistic interface**.5 Grounded in the political theory of agonistic pluralism, such an interface would not hide or smooth over conflict. Instead, it would actively surface competing visual interpretations of a prompt, confronting the user with the inherent political tensions and struggles over meaning in image generation. This approach would not promise a "neutral" or "unbiased" output but would instead empower users to reflect on, engage with, and consciously navigate the sociopolitical dimensions of their creative choices. It transforms the interface from a tool of seamless production into a space for critical reflection.

## **Conclusion: Reclaiming the Visual Field**

This report has undertaken a systematic decolonial critique of text-to-image generative models, diagnosing the mechanisms of cultural hegemony that result in the widespread phenomenon of visual flattening. We have introduced the concept of **Promptual Colonialism** to describe how these systems, far from being neutral tools, are embedded within a "colonial matrix of power" that is rooted in biased data, Eurocentric alignment, and a political economy that rewards homogenization. The core logic of these systems has evolved beyond mere data colonialism to enact a more profound **Enunciative Colonialism**, appropriating the very capacity of cultures to represent themselves.

Through the application of our multi-lens diagnostic framework, we have demonstrated how this hegemony manifests at every level of the generative stack. The **Typological Drift Lens** revealed how aesthetic regression towards a Western mean is hard-coded into the model's training and curation. The **Failure Stack Lens** provided a catalog of epistemic blind spots, documenting how models systematically misrepresent and decontextualize the visual languages of Indigenous, African, South Asian, and other non-Western cultures, often resulting in an **"Aesthetic Uncanny Valley"** that constitutes a unique form of affective harm. The **Cognitive Friction** and **Cross-Linguistic Divergence** lenses quantified the unequal burden placed on non-Anglophone and non-Western users, who must labor against the model's defaults to achieve basic fidelity. Finally, the **Symbolic Relativity**, **Prompt Ecosystem**, and **Affective Trust** lenses exposed the processes of semiotic collapse, the economic incentives that fuel it, and the resulting erosion of trust among marginalized creative communities.

The path forward cannot be a simple technical fix. It demands a two-pronged approach that is both political and practical. The first prong is **grassroots resistance and reclamation**. Artists and cultural communities must be empowered to seize the means of visual production. This involves the strategic adoption of open-source tools like **LoRA** and **ControlNet**, not as a perfect solution, but as a decolonial tactic to fine-tune models with their own visual archives and enforce the structural integrity of their cultural symbols. This is an act of reclaiming enunciative power from the algorithmic metropole, though it comes with the burden of "decolonial debt" that must be addressed through community support and funding.

The second prong is **systemic change and accountability** from developers, researchers, and platform governors. This requires a fundamental re-engineering of the stack based on principles of epistemic justice. It means moving towards radical data diversity through transparent, community-governed datasets; embracing decolonial AI alignment that respects pluriversal ethics and context-specific values; and designing **agonistic interfaces** that foster critical engagement rather than seamless, unthinking consumption.

Achieving a truly pluralistic and equitable visual future in the age of AI is not about the futile search for a perfectly unbiased algorithm. It is about the ongoing political struggle to dismantle the colonial power structures embedded in our current technologies and to build new systems founded on cultural sovereignty, participatory design, and mutual respect. This is the urgent and necessary work of decolonizing the machinic gaze.
