# AI’s Struggle with Reality: Why Hallucinations Might Be a Feature, Not a Bug

**Abstract:** Artificial Intelligence (AI) systems, particularly generative models like large language models (LLMs), have demonstrated an uncanny ability to produce information that is fictitious or misleading – a phenomenon often dubbed “AI hallucination.” This paper provides an interdisciplinary examination of AI hallucinations, comparing them with human cognitive processes and exploring their technical, creative, and ethical dimensions. We review the historical evolution of the term *hallucination* in AI literature, from early positive connotations in image processing to its current use denoting factual errors ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=In%201995%2C%20Stephen%20Thaler%20demonstrated,14)) ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=In%20the%20late%202010s%2C%20the,18)). We outline the neural architectures and training dynamics that give rise to hallucinations in LLMs, and survey emerging strategies to detect and mitigate them (such as retrieval augmentation and fine-tuning) ([When AI Gets It Wrong: Addressing AI Hallucinations and Bias \- MIT Sloan Teaching & Learning Technologies](https://mitsloanedtech.mit.edu/ai/basics/addressing-ai-hallucinations-and-bias/#:~:text=1,accuracy%20in%20their%20outputs%20is)) ([Why ChatGPT and Bing Chat are so good at making things up | Ars Technica](https://web.archive.org/web/20230611024338/https://arstechnica.com/information-technology/2023/04/why-ai-chatbots-are-the-ultimate-bs-machines-and-how-people-hope-to-fix-them/#:~:text=Further%20Reading)). In parallel, we draw upon neuroscience and psychology to illuminate how the brain’s penchant for **“controlled hallucination”** in perception ([The Brain as a Prediction Machine: The Key to Consciousness? | Psychology Today](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/blog/finding-purpose/202201/the-brain-prediction-machine-the-key-consciousness#:~:text=7,in%20Vancouver%2C%20BC%2C%20%2089)) and the mechanisms of human creativity (e.g. divergent thinking and confabulation) might mirror and inform AI’s behavior. While AI hallucinations pose risks – from factual misinformation to erosion of public trust – they also hint at creative potential, prompting debate on whether these “bugs” can be reframed as features under the right conditions ([“Hallucinating” AIs Sound Creative, but Let’s Not Celebrate Being Wrong | The MIT Press Reader](https://thereader.mitpress.mit.edu/hallucinating-ais-sound-creative-but-lets-not-celebrate-being-wrong/#:~:text=When%20Sam%20Altman%2C%20OpenAI%E2%80%99s%20CEO%2C,the%20many%20concerns%20about%20accuracy)) ([Harnessing Hallucinations to Make AI More Creative | Psychology Today](https://www.psychologytoday.com/intl/blog/the-digital-self/202501/harnessing-hallucinations-to-make-ai-more-creative#:~:text=Key%20points)). Through comparative analysis of language-only versus multimodal AI systems, discussion of ethical safeguards, and examples of real-world applications in art, science, and education, we synthesize insights across disciplines. We argue that understanding and managing AI hallucinations will require collaboration between technologists, cognitive scientists, and ethicists to harness AI’s imaginative capabilities while ensuring reliability. We conclude with prospective directions for research and design that balance innovation with grounding in reality.

## **Introduction**

The emergence of powerful generative AI models like GPT-3 and GPT-4 has brought unprecedented capabilities in language understanding and content creation. These models can produce fluent text, answer questions, and even create images, but they often do so with a notable caveat: they sometimes generate information that is substantially incorrect or entirely fabricated while presenting it with confidence. Such outputs have been termed “hallucinations” in the AI community, by loose analogy to human perceptual hallucinations ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=In%20the%20field%20of%20artificial,6)). For example, an AI chatbot might state a false historical “fact” or cite a non-existent scientific paper with complete assurance. Recent analyses highlighted the scale of this issue – by 2023, some chatbots were estimated to include false information in roughly **20–30%** of their responses ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=generated%20content,3)). This high incidence of AI-generated falsehoods has raised alarms regarding the **reliability** of AI systems in real-world deployments ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=generated%20content,3)). The problem is especially critical in domains where accuracy is paramount, such as healthcare, law, or scientific research.

Beyond practical concerns, AI hallucinations spark fundamental questions: *Why* do these systems hallucinate, and what does this reveal about the nature of AI “intelligence” versus human cognition? The notion of an AI hallucinating blurs the line between a straightforward software bug and a deeper analogy to how humans sometimes perceive or imagine things that are not there. Indeed, some researchers argue the term “hallucination” is an anthropomorphic misnomer that might mislead people about how AI works ([Why ChatGPT and Bing Chat are so good at making things up | Ars Technica](https://web.archive.org/web/20230611024338/https://arstechnica.com/information-technology/2023/04/why-ai-chatbots-are-the-ultimate-bs-machines-and-how-people-hope-to-fix-them/#:~:text=In%20academic%20literature%2C%20AI%20researchers,responsibility%20for%20the%20outputs%20themselves)). Others, however, find the analogy thought-provoking, as it forces us to consider whether AI’s generative mistakes have parallels in human memory errors or creative imagination ([Why ChatGPT and Bing Chat are so good at making things up | Ars Technica](https://web.archive.org/web/20230611024338/https://arstechnica.com/information-technology/2023/04/why-ai-chatbots-are-the-ultimate-bs-machines-and-how-people-hope-to-fix-them/#:~:text=vein%2C%20we%20feel%20the%20term,work%2C%20as%20we%27ll%20explore%20below)). These interdisciplinary considerations make the study of AI hallucinations not just a technical challenge but also a window into the **comparative study of minds** – artificial and natural.

**Thesis statement:** In this paper, we undertake a comprehensive analysis of AI hallucinations through an interdisciplinary lens. We will (1) review the concept’s historical and theoretical underpinnings, (2) examine the technical mechanisms in modern AI that lead to hallucinations and how researchers are trying to curb them, (3) compare the behaviors of different AI systems (text-based LLMs vs. multimodal models) regarding hallucination, (4) draw insights from neuroscience and psychology about human hallucinations and creativity to shed light on AI’s behavior, (5) discuss the ethical and societal implications of AI outputs that deviate from reality, and (6) explore real-world use cases where harnessing or mitigating hallucinations is pertinent. By integrating perspectives from computer science, cognitive science, and ethics, we aim to deepen understanding of AI hallucinations and outline strategies for future AI that is both creative and trustworthy. The following sections provide a structured exploration of these themes, beginning with a review of how the concept of hallucination entered the AI discourse and how it is defined in current research.

## **Literature Review**

**Historical Perspectives on “Hallucination” in AI:** The term “hallucination” in AI has evolved significantly over time. Its earliest usage in the mid-1990s was notably *literal* – researchers like Stephen Thaler explored how artificial neural networks could generate phantom sensations or imagery when noise was introduced, effectively causing the network to “see” patterns that weren’t truly there ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=In%201995%2C%20Stephen%20Thaler%20demonstrated,14)). Thaler’s work demonstrated that random perturbations in a neural network’s weights could lead to the network producing outputs decoupled from any real input, analogously to a human hallucinating ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=In%201995%2C%20Stephen%20Thaler%20demonstrated,14)). In the early 2000s, however, the term took on a very different (and positive) connotation in computer vision. For instance, **“face hallucination”** referred to techniques for enhancing or upsampling images – essentially, adding **imagined detail** to a low-resolution face image to create a higher-resolution one ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=In%20the%20early%202000s%2C%20the,16)). This usage implied constructive imagination rather than error: the system was intentionally **hallucinating details** to improve image quality, with “hallucination” meaning clever guesswork to fill in missing data. It was only in the late 2010s that *hallucination* shifted to its current meaning of *undesirable*, incorrect outputs in AI. Notably, by 2017–2018, researchers in machine translation and image recognition started using “hallucination” to describe instances where neural models produced content entirely unrelated to the input – a clear error ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=In%20the%20late%202010s%2C%20the,18)). An example from 2017 is neural translation systems sometimes generating sentences that had no overlap with the source text, essentially making things up when they failed to find a good translation ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=In%20the%20late%202010s%2C%20the,18)). Similarly, in 2018, adversarial attacks on vision systems caused them to detect objects that did not exist in an image, an effect also labeled as hallucination ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=In%20the%20late%202010s%2C%20the,18)). By the early 2020s, with the rise of LLM-based chatbots, *hallucination* became a household term. Media reports and AI developers frequently used it to describe the peculiar tendency of models like GPT-3 or ChatGPT to output fluent but incorrect information ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=For%20example%2C%20a%20chatbot%20,3)) ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=23.%20,why%20experts%20don%27t%20like%20the)). The Cambridge Dictionary even updated its definition of “hallucinate” in 2023 to include “to produce a false or incorrect response in the context of AI,” cementing the term’s place in popular discourse ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=The%20term%20,to%20describe)) ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=In%202023%2C%20the%20Cambridge%20dictionary,24)).

**Defining AI Hallucinations:** In contemporary AI research, a **hallucination** typically refers to any content generated by an AI that is *demonstrably false or not justified* by the model’s inputs or knowledge base ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=In%20the%20field%20of%20artificial,6)). Ji *et al.* (2022) provide a broad definition in their survey of hallucinations in natural language generation: hallucinations occur when an AI output is *unfaithful* either to the provided source data (in tasks like summarization or translation) or to factual reality in open-ended generation ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=6.%20,Retrieved%2015%20January%202023)). For example, an abstractive summarization model might introduce a detail not present in the source document – this would be considered a hallucination because the detail is invented. In open-ended dialogue, an LLM might state a wrong date for a historical event; this too is a hallucination, as it conflicts with verifiable fact. Importantly, the AI is *not aware* that it is producing false information – it is simply generating the most statistically likely continuation of a prompt based on its training, which can inadvertently include falsehoods. The analogy to human hallucination is thus **imperfect**: in human psychology, a hallucination is usually a false sensory perception (seeing or hearing things that aren’t there) ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=In%20the%20field%20of%20artificial,6)), whereas an AI hallucination is a cognitive error – an artifact of the AI’s generative process yielding untrue statements or images. As one article quipped, the AI isn’t “seeing” things that aren’t there; rather, it’s *“bullshitting”* in a linguistically fluent way ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=In%20the%20field%20of%20artificial,6)) ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=1.%20,ChatGPT%20and%20Bing%20Chat%20are)). Some scholars prefer the term **“confabulation”** for AI, drawing a parallel to a neurological condition where patients fabricate stories to fill memory gaps ([Why ChatGPT and Bing Chat are so good at making things up | Ars Technica](https://web.archive.org/web/20230611024338/https://arstechnica.com/information-technology/2023/04/why-ai-chatbots-are-the-ultimate-bs-machines-and-how-people-hope-to-fix-them/#:~:text=vein%2C%20we%20feel%20the%20term,work%2C%20as%20we%27ll%20explore%20below)). In clinical psychology, confabulation involves no intent to deceive – the person genuinely believes the fabricated memory. Similarly, an AI confabulates by stitching together plausible-sounding pieces of information when it lacks true knowledge, without an understanding that it’s wrong ([Why ChatGPT and Bing Chat are so good at making things up | Ars Technica](https://web.archive.org/web/20230611024338/https://arstechnica.com/information-technology/2023/04/why-ai-chatbots-are-the-ultimate-bs-machines-and-how-people-hope-to-fix-them/#:~:text=vein%2C%20we%20feel%20the%20term,work%2C%20as%20we%27ll%20explore%20below)). This distinction in terminology is more than semantics; it highlights that AI outputs stem from algorithmic pattern-matching rather than purposeful deceit or random hallucination as in a delirious mind. Nonetheless, the term “hallucination” has stuck in the AI field, perhaps because it vividly conveys the sense of *illusory truth* that these AI outputs can project.

**Human Cognition Comparisons:** The emergence of AI hallucinations has prompted comparisons with human mental errors and creative thought processes. On one hand, humans certainly experience misperceptions and memory mistakes: we have optical illusions, false memories, and even pathological hallucinations in conditions like schizophrenia. But are these the right analogies for what AI does? Some experts caution against directly equating AI and human hallucinations, warning that it **“anthropomorphizes the software”**, implying human-like perception in a context where none exists ([Why ChatGPT and Bing Chat are so good at making things up | Ars Technica](https://web.archive.org/web/20230611024338/https://arstechnica.com/information-technology/2023/04/why-ai-chatbots-are-the-ultimate-bs-machines-and-how-people-hope-to-fix-them/#:~:text=In%20academic%20literature%2C%20AI%20researchers,responsibility%20for%20the%20outputs%20themselves)). Indeed, calling an AI’s mistake a hallucination might inadvertently suggest the AI has subjective experiences or a visual imagination, which is not the case – it operates on pattern completion. Critics argue this framing could also allow creators of AI to shirk responsibility, by treating systematic errors as if they were unpredictable quirks (“the AI had a hallucination”) rather than fixable flaws ([Why ChatGPT and Bing Chat are so good at making things up | Ars Technica](https://web.archive.org/web/20230611024338/https://arstechnica.com/information-technology/2023/04/why-ai-chatbots-are-the-ultimate-bs-machines-and-how-people-hope-to-fix-them/#:~:text=mainstream%20because%20some%20people%20feel,responsibility%20for%20the%20outputs%20themselves)). On the other hand, the concept of AI confabulation resonates with cognitive science. AI’s tendency to fill in gaps with plausible fabrications has been likened to the **creative gap-filling** our own brains do ([Why ChatGPT and Bing Chat are so good at making things up | Ars Technica](https://web.archive.org/web/20230611024338/https://arstechnica.com/information-technology/2023/04/why-ai-chatbots-are-the-ultimate-bs-machines-and-how-people-hope-to-fix-them/#:~:text=vein%2C%20we%20feel%20the%20term,work%2C%20as%20we%27ll%20explore%20below)). For instance, in neuropsychology, when the brain’s memory has missing pieces, it may subconsciously invent details to form a coherent story – a process observed in some amnesic patients. This is analogous to how an LLM, when faced with a query beyond its knowledge, will still produce an answer by drawing on whatever associations it can, essentially *guessing* to avoid saying “I don’t know.” Remarkably, this parallel extends to normal cognition as well. Neuroscientist Anil **Seth** describes our everyday perception as a form of “**controlled hallucination**,” wherein the brain constantly generates predictions about the world and corrects them with sensory input ([The Brain as a Prediction Machine: The Key to Consciousness? | Psychology Today](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/blog/finding-purpose/202201/the-brain-prediction-machine-the-key-consciousness#:~:text=7,in%20Vancouver%2C%20BC%2C%20%2089)). Seth famously noted: *“We’re all hallucinating all the time; when we agree about our hallucinations, we call it reality.”* ([The Brain as a Prediction Machine: The Key to Consciousness? | Psychology Today](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/blog/finding-purpose/202201/the-brain-prediction-machine-the-key-consciousness#:~:text=7,in%20Vancouver%2C%20BC%2C%20%2089)). In other words, human perception is an active construction process – normally tethered to reality by our senses, but prone to error when those tethers weaken. This perspective offers a poetic comparison: AI, lacking direct sensory grounding or an understanding of reality, can be seen as **hallucinating uncontrollably**, whereas the human brain hallucinates in a controlled manner, reined in by real-world feedback ([The Brain as a Prediction Machine: The Key to Consciousness? | Psychology Today](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/blog/finding-purpose/202201/the-brain-prediction-machine-the-key-consciousness#:~:text=7,in%20Vancouver%2C%20BC%2C%20%2089)). Additionally, theories of human creativity suggest that generating novel ideas often involves a degree of randomness or “blind variation” akin to hallucination. Campbell’s (1960) classic theory of creativity and its later elaborations by Simonton posit that creative thought consists of a two-stage process: first, a **blind generation** of many idea variants (without full regard for truth or practicality), and second, a **selective retention** of the ideas that prove useful or valid ([ERIC \- EJ577411 \- Donald Campbell's Model of the Creative Process: Creativity as Blind Variation and Selective Retention., Journal of Creative Behavior, 1998](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ577411#:~:text=This%20introductory%20article%20discusses%20a,%28Author%2FCR)). The “blind variation” stage is essentially unguided – much like an AI freely producing an output – and can produce many nonsensical combinations. Humans rely on critical thinking, logic, or external feedback as a selection mechanism to dismiss the nonsense and keep the good ideas ([ERIC \- EJ577411 \- Donald Campbell's Model of the Creative Process: Creativity as Blind Variation and Selective Retention., Journal of Creative Behavior, 1998](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ577411#:~:text=This%20introductory%20article%20discusses%20a,%28Author%2FCR)). In this light, an LLM’s hallucinations can be seen as analogous to the **raw ideation** phase of creativity, lacking the robust filtering that human reasoning or a fact-check might provide. This analogy has inspired some researchers to ask whether *some* level of hallucination is intrinsically tied to creativity in both humans and machines – a theme we will revisit when discussing the potential upside of AI hallucinations.

In summary, the literature reveals that “AI hallucination” is a multifaceted concept. It has roots in both technical jargon and cognitive metaphor. Historically, its meaning shifted from *intended imagination* to *unintended error* as AI models became more complex and autonomous in generation. Definitions coalesced around the idea of outputs that are unfaithful to reality or input data, and debate arose on whether to frame this as hallucination or confabulation. Comparing AI to human cognition, we find intriguing similarities in the gap-filling and creative extrapolation processes, but also key differences in how those processes are constrained. These insights set the stage for examining the technical foundations of why AI models hallucinate and what can be done about it, which we turn to next.

## 

## **Technical Foundations of AI Hallucinations**

**Neural Architectures and Mechanisms:** Modern AI models that exhibit hallucinations are typically based on deep neural networks trained on large-scale data. For language tasks, the dominant architecture is the **Transformer**\-based language model (e.g. GPT series, BERT, etc.). These models are trained with a simple objective: given some preceding text, predict the most likely next word (or sequence of words). This next-token prediction framework, while powerful, is agnostic to truth – the model is never explicitly told what is factually correct, only what is likely given its training corpus. As a result, the model learns to generate text that sounds plausible and contextually relevant, but it has **no direct mechanism to verify factual accuracy** ([When AI Gets It Wrong: Addressing AI Hallucinations and Bias \- MIT Sloan Teaching & Learning Technologies](https://mitsloanedtech.mit.edu/ai/basics/addressing-ai-hallucinations-and-bias/#:~:text=1,accuracy%20in%20their%20outputs%20is)). Essentially, an LLM is an advanced autocomplete: it extends prompts by statistically sampling the continuation that best fits its learned patterns ([When AI Gets It Wrong: Addressing AI Hallucinations and Bias \- MIT Sloan Teaching & Learning Technologies](https://mitsloanedtech.mit.edu/ai/basics/addressing-ai-hallucinations-and-bias/#:~:text=1,accuracy%20in%20their%20outputs%20is)). If the training data contain conflicting or erroneous statements (and across billions of words, there will be many), the model can just as easily reproduce those. Furthermore, because the model must always output *something*, it has a tendency to **invent an answer** if it doesn’t know one – since “I don’t know” was rarely the most common continuation in its training data (unless specifically trained for that). Another way to understand the mechanism is through the concept of **confabulation** mentioned earlier: when faced with a query beyond its knowledge, the model’s vast web of associations may still produce an answer that statistically resembles a legitimate response, effectively filling the knowledge gap with a fabricated but fluent answer ([Why ChatGPT and Bing Chat are so good at making things up | Ars Technica](https://web.archive.org/web/20230611024338/https://arstechnica.com/information-technology/2023/04/why-ai-chatbots-are-the-ultimate-bs-machines-and-how-people-hope-to-fix-them/#:~:text=vein%2C%20we%20feel%20the%20term,work%2C%20as%20we%27ll%20explore%20below)).

The architecture itself (e.g., having many layers of self-attention) doesn’t *directly* cause hallucinations; rather, it’s the combination of architecture with training paradigm and data limitations. Key contributing factors include:

* **Lack of Grounding:** LLMs typically have no connection to an external knowledge base or the real world once training is finished. They rely solely on internalized patterns. Without grounding, they cannot check an assertion against reality (unlike a database-backed system). For instance, if asked “Who won the 1975 World Series?”, an ungrounded LLM will generate an answer from memory patterns – if its training data didn’t contain the correct answer or if it confuses it with another year, it will still produce a confident response, which could be wrong.

* **Training Data Quality and Cut-off:** These models often have a knowledge cut-off date (e.g., GPT-3 was trained on data up to 2019/2020). They might hallucinate answers to questions about events after the cut-off, simply because they have no data on them. Even within the training window, if certain facts were scarce or overshadowed by misinformation in the training set, the model might learn the wrong thing. The model doesn’t *know* which statements in its training were correct or incorrect; it just absorbs it all. As a result, it may regurgitate common misconceptions or mix up facts (e.g., conflating two different people or events that often co-occurred in text).

* **Objective Functions and Overconfidence:** Neural language models are trained to maximize the likelihood of the training data. There is no built-in penalty for being wrong *per se* – only for being implausible or dissimilar to training examples. This means an LLM is optimized to produce answers that **sound right** rather than *are* right. During decoding (generation), especially with strategies that favor high-probability words, the model can end up stating falsehoods very confidently because those words had high probability given its learned context. Paradoxically, this can make the AI seem more authoritative even when it’s incorrect, a phenomenon one researcher described as “*the ultimate BS machine*” behavior ([Why ChatGPT and Bing Chat are so good at making things up | Ars Technica](https://web.archive.org/web/20230611024338/https://arstechnica.com/information-technology/2023/04/why-ai-chatbots-are-the-ultimate-bs-machines-and-how-people-hope-to-fix-them/#:~:text=It%27s%20a%20big%20problem%20when,to%20prison%2C%20a%20complete%20fabrication)) – it doesn’t know the truth, but it speaks as if it does.

* **Model Size and Overfitting of Patterns:** Very large models have a huge capacity to memorize or recombine training data. Sometimes hallucinations occur by **misretrieval** of a training fact – e.g., mixing up names or sources. The model might recall that *something* was associated with a keyword, but not precisely what, leading to a kind of fuzzy reconstruction that results in a wrong statement. Smaller models, on the other hand, might hallucinate simply because they lack knowledge and resort to generic fillers.

Researchers have categorized hallucinations in various ways. One taxonomy ([arXiv:2005.00661v1 \[cs.CL\] 2 May 2020](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2005.00661#:~:text=To%20distinguish%20hallucinations%20further%20in,Intrinsic%20hallucinations%20are%20consequences%20of)) ([Insights into Classifying and Mitigating LLMs' Hallucinations \- arXiv.org](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2311.08117#:~:text=Insights%20into%20Classifying%20and%20Mitigating,knowledge%20that%20may%20improve%20the)) distinguishes **intrinsic** vs. **extrinsic** hallucinations (especially in tasks like summarization and translation). An *intrinsic hallucination* is when the generated output contradicts or distorts information that *was present* in the input (for example, a summary that misstates a key fact from the source article) ([Survey of Hallucination in Natural Language Generation \- arXiv.org](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2202.03629#:~:text=Intrinsic%20Hallucinations%3A%20The%20generated%20output,For)). An *extrinsic hallucination* is when the model introduces information that was *not present* in the input, which could be irrelevant or made-up but not directly contradicting the source ([Insights into Classifying and Mitigating LLMs' Hallucinations \- arXiv.org](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2311.08117#:~:text=Insights%20into%20Classifying%20and%20Mitigating,knowledge%20that%20may%20improve%20the)). In open-ended generation without a source, we can think in terms of **factual** vs. **logical** hallucinations. A factual hallucination is a false statement about the world (e.g., claiming a historical figure was alive in a year they were not), whereas a logical hallucination would be a nonsensical or incoherent statement that defies common sense or the context (though modern LLMs, being highly trained on form, rarely produce gibberish unless deliberately pushed; hallucinations are often *coherent-sounding* nonsense). Understanding these distinctions helps in designing detection methods and choosing mitigation strategies, as we discuss next.

**Mitigation Strategies:** Addressing AI hallucinations is an active area of research, and multiple approaches have been proposed and are often used in combination:

* **Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF):** One breakthrough in making AI like ChatGPT more reliable was using RLHF to fine-tune the model’s outputs. During this process, human annotators rate or select the best model responses, and the model is fine-tuned to prefer those. If done well, the model learns to avoid blatantly false answers or at least respond with appropriate uncertainty when it isn’t sure. Indeed, observers noted that ChatGPT (fine-tuned with RLHF) was *less prone* to certain types of hallucination than its base model GPT-3 ([Why ChatGPT and Bing Chat are so good at making things up | Ars Technica](https://web.archive.org/web/20230611024338/https://arstechnica.com/information-technology/2023/04/why-ai-chatbots-are-the-ultimate-bs-machines-and-how-people-hope-to-fix-them/#:~:text=And%20yet%20despite%20ChatGPT%27s%20predilection,answers%20might%20not%20be%20accurate)) ([Why ChatGPT and Bing Chat are so good at making things up | Ars Technica](https://web.archive.org/web/20230611024338/https://arstechnica.com/information-technology/2023/04/why-ai-chatbots-are-the-ultimate-bs-machines-and-how-people-hope-to-fix-them/#:~:text=Further%20Reading)). For example, ChatGPT will often refuse to fabricate a citation if directly asked for one, whereas the base model might have eagerly generated a fake citation. However, RLHF is not foolproof – it reduces the incidence but doesn’t eliminate hallucinations. It may also lead the model to be overcautious or hide its lack of knowledge in subtle ways.

* **Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG):** Another strategy attaches a **retrieval system** (such as a search engine or database lookup) to the language model. When asked a factual question, the system first fetches relevant documents (e.g., from Wikipedia or a company knowledge base) and provides them to the model to condition on. This grounds the model in up-to-date, real information and greatly reduces the chance of pure fabrication, since the model can draw from retrieved evidence. For instance, a question-answering system might retrieve the Wikipedia page about a historical event before the model answers, thus ensuring the model’s answer is supported by that text. This technique has been effective in open-domain QA and has been adopted in some production systems (like Bing’s AI chat which cites web sources). It essentially gives the AI a form of “open-book exam” instead of making it rely on memory alone. Even for multimodal models in specialized fields (like medical image captioning), researchers have proposed “Visual RAG” – retrieving similar images or medical references to help ground the model’s descriptions ([\[2502.15040\] Reducing Hallucinations of Medical Multimodal Large Language Models with Visual Retrieval-Augmented Generation](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.15040#:~:text=,have%20less%20positive%20training%20data)) ([\[2502.15040\] Reducing Hallucinations of Medical Multimodal Large Language Models with Visual Retrieval-Augmented Generation](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.15040#:~:text=Multicare%20medical%20image%20caption%20generation,F1%20score)).

* **Knowledge Graphs and Structured Data:** Related to retrieval, some approaches integrate structured knowledge (like knowledge graphs of entities and relations) into the model’s generation process ([Can Knowledge Graphs Reduce Hallucinations in LLMs? : A Survey](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.07914#:~:text=Can%20Knowledge%20Graphs%20Reduce%20Hallucinations,a%20source%20of%20external)). By cross-checking the model’s output against a knowledge graph, one can either constrain the output or post-correct it. For example, if a model says “Paris is the capital of Germany,” a knowledge graph could flag that as incorrect (since it knows Berlin is the capital of Germany) and force a correction. This is more rigid than free-form retrieval but can catch clear factual errors on known entities.

* **Prompting and Instruction Tuning:** Clever prompt design can sometimes reduce hallucinations. By instructing the model to be accurate and to only speak about what it can support, or by asking it to “think step by step” (chain-of-thought prompting), we can nudge it toward caution. An *instruction-tuned* model (one trained on many examples of following instructions correctly) tends to handle queries more faithfully than a plain language model prompt. That said, prompt-based fixes have limits. A noteworthy tactic is to ask the model to provide its reasoning or evidence for an answer; if the reasoning chain includes a false statement, the final answer is likely false, so one could programmatically catch that. Alternatively, one model can be used to critique or fact-check another model’s output – a form of ensemble approach where an “critic” model tries to identify hallucinations in the first model’s response.

* **Output Verification (Post-hoc):** Another line of defense is to verify the model’s output after generation. For text, this might involve using fact-checking algorithms or calling external APIs to validate claims. For example, if the model outputs a statistical claim or a reference, a system could attempt to look that up. If verification fails, the system might either flag the response to the user or attempt a second-pass answer with corrections. In essence, this treats the generative model as a draft generator and uses a discriminator or validator module to approve or reject content. Some research has trained separate classifiers to detect likely hallucinated sentences in summaries or translations by learning from known errors ([Insights into Classifying and Mitigating LLMs' Hallucinations \- arXiv.org](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2311.08117#:~:text=Insights%20into%20Classifying%20and%20Mitigating,knowledge%20that%20may%20improve%20the)).

* **Architectural Innovations:** Researchers have also begun exploring architectural changes to reduce hallucinations. One idea is to equip models with an explicit uncertainty estimation. If the model could output a confidence score or an “I don’t know” token distribution when unsure, it might avoid stating falsehoods strongly. There have been attempts to train models to say “I’m not sure” when the input is outside their knowledge, but this behavior is hard to imbue reliably – it often requires explicit supervised data indicating when an answer is unknown. Another approach is **dialogue tuning**, where the model learns to ask clarifying questions or admit confusion instead of guessing. Moreover, a 2018 study by Hanneke *et al.* (aptly titled “Actively Avoiding Nonsense”) looked at modifying training to penalize outputs that didn’t make sense, essentially trying to bias the model against hallucinating by design ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=arXiv%3A2303.08774%20%5Bcs.CL%5D.%2029.%20,001)). Future architectures may incorporate modules that cross-verify parts of an answer (e.g., a “calculator” for arithmetic or a “calendar” for dates), thereby constraining the model with hard checks.

Despite these efforts, no current method guarantees an AI won’t hallucinate. The effectiveness varies with context: models may be quite factual on well-covered topics but can hallucinate on obscure queries. A revealing observation by a prompt engineering expert, Riley Goodside, was that ChatGPT’s success is partly because *“it manages to suppress confabulation enough to make it unnoticeable for many common questions.”* ([Why ChatGPT and Bing Chat are so good at making things up | Ars Technica](https://web.archive.org/web/20230611024338/https://arstechnica.com/information-technology/2023/04/why-ai-chatbots-are-the-ultimate-bs-machines-and-how-people-hope-to-fix-them/#:~:text=Further%20Reading)) In other words, progress has been made to reduce obvious mistakes on the kind of questions people frequently ask, but push the model into less trodden territory and the old behavior emerges. Total elimination of hallucinations may require fundamentally new AI paradigms (like incorporating logic reasoning or verified knowledge at the core).

**Hallucinations as Creative Potential:** Interestingly, the conversation around AI hallucinations is not purely about mitigation; there is a growing discourse on harnessing these errant outputs for creative purposes. Sam Altman, CEO of OpenAI, stirred discussion by suggesting that **hallucinations are “actually a good thing” in terms of creativity** – essentially arguing that the very ability of GPT models to produce unexpected, off-script ideas is what makes them powerful innovators, not just knowledge retrievers ([“Hallucinating” AIs Sound Creative, but Let’s Not Celebrate Being Wrong | The MIT Press Reader](https://thereader.mitpress.mit.edu/hallucinating-ais-sound-creative-but-lets-not-celebrate-being-wrong/#:~:text=When%20Sam%20Altman%2C%20OpenAI%E2%80%99s%20CEO%2C,the%20many%20concerns%20about%20accuracy)). His point was that if a model only regurgitated known facts perfectly, it might be reliable but not imaginative; the occasional wild leap (even if factually wrong) indicates a form of generalization that can be useful in creative tasks. This perspective aligns with the notion that **novelty** often comes at the expense of accuracy. AI researchers Mukherjee and Chang (2023) describe a “novelty-usefulness tradeoff” in generative AI ([Managing the Creative Frontier of Generative AI: The Novelty-Usefulness Tradeoff | California Management Review](https://cmr.berkeley.edu/2023/07/managing-the-creative-frontier-of-generative-ai-the-novelty-usefulness-tradeoff#:~:text=Creativity%2C%20a%20defining%20trait%20of,is%20this%20interest%20in%20AI)) ([Managing the Creative Frontier of Generative AI: The Novelty-Usefulness Tradeoff | California Management Review](https://cmr.berkeley.edu/2023/07/managing-the-creative-frontier-of-generative-ai-the-novelty-usefulness-tradeoff#:~:text=The%20importance%20of%20creativity%20in,conceivable%20through%20human%20thought%20alone)): as a model pushes toward more novel outputs (think of more divergent, out-of-the-box completions), it risks producing results that are less immediately useful or correct. Tightly controlling hallucinations could mean constraining the model’s creativity. For example, in creative writing or brainstorming, a bizarre or incorrect idea from the AI might still inspire a human user or lead to a novel solution when viewed from a different angle. There is empirical exploration of this in domains like drug discovery and design – areas that require proposing entirely new molecules or concepts. A recent survey by Lee *et al.* (2023) takes a “creativity perspective” on LLM hallucinations, arguing that these models’ propensity to stray beyond known information can **foster innovation** if properly directed ([A Survey on Large Language Model Hallucination via a Creativity Perspective](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.06647#:~:text=Perspective%20arxiv,reliability%20in%20critical%20applications)) ([A Survey on Large Language Model Hallucination via a Creativity Perspective](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.06647v1#:~:text=A%20Survey%20on%20Large%20Language,correlation%20through%20rigorous%20mathematical%20analysis)). They even attempt to categorize “good” vs. “bad” hallucinations, suggesting metrics to evaluate the *quality* of a hallucinated idea (does it inspire useful lines of inquiry, even if it’s unverifiable?).

In practical terms, some AI applications already leverage controlled hallucination for creativity. For instance, generative art models like DeepDream (discussed later) intentionally amplify patterns to create hallucinated imagery as art. In storytelling, AI writing tools might inject random twists in a narrative that a human author can choose to refine or discard, effectively serving as a collaborator that suggests imaginative (if sometimes illogical) plot developments. The key is **context**: in critical factual contexts, hallucinations are harmful, but in exploratory creative contexts, they can be seeds of originality. One Psychology Today report encapsulated this idea by noting that reframing AI’s so-called flaws might **“reveal their potential as catalysts for innovation.”** ([Harnessing Hallucinations to Make AI More Creative | Psychology Today](https://www.psychologytoday.com/intl/blog/the-digital-self/202501/harnessing-hallucinations-to-make-ai-more-creative#:~:text=Key%20points)) In the realm of drug discovery, for example, allowing an AI to generate chemically “implausible” structures (which are hallucinations from the standpoint of known chemistry) has led researchers to consider entirely new compounds that might have been overlooked by traditional methods ([Harnessing Hallucinations to Make AI More Creative | Psychology Today](https://www.psychologytoday.com/intl/blog/the-digital-self/202501/harnessing-hallucinations-to-make-ai-more-creative#:~:text=,potential%20as%20catalysts%20for%20innovation)) ([Harnessing Hallucinations to Make AI More Creative | Psychology Today](https://www.psychologytoday.com/intl/blog/the-digital-self/202501/harnessing-hallucinations-to-make-ai-more-creative#:~:text=Hallucinations%20as%20Catalysts%20for%20Creativity)). Of course, those compounds then need to be tested for actual viability, reinforcing the theme that human verification and guidance must accompany AI creativity.

In summary, the technical foundation of AI hallucinations lies in the combination of how AI models are built and trained. Their creative, associative brains are double-edged swords – producing both brilliant leaps and confounding mistakes. Mitigation strategies are being developed and improved, aimed at reducing unhelpful hallucinations especially in high-stakes usage. Simultaneously, a nuanced view is emerging that in certain settings we might *embrace* the generative leaps of AI (while filtering them) to boost creativity. The next section will compare how these phenomena manifest across different AI systems, from pure language models to those handling multiple modalities, and what that implies for the design of future AI.

## 

## **Comparative Analysis Across AI Systems**

Not all AI systems hallucinate in the same way or frequency. It is instructive to compare **large language models (LLMs)** (which deal purely with text) with **multimodal models** (which handle images and text, or other combinations), as well as specialized vs. general models. Differences in input/output modality and training objectives lead to different expressions of the hallucination problem.

**LLMs (Text-Only) vs. Multimodal Models:** LLMs, such as GPT-3, GPT-4, or PaLM, generate text based on text input. Their hallucinations typically take the form of **factually incorrect statements** or **unfounded assertions in text**. For example, an LLM might hallucinate a fictitious historical event or quote, or misidentify a person with a plausible-sounding but wrong name. Because language is very flexible, LLMs can produce an infinite variety of falsehoods while still sounding coherent. When an LLM is integrated into a dialog agent, hallucinations might also involve the system adopting a persona or emotions that were not intended (though this borders on the related issue of role-playing by the model, not factual error).

Multimodal models combine vision and language (and potentially audio or others). A common type is a **Vision-Language Model (VLM)** that can, for instance, caption images or answer questions about an image (Visual QA). These models, like OpenAI’s CLIP+GPT or Google’s Flamingo, have to align what they *see* with what they *say*. A hallmark error in these systems is the **object hallucination** in image captions – the model might confidently describe an object that is **not actually present** in the image. For instance, given a photo of a person in a room with a cat, a captioning model might hallucinate a description of a **dog** sitting next to them, if in its training data images of people with pets more often contained dogs. This was noted in early image captioning research: models would add objects (like birds in a sky) because they *expected* them, even when they weren’t there, betraying a bias or a pattern learned from the training distribution. In technical terms, the model’s latent representation strongly associates certain contexts with certain objects, causing a false positive detection in description. A 2024 survey on multimodal LLMs (also called LVLMs – Large Vision-Language Models) highlights that despite impressive capabilities, these systems **“often generate outputs that are inconsistent with the visual content”**, which is essentially the definition of hallucination in that domain ([\[2404.18930\] Hallucination of Multimodal Large Language Models: A Survey](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.18930#:~:text=,We%20review%20recent)). For example, asked to describe an image with a red circle and a blue square, a hallucinating VLM might mention a green triangle that doesn’t exist, showing a lapse in grounding the text to the actual image. This poses serious concerns in applications like medical imaging, where a false positive or negative description could mislead doctors ([\[2502.15040\] Reducing Hallucinations of Medical Multimodal Large Language Models with Visual Retrieval-Augmented Generation](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.15040#:~:text=,asks%20whether%20a%20medical%20entities)).

The difference in modalities also influences **perceptibility** of hallucinations. A textual hallucination might not be immediately obvious to a reader unless they know the facts or check, because any grammatically correct sentence looks legitimate on the surface. In contrast, a visual hallucination in an image caption can sometimes be spotted by a human if they see the image doesn’t contain what the caption claims (assuming the human has access to the same input). However, in many human-AI interactions with multimodal models, the human might only see the AI’s output. For instance, if a blind user relies on an AI to describe an image, they have no way to detect a hallucinated object in the description – which is why reliability is crucial in assistive tech.

**Frequency and Nature of Errors:** Anecdotally, early versions of image-generating models (like GANs or diffusion models) also “hallucinated” bizarre artifacts – e.g., extra limbs on a person, or text that is gibberish when images try to contain writing. In image generation, these are not usually called hallucinations (since the whole output is fictional by design), but they are analogous: the model creates elements that may not make sense relative to the prompt or to coherent reality (like a person with three arms). With recent advances (e.g., Stable Diffusion 2, DALL-E 3), such artifacts have been reduced, but not entirely eliminated.

Interestingly, multimodal systems that include language components can compound errors. Consider an AI that takes an image, then produces a textual description, which is then fed into an LLM for a conversation. If the initial image description had a hallucination (say it added “there is a dog in the room” when there isn’t), the LLM might build on that and talk at length about the dog – a cascade of hallucination. This highlights the importance of solid cross-modal grounding. Researchers have begun creating benchmarks specifically to stress-test multimodal models for hallucination, such as asking detailed spatial questions about images to see if the model’s answer deviates from visible truth ([xieyuquanxx/awesome-Large-MultiModal-Hallucination \- GitHub](https://github.com/xieyuquanxx/awesome-Large-MultiModal-Hallucination#:~:text=POPE%3A%20Evaluating%20Object%20Hallucination%20in,1.5)). Ensuring consistency between modalities is a key challenge; it requires that the model’s internal representation of the image is accurate so that any generated language aligns with it.

**Evaluation and Detection:** For purely textual models, evaluation of hallucinations often requires human judgment or comparison to reference knowledge (because an answer might be phrased differently yet still correct, so automated metrics struggle). There are factuality benchmarks like **TruthfulQA** which measure how often a model produces true vs. false answers on tricky questions. Large differences have been observed between models; for instance, GPT-4 was found to be more factual than GPT-3.5 on these benchmarks, though still far from perfect. Automatic detectors (classifiers that label sentences as factually correct or not) are being researched but are not fully reliable, since they themselves can be fooled by the fluent language of an LLM.

In multimodal models, one can use datasets where images have ground-truth captions or object labels. Metrics like **object hallucination rate** have been defined – e.g., the percentage of generated captions that mention objects the ground truth annotations did not include ([xieyuquanxx/awesome-Large-MultiModal-Hallucination \- GitHub](https://github.com/xieyuquanxx/awesome-Large-MultiModal-Hallucination#:~:text=POPE%3A%20Evaluating%20Object%20Hallucination%20in,1.5)). A high hallucination rate indicates the model is adding things. In 2023, Li *et al.* introduced an evaluation specifically for object existence in VQA, essentially asking models yes/no questions like “Is there a \[object\] in the image?” for objects that aren’t there, to see if the model hallucinates them ([xieyuquanxx/awesome-Large-MultiModal-Hallucination \- GitHub](https://github.com/xieyuquanxx/awesome-Large-MultiModal-Hallucination#:~:text=POPE%3A%20Evaluating%20Object%20Hallucination%20in,1.5)). This kind of focused evaluation helps quantify improvements as new model versions (potentially with better vision encoders or training on cleaned data) come out.

**Future Design Considerations:** Comparing LLMs and multimodal models suggests some future directions for AI design:

1. **Enhanced Grounding:** For any AI that should describe reality (textual or visual), giving it tools to ground its statements is vital. This could mean integrating sensors or image analysis for an LLM that needs to be factual about the physical world, and vice versa, giving a vision model a stronger language prior that is fact-checked. Some multimodal systems now incorporate feedback loops – e.g., if a description is generated, the system might internally re-visualize or cross-check if the described object could appear in the image. This is analogous to how a human might double-check: “Did I really see a dog? Let me look again.”

2. **Modality Coordination:** Designing architectures where multiple modalities can **correct each other** may reduce hallucinations. For instance, a system could generate a preliminary text description from an image and then use an image reconstruction module to see if that text can regenerate the original image. If not, it indicates something in the text was not actually present. Such cycle-consistency checks are an idea from unsupervised learning that could be applied here.

3. **User Interaction to Curb Hallucinations:** Another consideration is allowing end-users to easily spot-check an AI’s claims. In LLM-based chat, this has translated to providing sources or citations for factual statements. If the model says a statistic, an ideal system would also present the source it’s drawing from (as Bing’s chatbot attempts to do by linking to web results). For multimodal assistants, if an AI says “there is a dog in the image”, the interface could highlight the region of the image it believes is a dog – if there is nothing there, the user knows that part of the description is suspect. This is a form of **explainability** that also helps diagnose hallucinations.

4. **Specialized vs Generalist Models:** Interestingly, highly specialized models (e.g., a medical diagnostic image model) might hallucinate less in their domain because they’re tightly constrained by domain knowledge. But if they do, the stakes are high. There is debate whether an all-purpose general model is more prone to hallucination than many task-specific models. The trend in industry has been towards large general models that are then slightly adapted. Ensuring these general models can *toggle* between a creative mode and a precise mode might be one future design goal. For example, a model might have a “strict factual” setting for when it’s used in a search engine or an educational tool, and a “creative writing” setting where hallucinations (like inventing fictional characters or scenarios) are acceptable or even desired.

In comparing AI systems, a cautionary tale emerged in late 2022: **Meta’s Galactica model**, which was a large language model specialized for scientific text generation ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=32.%20,original%20on%2010%20April%202023)). It was intended to assist scientists by summarizing papers or answering scientific queries. However, soon after its demo release, users found it could produce extremely authoritative-sounding but completely wrong scientific explanations and even fake research article content. In one example, it generated a bogus paper about the benefits of eating glass, complete with fake citations. The backlash was swift; **Galactica was taken down after just a few days** ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=32.%20,original%20on%2010%20April%202023)). This incident underscored that even when a model is tuned for a domain, it can hallucinate dangerously if not properly grounded. It also highlighted differences in tolerance: scientists and experts quickly noticed the hallucinations and reacted strongly, whereas lay users with general chatbots might not catch them as easily. Thus, domain-specific evaluations are as important as general ones.

Overall, this comparative look emphasizes that **hallucination is a general challenge across AI modalities** – whenever an AI is tasked with generating novel content (text, image, etc.) that is expected to align with reality, there is a risk of divergence. Each modality and use-case brings its own nuances, but lessons transfer: the importance of grounding, the value of oversight, and the careful balance between creative freedom and accuracy. With these technical and comparative insights in mind, we can now delve into what neuroscience and psychology tell us about hallucinations and creativity, which can further inform how we think about human-AI parallels and collaborations.

## 

## **Neuroscientific and Psychological Insights**

Understanding AI hallucinations can be enriched by examining how the human brain generates, filters, and sometimes misinterprets information. While the architectures are entirely different, we find **analogous themes**: prediction, imagination, and the thin line between creativity and delusion.

**Brain Mechanisms of Hallucination:** In human neuroscience, hallucinations are most often discussed in the context of perceptual experiences. Unlike an AI, a human hallucination typically means seeing or hearing something that isn’t present in the external stimulus. This can occur in certain mental illnesses (such as schizophrenia or Parkinson’s disease), under the influence of psychedelics, or in extreme conditions like sensory deprivation. One prominent theory that explains both normal perception and hallucination is the **predictive coding (Bayesian brain) theory**. It posits that the brain is constantly making predictions about incoming sensory data at various hierarchical levels and then updating or correcting those predictions based on the actual input ([The Brain as a Prediction Machine: The Key to Consciousness? | Psychology Today](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/blog/finding-purpose/202201/the-brain-prediction-machine-the-key-consciousness#:~:text=7,in%20Vancouver%2C%20BC%2C%20%2089)). Under normal conditions, a balance is maintained: the sensory evidence aligns and fine-tunes the predictions, yielding a coherent perception of reality. But if this balance tilts – say, the brain’s internal predictions outweigh the actual sensory input – you can get a hallucination. Anil Seth’s notion of reality as a “**controlled hallucination**” encapsulates this: our brains hallucinate a consistent world, constrained by sensory data ([The Brain as a Prediction Machine: The Key to Consciousness? | Psychology Today](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/blog/finding-purpose/202201/the-brain-prediction-machine-the-key-consciousness#:~:text=7,in%20Vancouver%2C%20BC%2C%20%2089)). When unconstrained (as in dreaming or hallucinating), the brain’s intrinsic activity can produce perceptions that diverge from reality (e.g., seeing patterns in random noise – a bit like DeepDream’s algorithmic pareidolia). Interestingly, the kind of hallucinations human brains produce are often **patterned**: people might see faces in the clouds, or hear whispers in noise – our brains hallucinate *familiar patterns*. This is analogous to how an AI might hallucinate in familiar ways (like a language model creating a plausible-sounding name or a vision model seeing dogs everywhere if it has “dogs on the brain” from training) ([Why DeepDream Dreams of Doggies | Aaron Hertzmann’s blog](https://aaronhertzmann.com/2020/12/29/deepdream.html#:~:text=blog%20aaronhertzmann,makes%20creatures%2C%20buildings%2C%20and%20vehicles)) ([Google DeepDream robot: 10 weirdest images produced by AI 'inceptionism' and users online | IBTimes UK](https://www.ibtimes.co.uk/google-deepdream-robot-10-weirdest-images-produced-by-ai-inceptionism-users-online-1509518#:~:text=Sometimes%2C%20the%20network%20interprets%20shapes,clouds%20or%20faces%20as%20animals)).

Neuroimaging studies of hallucinating patients show heightened activity in sensory areas in the absence of stimuli, and irregular connectivity between frontal (higher-order) regions and posterior (sensory) regions. This suggests a failure in the error-checking mechanism: the frontal predictions aren’t getting corrected properly. We might draw a parallel to AI: an LLM hallucinating might be seen as akin to a brain whose predictive model is running without a grounding error signal. The “error signal” for an AI would be some feedback from reality (which, unless we build it in, the AI doesn’t get once deployed). In essence, today’s AI lacks a sensory gate – it only has a prior (its training) and no guaranteed correction loop with real-world truth.

**Creativity Theories and Hallucination:** Human creativity is a complex cognitive function that involves generating novel ideas that are also valuable or meaningful. Psychologists have distinguished **divergent thinking** (generating many diverse ideas) and **convergent thinking** (narrowing down to the best idea) as two components of creativity ([Managing the Creative Frontier of Generative AI: The Novelty-Usefulness Tradeoff | California Management Review](https://cmr.berkeley.edu/2023/07/managing-the-creative-frontier-of-generative-ai-the-novelty-usefulness-tradeoff#:~:text=Creativity%2C%20a%20defining%20trait%20of,like%2C%20or%20does)) ([Managing the Creative Frontier of Generative AI: The Novelty-Usefulness Tradeoff | California Management Review](https://cmr.berkeley.edu/2023/07/managing-the-creative-frontier-of-generative-ai-the-novelty-usefulness-tradeoff#:~:text=dynamic%20interactions%2C%20collaboration%2C%20and%20innovation%2C,11)). Divergent thinking is where the mind must wander, make remote associations, and even consider illogical possibilities – in a sense, to *hallucinate* possibilities beyond the ordinary. Convergent thinking then tests these against reality or constraints to arrive at a workable solution. This maps well onto our earlier discussion of blind variation (divergent) and selective retention (convergent) ([ERIC \- EJ577411 \- Donald Campbell's Model of the Creative Process: Creativity as Blind Variation and Selective Retention., Journal of Creative Behavior, 1998](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ577411#:~:text=This%20introductory%20article%20discusses%20a,%28Author%2FCR)). Neuroscientific studies suggest that during creative ideation, the brain’s **default mode network** (DMN) – associated with imagination, daydreaming, and internally-directed thought – becomes active, while the **executive control network** may down-regulate just enough to allow unusual ideas to form, but not so much that everything becomes disorganized. Highly creative people might have a brain that more readily enters a pseudo-hallucinatory state of loose associations (think of the phrase “creative insight” which sometimes comes in dreams or moments of defocused attention). This has led some researchers to note a correlation between creativity and certain psychopathologies: for example, there’s a hypothesis that schizotypy (a personality trait involving magical thinking or odd perceptions) at subclinical levels can correlate with creativity, because it entails a reduction in latent inhibition – essentially, less filtering of ideas. In practice, the difference between a useful creative idea and a crazy hallucination is whether the person can **evaluate and implement** it appropriately. Salvador Dali once quipped, “There is only one difference between a madman and me. I am not mad.” – implying the difference was his control over his imaginative visions.

When we draw this back to AI: current AI systems are heavily tilted towards divergent generation without built-in convergence. They lack an equivalent of the executive control network or a reality feedback that humans apply. The interdisciplinary insight here is that perhaps we could design AI with a two-stage process more explicitly – one generative, one evaluative – akin to human creativity. In fact, some computational creativity systems in research do separate phases of generation and selection (sometimes using evolutionary algorithms: mutate ideas, then have a fitness function to select). An LLM could potentially benefit from a second model (or second pass) that acts as the “critic”, simulating convergent thinking. This is already happening in small ways, such as when GPT-4 is used to critique GPT-3’s answers or when a single model is asked to reflect and revise its answer.

**Human-AI Creative Synergy:** The interplay of human and AI “imaginations” is a fascinating frontier. Humans are very good at evaluating context, understanding real-world constraints, and making value judgments, whereas AI can generate a massive breadth of options and combinations at lightning speed. This complementarity suggests a synergy: **human-AI co-creativity**. In a co-creative process, the AI might propose a wild idea (which could be considered a hallucination in a strict sense), and the human can recognize a gem of creative potential in it or refine it further ([Human-AI Co-Creation: Collaborative creation processes between humans ...](https://www.justthink.ai/blog/collaborative-creation-processes-between-humans-and-ai#:~:text=Human,new%20era%20of%20collaborative%20creation)) ([Human–AI Co-Drawing: Studying Creative Efficacy and Eye ... \- MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/14/18/8203#:~:text=Artificial%20intelligence%20,to%20investigate%20the%20impact)). For instance, a novelist could use an AI writing assistant to generate an unexpected plot twist – the AI might output something that doesn’t fit the story canon (a hallucination relative to the established narrative), but it might inspire a new direction the author finds intriguing. In design fields, AI tools can generate numerous design mockups or art variations, some of which are incoherent, but others might spark new directions for the human designer. The key is the human in the loop provides the convergent thinking, picking the outputs that make sense or have merit. This interactive process is already being explored in tools for storywriting, visual art, and music composition.

Neuroscience also offers a metaphor for this synergy: consider the relationship between the unconscious mind (which bubbles up ideas and insights, often unpredictably) and the conscious mind (which analyzes and implements ideas). One could imagine AI as a kind of “artificial unconscious,” continuously generating possibilities, with the human as the conscious arbiter. Over time, as AI systems become more sophisticated, they might incorporate more of the evaluation process themselves, but the human-AI team might always be stronger by combining different strengths. There is ongoing research on *hybrid intelligence* systems that blend human and AI contributions in real-time. Some studies indicate that when humans are aware of AI’s tendency to err, they can better **steer or edit AI outputs** to get the benefits (speed, novelty) without blindly trusting the content ([Students’ Use of Generative AI: The Threat of Hallucinations](https://cdt.org/insights/students-use-of-generative-ai-the-threat-of-hallucinations/#:~:text=Students%E2%80%99%20Use%20of%20Generative%20AI%3A,health%20issue%2C%20which%20could)) ([Students Trust ChatGPT Too Much. What About Everyone Else?](https://ai.northeastern.edu/news/students-trust-chatgpt-too-much-what-about-everyone-else#:~:text=Else%3F%20ai,they%20include%20numerous%20factual%20errors)). This requires user training and good interface design.

From a psychological perspective, one must also consider *public perception and cognitive bias* in the use of AI outputs. Humans have a known bias to assume confidently stated information is true (the **illusory truth effect**). AI’s authoritative tone can trick even knowledgeable users if they are not careful. For example, college students presented with ChatGPT-written answers sometimes trust them enough to turn in as homework, even if there are obvious errors ([Students Trust ChatGPT Too Much. What About Everyone Else?](https://ai.northeastern.edu/news/students-trust-chatgpt-too-much-what-about-everyone-else#:~:text=Else%3F%20ai,they%20include%20numerous%20factual%20errors)). Understanding this, psychologists and educators emphasize the need to train users in **critical thinking** when using AI: essentially, to take on the convergent thinking role and question the AI’s divergent output. In an educational context, some suggest actually using AI hallucinations as *teachable moments*. By showing students an AI-generated essay with some truths and some falsehoods, teachers can ask them to discern which is which, thereby actively engaging their fact-checking and critical analysis skills ([Explore “Hallucinations” to Better Understand AI’s Affordances and ...](https://academictech.uchicago.edu/2024/03/21/explore-hallucinations-to-better-understand-ais-affordances-and-risks-part-one/#:~:text=Explore%20%E2%80%9CHallucinations%E2%80%9D%20to%20Better%20Understand,for%20education%20and%20social)). This mirrors exercises we do with human-generated content (like analyzing fake news), and could equally apply to AI.

Neuroscience might also inspire future AI architectures that more closely mimic how humans avoid hallucinations. Our brains integrate multisensory information – if one sense hallucinated something, others can veto it (e.g., you thought you saw a flash of light, but there was no sound or any other change, so you dismiss it as a trick of the eye). Likewise, an AI that uses multi-sensor cross-checks could be more robust. Additionally, neuromodulatory systems in the brain (like dopamine) regulate the balance of exploration vs. exploitation, which influences creativity and possibly hallucination (too much dopamine can lead to psychosis, too little can make thought rigid). Perhaps AI could have tunable “creativity knobs” that adjust a similar balance. In fact, parameters like “temperature” in an LLM’s sampling strategy already serve this role: a high temperature makes the output more varied (more creative, more risk of hallucination), a low temperature makes it conservative (stick to likely answers, less creative, lower chance of error). Users and developers effectively act as neuromodulators by choosing these settings for a task.

In conclusion, insights from neuroscience and psychology underscore a core idea: **imagination and error are intertwined**. The human brain’s ability to simulate possibilities is immensely powerful for creativity but occasionally yields false perceptions. We manage this with complex feedback and control systems. AI currently possesses tremendous imaginative capacity with comparatively little built-in control, which explains both its creative outputs and its propensity for mistakes. Going forward, bridging human cognitive strategies and AI design – for instance, emulating the brain’s error correction or combining AI’s divergent thinking with human/AI convergent steps – could lead to systems that are both innovative and accurate. The human-AI partnership is an area where the strengths of each can mitigate the other’s weaknesses: the goal is *synergy*, not just emulation. These neuro-cognitive perspectives also feed directly into the ethical and societal considerations, since how we manage hallucinations affects trust and safety, as we discuss next.

## 

## **Ethical and Societal Implications**

The propensity of AI systems to hallucinate information carries significant ethical and societal ramifications. In this section, we examine the risks posed by AI hallucinations, public perceptions and misperceptions of the issue, and the emerging discussions on regulation and responsibility.

**Risk Assessment:** AI hallucinations can lead to **tangible harm** when they occur in high-stakes settings. Perhaps the most straightforward risk is the spread of misinformation. If a conversational AI confidently asserts a false fact (e.g., a medical falsehood or a fake news item), users who trust the AI could be misled. This becomes especially dangerous if the AI is used for advice – for instance, health or legal advice. A notable real-world case occurred in the legal domain: an attorney used ChatGPT to help write a legal brief, and the AI provided several **fictitious case citations and quotes** that the attorney then submitted to the court ([When AI Gets It Wrong: Addressing AI Hallucinations and Bias \- MIT Sloan Teaching & Learning Technologies](https://mitsloanedtech.mit.edu/ai/basics/addressing-ai-hallucinations-and-bias/#:~:text=For%20an%20example%20of%20how,Weiser%2C%202023)). The judge discovered these cited cases did not exist, resulting in professional sanctions for the attorney. This incident (the *Mata v. Avianca* case in 2023\) was a wake-up call that AI hallucinations are not harmless glitches; they can have serious professional and legal consequences. It highlighted that while AI can assist with drafting, human verification is non-negotiable – and failing to do so can amount to negligence. In fields like healthcare, one shudders at the thought of an AI doctor analogously hallucinating a treatment recommendation. If a medical chatbot incorrectly tells a patient to take a certain drug dosage that doesn’t exist or mix medications unsafely, the result could be injury or worse. Ensuring AI reliability in these contexts is literally a life-and-death matter, which is why thus far AI is kept assistive, not authoritative, in medicine (e.g., suggesting diagnoses that doctors must confirm).

Another category of risk is **defamation and reputational damage**. Because LLMs sometimes mix up information about people, there have been cases where the AI falsely accused individuals of wrongdoing. The Ars Technica piece described how ChatGPT *“placed \[a law professor\] on a list of legal scholars who had sexually harassed someone” – an event that never occurred* ([Why ChatGPT and Bing Chat are so good at making things up | Ars Technica](https://web.archive.org/web/20230611024338/https://arstechnica.com/information-technology/2023/04/why-ai-chatbots-are-the-ultimate-bs-machines-and-how-people-hope-to-fix-them/#:~:text=It%27s%20a%20big%20problem%20when,to%20prison%2C%20a%20complete%20fabrication)). Similarly, an Australian mayor was falsely stated by an AI to have been convicted of bribery ([Why ChatGPT and Bing Chat are so good at making things up | Ars Technica](https://web.archive.org/web/20230611024338/https://arstechnica.com/information-technology/2023/04/why-ai-chatbots-are-the-ultimate-bs-machines-and-how-people-hope-to-fix-them/#:~:text=reported%20%20on%20a%20law,to%20prison%2C%20a%20complete%20fabrication)). These kinds of hallucinations are essentially AI *libel*. They can tarnish reputations if not caught, and indeed have led to discussions about legal liability. In these instances, the individuals affected considered lawsuits against the makers of the AI for the defamation. This raises the complex question: **Who is responsible when an AI defames someone?** The AI has no intent; it’s an automated process. Is it the developer, the deployer, or is it an unfortunate “no one” scenario because it’s a stochastic parroting of training data? Legally, this is uncharted territory. From an ethical standpoint, however, if AI systems are to be deployed broadly, there must be mechanisms to prevent or redress such harms – whether through better training (e.g., filtering out or warning when making unverified personal claims) or through after-the-fact remedies (like a way to trace outputs back and issue corrections).

A subtler societal risk is the **erosion of trust in information ecosystems**. If AI-generated content becomes widespread, and we know it can contain convincing falsehoods, people may start to distrust digital information in general. We already face “fake news” challenges with human-generated misinformation; AI could flood the world with even more, at greater scale. This could lead to general cynicism (“you can’t trust anything on the internet anymore”) or, conversely, for those unaware of the issue, being duped by a false sense of authority from AI. Ironically, both outcomes are harmful: too much skepticism can lead to dismissing real expertise (imagine people start thinking *all* news might be AI-fabricated), while too little skepticism leads to gullibility. The middle ground of informed caution is hard to maintain at scale. Educating the public about AI’s limits is crucial. For instance, OpenAI and other companies have started adding user-facing disclaimers like: “This AI may produce inaccurate information.” But not everyone reads or heeds those warnings.

**Public Perception:** The term “hallucination” itself has grabbed public imagination – it was even chosen as Cambridge Dictionary’s Word of the Year (in the AI context) in 2023 ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=24.%20,why%20experts%20don%27t%20like%20the)). On one hand, this indicates a broad awareness of the phenomenon; on the other, it might also cause confusion. People might anthropomorphize AI too much, imagining it “dreams” or has a mind’s eye. Experts have mixed feelings about the term, as discussed. A Northeastern University news article echoed many AI researchers in saying the term is **misleading and potentially exculpatory** ([Why ChatGPT and Bing Chat are so good at making things up | Ars Technica](https://web.archive.org/web/20230611024338/https://arstechnica.com/information-technology/2023/04/why-ai-chatbots-are-the-ultimate-bs-machines-and-how-people-hope-to-fix-them/#:~:text=In%20academic%20literature%2C%20AI%20researchers,responsibility%20for%20the%20outputs%20themselves)). If the public thinks of AI hallucinations like human hallucinations, they might wrongly assume they are rare freak events (since in healthy humans hallucinations are rare). In reality, as we’ve shown, AI hallucinations are not rare; they’re a systemic byproduct of how these models work. So managing expectations is important: users should expect that *any* single AI-generated output could have errors. It’s not a weird exception when it happens; it’s an expected possibility at some rate. This is why some suggest using more mundane terms like “error” or “fabrication” rather than hallucination ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=in%20some%20cases%2C%20AI%20hallucinations,57)). One can argue, calling it a “fabrication” puts responsibility back on the developer to fix the fabrication problem (like you’d fix a bug), whereas “hallucination” might encourage an attitude of “oh, it’s just a crazy AI moment” – amusing but not accountable.

Public perception is also influenced by media coverage of dramatic AI failures. The Galactica incident, the lawyer’s case, Bing’s early foibles (where Bing’s chatbot produced emotional, erratic responses, including some factual errors) – these make headlines. Some portion of the public might conclude AI is dangerously unreliable across the board. Another portion might ignore these and focus only on success stories. Public opinion will shape how much people are willing to trust AI in various roles. For example, if people hear that “chatbots make up half their citations” ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=On%20top%20of%20providing%20incorrect,54)) – indeed one study found 47% of ChatGPT’s provided references in medical answers were fabricated ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=On%20top%20of%20providing%20incorrect,54)) – they will be (rightly) wary of using it for academic purposes. This has led universities and journals to issue statements warning against or even banning unverified use of AI in scholarly work ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=,53)) ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=language%20models%20in%20the%20Academic,57)). As a result, we see a developing norm: **AI should not be used as a source of truth, but as a tool**. It’s akin to using a calculator that sometimes adds incorrectly – you wouldn’t use it for critical accounting unless you had a way to double-check results.

**Regulation and Responsibility:** Given the potential harms, there are increasing calls to regulate AI systems, particularly large generative models. One avenue of regulation is holding companies accountable for misinformation their systems produce. This is tricky because of Section 230-like protections in some jurisdictions (which protect platforms from liability for user-generated content – but AI content is not user-generated, it’s machine-generated). If AI is seen as a product or service, consumer protection laws might require a certain level of quality. For example, if a navigation app consistently gave wrong directions, consumers could sue or regulators could step in. If an AI consistently gives wrong factual statements leading to harm, should that be seen similarly? Some experts say yes – companies shouldn’t be let off the hook by saying “it’s just how the model works.” They might need to implement safer models or limit usage where it’s unsafe.

Regulators in the EU, through the proposed **EU AI Act**, and other bodies like the OECD are examining rules around high-risk AI systems. One idea is requiring **transparency**: AI outputs should be labeled, and perhaps whenever an AI system is used in a context that affects people’s rights (like hiring, credit decisions, legal advice), it must meet higher standards of accuracy and explainability. In the context of hallucinations, transparency could mean the system must clearly signal uncertainty. Indeed, some guidelines suggest that if an AI is not sure, it should say so or refrain from answering. OpenAI has implemented a partial solution where ChatGPT often warns, “I’m not certain, but here’s my guess.” This is good, but not consistent; sometimes it will still just answer.

Another regulatory approach is the idea of an **AI quality audit**. Similar to how pharmaceutical companies must trial a drug for safety and efficacy, perhaps AI models might require testing for hallucination rates in critical scenarios. If a model intended for medical use hallucinates symptoms or treatments above a very low threshold, it might not get approval.

On the flip side, regulators also worry about over-regulation stifling innovation. Some argue that the focus should be on **user education and collaborative control** rather than blanket bans. For example, outright forbidding AI in legal practice due to hallucinations could slow down adoption of helpful tools; instead, require attorneys to verify and perhaps sign off that “no content was accepted without verification.” That is actually something some law firms have begun implementing internally as policy after the Avianca case.

From a societal point of view, there’s also an ethical design principle at play: AI should be **honest** about its limits. Some ethicists propose that AI assistants should have a kind of calibrated self-awareness (not consciousness, but a programmed introspection) to not answer when likely to be hallucinating. Bing’s chatbot at one point would answer “I’m sorry, I don’t have enough information to answer that” for certain queries – a behavior likely programmed to avoid guesswork. Balancing usefulness and truthfulness is tough – users don’t like “I don’t know” too often either, especially if the answer exists. So there’s a temptation for companies to make the AI answer everything to appear smart. Ethically, though, it might be better for long-term trust if these systems sometimes say “I’m not confident in the answer to that.” There’s research into calibrating a model’s confidence with its accuracy, which could be mandated for critical applications.

**Societal Benefit vs. Harm:** There is a broader societal question: do the creative benefits of hallucination (as discussed earlier) outweigh the risks, and in what domains? In entertainment or art, hallucinations are benign or even desired. In education, they can be turned into lessons (as long as teacher or student knows to check). In factual knowledge services, they are mostly harmful. Society might decide that for certain uses (like news summarization, medical Q\&A), AI must be tethered to sources (perhaps even only quote verified info). For other uses (like fantasy story generation), the imaginative freedom can be allowed. The challenge is that one AI model can be used for *both* types of tasks by different users. This implies that context and perhaps *regulatory tuning* should travel with the model. The AI might need modes or have usage-specific guardrails.

Finally, there’s the issue of **who gets to regulate** – governments, industry self-regulation, or a combination. Tech companies are aware that if they don’t improve these systems’ reliability, a public backlash or heavy-handed regulation could result. Already, some jurisdictions are looking at whether AI output falls under product liability. In response, companies might implement more aggressive filtering (for example, OpenAI is working on techniques to make models refuse to answer potentially factual queries if not confident). However, too many refusals and the utility drops, which can also anger users. Some observers note that hallucinations are currently an accepted risk because the alternative (not releasing these models) seems worse given all their benefits. The public somewhat accepts that early models aren’t perfect – like how early cars were less safe, but society didn’t ban cars, it improved them and added traffic laws and seatbelts.

In summary, ethically we are striving for **AI that is as truthful and safe as possible**, and societally we must manage how people use and understand these tools. There is a push-pull between innovation and precaution. What’s clear is that no serious stakeholder considers hallucinations a purely academic problem; it’s recognized as a barrier to trust and a potential source of harm that must be addressed through a mix of technical, educational, and regulatory measures ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=language%20models%20in%20the%20Academic,57)). Only by doing so can we reap the benefits of AI systems at scale without undermining information integrity and public confidence.

## 

## **Real-World Applications**

AI hallucinations present challenges, but they also play a role (sometimes even a productive one) in various real-world applications of AI. Here we explore how hallucinations manifest in domains like art, science, and education – and how practitioners are leveraging or coping with them.

**Creative Arts:** The art world has a history of turning limitations or aberrations of technology into new art forms. AI is no exception. A prominent example is **Google’s DeepDream** algorithm, introduced in 2015, which intentionally induces a vision model to over-interpret images. The result is **surreal, dream-like art** created by amplifying the model’s “hallucinations.” In DeepDream images, ordinary photos become populated with strange **swirling patterns, eyes, and animal-like features**, reflecting the neural network’s visual biases ([Google DeepDream robot: 10 weirdest images produced by AI 'inceptionism' and users online | IBTimes UK](https://www.ibtimes.co.uk/google-deepdream-robot-10-weirdest-images-produced-by-ai-inceptionism-users-online-1509518#:~:text=Sometimes%2C%20the%20network%20interprets%20shapes,clouds%20or%20faces%20as%20animals)) ([Google DeepDream robot: 10 weirdest images produced by AI 'inceptionism' and users online | IBTimes UK](https://www.ibtimes.co.uk/google-deepdream-robot-10-weirdest-images-produced-by-ai-inceptionism-users-online-1509518#:~:text=This%20creates%20unusual%20effects%20layered,and%20animals%2C%20and%20stunning%20textures)). Artists and engineers quickly embraced this technique, hosting gallery shows of DeepDream-generated imagery. It was noted that the neural net often “saw” dogs and eyes everywhere – a direct result of being trained on ImageNet where dog images were plentiful ([Why DeepDream Dreams of Doggies | Aaron Hertzmann’s blog](https://aaronhertzmann.com/2020/12/29/deepdream.html#:~:text=blog%20aaronhertzmann,makes%20creatures%2C%20buildings%2C%20and%20vehicles)). Rather than avoid this, DeepDream art revels in it, producing what one might call *algorithmic psychedelia*. **Figure 1** shows an example of a DeepDream-transformed image (in this case, a scene from *Mad Max: Fury Road*) that has been laden with the characteristic dogs, eyes, and bizarre edifices of the DeepDream style ([Google DeepDream robot: 10 weirdest images produced by AI 'inceptionism' and users online | IBTimes UK](https://www.ibtimes.co.uk/google-deepdream-robot-10-weirdest-images-produced-by-ai-inceptionism-users-online-1509518#:~:text=Sometimes%2C%20the%20network%20interprets%20shapes,clouds%20or%20faces%20as%20animals)) ([Google DeepDream robot: 10 weirdest images produced by AI 'inceptionism' and users online | IBTimes UK](https://www.ibtimes.co.uk/google-deepdream-robot-10-weirdest-images-produced-by-ai-inceptionism-users-online-1509518)). The hallucination here is the art: the AI’s misinterpretations create novel visuals that no human would have imagined, yet human artists curated and guided these outputs into aesthetic pieces. This exemplifies turning an AI bug into a feature.

Beyond DeepDream, modern generative models like **DALL-E, Midjourney, and Stable Diffusion** allow users to create artwork from text prompts. These models sometimes introduce unexpected elements or styles not explicitly in the prompt – arguably minor hallucinations – which can actually enhance creativity. Many artists working with these tools do a form of **iterative curation**: they generate multiple images, observe the unprompted details the AI adds, and may decide those “hallucinated” details inspire a new direction, or they may refine the prompt to remove unwanted bits. For instance, an AI might add a flying bird in a landscape where none was asked for; the artist might either be delighted by the bird and keep it or explicitly tell the AI “no birds” next round. Thus, artists have a dialog with the AI’s imagination. In digital music, AI systems sometimes generate odd harmonies or rhythms – while many outputs are discarded, a musician might pick a few bars of an AI-generated melody that have a weird appeal and integrate them into a human-composed piece.

**Scientific Discovery and Ideation:** As noted earlier, the science and innovation sector is exploring AI as a brainstorming partner. While an AI’s factual errors are problematic in a formal paper, in the **idea generation phase** they can be tolerated or even encouraged. One example is in **drug discovery**. Pharmaceutical researchers have started using generative models to propose novel molecular structures with desired properties (e.g., fit to a protein target). These models, if unconstrained, might churn out chemically implausible structures – effectively hallucinating molecules that violate known chemistry rules or are completely novel in composition. Surprisingly, some of these wild suggestions can lead chemists to consider new modifications to known compounds. A much-cited experiment (mentioned before) demonstrated that an AI model could generate 40,000 hypothetical toxic molecules, many more lethal than known chemical weapons ([Researchers Flip Pharmaceutical AI to Create 40,000 Biowarfare ... \- VICE](https://www.vice.com/en/article/researchers-flip-pharmaceutical-ai-to-create-40000-biowarfare-agents/#:~:text=VICE%20www,novel%20molecules%20that%20the)) ([AI comes up with 40,000 toxic chemicals in 6 hours; scientists ... \- WION](https://www.wionews.com/technology/ai-comes-up-with-40000-toxic-chemicals-in-6-hours-scientists-warn-could-be-used-to-make-biological-weapons-561636#:~:text=WION%20www,s%20Defense%20Science%20and)). While this was presented as a cautionary tale about AI misuse, it also revealed the breadth of chemical space AI can imagine. In a positive sense, similar models could enumerate lots of potential drug candidates beyond the scope of human intuition. The key is then subjecting them to filtering by chemical validity and testing. Here the AI’s hallucination (inventing molecules) is literally the point – we *want* novel compounds – but we need human or automated screening to pick which ones aren’t nonsense or dangerous. This combo of AI creativity with rigorous evaluation accelerates the early stages of drug design.

In scientific research more broadly, some academics use LLMs as a **“sparring partner”** to test hypotheses or generate alternative explanations. An AI might propose something that is wrong, but in analyzing why it’s wrong, a researcher might clarify their own thinking or even have an unexpected insight. There are anecdotes of using GPT-3 to suggest research questions or to list possible connections between concepts, where maybe 9 out of 10 suggestions are useless but the tenth is intriguing. The caveat, of course, is that relying on AI for scientific content can be dangerous – as the Galactica episode taught us, an AI that writes “scientifically” can easily fool a non-expert into thinking a false statement is true if it uses jargon confidently ([Why ChatGPT and Bing Chat are so good at making things up | Ars Technica](https://web.archive.org/web/20230611024338/https://arstechnica.com/information-technology/2023/04/why-ai-chatbots-are-the-ultimate-bs-machines-and-how-people-hope-to-fix-them/#:~:text=It%27s%20a%20big%20problem%20when,to%20prison%2C%20a%20complete%20fabrication)). So in practice, scientists mostly use AI for low-risk creative aid (brainstorming, summarizing known info with caution) rather than trusting it for new claims.

**Education and Learning:** AI’s role in education is double-edged because of hallucinations. On one side, tools like ChatGPT can act as tutors or study aids, explaining concepts, answering questions, and generating practice problems. Many students have found these tools helpful. However, educators are wary – if the AI provides an incorrect explanation or answer, students might learn something wrong. Educators are developing strategies to integrate AI while mitigating its inaccuracies. One approach is **teaching students to use AI critically**: for example, a class assignment might involve asking ChatGPT a question, then verifying its answer and correcting it. This turns the hallucination issue into a lesson in research and skepticism, as mentioned previously. The Center for Democracy & Technology pointed out the concern that students might turn to AI for answers on important personal or academic matters and get misleading information ([Students’ Use of Generative AI: The Threat of Hallucinations](https://cdt.org/insights/students-use-of-generative-ai-the-threat-of-hallucinations/#:~:text=Students%E2%80%99%20Use%20of%20Generative%20AI%3A,health%20issue%2C%20which%20could)) ([Students’ Use of Generative AI: The Threat of Hallucinations](https://cdt.org/insights/students-use-of-generative-ai-the-threat-of-hallucinations/#:~:text=personal%20use%20for%20important%20issues,health%20issue%2C%20which%20could)). Addressing this, some schools now provide guidelines: use AI for brainstorming ideas or getting explanations of concepts you already learned (to see it phrased differently), but do not use it as an authoritative source for something you haven’t covered, and always cross-check important facts.

There are also AI-powered educational software that deliberately include a human teacher in the loop. For instance, an AI might draft a personalized learning plan or explain a math problem step-by-step, but it’s reviewed by a teacher or the student is prompted to confirm each step. If the AI happens to slip in a wrong step (hallucinating a mathematical justification that isn’t valid), the student or teacher can catch it. In this sense, the educational value can come not just from the AI’s direct instruction but from the student’s engagement in **evaluating** the AI. Some have argued this could build critical thinking skills – students may become more astute at fact-checking and sourcing because they know the AI might be wrong sometimes. This is an interesting outcome: rather than rote trust, students develop an investigative approach to learning.

On the flip side, if students are unaware of hallucination issues, they might over-trust AI and not learn the material properly. A global study (PLOS 2023\) noted that a large fraction of higher education students were using ChatGPT to summarize texts or brainstorm ([Higher education students’ perceptions of ChatGPT: A global study of ...](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0315011#:~:text=Higher%20education%20students%E2%80%99%20perceptions%20of,it%20for%20professional%20and)). Summarizing is relatively safe (less chance to hallucinate if the AI can be constrained to the original text), but brainstorming could introduce errors. Therefore, some universities are offering workshops on “How to use AI tools wisely,” emphasizing verification. We may even see curricula include modules on AI literacy: knowing that coherence ≠ correctness.

**Other Applications:** In fields like customer service or business, AI chatbots are being used to handle inquiries. Companies must be careful to prevent the bots from hallucinating policies or product details that aren’t real, as that could mislead customers. A strategy here is to limit the AI’s scope: some bots retrieve answers from a company’s FAQ database (like a closed-book QA over a known set of documents). This greatly limits hallucinations because the bot is not free-wheeling; it tries to stay anchored to provided text. In creative industries like game design, AI can be used to generate storylines or character dialogue. Minor hallucinations (like a character mentioning an event that didn’t happen in-game) need to be edited out, but designers enjoy the speed at which AI can produce a variety of narrative threads.

In journalism, AI tools can draft news pieces, but reputable outlets strictly fact-check them. News organizations have learned from some early fumbles (one outlet had to issue corrections after an AI-written article on football matches contained wrong scores and player names that were hallucinated). Now, either they don’t use AI for sensitive factual reporting or they ensure human editors verify every detail. Some outlets use AI more for less critical content (like formulating templated finance reports or weather forecasts from data, where there’s little room for hallucination because the inputs are structured data).

**Turning Hallucinations into Innovation:** A theme emerging in real-world use is *controlled environments*. When hallucinations can be caught or don’t cause harm, they can be used creatively. We see this in art (where the worst case is just a weird image – not a problem) and in internal brainstorming (where experts vet the outputs). Conversely, in public-facing or critical systems, hallucinations are heavily curtailed by design or process. There’s also a push in some applications to always provide sources for factual claims – for instance, Bing’s AI cites web pages for each statement, and some newer systems like meta’s LLaMA2 when used with retrieval will present reference text. This way, the user sees the basis and can judge if the AI might be stretching beyond it.

Real-world lessons are feeding back into research: developers now have many examples of how and where hallucinations happen in deployment, which helps create better tests and training. The dynamic nature of human-AI interaction in these applications also shows that *transparency and user control* can mitigate a lot of issues. If a user can click and see “source: Wikipedia” or “source: company policy document,” they can trust the output more – and if the AI didn’t cite anything, they might suspect it could be making it up.

In conclusion, AI hallucinations are not just a lab curiosity; they are being encountered daily in practical applications. Different fields handle them differently: some exploit them for creativity, others guard against them for accuracy. The key is context. Where precision is king, systems must be engineered to minimize hallucinations or flag uncertainty. Where exploration is the goal, a bit of hallucination can be embraced. This real-world push-pull ensures that the concept of hallucination is now part of the vocabulary of artists, scientists, and educators working with AI, not just AI developers. As AI continues to integrate into various sectors, mastering the balance – encouraging innovation while containing misinformation – will be a continual effort.

## **Synthesis and Future Directions**

Bringing together insights from technology, neuroscience, psychology, and real-world practice, we synthesize a comprehensive view of AI hallucinations and outline future directions. A recurring theme is the **duality** of hallucinations: they are both a **bug** to be fixed and a potential **feature** to be harnessed, depending on context and management.

From the technical analysis, we see that hallucinations are an emergent property of powerful generative models that lack innate grounding. In many ways, the AI community is trying to *bolt on* a simulated form of common sense and factual awareness after the fact. Future AI systems might take a different approach: instead of a single monolithic model that does everything (and hallucinates when stretched), we may develop **hybrid systems** that explicitly combine modules – one that generates and one that verifies, or one that imagines and one that perceives. This could be inspired by the brain’s architecture: sensory input (to ground), memory (for knowledge), and executive functions (for reasoning). There is already movement in this direction, for example, the idea of a “System 1” (fast, creative, intuitive) and “System 2” (slow, logical, checkable) within AI, analogous to psychologist Daniel Kahneman’s theory of human cognition. A future AI might carry an internal dual process: first draft an answer with a creative network, then review it with a rigorous network before output. Some prototypes of this exist (like transformer models that do a self-consistency check by generating multiple answers internally and seeing if they agree). As computing resources grow, running multiple passes or having ensembles of sub-models could become feasible even in real-time systems.

Interdisciplinary collaboration will be key to these advancements. **Neuroscience** might inform new learning paradigms; for example, predictive coding algorithms could train AI to better estimate when a claim doesn’t match “reality” by simulating feedback. **Psychology** can guide user interface design to ensure AI systems communicate in a way that aligns with human expectations and cognitive styles. It can also inform how to calibrate AI assertiveness – perhaps by studying how human experts phrase uncertain information and emulating that (e.g., using hedges like “it’s likely that…” when confidence is low, which current models do to some extent). Meanwhile, **computer science** research will continue to reduce hallucinations through data (more high-quality, factual data) and algorithms (improved loss functions that penalize inconsistency with known facts, etc.).

A speculative but intriguing avenue is the development of **artificial imagination modules** separate from artificial knowledge bases. One could imagine next-gen AI where there’s a knowledge graph of verified facts (like an encyclopedia the AI can query) and a creative generator (like an LLM) that proposes ideas. The AI could intentionally “fantasize” scenarios (like a simulation engine) but also check them. This separation might let us explicitly dial the creativity up or down. It might also make it easier to trace how a given hallucination occurred: was it because the knowledge module lacked data, or because the imagination ran wild despite data? Transparency and traceability of AI decisions are major goals for building trust. Already, techniques like *chain-of-thought prompting* give a glimpse into the model’s reasoning by making it output intermediate steps. Future AI might maintain an internal logic trace that can be examined to see where a hallucination crept in (“At step 3, the system assumed X which is not true, leading to a wrong conclusion at step 5”).

Another future direction is **context-aware AI**. If an AI can detect the context of its use, it might adjust its behavior. For example, if it knows it’s playing a creative role (writing a story or a joke), it can feel free to hallucinate for entertainment. If it knows it’s in an academic or legal setting, it could switch to a stringent mode where it refuses to produce content not supported by sources. Achieving this might involve environment tags or user instructions that the AI can strongly condition on. More ambitiously, one can imagine AI that learns from *real-time user feedback*: if a user corrects the AI, the system could update its internal state or at least apologize and not repeat the mistake in the same session. Some current chatbots do have short-term memory of feedback, but long-term learning from such interactions is an open area (due to risk of drift if malicious or incorrect user feedback is given).

Interdisciplinary scholarship might also explore **philosophical questions** raised by AI hallucinations. For instance, what does it mean for creativity if a machine can generate original-seeming ideas by essentially making stochastic errors? This challenges some traditional notions of human uniqueness in creativity. As AI gets integrated, we may co-create so closely that attributing an idea to a human or AI becomes blurred – who “hallucinated” that solution, the person or the tool? Some foresee a future where human-AI teams are the norm in creative industries, and success comes from how well the human can steer the AI’s generative power. This requires a shift in education and work culture, training people to be effective AI collaborators (sometimes dubbed “prompt engineering” skills, but it’s more than just writing prompts – it’s reading AI output critically, giving iterative guidance, etc.).

The ethical and regulatory landscape will likely evolve too. If we project a decade ahead, we might have industry standards for factual accuracy in AI depending on application. There could be certifications (like an FDA for AI) that mark a system as safe for medical advice because it’s been tested extensively to rarely hallucinate and always show sources. Conversely, pure creative AI (for art, etc.) might carry a label “for creative use only – not factual.” Watermarking AI-generated content is another idea so that if something like a fake news article appears, it can be traced and identified as AI-written, prompting extra verification. OpenAI, Google, and others are researching watermarking techniques. This can mitigate the misinformation risk: if major platforms detect an AI watermark, they might downrank that content or attach a warning.

The interplay between AI and human cognition research could deepen in surprising ways. Perhaps studying why AI makes specific mistakes will shed light on human cognitive biases too, an area of “AI as cognitive mirror.” Already, some researchers compare AI confabulations to human false memories, suggesting that analyzing the patterns might help psychologists understand memory integration errors. Conversely, cognitive psychology might suggest training regimes for AI: for example, humans learn by active questioning of their environment (children incessantly ask “why?”). Maybe future AI will do a form of active learning in deployment – if uncertain, it might query a database or ask the user a question instead of guessing. That flips the script from one where AI always answers to one where it can occasionally become the questioner to gather missing info (something currently rare outside of clarification in dialogue).

Another future prospect is **improving multimodal understanding** to reduce hallucination. For instance, an AI with both vision and language might cross-verify: “I’m talking about an object, can I picture it? Does it exist in the image I have (or can find)?” As robotics advances, AI that controls physical sensors (cameras, mics) could literally check reality: if unsure about a detail, maybe a home assistant robot could physically look or ask the user for confirmation. This grounds AI in the physical world more directly.

Finally, we should consider the role of **ongoing human oversight**. Rather than fully autonomous AI in critical roles, a likely near-term future is AI that works under human supervision (human-on-the-loop or in-the-loop). This ensures a safety net for hallucinations. As trust builds and technology improves, maybe some tasks can be delegated completely, but likely with heavy testing. Aviation is a parallel: we have autopilot (and soon autonomous drones), but there’s a lot of fail-safes and typically a human pilot on standby. Similarly, an AI doctor might handle routine cases automatically but a human specialist reviews edge cases or anything the AI flags as uncertain. Building AI that knows when to hand off to a human is as important as making it right most of the time.

In synthesis, the path forward entails a combination of **technological innovation**, **interdisciplinary insight**, and **governance**. AI hallucinations are at the crux of what makes AI both fascinating and fallible. Addressing them will likely bring us closer to AI that not only performs tasks but also understands (to some degree) the content it generates. As one researcher put it, we want to move from next-token prediction to **next-fact prediction**, bridging the gap between pattern and truth. Achieving this is a grand challenge, requiring ideas from machine learning, logic, cognitive science, and beyond. The journey itself will deepen our understanding of intelligence – artificial and natural – and drive us to craft AI that works harmoniously with human society’s needs and values.

## **Conclusion**

AI hallucinations, once an obscure quirk observed in neural networks, have become a central concern in the age of large-scale generative AI. In this paper, we have traversed a broad landscape – from the historical coining of the term *hallucination* in AI research, through technical analyses of why models like GPT hallucinate and how we might curb it, to comparisons with human cognition and creativity, and finally to the ethical, societal, and practical implications of this phenomenon. This interdisciplinary exploration reveals that AI hallucinations are not merely “bugs” to eliminate, but a complex phenomenon that touches on fundamental aspects of intelligence and imagination.

We find that **hallucinations encapsulate both the limits and the possibilities of AI**. They underscore that today’s AI, for all its linguistic and perceptual prowess, lacks a genuine understanding of truth – it is a master imitator without a truth compass. At the same time, the ability to generate beyond the bounds of existing knowledge (even if it means making things up) is intimately tied to creativity and discovery. The challenge ahead is to disentangle the useful generative capacity from the undesirable falsehoods. Achieving this will likely involve hybridizing AI architectures with retrieval systems, embedding more explicit reasoning capabilities, and drawing inspiration from how the human brain constrains its own imaginative projections through perception and reflection.

From a human-AI interaction standpoint, our study highlights the need for **user education and transparency**. Users of AI systems must be made aware – through both documentation and the AI’s own communicative behavior – that “plausible doesn’t guarantee correct.” Equipping AI with the ability to explain its reasoning or cite its sources is a promising route to make its outputs more interpretable and checkable ([When AI Gets It Wrong: Addressing AI Hallucinations and Bias \- MIT Sloan Teaching & Learning Technologies](https://mitsloanedtech.mit.edu/ai/basics/addressing-ai-hallucinations-and-bias/#:~:text=1,accuracy%20in%20their%20outputs%20is)) ([Why ChatGPT and Bing Chat are so good at making things up | Ars Technica](https://web.archive.org/web/20230611024338/https://arstechnica.com/information-technology/2023/04/why-ai-chatbots-are-the-ultimate-bs-machines-and-how-people-hope-to-fix-them/#:~:text=In%20academic%20literature%2C%20AI%20researchers,responsibility%20for%20the%20outputs%20themselves)). Likewise, cultivating an attitude of collaboration (where AI is a creative partner and tool, not an infallible oracle) will serve users well. This aligns with perspectives from psychology and education: critical thinking and skepticism are more important than ever in the AI era, and our educational systems should adapt to emphasize these skills ([Explore “Hallucinations” to Better Understand AI’s Affordances and ...](https://academictech.uchicago.edu/2024/03/21/explore-hallucinations-to-better-understand-ais-affordances-and-risks-part-one/#:~:text=,for%20education%20and%20social)) ([Hallucination (artificial intelligence) \- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination_\(artificial_intelligence\)#:~:text=recorded%20in%20multiple%20cases%20to,51)).

Ethically, we stress that developers and organizations deploying AI have a responsibility to mitigate risks of hallucinations. This could mean restricting certain applications until robust safeguards are in place, continuously monitoring AI outputs in the wild, and being responsive to corrections. As regulation looms, proactive self-regulation and standards-setting by the AI industry can ensure that technology is developed in a way that prioritizes **accuracy, safety, and respect for truth**. In parallel, embracing contributions from neuroscientists, cognitive scientists, and domain experts will enrich AI development, ensuring it is informed by how natural intelligence avoids and handles errors ([The Brain as a Prediction Machine: The Key to Consciousness? | Psychology Today](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/blog/finding-purpose/202201/the-brain-prediction-machine-the-key-consciousness#:~:text=7,in%20Vancouver%2C%20BC%2C%20%2089)) ([ERIC \- EJ577411 \- Donald Campbell's Model of the Creative Process: Creativity as Blind Variation and Selective Retention., Journal of Creative Behavior, 1998](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ577411#:~:text=This%20introductory%20article%20discusses%20a,%28Author%2FCR)).

In conclusion, AI hallucinations sit at the intersection of error and innovation. They remind us that current AI, despite its veneer of coherence, does not truly “know” in the way humans do – a gap that must be acknowledged and addressed as we integrate AI systems into society. Yet, in grappling with this gap, we may unlock new levels of AI capability. By curbing the risks of hallucinations while leveraging their creative spark, we can aim for AI that is both imaginative and trustworthy. The journey to that goal will undoubtedly demand ongoing interdisciplinary dialogue and careful balancing of creativity and control. It is our hope that this paper has provided a clear and nuanced understanding of AI hallucinations, and that it fosters further research and collaboration across fields to navigate the promises and pitfalls of AI’s imaginative prowess.
