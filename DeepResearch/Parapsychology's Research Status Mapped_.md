# **Mapping the Margins of Science: A Multi-Lens Inquiry into Parapsychology's Contemporary Research Status**

## **Introduction: Parapsychology at the Scientific Margins – An STS Inquiry**

Parapsychology, broadly defined as the scientific investigation of purported psychic (psi) phenomena such as extrasensory perception (ESP) and psychokinesis (PK) 1, occupies a unique and contested position within the landscape of modern science. Its subject matter—experiences and interactions that appear to transcend conventional understandings of time, space, and causality—places it at the periphery of established scientific disciplines.1 For over a century, the field has sought legitimacy and integration while facing persistent skepticism and accusations of pseudoscience from the mainstream scientific community.1 This enduring tension makes parapsychology a compelling case study for Science and Technology Studies (STS), offering valuable perspectives on the social construction of scientific legitimacy, the dynamics of boundary work, the persistence of marginalized fields, and the complex interplay between knowledge production, institutional structures, and societal beliefs.3

This report undertakes a multi-lens inquiry into the contemporary research status of parapsychology, focusing on the period encompassing the early 2020s. The central objective is to systematically assess whether parapsychology currently functions as an active, albeit marginalized, scientific field characterized by institutional support, knowledge production, and epistemic engagement, or if it operates primarily as a self-contained, fringe knowledge ecosystem largely disconnected from broader scientific discourse and resources \[Query Objective\].

Adopting an approach grounded in STS principles, this investigation employs multiple analytical lenses to triangulate the field's status. These lenses examine: (1) the **Institutional Ecosystem**, mapping the universities, laboratories, and organizations where research occurs; (2) the **Network of Investigators**, identifying key researchers and professional bodies; (3) **Knowledge Dissemination**, evaluating publication outlets and their impact; (4) **Resource Mobilization**, tracking funding sources and economic realities; (5) **Contested Legitimacy**, analyzing demarcation efforts and epistemic debates; and (6) **Sociological Dimensions**, exploring the interplay between public perception, media, and the scientific field \[Query Framework\]. The inquiry maintains an epistemically agnostic stance, focusing on methodological rigor and evidence-based assessment rather than endorsing or dismissing the reality of psi phenomena *a priori*.

The intended audience for this report includes researchers in STS, academic policymakers, grant evaluators, and philosophers of science \[Query Audience\]. The analysis draws upon a range of sources detailing the activities, structures, and debates surrounding parapsychology. The report proceeds by systematically examining the field through each analytical lens, culminating in a synthesis that addresses the core question of parapsychology's current position at the margins of science.

## **I. The Institutional Ecosystem: Centers of Activity**

The institutional landscape of parapsychology reveals a field characterized by niche persistence within academia alongside a reliance on independent organizations. While its historical roots trace back to university settings, such as the Duke University Parapsychology Labs established in the 1930s 6, its contemporary presence within academia is limited to a small number of specialized units, primarily sustained through external funding and operating within specific departmental contexts.

**A. University-Based Research Units: Mapping the Global Presence**

Despite significant challenges to mainstream integration, several university-based units dedicated to parapsychological or related research persist, primarily in the United States and the United Kingdom, with a notable presence in Sweden. These units often represent legacies of specific endowments or the efforts of dedicated faculty members.

* **University of Virginia (UVA) Division of Perceptual Studies (DOPS):** Founded in 1967 by Dr. Ian Stevenson within the Department of Psychiatry and Neurobehavioral Sciences, DOPS represents one of the longest-standing academic centers.8 Its research focuses on the nature of consciousness, particularly evidence suggesting survival beyond bodily death, including near-death experiences (NDEs), out-of-body experiences (OBEs), and children's reports of past-life memories.8 DOPS employs empirical methods, including neuroimaging (EEG) and systematic case collection (maintaining a database of over 2,200 reincarnation-type cases).8 Key faculty include or have included Drs. Bruce Greyson, Jim Tucker (now retired), Kim Penberthy, Marieta Pehlivanova, and Marina Weiler.8 Crucially, DOPS operates without financial support from the university, relying entirely on donations and external funding.8  
* **University of Edinburgh Koestler Parapsychology Unit (KPU):** Established in 1985 through a significant bequest from Arthur Koestler, the KPU holds the only endowed chair of parapsychology in the UK.14 Situated within the Psychology Department, it conducts research into the existence of psi, anomalous experiences, belief formation, deception, and historical/conceptual issues.11 The unit, led for many years by Professor Caroline Watt (now Emeritus) 14, offers PhD supervision and maintains a study registry.15 Its definition of parapsychology explicitly includes studying "what's not psychic but looks like it," indicating a scope that encompasses conventional explanations for anomalous experiences.17  
* **Lund University Center for Research on Consciousness and Anomalous Psychology (CERCAP):** Directed by Professor Etzel Cardeña, CERCAP is located within Lund's Department of Psychology in Sweden.18 It focuses on the scientific study of unusual (but not necessarily pathological) experiences, including psi phenomena, hypnosis, dissociation, and altered states of consciousness.19 CERCAP offers advanced undergraduate courses and postgraduate supervision, aiming to train students and foster collaborations.16 It also sponsors the open-access *Journal of Anomalous Experience and Cognition* (JAEX).18  
* **University of Northampton Exceptional Experiences and Consciousness Studies (EECS):** Evolving from the former Centre for the Study of Anomalous Psychological Processes (CSAPP), EECS is a research group within the Psychology division of the Faculty of Health, Education and Society.27 Its research encompasses parapsychology, transpersonal psychology, and consciousness studies.11 Key figures associated with the unit include Professor Chris Roe (former CSAPP Director), Professor Deborah Delanoy (Founder/Past Director), Dr. Callum Cooper, and Dr. David Saunders.28 The university offers undergraduate modules in parapsychology and postgraduate research opportunities (MSc, PhD).11 EECS also curates an archive of historical paranormal research materials donated by Aucott & Thomas.27  
* **Goldsmiths, University of London Anomalistic Psychology Research Unit (APRU):** Founded in 2000 by Professor Chris French (now Emeritus Head) 35, the APRU represents a distinct but related field: *anomalistic psychology*. Its explicit aim is to explain paranormal beliefs and ostensibly paranormal experiences using known psychological and physical factors, without assuming psi is involved.11 Operating within the Psychology Department, it utilizes departmental resources rather than having dedicated labs.35 The unit has produced numerous publications and its associated course is accredited by the British Psychological Society (BPS).35 While not conducting psi research itself, APRU's existence and approach are significant in the context of parapsychology's legitimacy struggles.  
* **Other Pockets of Activity:** Various other universities are mentioned as hosting or having hosted related research or courses, including the University of Arizona (consciousness studies, mediumship research) 11, University of West Georgia 43, University of Hertfordshire 43, University of California Santa Barbara (privately funded NeuroCasuality Lab focusing on precognition) 11, University of Gothenburg (Adrian Parker, consciousness/altered states) 24, and institutions offering related degrees like Atlantic University or the University of Philosophical Research.11 The current activity level and stability of these smaller pockets require ongoing verification, but they suggest a dispersed, low-level interest beyond the main centers.

The persistence of these university units, often within Psychology departments 11 or related fields like Psychiatry 9, demonstrates a strategy of niche adaptation. Lacking broad institutional acceptance and core funding, these units survive by securing specific institutional spaces, often leveraging external endowments (KPU 14) or philanthropic donations (DOPS 8), thereby adapting to a marginal position rather than achieving full integration.

**B. Independent Organizations: Roles and Contributions**

Complementing the limited university presence is a network of independent research centers and professional organizations that provide crucial infrastructure, funding, and community support for the field.

* **Rhine Research Center:** As the direct successor to the influential Duke Parapsychology Lab, the Rhine Center carries significant historical weight.6 Operating independently since 1965, it pursues research (currently focusing on PK, bioenergy, healing, ESP 45), publishes the *Journal of Parapsychology* 6, offers online educational programs 6, and fosters a community for those interested in psi phenomena.6  
* **Parapsychological Association (PA):** Founded in 1957, the PA serves as the primary international professional society for credentialed researchers in the field.7 It organizes annual conventions, facilitates scholarly exchange, publishes proceedings and the *Journal of Parapsychology* (via affiliation), and maintains ethical standards.46 Its affiliation with the American Association for the Advancement of Science (AAAS) since 1969, though controversial, represents a key marker of attempted integration with mainstream science.7 The PA also administered research grants (PARE, Roller, Morris funds), although these were suspended as of late 2020 due to financial difficulties.48  
* **Parapsychology Foundation (PF):** Established in 1951, the PF is a non-profit entity dedicated to supporting scientific and academic research into psi through various grants and fellowships (e.g., Coly Grants, Garrett Scholarship, Bolton Fellowship).53 It also provides informational resources, publishes materials (including the *International Journal of Parapsychology*), maintains a library, and sponsors initiatives like the JAEX journal.25  
* **Society for Scientific Exploration (SSE):** Founded in 1982, the SSE offers a broader platform for studying unusual and unexplained phenomena, encompassing parapsychology but also areas like UFOlogy and alternative medicine.54 It publishes the *Journal of Scientific Exploration* (JSE) and *EdgeScience* magazine, holds conferences, and welcomes public membership.54 While providing a forum for academics interested in anomalies, its broad scope and inclusion of topics widely considered pseudoscientific have drawn criticism.54  
* **Institute of Noetic Sciences (IONS):** Founded in 1973 by astronaut Edgar Mitchell, IONS is a non-profit research center focused on consciousness, interconnection, and human potential.18 It conducts research (with notable figures like Dean Radin 18), hosts events, and relies on donations and membership for funding.58  
* **Other Related Bodies:** Older organizations like the Society for Psychical Research (SPR) in the UK (founded 1882\) 59 and the American Society for Psychical Research (ASPR) 7 continue to exist, alongside more specialized groups like the International Association for Near-Death Studies (IANDS) 7 and newer entities like the Windbridge Research Center.18

These independent centers function as crucial parallel structures, providing continuity and resources (research funding, publication venues, community hubs) that are often unavailable or insufficient within the academic mainstream. They compensate for the field's limited institutionalization within universities.

**C. Integration or Isolation within Academia**

The evidence suggests that parapsychology remains largely isolated from mainstream academia, despite the persistence of a few dedicated units. The reliance on external/private funding for these units 8, the limited number of faculty positions dedicated to the field, and the scarcity of parapsychology courses within standard university curricula point towards marginalization rather than integration.16 While departmental affiliations (mostly Psychology 11) suggest an attempt at disciplinary alignment, the overall picture is one of limited footholds rather than broad acceptance.

The existence of Anomalistic Psychology (AP) units, such as Goldsmiths' APRU 35, further highlights this dynamic. AP offers a framework for studying "paranormal" topics by seeking conventional psychological explanations.40 This approach gains more academic traction (e.g., BPS accreditation 42) precisely because it does *not* endorse the core premise of parapsychology (the potential reality of psi). In doing so, AP implicitly reinforces the boundary separating parapsychology from accepted scientific inquiry, acting as a form of institutionalized skepticism that legitimizes discussion of the *topic* while marginalizing parapsychology's *approach*.

**II. The Network of Investigators: Actors and Affiliations**

The community of researchers engaged with parapsychology is relatively small and operates within a network structured by both academic affiliations and independent organizations. Professional associations play a vital role in connecting these often geographically dispersed individuals and in projecting a collective scientific identity.

**A. Profiling Active Researchers: Credentials, Affiliations, and Research Foci**

The field includes individuals with formal academic credentials, typically PhDs in psychology or related disciplines, who are based at the university units described previously. Prominent examples include Etzel Cardeña (Lund University) 18, Caroline Watt (Emeritus, Edinburgh) 14, Chris Roe (Northampton) 31, and researchers at UVA DOPS like Bruce Greyson and Marieta Pehlivanova.8 Their research often aligns with the specific focus of their respective units, such as Cardeña's work on anomalous experiences, consciousness, and psi 21, or DOPS' focus on survival-related phenomena.8

Alongside these academics are researchers primarily affiliated with independent organizations or operating as independent scholars. Dean Radin, as Chief Scientist at IONS 18, exemplifies this category. Historically significant figures like J.B. Rhine operated initially within Duke University but later established the independent Rhine Center.6 Other active or recently active figures mentioned include Nancy Zingrone and the late Carlos Alvarado, known for historical research and online education 18, and Michael Grosso.18 This distinction between university-based academics and those operating independently is crucial for understanding the field's structure and varying degrees of institutional integration.

**B. The Role of Professional Associations (PA, SSE) in Community Building**

Given the limited number of dedicated research centers and the geographical dispersion of researchers, professional associations are indispensable for maintaining the field's coherence and identity.

* The **Parapsychological Association (PA)** acts as the core professional body specifically for parapsychologists worldwide.7 Its annual conventions provide a vital forum for presenting research and fostering collaboration.46 By publishing proceedings and affiliating with the *Journal of Parapsychology*, it facilitates knowledge dissemination within the community.7 Its grant programs (though currently suspended 52) offered crucial, albeit limited, research support.52 Furthermore, the PA establishes ethical and professional standards, contributing to the field's self-regulation.49  
* The **Society for Scientific Exploration (SSE)** caters to a broader interest in scientific anomalies, attracting academics from diverse fields who might be hesitant to associate solely with parapsychology.54 Its conferences and publications (*JSE*, *EdgeScience*) offer alternative venues for disseminating research on unconventional topics.54 While its scope extends beyond psi, it serves as an important node in the network, potentially bridging gaps between parapsychology and researchers in other fields interested in anomalies.

These associations perform essential functions in building and sustaining the research community. They are also central to the field's ongoing efforts to project a scientific identity and achieve legitimacy. The PA's hard-won affiliation with the AAAS 7, the establishment of peer-reviewed journals 6, and the development of ethical codes 49 can be understood as strategic actions—forms of boundary work—aimed at demarcating parapsychology as a legitimate scientific endeavor distinct from popular occultism or uncritical belief, while simultaneously seeking recognition from the mainstream scientific establishment.3

**C. Navigating Boundaries: Career Paths Between Mainstream and Fringe Science**

The available information provides little evidence of significant movement *between* established mainstream scientific careers and dedicated parapsychology research. It appears more common for individuals to specialize in parapsychology relatively early or to carve out a specific niche within a broader academic department, often facing considerable skepticism.43 The very existence of dedicated units and independent centers suggests the difficulties inherent in fully integrating parapsychological research into standard departmental structures and career paths. The professional risks associated with the field, including potential difficulties in securing funding and positions, likely deter many from crossing the boundary from mainstream science into parapsychology.43

The path taken by anomalistic psychologists like Chris French offers a contrasting trajectory: engaging with paranormal topics through the lens of conventional psychology provides a more academically accepted route.35 This highlights the permeability of the boundary depending on the framing and theoretical commitments adopted. While early pioneers like Rhine aimed for integration 4, the subsequent history suggests a persistent divergence for many pursuing psi research.

The overall researcher landscape thus reflects the field's marginalization. It comprises a small core of academics operating within specific university niches, often reliant on external support, complemented by a network of researchers affiliated with independent centers. Professional associations are critical for binding this dispersed community and advancing its collective project of scientific legitimacy.

**III. Knowledge Dissemination: Publication Landscape and Impact**

The communication and validation of research findings in parapsychology primarily occur through a dedicated ecosystem of specialized journals, supplemented by occasional, often controversial, appearances in mainstream scientific literature. The characteristics of this publication landscape—particularly journal indexing and impact metrics—reflect the field's ongoing struggle for broader scientific visibility and integration.

**A. The Ecology of Parapsychology Journals: Scope, Frequency, and Reach**

A small number of peer-reviewed journals serve as the primary outlets for parapsychological research. Key among these are:

* ***Journal of Parapsychology (JP):*** Founded in 1937 at Duke University by J.B. Rhine, JP is arguably the field's flagship journal.6 Published biannually by the Rhine Research Center (Parapsychology Press), it covers empirical, theoretical, and methodological papers on psi (ESP, PK), consciousness, and anomalous experiences across various disciplines.63 It adheres to APA style and anonymous peer review.63  
* ***Journal of Scientific Exploration (JSE):*** Established in 1987 by the Society for Scientific Exploration (SSE), JSE is published quarterly.56 Its scope is broader than JP, covering a wide spectrum of anomalies and unconventional topics, including parapsychology but also extending to areas like UFOlogy and alternative medicine, as well as philosophical issues.54 It also claims peer review and is now open access.54  
* ***Journal of the American Society for Psychical Research (JASPR):*** Associated with the ASPR, this journal also reports on the work of the PA.7  
* ***Journal of Anomalous Experience and Cognition (JAEX):*** A newer, open-access, no-fee journal launched in 2021, maintained by Lund University and sponsored by CERCAP and the Parapsychology Foundation.18 It focuses specifically on anomalous experiences (mystical, OBEs) and anomalous cognition (psi), welcoming diverse methodologies including registered reports.25

These journals provide essential platforms for communication within the field, adhering to standard academic practices like peer review.6 However, their reach is largely confined to the parapsychology community and those specifically seeking out this literature.

**B. Indexing and Visibility: Presence in Academic Databases**

Indexing in major academic databases like Scopus, Web of Science (WoS), PsycINFO, and PubMed is crucial for a field's visibility, discoverability, and perceived legitimacy.71 Parapsychology journals have had mixed success in achieving and maintaining such indexing.

* ***Journal of Parapsychology (JP):*** Is indexed in PsycINFO (APA's database for psychology) and Scopus (Elsevier's multidisciplinary database).63 It is also found in other databases like EBSCO and ProQuest collections.73 However, there is no indication from the sources that it is indexed in WoS (Clarivate), which is necessary for receiving an official Journal Impact Factor (JIF).71  
* ***Journal of Scientific Exploration (JSE):*** The journal's own website claims current indexing in Scopus and DOAJ.64 However, Scopus's own database information, as reported by Scimago Journal & Country Rank, indicates that JSE was *discontinued* from Scopus coverage as of 2018\.69 This discrepancy is significant; if discontinued, it represents a major loss of visibility in a key database. JSE is not mentioned as being indexed in WoS or PsycINFO.  
* ***Journal of Anomalous Experience and Cognition (JAEX):*** As a relatively new journal (launched 2021), it is likely indexed in DOAJ (Directory of Open Access Journals) but probably not yet, or perhaps never, in the major databases like Scopus, WoS, or PubMed.25

This indexing pattern reveals a significant challenge. While JP maintains a presence in PsycINFO and Scopus, the potential loss of Scopus indexing for JSE and the general absence from WoS limits the field's integration into mainstream scientific information flows. Indexing decisions by these major databases act as powerful gatekeeping mechanisms, reflecting and reinforcing judgments about a field's scientific standing.71 Parapsychology's partial and contested indexing underscores its marginal position.

**C. Citation Patterns and Measured Impact**

Bibliometric indicators like the Scimago Journal Rank (SJR) and H-index provide quantitative measures of a journal's influence. Data for parapsychology journals indicate low impact relative to mainstream science.

* ***Journal of Parapsychology (JP):*** SJR data consistently places JP in the third or fourth quartile (Q3/Q4) within the "Psychology (miscellaneous)" category on Scopus, signifying low relative citation impact.68 Its H-index is modest (reported as 21-23) 68, and metrics like Cites per Document are generally low, although a temporary spike in SJR occurred around 2017\.68  
* ***Journal of Scientific Exploration (JSE):*** Prior to its apparent discontinuation from Scopus, JSE also typically ranked in the lower quartiles (Q4, occasionally Q2) in the "Multidisciplinary" category, with a comparable H-index (around 19-21).69

These low metrics suggest that research published in these dedicated journals is primarily cited within the parapsychology community itself, with limited engagement or impact on the broader scientific landscape. This contributes to a "ghettoization" of knowledge, where findings circulate within a specialized community but fail to significantly influence or be scrutinized by mainstream science, further reinforcing the field's marginal status.

**D. Bridging the Divide?: Parapsychology in Mainstream Scientific Literature**

While the bulk of parapsychological research is published in specialized outlets, occasional publications appear in mainstream, high-impact journals. These instances often generate significant attention but do not necessarily signal broader acceptance.

* **Rarity and Controversy:** Publishing experimental psi research in top-tier mainstream journals is difficult and infrequent, likely due to skepticism about the phenomena, methodological rigor, or theoretical coherence.1  
* **Cardeña's 2018 Review:** A significant exception was Etzel Cardeña's 2018 review article, "The Experimental Evidence for Parapsychological Phenomena," published in *American Psychologist*, the flagship journal of the APA.18 Cardeña argued that the cumulative evidence for psi from meta-analyses is comparable to that for established phenomena in psychology.83 This publication in a high-impact journal (IF \~12.3 88) garnered considerable attention, receiving over 159 citations 83 and provoking critical responses, notably from skeptics Reber and Alcock, who argued in the same journal that psi claims are *a priori* impossible and the data must therefore be flawed.80  
* **Historical Precedents:** Earlier examples include the publication of SRI's remote viewing research in *Nature* in 1974 91, which also sparked major controversy 92, and discussions or publications in journals like *Psychological Bulletin* 79, *Behavioral and Brain Sciences* 93, and *Annual Review of Psychology*.79

The reaction to Cardeña's paper illustrates a paradox: while publication in a mainstream venue offers significant visibility, it can also intensify demarcation efforts by critics who feel compelled to reassert the boundaries of legitimate science. Such publications highlight the field's controversial nature rather than resolving the debate or leading to sustained integration. They become focal points for the ongoing struggle over legitimacy.

**IV. Resource Mobilization: The Economics of Psi Research**

The economic landscape of parapsychology research is characterized by a striking reliance on private and philanthropic funding sources and a near-complete absence of support from major public granting agencies in the US and UK. This funding structure significantly shapes the field's research capacity, sustainability, and perceived legitimacy.

**A. Mapping the Funding Flow: Identifying Key Patrons**

Evidence consistently points to private donors, foundations, and internal organizational resources as the primary means of financial support for parapsychological research.

* **Foundations and Associations:** The Parapsychology Foundation (PF) exists specifically to provide grants funded through its own resources.53 The Parapsychological Association's (PA) research endowment (PARE) was established through a large private donation from Dr. Gertrude Schmeidler, a prominent researcher.52 The Society for Scientific Exploration (SSE) is explicitly donor and member-funded.55  
* **Independent Centers:** Organizations like the Institute of Noetic Sciences (IONS) operate as non-profits relying on individual contributions, membership fees, and targeted giving campaigns.58 The Rhine Research Center, independent since 1965, likely depends heavily on similar philanthropic support.6  
* **University Units:** Even units within universities often operate primarily on external funding rather than core institutional budgets. UVA's DOPS is entirely funded through donations.8 Edinburgh's KPU was established via the Koestler bequest.14 The privately funded status of the lab at UCSB was explicitly noted.11

This pattern contrasts sharply with mainstream scientific fields, which typically draw significant support from governmental agencies. While some historical instances of government funding exist (e.g., CIA/NASA interest in SRI's work in the 1970s 91), these appear to be exceptions linked to specific Cold War or intelligence interests and do not reflect the current funding environment.96 A survey from the late 1970s estimated the total annual US funding for parapsychology research at a mere $552,000, deemed insufficient even then to support more than a handful of scientists.95

**B. The Absence of Mainstream Funding: Evidence from Public Grant Agencies**

Searches for evidence of funding from major public science agencies in the US and UK reveal a consistent lack of support for research explicitly focused on parapsychology or psi phenomena.

* **NIH (US National Institutes of Health):** Searches yielded references to NIH funding for initiatives using the acronym "PSI" (Protein Structure Initiative 97) or institutions like the Paul Scherrer Institute (PSI) in Switzerland 98, but no indication of funding for parapsychological psi research. The term "psi" appears only coincidentally.  
* **NSF (US National Science Foundation):** Similarly, numerous references to NSF funding involve unrelated uses of "PSI" (Psi Chi Psychology Honor Society 99, Plans for Safe and Inclusive Research101, Puget Sound Institute 104) or general programs like REU 105 and PFI.106 No evidence suggests NSF funding for parapsychology, anomalous cognition, or psi phenomena research itself.  
* **UKRI / ESRC (UK Research and Innovation / Economic and Social Research Council):** References discuss overall UKRI budgets 107, funding for the unrelated company PsiQuantum 110, general STFC fellowships 112, ESRC funding for social science PhDs (including Psychology at Edinburgh, but without specific mention of parapsychology topics) 113, and unrelated uses of "PSI" (Policy Studies Institute 114). No evidence indicates UKRI or ESRC funding for parapsychological research.

This systematic absence of funding from the primary governmental bodies responsible for supporting scientific research in the US and UK constitutes a powerful form of institutional demarcation. The funding decisions of agencies like NIH, NSF, and UKRI act as de facto judgments on the scientific legitimacy and priority of research areas. The fact that unrelated projects or organizations using the acronym "PSI" receive funding, while parapsychological psi research does not, underscores the targeted nature of this exclusion. Funding (or the lack thereof) serves as a potent boundary mechanism, perhaps more influential in practice than formal statements by scientific societies.

**C. Grant Characteristics and Trends: Scale and Sustainability**

The funding that *is* available within the parapsychology ecosystem tends to be small-scale and potentially precarious, limiting the scope and sustainability of research efforts.

* **Scale:** Grants offered by organizations like the PA were typically in the range of US$2,000 to $5,000, primarily intended to cover direct research costs.52 While the PF offers various grants and fellowships, the overall funding pool appears limited compared to mainstream science.53  
* **Sustainability:** The field is described as having rare career opportunities due to funding limitations.43 The suspension of PA research grants in 2020 due to the financial impact of the COVID-19 pandemic highlights the vulnerability of these funding streams.48 There is no evidence of significant growth in funding over the past decade; rather, the picture suggests ongoing financial constraints.

This reliance on small, often unstable funding sources likely imposes significant limitations on the research that can be conducted. It restricts the ability to undertake large-scale studies, employ sophisticated (and expensive) methodologies like widespread neuroimaging, conduct extensive replication series, or provide long-term support for researchers. This financial precarity may create a challenging feedback loop: limited funding potentially leads to research that is smaller in scale or methodological scope, which may then be more susceptible to criticism regarding statistical power or robustness, further hindering efforts to secure greater funding and legitimacy.1

**V. Contested Legitimacy: Boundary Work and Epistemic Debates**

Parapsychology's scientific legitimacy remains deeply contested. This is evident in the stances taken by major scientific organizations, the ongoing debates about its methodology and theoretical basis, and the explicit efforts by both proponents and critics to define the boundaries of acceptable science.

**A. Framing by Scientific Gatekeepers: Stances of National Academies and Associations**

The positions of influential scientific bodies range from outright dismissal to qualified tolerance or procedural affiliation, reflecting the lack of consensus regarding parapsychology's status.

* **National Academy of Sciences (NAS) / National Research Council (NRC):** The 1987/88 NRC report, commissioned by the US Army to evaluate performance enhancement techniques, delivered a strongly negative assessment of parapsychology.65 Its conclusion that there was "no scientific justification... for the existence of parapsychological phenomena" based on 130 years of research remains a significant and frequently cited judgment by a high-status scientific body.122 Parapsychologists vehemently criticized the report for alleged bias in its committee composition (including prominent skeptics Ray Hyman and James Alcock) and its selective review of evidence, arguing it did not represent a fair assessment.122 Nonetheless, the report solidified an official stance of skepticism from a key scientific institution.  
* **American Association for the Advancement of Science (AAAS):** The PA's affiliation with AAAS since 1969 is often highlighted by proponents as a mark of legitimacy.7 However, this affiliation was achieved after considerable effort and controversy 50 and does not necessarily imply AAAS endorsement of psi phenomena. Critics view the affiliation as lending unwarranted credibility to a pseudoscience.125 Attempts to expel the PA from AAAS have failed.7 The affiliation appears to be primarily procedural, recognizing the PA as an organized group of scientists rather than validating its specific research claims.  
* **American Psychological Association (APA):** The APA lacks a clear, unified official position on parapsychology. Its actions present a mixed picture. On one hand, its flagship journal, *American Psychologist*, published Cardeña's 2018 review favorable to psi evidence, along with critical rebuttals, indicating a willingness to host the debate.80 Its database, PsycINFO, indexes the *Journal of Parapsychology*.63 Its general ethics code applies to psychologists in the field.49 On the other hand, historical surveys suggest psychologists are significantly more skeptical of psi than other academics 79, and the APA has not issued formal position statements on parapsychology comparable to those on other controversial topics.128  
* **British Psychological Society (BPS):** Similar to the APA, the BPS does not appear to have a single definitive stance. Its publications, like *The Psychologist* and the *Research Digest*, have featured discussions of parapsychology, anomalistic psychology, and related research units (like KPU).14 The BPS Research Digest even noted that Cardeña's review presented findings meriting investigation.81 The accreditation of the Anomalistic Psychology course at Goldsmiths 42 and the existence of a Transpersonal Psychology Section 33 suggest some institutional space for related topics, though not necessarily an endorsement of psi.  
* **Royal Society (UK):** No specific statements regarding parapsychology were found in the provided materials. General scientific skepticism remains prevalent.59

Overall, the stances of major scientific bodies are characterized more by skepticism (NAS/NRC) or procedural tolerance (AAAS, APA, BPS) than by endorsement.

**B. Demarcation Strategies: Parapsychology's Efforts to Establish Scientific Credibility**

In response to skepticism and marginalization, parapsychologists have employed various strategies to demarcate their field as a legitimate scientific enterprise. These efforts constitute active boundary work aimed at aligning the field with accepted scientific norms.

* **Methodological Rigor:** A central strategy, particularly since Rhine's era, has been the adoption and refinement of experimental methods borrowed from psychology and other sciences. This includes emphasizing controlled laboratory conditions, randomization, statistical analysis (including sophisticated techniques like meta-analysis), peer review, and adherence to reporting standards like APA style.2  
* **Institutional Anchoring:** Establishing research programs within universities (Section I.A) and seeking affiliation with mainstream scientific organizations like the AAAS 7 are attempts to gain institutional legitimacy by association.  
* **Professional Infrastructure:** Creating dedicated professional societies (PA 7), journals (JP, JSE, etc.), and ethical guidelines 49 mimics the structure of established scientific disciplines and promotes internal standards.  
* **Theoretical Engagement:** Proponents attempt to bridge the theoretical gap by linking psi phenomena to concepts from modern physics (e.g., quantum entanglement, non-locality 81) or by proposing new models of consciousness that could accommodate psi.2  
* **Distancing from Non-Science:** Parapsychologists often implicitly or explicitly differentiate their work from popular occultism, spiritualism, or fraudulent claims, emphasizing their commitment to scientific inquiry.3

**C. Persistent Critiques: Reproducibility, Methodology, and Theoretical Integration**

Despite these efforts, parapsychology faces enduring criticisms that challenge its scientific standing.

* **The Reproducibility Problem:** The most persistent and damaging critique is the alleged lack of consistent, independently replicable evidence for psi phenomena.1 While proponents point to meta-analyses showing statistically significant effects across studies 86, critics argue these effects are weak, inconsistent, potentially attributable to publication bias (the file-drawer problem) or questionable research practices (QRPs), and often decline over time.1  
* **Methodological Concerns:** Critics continue to raise concerns about methodological rigor, citing potential flaws in experimental design, inadequate controls against sensory leakage or subtle cues, insufficient randomization, potential for experimenter bias, and even occasional fraud.1  
* **Theoretical Vacuum:** A major stumbling block is the lack of a widely accepted, testable theory that can explain *how* psi might work and integrate it with fundamental principles of physics and biology.1 Psi is often defined negatively—as an anomaly unexplained by current science—which critics argue is insufficient for a scientific research program.1  
* **Small Effect Sizes:** The typically small magnitude of effects reported in laboratory psi experiments, often requiring statistical aggregation via meta-analysis to reach significance, makes the evidence less compelling to many scientists compared to phenomena with larger, more readily observable effects.87  
* **A Priori Impossibility:** Some critics maintain that psi phenomena are fundamentally incompatible with established scientific laws and therefore cannot exist, rendering empirical investigation futile and any positive results artifactual.80 This represents the strongest form of demarcation, based on perceived theoretical irreconcilability.

**D. Parapsychology and the Replication Crisis in Psychology**

The broader "replication crisis" that gained prominence in psychology and other fields after 2011 provides a complex backdrop for parapsychology's own long-standing reproducibility issues.134 Daryl Bem's controversial 2011 paper reporting evidence for precognition using standard social psychology methods, and the subsequent difficulties in replicating his findings, became a key catalyst for the crisis.136

This context offers parapsychology a rhetorical opening: proponents can argue that their field's replication challenges are not unique and may even be better documented due to a longer history of publishing replication attempts, both successful and unsuccessful.51 Cardeña's 2018 review explicitly leverages this by comparing psi evidence favorably to other psychological findings.83 However, the crisis has also led to increased emphasis on methodological rigor, pre-registration, statistical power, and transparency across all of science.135 This raises the evidentiary bar, potentially making it even harder for a field dealing with subtle, contested effects and extraordinary claims to gain acceptance based on marginal statistical significance.1 The crisis thus acts as a double-edged sword, normalizing replication difficulties while simultaneously demanding more robust evidence than ever before. Furthermore, the "psi paradox" hypothesis suggests that if psi exists, it might inherently complicate replication due to experimenter effects or observer-dependent phenomena, a problem potentially exacerbated by the intense focus on replication fidelity.137

**E. The Role of Anomalistic Psychology as a Critical Counterpoint**

Anomalistic Psychology (AP), exemplified by the work of Chris French and the APRU at Goldsmiths 35, provides a framework for studying paranormal *beliefs* and *experiences* from a conventional psychological perspective.17 By applying established psychological principles (cognitive biases, memory errors, perceptual distortions, sleep phenomena, social factors, etc.) 35, AP seeks to explain why people report seemingly paranormal events without invoking psi. This approach has achieved greater academic acceptance than parapsychology.42 AP functions as a critical counterpoint, offering non-psi explanations for the phenomena that parapsychology investigates, thereby reinforcing the boundary between mainstream psychology and the study of potentially genuine psi.

The demarcation of parapsychology is thus a complex, ongoing negotiation involving multiple actors and shifting arguments. Boundary work is performed not only by external critics imposing standards of evidence, methodology, and theoretical coherence, but also by parapsychologists themselves through their strategic adoption of scientific practices and institutional affiliations, and by adjacent fields like anomalistic psychology which offer alternative, more conventional explanatory frameworks.

**VI. Sociological Dimensions: Public Perception and Media Influence**

Beyond the internal dynamics of the scientific field, parapsychology's status is also shaped by its interaction with broader society, particularly through public beliefs about the paranormal and media representations of psi phenomena and investigations.

**A. Public Beliefs vs. Scientific Standing: Analyzing Survey Trends**

Survey data from the US and UK consistently reveal a high prevalence of belief in various paranormal phenomena among the general public, standing in stark contrast to the skepticism prevalent within the mainstream scientific community.

* **Widespread Belief:** Recent surveys indicate that a large majority of the population holds at least one paranormal belief. The 2018 Chapman University Survey of American Fears found that over 75% of Americans endorsed at least one of seven listed paranormal items.141 A 2025 study reported over 70% of people in the UK believe in something paranormal.142 Specific beliefs with high endorsement include haunted places (58% US 2018 141), ancient advanced civilizations like Atlantis (57% US 2018 141), and experiences interpreted as telepathy or precognition (around 40-50% US 2018 81). Belief in ESP has also polled high historically (e.g., 49% Gallup 1990 131; \>60% US 2003 81).  
* **Increasing Trends?:** The Chapman surveys suggest a potential *increase* in paranormal beliefs in the US between 2016 and 2018 across multiple items, including belief in ancient aliens, haunted places, Bigfoot, and psychokinesis.141 While requiring confirmation with longer-term data, this points towards paranormalism becoming more mainstream rather than declining.142  
* **The Disconnect:** This high, and possibly rising, level of public belief is strikingly disconnected from the marginal scientific status of parapsychology, the lack of mainstream funding, and the critical stance of many scientific institutions (Sections III, IV, V). Public acceptance does not translate into scientific legitimacy. This highlights the distinct criteria and authorities operating within the scientific field versus popular culture.  
* **Correlates of Belief:** Belief patterns vary across demographics. Some research suggests links between paranormal belief and social marginalization.142 Gender differences appear, with women potentially more drawn to certain New Age or spiritualist beliefs.144 The relationship with traditional religion is complex; while strong adherence to organized religion often correlates with lower paranormal belief (due to competing explanatory frameworks or condemnation of non-sanctioned supernaturalism 145), individuals identifying as "spiritual but not religious" or non-affiliated often show higher levels of paranormalism 146, suggesting paranormal beliefs may fill a niche left by declining traditional religious authority.142 Belief is also strongly linked to personal anomalous experiences rather than acceptance of scientific evidence.144

**B. Media Representations: Shaping Legitimacy and Interest**

Popular media plays a significant role in shaping public understanding and perception of the paranormal, often acting as a more influential source of information than scientific research itself.

* **Media Influence:** Exposure to media depicting paranormal events, particularly reality TV shows and documentaries presented as factual, is positively correlated with belief in the paranormal.147 The way media frames paranormal investigations—for instance, emphasizing technological gadgets and scientific-sounding jargon (the "trappings of science")—can significantly increase the perceived scientific credibility and authority of investigators in the eyes of the public, regardless of actual methodological rigor.147  
* **Construction of Authority:** This suggests that media acts as a powerful constructor of surrogate scientific authority for topics on the fringe.147 In the absence of clear endorsement from established science, audiences may rely on media portrayals to gauge the plausibility and legitimacy of paranormal claims and investigators.  
* **Misrepresentation and Sensationalism:** However, media representations are often criticized for sensationalizing or misrepresenting parapsychological research and phenomena.149 The focus on entertainment value can obscure the complexities, methodological debates, and tentative nature of actual scientific inquiry in the field, potentially leading to public confusion between serious research and paranormal entertainment.149 Historical controversies, like the one surrounding Uri Geller and SRI, were heavily amplified and shaped by media coverage.92  
* **Online Discourse:** Digital platforms like YouTube provide spaces for public discussion but are often characterized by polarized debates, misinformation, and hostility alongside genuine curiosity, further complicating the public understanding of parapsychology as a scientific field.149

**C. The Feedback Loop: Does Public Fascination Influence Research?**

The relationship between high public interest in the paranormal and the actual practice and funding of scientific parapsychology appears weak and indirect.

* **Limited Impact on Mainstream Science:** Widespread public belief and media fascination do not appear to significantly influence the decisions of major funding agencies or the overall stance of the mainstream scientific community.141 Scientific gatekeeping mechanisms seem largely insulated from popular opinion on this topic.  
* **Potential Indirect Effects:** Public interest *may* indirectly support the field by:  
  * **Fueling Private Donations:** Individuals intrigued by the paranormal, including wealthy donors, might be more inclined to support independent centers (IONS, Rhine) or university units (UVA) that rely on philanthropy.8 The popularity of media featuring DOPS research (e.g., Netflix documentary, YouTube views 10) could potentially attract such donors.  
  * **Facilitating Participant Recruitment:** A public interested in or experiencing anomalous phenomena provides a pool of potential participants for research studies, particularly those seeking individuals with reported psi abilities.87  
  * **Creating Demand for Explanation:** Persistent public interest might create some pressure for academia to *address* paranormal topics, potentially contributing to the niche occupied by Anomalistic Psychology 35 or prompting occasional mainstream reviews like Cardeña's.80  
* **Media's Ambiguous Role:** While media visibility can attract attention and potentially donors 10, sensationalized or uncritical portrayals can simultaneously damage the field's scientific credibility among peers and gatekeepers.149

In essence, while public fascination with the paranormal is undeniable and potentially growing, it operates largely in parallel to, rather than directly influencing, the scientific assessment and institutional support (or lack thereof) for parapsychology research. The "great disconnect" between popular belief and scientific standing remains a defining feature of the field's sociological context.

## **VII. Synthesis and Conclusion: Parapsychology at the Margins**

This multi-lens inquiry, drawing on principles from Science and Technology Studies, provides a nuanced assessment of the contemporary research status of parapsychology. Synthesizing the findings across the institutional, network, knowledge production, funding, legitimacy, and sociological dimensions reveals a field characterized by both persistence and profound marginalization.

**A. Assessing Integration: An Active Field or a Self-Contained Ecosystem?**

Parapsychology in the 2020s exhibits clear signs of activity. It maintains a physical presence through a small number of dedicated university research units and more numerous independent centers. It possesses a network of credentialed researchers, albeit small, connected through professional associations like the PA and SSE. It produces knowledge disseminated through specialized journals adhering to peer-review norms and occasionally penetrates mainstream scientific discourse. Research programs investigating various psi phenomena are ongoing at key centers.8

However, the evidence overwhelmingly indicates that this activity occurs largely within a self-contained ecosystem, significantly isolated from mainstream science. The field's reliance on precarious private funding and its exclusion from major public grant systems represent a critical barrier to integration. Its primary knowledge outputs circulate within low-impact, specialized journals with limited visibility in dominant indexing databases. It faces persistent, deeply rooted critiques regarding methodological rigor, reproducibility, and theoretical coherence from the broader scientific community. Endorsements from major scientific bodies are absent, replaced by skepticism or, at best, procedural tolerance. While public belief in the paranormal is widespread, it appears largely disconnected from, and unable to influence, the field's scientific standing or resource allocation.

Therefore, while not entirely dormant or isolated, parapsychology functions predominantly as a marginalized knowledge ecosystem. Its interactions with mainstream science are infrequent, often controversial, and insufficient to constitute meaningful integration. It persists by adapting to niches and relying on parallel support structures outside the primary channels of scientific validation and resource distribution.

**B. Key Indicators of Marginalization and Persistence**

Several key indicators encapsulate this dual status:

* **Marginalization:**  
  * **Funding Exclusion:** The absence of NIH, NSF, and UKRI funding is a primary marker.  
  * **Institutional Skepticism:** Critical reports (e.g., NAS/NRC 122) and lack of endorsement from bodies like APA and BPS.  
  * **Knowledge Ghettoization:** Reliance on low-impact, specialized journals with contested indexing status.  
  * **Epistemic Contestation:** Enduring debates over reproducibility, methodology, and theory.  
  * **"Extraordinary Claims" Burden:** The high bar for evidence required due to the nature of psi claims.1  
* **Persistence:**  
  * **Niche Institutionalization:** Survival of dedicated university units through endowments or specific departmental tolerance.  
  * **Independent Infrastructure:** Vital role of centers like Rhine, IONS, PF, and associations like PA and SSE in providing research, publication, funding, and community infrastructure.  
  * **Dedicated Researchers:** Commitment of a core group of credentialed researchers.  
  * **Specialized Publications:** Continued operation of dedicated journals.  
  * **Public Resonance:** Persistent (and possibly growing) public interest and belief, potentially fueling private support and participation.

**C. Implications for STS, Science Policy, and the Definition of Scientific Inquiry**

The case of parapsychology offers significant implications beyond the field itself:

* **For STS:** It provides a rich empirical site for studying boundary work in science 3, demonstrating how legitimacy is negotiated through methodological debates, institutional positioning, funding decisions, and interactions with public discourse. It highlights the challenges faced by fields that contest dominant paradigms and the mechanisms through which scientific authority is maintained and contested.  
* **For Science Policy:** The field's status raises difficult questions for policymakers and funding agencies regarding the criteria for supporting high-risk, unconventional, or paradigm-challenging research. It underscores how funding decisions act as powerful, often implicit, forms of scientific gatekeeping and boundary maintenance. It also points to the potential influence of institutional inertia and historical skepticism on contemporary resource allocation.  
* **For Philosophy of Science:** Parapsychology engages fundamental debates about the demarcation between science and pseudoscience 3, the role of replication and falsifiability 60, the nature of scientific evidence (especially for subtle or contested effects), and the potential limitations of current scientific models, particularly concerning consciousness and subjective experience.2 The "psi paradox" argument 140 introduces reflexive considerations about the role of the observer in scientific investigation, particularly relevant in light of the replication crisis.

In conclusion, this multi-lens inquiry reveals parapsychology in the 2020s as a field caught between its aspirations for scientific legitimacy and its reality as a marginalized discipline. It persists through dedicated efforts and niche adaptation but remains largely outside the structures of mainstream scientific validation and support. Its enduring presence at the margins serves as a continual, albeit contested, challenge to conventional scientific boundaries and offers a valuable lens through which to examine the complex processes that define and regulate scientific knowledge. The ultimate status of psi phenomena remains unresolved, but the sociological and epistemological dynamics surrounding the field provide enduring insights into the workings of science itself.
