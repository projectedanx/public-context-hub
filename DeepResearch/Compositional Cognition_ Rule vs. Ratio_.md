

# **Compositional Cognition: A Neuro-Cultural and Evolutionary Re-evaluation of Visual Heuristics**

## **Part I: Deconstructing the Western Canons: The Golden Ratio and the Rule of Thirds**

The study of visual composition has long been dominated by a set of heuristics presumed to hold universal aesthetic power. Foremost among these are the Golden Ratio and the Rule of Thirds, two principles that have achieved canonical status in Western art education and practice. This section undertakes a critical deconstruction of these canons, examining their historical trajectories and subjecting their claims to rigorous empirical scrutiny. The analysis reveals that their persistence owes less to innate, universal laws of beauty and more to processes of historical mythologizing, pedagogical simplification, and their appeal as cognitive shortcuts. Rather than being fundamental principles of perception, they emerge as culturally specific constructs whose aesthetic efficacy is, at best, contested and, at worst, unfounded.

### **The Divine Proportion: A History of Mathematical Idealism and Aesthetic Myth**

The Golden Ratio (GR), mathematically denoted by the Greek letter phi ($ \\phi $), represents a proportion where a line is divided into two parts of unequal length, such that the ratio of the whole line to the longer part is equal to the ratio of the longer part to the shorter part.1 This relationship, expressed algebraically as $ (a+b)/a \= a/b \= \\phi $, yields an irrational number approximately equal to 1.618.1 While its legacy is now deeply entwined with theories of beauty, its origins are purely mathematical, and its aesthetic authority is a relatively recent, and largely unsubstantiated, invention.

#### **Mathematical and Philosophical Origins**

The first known formal definition of this ratio appears in Euclid's *Elements* (c. 300 BCE), where it is described as the "extreme and mean ratio".2 For Euclid and his contemporaries, including the Pythagoreans who may have been aware of it earlier, the ratio was a subject of geometric interest, particularly for its frequent appearance in the construction of the pentagram and regular pentagon.4 There is no historical evidence to suggest that these early Greek mathematicians associated this specific proportion with any particular aesthetic quality.5 It was a mathematical curiosity, a "precious jewel" of geometry as Johannes Kepler would later call it, but not a formula for beauty.4

The ratio's connection to the Fibonacci sequence—where the ratio of consecutive numbers converges on $ \\phi $—was not discovered until much later, by Kepler in the early 17th century.2 This mathematical property, along with its expression as the solution to the quadratic equation $ x^2 \- x \- 1 \= 0 $, reinforced its status as a number with unique and elegant characteristics, but still within the domain of mathematics, not aesthetics.2

#### **The Renaissance Rebranding and Mythologizing**

The transformation of the extreme and mean ratio into a "divine proportion" began during the Renaissance. In 1509, the Italian mathematician Luca Pacioli published *De divina proportione*, a treatise illustrated by his contemporary, Leonardo da Vinci.3 Pacioli's title imbued the ratio with a mystical and religious significance, praising it as a symbol of divinely inspired order.3 However, a careful examination of Pacioli's work reveals that his primary advocacy was for the Vitruvian system of rational proportions. The modern interpretation that Pacioli promoted the Golden Ratio as a universal rule for pleasing composition has been traced to a historical error; his interest was more theological and mathematical than aesthetic.4 Leonardo da Vinci, who called the ratio the

*sectio aurea* (golden section), did not explicitly document its use as a compositional principle in his own artistic treatises.4

The modern romanticism surrounding the ratio gained significant momentum in the 19th century. The term "golden section" (*goldener Schnitt*) was coined by the German mathematician Martin Ohm in 1835\.2 Shortly after, the psychologist Adolf Zeising championed the idea that the Golden Ratio was a universal law governing beauty and morphology in nature, art, and the human body.6 It was this 19th-century movement that cemented the myth of the Golden Ratio as an ancient secret to beauty, a secret supposedly known and used by the great masters of antiquity and the Renaissance.

#### **The Empirical Reckoning: Contradictory Evidence and Null Findings**

Despite its pervasive mythos, the claim that the Golden Ratio is an objective determinant of aesthetic preference finds little support in modern empirical research. In fact, a substantial body of evidence directly contradicts or fails to validate its supposed power.

Claims that the human form, particularly the "ideal" or "beautiful" face, adheres to the Golden Ratio have been systematically debunked. Recent reviews of the clinical and anatomical literature find no convincing evidence to support this link, concluding that its use in fields like aesthetic surgery or orthodontics is not based on empirical data.6 The idea that artists like Phidias or Leonardo da Vinci intentionally used the ratio in their works is based largely on speculation and the retroactive fitting of golden rectangles and spirals onto their creations, a practice that suffers from significant confirmation bias.3 As many analysts have pointed out, with a complex image, one can almost always find lines that approximate the ratio, regardless of the artist's intent.7

Psychological experiments on aesthetic preference have yielded similarly weak and inconsistent results. While the pioneering work of Gustav Fechner in the 1860s suggested a preference for rectangles with proportions close to the Golden Ratio, subsequent research has been fraught with contradictory findings.12 Many studies have failed to replicate Fechner's results, while others have been criticized for methodological flaws that may have perpetuated the myth.12 More recent investigations using implicit measures like the Implicit Association Test (IAT) have found that while people may implicitly associate the GR with positive concepts, this preference is often weaker than a preference for simple symmetry. Furthermore, any

*explicit* preference for the Golden Ratio appears to be correlated with prior art knowledge, suggesting it is a learned cultural value rather than an innate perceptual bias.12

The persistence of the Golden Ratio in popular discourse, despite the lack of robust evidence, points to its function not as an empirical law but as a powerful narrative. The idea that beauty can be distilled into a single, elegant mathematical formula is deeply appealing. It provides a sense of order and objectivity in the highly subjective realm of aesthetics. This narrative, born from a 19th-century romantic idealization of a mathematical concept, has proven more resilient than the scientific evidence against it. It functions as a form of "noble lie," a compelling story that provides a simple, universal explanation for a complex phenomenon, thereby satisfying a deep-seated cognitive desire for pattern and certainty. The history of the Golden Ratio is therefore not a story of an ancient artistic secret, but a case study in how a mathematical idea can be transformed into a cultural myth.

### **The Rule of Thirds: From Tonal Balance to a Tic-Tac-Toe Grid**

The Rule of Thirds (RoT) is arguably the most widely taught compositional heuristic in photography and design. Presented as a simple grid, it instructs practitioners to place key elements along its lines or at their intersections to create more "interesting" and "dynamic" compositions.13 However, like the Golden Ratio, the modern incarnation of the RoT is a significant simplification—and arguably a corruption—of its original, more nuanced conception. Its dominance appears to stem from its pedagogical simplicity rather than any proven aesthetic superiority.

#### **The Authentic Origin**

The first documented use of the phrase "Rule of thirds" can be traced to the English painter and writer John Thomas Smith in his 1797 book, *Remarks on Rural Scenery*.13 Smith's discussion, however, had nothing to do with the placement of objects on intersecting grid lines. Instead, he was building on an observation by Sir Joshua Reynolds about the balance of light and dark in a painting.13 Smith proposed that a more "harmonizing proportion" in a landscape would be achieved by dividing the canvas into unequal parts, specifically a ratio of roughly one-third to two-thirds.

For example, a landscape might be composed of one-third land and two-thirds sky, or vice versa. He extended this idea to the distribution of any major compositional element, whether light, shade, form, or color.16 Smith's rule was about the proportional balance of

*areas* and *masses* within the frame, an idea aimed at avoiding the static quality of a perfectly centered or halved composition.13 It was a sophisticated principle of visual weight and balance, not a simple placement template.

#### **The Photographic Corruption**

The transformation of Smith's principle into the modern tic-tac-toe grid occurred over the following century, primarily within the context of the emerging field of photography.18 Early photography textbooks, aiming to elevate the new medium to the status of art, borrowed and simplified principles from painting.19 The idea of moving the subject off-center was formalized in a 1908 textbook, which suggested placing the "principle object of interest" near, but not on, the intersections of a 3x3 grid.18

This unnamed tip was a soft guideline meant to discourage static central compositions. Over the decades, particularly with its popularization in hobbyist magazines and even U.S. Army manuals, this soft guideline hardened into a rigid "rule".18 The nuanced concept of proportional balance was lost, replaced by a simple, prescriptive grid overlay that is now a standard feature in most digital cameras and smartphones.20 This modern RoT is often taught as a simplified version of the Golden Ratio, though their historical origins are entirely distinct.22 The RoT's grid lines at 33% and 67% are a rough approximation of the GR's lines at approximately 38% and 62%, but the conceptual basis is different.22

#### **Empirical Challenges to the Modern Rule**

When subjected to empirical testing, the modern Rule of Thirds performs poorly. Its core tenet—that off-center placement is aesthetically superior—is directly challenged by several key findings.

A compelling 2018 study tested preferences for compositions of a single object against a plain background. The results were unequivocal: participants overwhelmingly preferred the object to be centered in the frame, regardless of its size.25 This finding directly contradicts the RoT and suggests that for simple compositions, centering is the most aesthetically pleasing choice for most viewers. The study speculated that the RoT might be a learned convention within the professional photography community, creating a disconnect between the preferences of trained practitioners and the general public.25

Further research using computational analysis of large image datasets has also questioned the rule's prevalence. One study analyzed a large set of high-quality photographs and paintings using saliency maps to determine where focus points were located. It concluded that the RoT seemed to play "only a minor, if any, role" in the composition of these aesthetically valued images.26

The modern Rule of Thirds persists not because of its demonstrated aesthetic power, but because of its high cognitive fluency. It is an exceptionally easy heuristic to understand, remember, and apply, especially with the aid of digital grid overlays.20 It serves as a useful "gateway" principle for beginners, providing a simple, actionable step to move beyond basic point-and-shoot composition.27 However, this pedagogical utility has been mistaken for aesthetic truth. The rule's value lies in its function as a cognitive shortcut—a simple rule of thumb that is "good enough" in many situations—rather than as a fundamental law of visual perception. Its historical journey from a nuanced principle of tonal balance to a rigid geometric grid is a classic example of a complex idea being simplified for mass consumption, losing its original meaning and empirical grounding in the process.

## **Part II: The Neurological Gaze: Measuring the Mind's Response to Composition**

To move beyond historical critique and subjective claims, a robust empirical framework is required. This section details the methodologies and cognitive theories used to quantify the human response to visual composition. It begins with a reflexive analysis of the primary neuroimaging tools, fMRI and EEG, highlighting how their intrinsic capabilities and limitations shape our understanding of aesthetics. It then defines the specific visual behavior metrics—eye movement entropy, fixation latency, and visual rest states—that allow for a quantitative assessment of compositional efficacy. Finally, it outlines key cognitive frameworks, including processing fluency, visual complexity, and predictive coding, which provide the theoretical scaffolding for interpreting these neurological and behavioral data.

### **Probing the Aesthetic Brain: A Reflexive Analysis of fMRI and EEG Methodologies**

The nascent field of neuroaesthetics seeks to understand the biological basis of aesthetic experiences by measuring brain activity.28 The two most prominent tools for this endeavor, functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging (fMRI) and Electroencephalography (EEG), offer complementary but distinct windows into the aesthetic brain. Critically, the choice of methodology is not neutral; it carries inherent biases that can influence the very nature of the conclusions drawn.

#### **fMRI (Functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging)**

fMRI measures brain activity indirectly by detecting changes in blood flow, known as the Blood-Oxygen-Level-Dependent (BOLD) signal.28 Its primary strength is its excellent

*spatial resolution*, allowing researchers to pinpoint with high precision *which* brain regions are engaged during a task.28 In neuroaesthetics, fMRI studies have been instrumental in linking the experience of beauty to the brain's reward and valuation systems. Numerous studies have shown that when viewers find a stimulus (such as a painting or an attractive face) to be beautiful, there is increased activation in areas like the medial orbitofrontal cortex (OFC) and the ventral striatum, regions known to be involved in processing pleasure and reward.28 For example, one study presented viewers with canonically proportioned classical sculptures and versions that had been digitally altered to violate the Golden Ratio. The observation of the canonically beautiful forms was associated with significant activation in the right insula, an area involved in emotional experience.34

However, fMRI has significant limitations. Its *temporal resolution* is poor. The BOLD signal is a slow, hemodynamic response that peaks several seconds after the neural activity that triggers it, making it difficult to capture the rapid, sub-second cognitive dynamics of visual perception.28 Furthermore, the fMRI environment itself—a noisy, confining scanner tube—is highly artificial and may interfere with or alter the genuine aesthetic experience being studied.28

#### **EEG (Electroencephalography)**

EEG directly measures the electrical activity of the brain via electrodes placed on the scalp.35 Its cardinal advantage is its superb

*temporal resolution*, capable of tracking neural responses on a millisecond timescale. This allows researchers to analyze the precise timing and sequence of cognitive events.35 EEG data is often analyzed in two ways: through Event-Related Potentials (ERPs) and frequency band analysis. ERPs are specific voltage deflections time-locked to a stimulus, such as the P300 component, which reflects the cognitive processing of a salient or unexpected event.36 Frequency analysis examines oscillations in different bands, such as increased alpha power, which has been associated with internally directed attention and creative cognition.37 A study using EEG to compare balance and aesthetic judgments found that unbeautiful and imbalanced stimuli triggered larger negative brain waves in frontal regions in an early time window (300-500 ms), suggesting higher cognitive conflict.38

The primary weakness of EEG is its poor *spatial resolution*. Because electrical signals are smeared as they pass through the skull, it is difficult to precisely localize the source of the activity.35 EEG recordings are also highly susceptible to artifacts from muscle movements, eye blinks, and electrical interference, which requires extensive data cleaning.39

The choice between these methods reflects and reinforces different theoretical approaches to aesthetics. A researcher using fMRI is well-positioned to find correlations between subjective "liking" ratings and activation in the OFC, naturally supporting a conclusion that beauty is a form of hedonic reward.31 Conversely, a researcher using EEG is better equipped to find differences in the timing or amplitude of ERPs based on compositional features, leading to the conclusion that beauty is a function of how the brain

*processes* the visual information—for instance, the cognitive ease or difficulty of parsing a composition.38 This creates a methodological divide between pleasure-based and information-based theories of aesthetics. A truly comprehensive neuroaesthetics must therefore aim to integrate these approaches, using the temporal precision of EEG to map the cognitive dynamics of viewing and the spatial precision of fMRI to identify the neural circuits involved in the resulting valuation.

### **The Quantified Eye: Advanced Visual Behavior Metrics**

While neuroimaging probes the brain's internal response, eye-tracking technology provides a direct, quantitative measure of how a viewer visually engages with a composition. By moving beyond vague terms like "draws the eye," we can use specific metrics to test the claims of compositional heuristics.

#### **Eye Movement Entropy**

The scanpath of a viewer—the sequence of fixations and saccades across an image—can be analyzed for its predictability. Scanpath entropy is a measure derived from information theory that quantifies the degree of disorder or randomness in the viewing patterns of a group of observers.41 A composition with strong gaze-guiding properties will produce highly similar, ordered scanpaths across different viewers, resulting in

*low entropy*. Conversely, a composition that encourages free, unstructured exploration will produce more varied and unpredictable scanpaths, resulting in *high entropy*.41 This metric provides a powerful, quantitative way to assess a composition's ability to direct attention. For example, a low scanpath entropy for an advertisement would indicate that its design successfully guides viewers to key elements like the brand logo.41

#### **Fixation Latency & Visual Rest States**

Two other critical metrics are fixation latency and duration. **Fixation latency** refers to the time it takes for a viewer to first fixate on a predefined Area of Interest (AOI). A shorter latency indicates that an element has a high capacity to capture attention quickly.42

**Visual rest states**, or fixations, can be measured by their duration. The total dwelling time on an AOI serves as a robust proxy for sustained interest and deeper cognitive processing.44 Studies have shown that when participants are asked to crop photographs, the details they select to keep are regions that received significantly longer dwelling times during free viewing, compared to the details they discarded.44

#### **Saliency Models and Their Limits**

To predict where people will look, computer vision researchers have developed saliency models. These algorithms generate a "saliency map" that highlights regions of an image likely to attract attention based on bottom-up, low-level visual features such as high contrast, unique colors, and specific orientations.44 Early computational aesthetics research used these maps to quantify compositional principles, for example, by defining a "balanced" composition as one where the "center of mass" of the saliency map falls near the geometric center of the image.44

However, this approach has a critical flaw: it often ignores the powerful influence of top-down, semantic information. A small human face or a line of text can have enormous "visual weight" that is not captured by low-level feature analysis alone.46 Indeed, studies have shown that the predictive power of models for compositional balance improves significantly when high-level feature detectors—for humans, faces, or perspective cues—are added to the low-level saliency map.45 This demonstrates that true visual balance is a psychological phenomenon, not a purely physical one. The perceived equilibrium of a composition depends on a weighted combination of both bottom-up sensory salience and top-down cognitive and semantic importance. Any robust model of composition must account for the fact that a small, meaningful object can easily "outweigh" a large, featureless area.

### **Cognitive Frameworks for Aesthetic Experience**

To interpret the data from neuroimaging and eye-tracking, we need cognitive theories that explain *why* certain patterns of brain activity and visual behavior occur. Three key frameworks are processing fluency, visual complexity, and predictive coding.

#### **Processing Fluency**

The theory of processing fluency posits that aesthetic pleasure is a direct function of the ease (fluency) with which the mind processes a stimulus.47 Objects that are easy to process feel good. This is why features like symmetry, high figure-ground contrast, clear patterns, and prototypicality are often preferred—they facilitate fluent processing.48 For example, a symmetric pattern is easier to encode and remember than an asymmetric one, and this ease of processing is misattributed as a positive aesthetic feeling.49 This theory elegantly explains a wide range of basic preferences without recourse to complex artistic theories.47

#### **Visual Complexity and the Inverted-U**

Aesthetic preference is not, however, a simple matter of "easier is better." A large body of research has established an inverted-U relationship between visual complexity and liking.50 Stimuli that are too simple are often perceived as boring, while those that are too complex are perceived as chaotic or overwhelming. Preference tends to peak at a moderate level of complexity, which offers a "sweet spot" of engaging but comprehensible structure.50 This pattern has been observed for a variety of stimuli, from random polygons to landscape photographs.50 It is important to note, however, that this relationship can be context-dependent; in highly goal-oriented tasks, such as navigating a website, users often prefer lower complexity to reduce cognitive load.52

#### **Predictive Coding**

A more recent and potentially unifying framework is predictive coding.53 This theory proposes that the brain is fundamentally a prediction engine. It constantly generates and updates an internal model of the world to predict incoming sensory information. Perception is not a passive reception of data but an active process of matching top-down predictions with bottom-up sensory signals.53 When there is a mismatch, a "prediction error" signal is generated, which drives learning by causing the brain to update its model.

Within this framework, aesthetic experience can be understood as a function of these predictive dynamics.54 A highly fluent stimulus is one that generates very little prediction error; the brain's model predicts it perfectly. While metabolically efficient, this can be uninteresting. The pleasure of aesthetics, it is argued, comes from the process of

*prediction error reduction*. A compelling artwork presents an initial challenge—it is novel or ambiguous, generating prediction error. However, it also contains sufficient internal structure (through composition, pattern, etc.) to allow the brain to successfully update its model and reduce that error. This successful "sense-making" or "learning progress" is experienced as a rewarding flash of insight and pleasure.54

This predictive coding account elegantly integrates the other two frameworks. High processing fluency corresponds to a state of low, stable prediction error. The inverted-U of complexity reflects an optimal dynamic: a very simple stimulus generates no error to reduce (boring), while a very complex one generates irreducible error (confusing). A moderately complex, well-composed image provides a "just-right" challenge, sparking curiosity with initial error and then providing the satisfaction of resolving it. Compositional heuristics like the Rule of Thirds or the Golden Ratio, to the extent that they are effective, may function by providing a predictable underlying structure that aids the brain in this error-reduction process, making an image feel both engaging and coherent.

## **Part III: Expanding the Ontology: Non-Western Frameworks of Compositional Cognition**

A critical limitation of most aesthetic research is its profound Eurocentrism, which tacitly universalizes Western artistic conventions and cognitive models.57 To decenter this perspective, this section analyzes three non-Western compositional philosophies—the Japanese concepts of

Ma and Notan, and the Chinese philosophy of Shanshui—not merely as alternative "styles," but as distinct cognitive systems for organizing and interpreting visual information. These frameworks challenge the object-centric focus of Western rules and highlight the importance of relational, holistic, and experiential modes of perception.

### **The Active Void and the Luminous Shadow: Japanese Ma and Notan**

Japanese aesthetics offers powerful concepts that prioritize the relationship between elements over the elements themselves. Ma and Notan are two fundamental principles that shift the cognitive focus from the figure to the field, from the object to the space it inhabits and the patterns it creates.

#### **Ma (間) \- The Pregnant Pause**

Ma is often translated as "negative space," but this translation is insufficient as it implies emptiness or absence. A more accurate understanding is of an active, intentional, and meaningful interval—an "emptiness full of possibilities".59 Rooted in Zen Buddhist philosophy, which emphasizes emptiness (

śūnyatā) as a source of potential, Ma treats the space *between* objects as a positive and essential compositional element.61 It is the silence between musical notes that creates the rhythm, the unpainted silk in a landscape scroll that gives depth to the mountains, and the pause in a conversation that lends weight to the words.59

Cognitively, Ma functions by engaging the viewer's mind to participate in the completion of the work. By deliberately leaving areas undefined, the artist invites contemplation and activates the viewer's imagination to fill the void.60 This principle can be seen in the design of traditional Japanese gardens, where areas of raked gravel are not empty but represent vast oceans or the sky, and in the

tokonoma alcove of a room, where a single object is given prominence by the carefully curated space surrounding it.61 This strategic use of space manipulates perceptual phenomena like the Delboeuf illusion, where the perceived size and importance of an object are altered by the context of its surrounding space.62

#### **Notan (濃淡) \- The Harmony of Light and Dark**

Notan is a Japanese design principle meaning "dark-light harmony" or "balance".63 It involves simplifying a composition into its most fundamental abstract pattern of light and dark masses, often using just two or three values (e.g., black, white, and sometimes a mid-tone gray).63 The goal of a

Notan study is not to create a realistic rendering with shading and form, but to understand the underlying graphic structure of an image.64 By reducing a scene to flat shapes of value, the artist can more easily see and manipulate the composition's core energies: its balance, movement, and the relationship between positive and negative shapes.63

An effective Notan design features an unequal but balanced distribution of light and dark, a limited number of dominant shapes, and interesting "negative" shapes created by the "positive" ones.63 This concept, popularized in the West by Arthur Wesley Dow in the late 19th century, emphasizes that every composition is fundamentally an abstract arrangement of shapes, and mastering this abstract structure is key to creating compelling art.64

These Japanese principles stand in stark contrast to the object-centric nature of many Western compositional rules. Research in cross-cultural psychology has noted that Western viewers tend to employ an analytic cognitive style, focusing on focal objects and their attributes, while East Asian viewers tend to adopt a more holistic style, attending to the broader context and the relationships between elements.67 The Rule of Thirds and the Golden Ratio, with their emphasis on placing a primary subject at a "power point," can be seen as tools that reinforce this analytic, object-focused mode of viewing.

Ma and Notan, conversely, are cognitive technologies that compel a holistic mode of perception. Ma forces the viewer's attention onto the "background" or the interval, making the context as important as the object. Notan dissolves the distinction between object and background altogether, reducing the entire visual field to a single, unified pattern of interacting light and dark shapes. They are not merely different aesthetic choices; they are systems for training a different way of seeing, one that is inherently relational and context-sensitive.

### **The Mind in the Landscape: Spatial Cognition in Chinese Shanshui**

Chinese Shanshui (山水, literally "mountain-water") painting represents another profound departure from Western compositional norms. More than a mere genre, Shanshui is a complete philosophical and cognitive system for representing space, time, and the human relationship with the natural world.69

#### **Beyond Fixed-Point Perspective**

The most significant feature of Shanshui is its rejection of the single, fixed viewpoint that has dominated Western art since the Renaissance invention of linear perspective.71 A

Shanshui painter does not depict a landscape as it is seen from one static point in space. Instead, the artist employs multiple, shifting, and "floating" perspectives to represent the landscape as it is *known* and *experienced* through movement and contemplation.72 As the 11th-century painter Guo Xi wrote, "the shape of the mountain needs to be seen from various angles," and a single mountain in a painting can thus embody the shapes of hundreds.72 This approach allows the artist to depict scenes that would be impossible to see from a single vantage point, such as the front and back of a mountain simultaneously, creating a comprehensive and psychologically authentic representation of the environment.

#### **Time and Narrative in Composition**

This unique spatial cognition is intimately linked to the physical format of many Shanshui works, particularly the handscroll.71 A handscroll, which can be many meters long, is not viewed all at once. It is unrolled from right to left, revealing the landscape sequentially, section by section. This act of unrolling introduces a crucial temporal dimension to the viewing experience. The composition unfolds over time, taking the viewer on an imaginative journey through the painted world.71 The visual path is not a static scanpath across a fixed image, but a dynamic, narrative progression through a continuous and seamless visual field.

#### **The Fusion of Subject and Object**

Shanshui philosophy is deeply rooted in Taoist and Buddhist thought, emphasizing the "Integration of Man with Mountains and Rivers" (tian ren he yi).69 The painting is not an objective depiction of an external reality but an expression of the artist's inner spiritual and emotional state as it resonates with the spirit (

qi) of the landscape.70 The composition is therefore an externalization of an internal, cognitive-emotional map, a fusion of perception, memory, and feeling.

This complex system of representation can be compellingly interpreted through the modern framework of predictive coding. If the brain's perception of the world is not a passive snapshot but an active, constantly updated predictive model built from experience and multi-sensory input 53, then a

Shanshui painting is a far better analogue for this mental model than a static, single-perspective photograph. The painting, with its multiple viewpoints, synthesis of near and far, and temporal flow, is a visual record of the artist's integrated, time-extended cognitive map of an environment. The viewer, in turn, does not simply "see" the painting. By unrolling the scroll, they actively re-enact the artist's journey, updating their own predictive model of the landscape as it unfolds. This makes the act of viewing a Shanshui scroll a participatory cognitive process, a dialogue between the viewer's mind and the artist's recorded consciousness.

### **A Cross-Cultural Cognitive Lens and the Critique of Eurocentrism**

The analysis of Ma, Notan, and Shanshui provides a necessary corrective to the Eurocentric biases that have long pervaded aesthetic science. These non-Western systems demonstrate that foundational concepts like "balance," "space," and "composition" are not universal constants but are culturally constructed and cognitively distinct. The holistic, relational, and experiential nature of these Eastern ontologies aligns with documented differences in cognitive styles between cultures 67, suggesting a deep co-evolution of cultural practices and perceptual habits.

This underscores the critical need for methodological rigor and cultural self-awareness in aesthetic research. Simply applying Western-derived metrics or theories to non-Western art risks profound misinterpretation.57 Valid cross-cultural research requires a careful process of establishing conceptual and methodological equivalence, ensuring that the psychological constructs being measured are meaningful and comparable across different cultural contexts.75 By abandoning the search for a single, universal "rule" of beauty and instead studying the diversity of these cognitive-aesthetic systems, we can achieve a richer and more accurate understanding of the many ways humans create and perceive a meaningful visual world.

## **Part IV: An Evolutionary Lens on Composition**

To understand the deep roots of compositional preferences, it is necessary to look beyond cultural history to our shared evolutionary past. This section explores how the principles of visual composition might tap into fundamental aspects of human cognition that were shaped by natural selection. The analysis proceeds along two complementary lines of inquiry. The "Survival-Scanning Hypothesis" examines how compositional elements might engage innate, adaptive biases related to survival, such as landscape assessment and threat detection. The "Symbolic Resonance Hypothesis" explores how composition functions as a culturally evolved technology, creating shared visual grammars that resonate with and reinforce social meaning.

### **The Survival-Scanning Hypothesis: Adaptive Biases in Visual Exploration**

The idea that our aesthetic responses are linked to our evolutionary history is a central tenet of evolutionary psychology.77 Our brains did not evolve in art galleries, but in complex natural environments where the ability to quickly and accurately assess surroundings was critical for survival. This has led to the development of innate perceptual biases that may form the bedrock of our aesthetic preferences.

#### **Landscape Aesthetics and Habitat Selection**

Theories like the Savanna Hypothesis and Habitat Theory propose that humans have an innate preference for landscapes that resemble our ancestral environments in the Pleistocene African savanna.78 These environments, characterized by open grasslands with scattered trees and proximity to water, offered a balance of "prospect" (a clear, unobstructed view to spot resources and threats) and "refuge" (places to hide and be safe).78 This preference is not merely a whim but an adaptive trait; individuals who were drawn to and successfully settled in such resource-rich, low-risk environments would have had a survival and reproductive advantage.78 While some studies have found cross-cultural preferences for savanna-like landscapes, others have shown strong preferences for familiar, local biomes, suggesting that this innate predisposition is heavily modulated by cultural learning and experience.79

#### **The Horizon Line as a Primal Cue**

Within this framework, the placement of the horizon line in a composition becomes a powerful tool for manipulating these evolved spatial preferences.82 The horizon is a fundamental orienting feature of our visual world. Its placement in an image dictates the viewer's perceived relationship to the scene.84

* A **low horizon line** dedicates more of the frame to the sky, creating a sense of openness, expansiveness, and freedom.85 From a survival perspective, this emphasizes the "prospect" element, allowing for the scanning of weather patterns or distant threats.  
* A **high horizon line** dedicates more of the frame to the ground, emphasizing the foreground and middle ground.85 This provides more information about the immediate terrain—potential resources, pathways, or places of refuge. It creates a sense of intimacy and presence within the scene.82

  The choice of horizon placement is therefore not just a formal decision but a way of tapping into our primal need to understand our spatial relationship to an environment.

#### **Salient Threat Detection**

Our visual system is not a neutral information processor; it is biased to prioritize stimuli with high survival relevance. A compelling line of research demonstrates that humans and other primates are exceptionally fast at detecting evolutionarily relevant threats, such as snakes and spiders, even when presented with other fear-relevant or neutral stimuli.86 This suggests the existence of dedicated neural pathways that are "tuned" to rapidly identify specific patterns associated with ancestral dangers.86 This "survival processing advantage" extends to memory as well; information processed for its relevance to a survival scenario is retained better than information processed in other ways, suggesting memory systems are optimized for fitness-relevant information.77

These evolved biases suggest that effective compositional techniques may function as "supernormal stimuli"—artificial constructs that exaggerate the very cues our brains are designed to seek for survival assessment. A landscape painting that uses a low horizon, converging lines, and atmospheric perspective to create an exaggerated sense of depth and "prospect" might be more aesthetically rewarding than a real-life scene because it provides a purer, more potent dose of the desired survival cue.85 Similarly, a compositional rule like the Rule of Thirds, by placing a focal element off-center, might artificially enhance its saliency, hijacking the attentional mechanisms that evolved to quickly spot an anomaly—a potential predator in the grass or a fruit in a tree. From this perspective, composition is not a quest for abstract beauty, but a strategic manipulation of our deeply ingrained perceptual-survival toolkit.

### **The Symbolic Resonance Hypothesis: The Cultural Evolution of Aesthetic Heuristics**

While innate biases provide a foundation, they cannot fully explain the vast diversity and complexity of human art. Aesthetics is also a product of cultural evolution, where compositional principles develop into shared symbolic systems that communicate meaning and reinforce social values.

#### **Art as a Cognitive Technology**

Compositional rules can be understood as a form of cognitive technology—a culturally transmitted "software" that runs on our biological "hardware".88 Shared conventions like symmetry, balance, and proportion create a visual grammar that allows for efficient and predictable communication within a culture.90 When an artist and a viewer both understand this grammar, the artist can create works that are more easily processed and interpreted, leading to a more fluent and pleasurable experience for the viewer.

#### **The Case of Symmetry**

Symmetry is a powerful example of this interplay between biology and culture. A preference for symmetry appears to have deep biological roots; it is detected rapidly by our visual system and is often associated with health and genetic fitness in potential mates.3 Studies with young children show that a perceptual sensitivity to symmetry emerges very early in development, providing a cognitive prerequisite for an aesthetic preference to form.49 However, the

*meaning* of symmetry is culturally constructed. Across many cultures, symmetry has been used symbolically to represent abstract concepts like divinity, order, stability, and rationality.94 The symmetrical design of a cathedral facade or a mandala is not just pleasing because it is easy to process; it is meaningful because it resonates with a culturally learned symbolic code.

#### **Balance and Tension as Psychological Metaphors**

Similarly, the principle of visual balance taps into our physical, embodied experience of the world. We have an innate, fundamental need for physical balance, and unbalanced states create a sense of unease.95 Art and design leverage this by creating visual analogues for these physical states.

* **Symmetrical balance** feels static, calm, and stable, evoking feelings of order and tranquility.94  
* **Asymmetrical balance**, where dissimilar elements are arranged to create equilibrium, feels more dynamic, active, and modern. It creates energy and movement.95  
* **Visual tension** is created by intentionally disrupting balance, using unstable arrangements or conflicting elements to evoke psychological states of anxiety, excitement, or conflict in the viewer.96

  These responses are not arbitrary; they are grounded in psychological metaphors where visual stability is mapped onto emotional stability, and visual instability is mapped onto emotional dynamism or distress.

This leads to a model of gene-culture co-evolution in aesthetics. Our innate perceptual and cognitive biases—the "hardware" shaped by natural selection—create a landscape of possibilities. Certain arrangements are easier to process, certain patterns are more salient. Cultural traditions—the "software"—then evolve to explore and exploit this landscape. A culture might develop an artistic tradition that uses symmetry to represent divinity. Individuals raised in that culture are repeatedly exposed to this association, strengthening the neural pathways that link the perception of symmetry with high-level concepts of the sacred.73 This feedback loop, driven by cultural transmission and neural plasticity, is what makes a learned, symbolic convention feel "natural," "right," or "beautiful" to members of that culture. This model can account for both the universal tendencies in art (based on our shared biological hardware) and the profound cultural differences (based on our diverse and evolving cultural software).

## **Part V: Synthesis and the Proposition of Next-Best Heuristics**

The preceding analysis has deconstructed the historical and empirical foundations of dominant Western compositional rules, established a rigorous framework for their neuro-cognitive evaluation, and expanded the inquiry to include non-Western and evolutionary perspectives. This final part synthesizes these threads into two key deliverables: a comparative scorecard that systematically evaluates the different compositional ontologies, and a forward-looking proposal for a new set of "next-best" cognitive heuristics that move beyond the limitations of outdated geometric rules.

### **A Comparative Analysis of Compositional Ontologies**

The widespread teaching of the Golden Ratio and the Rule of Thirds as foundational principles of "good" composition is not supported by a robust body of evidence. The Golden Ratio's aesthetic authority is largely a 19th-century mythologization of a mathematical curiosity, with modern empirical studies consistently yielding null or contradictory findings regarding its supposed universal appeal.6 The Rule of Thirds, in its modern form as a tic-tac-toe grid, is a pedagogical simplification that has become detached from its more nuanced origin as a principle of tonal balance.17 Empirical tests show that it is often outperformed by simple centering for single-subject compositions and plays a minor role in the structure of many high-quality artworks.25

In contrast, non-Western ontologies like Japanese Ma and Notan, and Chinese Shanshui, offer more sophisticated and cognitively grounded approaches. Ma (the active void) and Notan (light-dark harmony) are inherently relational principles that compel a holistic mode of processing, shifting attention from focal objects to the interplay of the entire visual field.59 This aligns with documented cross-cultural differences in perception and encourages a deeper, more participatory form of engagement.67

Shanshui (mountain-water painting) rejects fixed-point perspective in favor of a dynamic, multi-perspective representation that unfolds over time, creating a visual analogue for the mind's own experiential and predictive model of an environment.71

From an evolutionary standpoint, the Western rules can be seen as attempts to create "supernormal stimuli" that hijack innate survival-scanning mechanisms, for instance by artificially enhancing saliency.78 The Eastern principles, particularly

Shanshui, align more closely with a symbolic resonance model, where the composition is an externalization of an internal cognitive-emotional state, inviting the viewer into a shared experience rather than simply directing their gaze.70 The following scorecard summarizes this comparative analysis.

### **Beyond the Grid: Proposing Next-Best Cognitive Heuristics**

Given the empirical weakness and cultural specificity of the Rule of Thirds and the Golden Ratio, a new framework for compositional heuristics is needed. This framework should move away from rigid, geometric templates and toward more flexible, context-aware principles grounded in the fundamental cognitive and perceptual processes identified in this report. The following three heuristics are proposed as a starting point for such a framework.

#### **Proposed Heuristic 1: Saliency-Weighted Balance**

This heuristic redefines compositional balance not as a geometric property, but as a psychological one. It posits that a composition achieves balance when the *cognitive weight* of its elements is in equilibrium around a perceived fulcrum. This cognitive weight is a composite measure, determined by a combination of factors:

* **Bottom-Up Saliency:** Low-level features like color intensity, luminance contrast, size, and orientation that make an element stand out from its background.44  
* **Top-Down Saliency:** High-level semantic meaning that captures attention. This includes the presence of faces, human figures, animals, text, or any culturally significant symbol. As research shows, a small but semantically rich object like a face can psychologically "balance" a much larger, but semantically poor, area of the composition.45  
* **Positional Weight:** The principle, articulated by Arnheim, that an element's weight increases with its distance from the center of the frame.44

Instead of using a fixed grid, this heuristic encourages the creator to intuitively or computationally assess the combined weight of all elements and arrange them to create a sense of stable, yet potentially dynamic, equilibrium.

#### **Proposed Heuristic 2: Predictive-Error Optimization**

This heuristic is derived directly from the predictive coding model of cognition and aesthetics.53 It reframes composition as the deliberate management of the viewer's cognitive and affective experience over time. The goal is to create an optimal "learning trajectory" for the viewer's brain by modulating prediction error. A successful composition should:

1. **Generate Initial Interest:** It must be sufficiently novel, complex, or ambiguous to generate an initial state of prediction error. A composition that is perfectly predictable from the outset is boring.51  
2. **Provide a Path to Resolution:** It must contain sufficient internal structure—through pattern, rhythm, balance, and other compositional cues—to allow the viewer's brain to successfully update its predictive model and reduce the initial error. This process of resolving uncertainty is experienced as a rewarding feeling of pleasure and insight.54

This principle replaces rigid rules with a dynamic goal: to balance surprise with comprehension, and complexity with clarity. It encourages the creator to think of the viewer not as a passive recipient of a beautiful object, but as an active participant in a process of discovery.

#### **Proposed Heuristic 3: Narrative Gaze Guidance**

This heuristic is explicitly goal-oriented and top-down. It posits that the primary function of composition is to guide the viewer's scanpath in a sequence that supports the image's intended purpose or narrative. The "rule" is not universal but is derived from the specific communicative goal of the image.

* For a commercial advertisement, the optimal composition might create a very low-entropy scanpath that moves predictably from a model's eyes to the product and then to the brand logo, minimizing distraction and maximizing message retention.41  
* For a documentary photograph, the composition might guide the eye through a sequence of elements that tells a story, establishing a character, their environment, and an action in a logical order.  
* For a piece of abstract expressionist art, the goal might be the opposite: to encourage a high-entropy, exploratory scanpath that evokes feelings of energy, freedom, or chaos, allowing the viewer to construct their own meaning.

This heuristic shifts the focus from creating a "balanced" or "pleasing" image in the abstract to creating an *effective* image that achieves a specific cognitive or emotional outcome in the viewer. It integrates the principles of visual behavior with the communicative intent of the creator, providing a flexible and powerful framework for compositional design.
