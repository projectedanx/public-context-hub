# **Emergent Reasoning in a Null-to-Expert Multi‑Agent System**

## **Introduction and Framework Overview**

Emergent reasoning refers to the spontaneous development of complex problem-solving behavior that was not explicitly pre-programmed, often arising from the interaction of simpler components. In large language models (LLMs), such capabilities can “emerge” when models are scaled or when they engage in multi-step or multi-agent processes beyond single-turn prompts. Here we propose a **Null-to-Expert (N2E) multi-agent framework** to simulate the emergence of generalizable reasoning. The term “N2E” implies that two initially naïve LLM agents (with no specialized knowledge beyond their base training) **co-evolve** their reasoning competence from a near-null starting point to an expert-like level through iterative interaction. Rather than focusing on immediate task accuracy, this framework emphasizes **causally traceable, semantically resilient reasoning pathways** – in other words, reasoning processes that remain logically coherent and **whose cause-and-effect structure can be examined step by step**.

In essence, the N2E system is inspired by the idea that intelligence can be amplified via collaboration, echoing Minsky’s “Society of Mind” notion that multiple interacting agents can collectively yield more robust cognition. Two agents with distinct but complementary roles engage in an ongoing dialog, refining each other’s outputs. Key techniques enabling their improvement include **fragmented context prompting** (feeding each agent pieces of context in sequence, rather than the full problem at once), **self-reflection** (agents reviewing and critiquing their own or each other’s outputs), and **semantic self-repair** (detecting and correcting shifts or errors in meaning). Over time, this iterative process is expected to produce **emergent heuristics** (novel strategies the agents were not explicitly given) and maintain high **semantic integrity** (preserving the correct context and facts throughout the dialogue). The following sections detail the role of each agent, the iterative reasoning procedure (the **EPE loop**), monitoring protocols for semantic drift, self-diagnosis methods for error correction, the output artifacts produced, and the metrics for success. We also explore how **meta-prompts** (prompts about prompting) can be employed to further enhance the agents’ reasoning capabilities.

## **Role Specification of Agent A and Agent B**

**Agent A (Reasoning-Priming Agent)** – Agent A is responsible for driving the primary reasoning process. This agent initiates the chain-of-thought for a given problem or query, effectively “priming” the reasoning pathway. It attempts to break down the problem into structured steps or hypotheses, acting as the first mover in each iteration. Agent A is encouraged (via its prompt design) to think aloud in a logical, stepwise manner, generating partial solutions or plans that can be scrutinized. Initially, Agent A may be naïve, producing simplistic or flawed reasoning. However, its outputs serve as the raw material for Agent B’s analysis. Over successive exchanges, Agent A integrates feedback and explanations from Agent B to refine its approach. In essence, Agent A’s task is to **propose** – to lay out *what* might be a solution strategy or intermediate inference, even if it’s tentative. It focuses on **reasoning breadth and progression**, ensuring the dialogue moves forward by tackling one aspect of the problem at a time.

**Agent B (Complementary Explainer Agent)** – Agent B’s role complements Agent A by **analyzing, elaborating, and correcting** the reasoning steps that Agent A produces. When Agent A presents a chain-of-thought or a tentative conclusion, Agent B acts as an explainer, validator, and sometimes a critic. Its core task is to take Agent A’s output and **provide a detailed explanation or interpretation**, filling in implicit gaps and checking consistency. If Agent A’s reasoning is unclear or contains errors, Agent B is expected to spot these issues and articulate them. For example, if Agent A jumps to a conclusion without justification, Agent B might point out the missing link or expand the reasoning to bridge the gap. This agent ensures that each step is **well-grounded and transparent**, improving the clarity of the evolving solution. Agent B effectively answers *“why”* or *“how”* Agent A’s propositions make sense (or not), thereby **verifying and enriching the reasoning**. Through this process, Agent B enforces semantic integrity by highlighting contradictions or drifts from the original context.

**Interaction Dynamics:** The two agents function in a loop of **proposal and explanation**. In each cycle, Agent A produces reasoning content, and Agent B responds with an explanation. This dynamic is analogous to having a “solver” and an “examiner.” Notably, each agent is an LLM with its own prompt that specifies its role and objectives. Research on multi-agent discussions indicates that providing distinct role prompts ensures each agent contributes unique perspectives and skills to the dialogue. In our framework, Agent A’s prompt might encourage it to *focus on logical progression and next steps*, whereas Agent B’s prompt stresses *critical analysis, clarity, and adherence to known facts*. By explicitly defining these roles, we create a division of labor that leverages the strengths of LLMs in both generation and evaluation modes. This specialization is similar in spirit to other multi-agent reasoning systems like **MALT**, which divided reasoning into generation, verification, and refinement roles to improve overall performance. Although our N2E system has only two agents, Agent B’s explainer role inherently performs a mix of verification (checking Agent A’s work) and refinement (elaborating on or adjusting it). Over time, as both agents exchange information, they **co-evolve**: Agent A starts to anticipate the kind of rigor Agent B will demand, and Agent B builds on increasingly sophisticated outputs from Agent A. This co-evolution is what gradually transforms their initially naïve reasoning into expert-level reasoning. Crucially, all interactions are in natural language, making the reasoning process human-interpretable and *causally traceable*. Every conclusion can be traced back to specific utterances by either Agent A or B, satisfying the need for transparency in the reasoning pathway.

To summarize their roles and responsibilities, the following table highlights the key characteristics of each agent:

| Agent | Role & Title | Responsibilities |
| ----- | ----- | ----- |
| **Agent A** | *Reasoning-Priming Agent* | – Initiate reasoning with a chain-of-thought proposal for the problem.\<br\>– Break the problem into sub-tasks or hypotheses in a logical order.\<br\>– Incorporate feedback from Agent B in the next iteration to refine or adjust the reasoning.\<br\>– Ensure the dialogue progresses by proposing next steps or questions to tackle. |
| **Agent B** | *Complementary Explainer Agent* | – Interpret and elaborate on Agent A’s output, explaining each step in detail.\<br\>– Verify consistency with initial context and semantic constraints, noting any errors or omissions.\<br\>– Provide feedback, corrections, or examples that clarify Agent A’s reasoning for the next cycle.\<br\>– Help maintain semantic integrity by flagging contradictions or drift from the topic. |

Both agents start *without any special training beyond prompts* – they are conceptually “null” in domain expertise, gaining effectiveness only through interaction. By assigning them these clear roles, we create a scaffold for advanced reasoning to emerge from their cooperation. Studies have shown that even when using the same base LLM, assigning complementary roles and letting agents “discuss” can yield better reasoning than a single agent working alone – especially in zero-shot settings without worked examples. This is because Agent B can catch mistakes Agent A makes, and Agent A, freed from needing to explain everything perfectly in one go, can explore partial solutions which Agent B then helps solidify.

## **N2E Reasoning Procedure and Iterative Prompt Exchange (EPE Loop)**

The core of the N2E system is an **iterative reasoning loop** in which Agent A and Agent B continuously exchange prompts and responses. We term this the **EPE Loop** (Exchange–Prompt–Exchange), reflecting how the two agents take turns exchanging information and prompting each other. The procedure is designed to guide the agents from a basic initial attempt toward a refined solution, with mechanisms in place to monitor and correct any deviations in meaning (**semantic drift**) and to introspect on errors (**self-diagnosis**). Below, we break down the procedure into structured stages:

### **1\. Initializing Semantic Constraints and Context**

Before the dialogue begins, we establish the initial context and any **semantic integrity constraints (SICs)** that must hold true throughout the interaction. Semantic constraints are essentially guardrails – they include the core facts, definitions, or rules of the problem that should not be violated. For example, if the problem is a math word problem, a semantic constraint might be the exact quantities involved or a formula that must be applied; if it’s a logical puzzle, it could be the premises that must remain consistent. These can be provided to the agents in a **shared introduction prompt** or as part of each agent’s initial system instruction. The idea is akin to setting *ground truth anchors* that the conversation should respect. Recent research in AI-augmented systems refers to these as **Semantic Integrity Constraints (SICs)** – declarative rules that ensure outputs remain factually and logically consistent with known data. By integrating SICs upfront, we reduce the chance of hallucinations or off-track reasoning. For instance, if the task is to identify a scientific causal relationship, a SIC might enforce that any proposed cause must come from within the given data and not outside knowledge.

Concretely, we might initialize the conversation with a statement of the problem and a bullet list of critical facts or constraints. Both Agent A and B receive this. For example:

* *System:* “**Task:** Determine if the proposition follows from the premises. **Premises:** (list of premises). **Constraint:** The solution must not introduce new premises or contradict given ones.”

Such a shared context ensures a common starting point. Additionally, each agent’s individual instruction (which defines their role) will remind them of these constraints. Agent B in particular might be tasked to explicitly check each of Agent A’s outputs against these constraints, as part of its explainer role. *Grounding constraints* are a key class of SICs that maintain factual consistency – Agent B can be encouraged to produce a brief “consistency check” if something seems off (for example: *“Agent A’s statement that X \> Y contradicts the premise that Y is greater than X.”*).

By setting up semantic constraints, we have a reference to detect **semantic drift**. *Semantic drift* means that as the dialogue continues, the content might slowly veer away from the original context or factual accuracy. Long LLM-generated texts are known to sometimes begin correctly but then “drift away” into inaccuracies. In multi-turn settings where instructions are split across turns, drift can manifest as early wrong assumptions that get compounded with each turn. The initial constraints serve as anchors to periodically pull the reasoning back on track. We can also introduce at this stage any **meta-prompts** that guide the process (discussed later in detail). For example, a meta-prompt might instruct an agent: “If you realize the conversation has strayed from the premises, explicitly say so and help correct it.”

### **2\. Iterative Prompt Exchange Loop (EPE Loop)**

Once the stage is set, the agents enter the **Exchange–Prompt–Exchange (EPE) Loop**, which continues for a fixed number of iterations or until they mutually determine that a satisfactory reasoning chain or answer has been reached. Each iteration consists of two main steps (one per agent):

**Step A (Agent A’s Turn – Proposal):** Agent A receives the current context. On the very first turn, this context is just the initial problem description and constraints (since no prior discussion exists). In subsequent turns, the context includes Agent B’s latest explanation/feedback from the previous iteration, plus any persistent notes (like a running summary or an updated list of known facts). Agent A then generates a response intended to advance the reasoning. Typically, this takes the form of a **chain-of-thought proposal**: a series of logical steps, an outline of a solution, or a hypothesis that moves the discussion forward. For example, Agent A might start by restating the problem in its own words and suggesting a first inference. In a later turn, Agent A might attempt a next sub-calculation or propose an answer based on B’s prior clarification. Throughout, Agent A operates with **fragmented context prompting** – it utilizes the information given by Agent B, but often that information is a *fragment* or distilled piece of the whole picture. This approach of feeding the model context in pieces mirrors how humans solve complex problems stepwise, rather than trying to mentally hold an entire large context at once. It also intentionally tests the model’s ability to maintain coherence when information is distributed over turns. (Notably, LLMs can suffer performance loss when context is split across turns versus provided in one prompt, which is why the drift monitoring is crucial.)

When Agent A speaks, we encourage it to use a structured format (if appropriate), like enumerating steps (Step 1, Step 2, …) or writing down intermediate results. This **structured reasoning chain** becomes an artifact that Agent B (and later, an observer) can analyze. It’s important that Agent A is not simply blurting out an answer; instead, it's *priming the reasoning* by detailing its thought process. This is inspired by techniques like **Chain-of-Thought prompting**, which have models list intermediate reasoning and have been shown to significantly improve complex task performance. The difference here is that Agent A doesn’t have to be correct on the first try – it just needs to be transparent. For instance, Agent A may say: *“Step 1: I interpret premise A… Step 2: Based on A and premise B, I guess X might be true… (etc.)”*. Even if guess X is wrong, articulating it gives Agent B something to work with.

**Step B (Agent B’s Turn – Explanation & Feedback):** After Agent A’s turn, the conversation (now containing Agent A’s latest message) is fed to Agent B. Agent B’s job is to **complement and respond** to Agent A’s reasoning. It will typically do the following in its reply:

* **Elaborate or re-express Agent A’s points:** Agent B might rephrase Agent A’s steps in clearer terms, or flesh out details that were skipped. For example, “Agent A assumed X might be true; this would follow only if we also know Y, which wasn’t stated. Let’s examine if Y holds…”.  
* **Verify each step against known facts and constraints:** If Agent A introduced something not supported, Agent B points it out (politely, since they are collaborating). For instance, “In Step 2, you used the value 5, but initially the value was 7\. This is a discrepancy, so let’s correct that.” This catching of errors helps prevent **wrong answer propagation**, a known issue where one agent’s mistake can mislead the other.  
* **Perform micro-reasoning to support or refute Agent A’s claims:** Agent B can do calculations or logical inferences that Agent A skipped. If Agent A said “therefore X \= 10” without showing why, Agent B might actually do the math or logic and confirm “Yes, X does equal 10 because…”, or conversely, “Actually X would equal 12, not 10, according to the formula.”  
* **Maintain alignment with semantics:** Agent B actively checks that the focus of the conversation hasn’t drifted. It uses the initial constraints as reference. If it detects drift, Agent B activates the **self-diagnosis protocol** (see next section) to address it. For example, Agent B might say, *“It seems we have moved away from the original question about causality; let’s refocus using the premises given.”* This acts like a correction mechanism to counteract the well-documented tendency of dialogues to diverge from the user’s intent over many turns. In practice, this might involve re-injecting key pieces of the prompt that were omitted, effectively “refreshing” the context to what’s relevant.

Agent B concludes its turn by possibly suggesting the next steps or asking a question back to Agent A. The exchange is **bi-directional**: sometimes Agent B might pose a clarifying question for Agent A (“Could you clarify why you assumed Y is true?”), effectively prompting Agent A’s next move. This to-and-fro continues the EPE loop.

**Fragmented Context & Memory:** Because each agent only sees what is provided (and we may not always supply the entire conversation history to simulate limited memory), the system might employ a running **memory summary** or state that is passed along. For example, a condensed summary of the chain-of-thought so far could be prepended for Agent A on each turn, ensuring it doesn’t forget earlier conclusions. This summary can be updated by Agent B, since B often has the clearer picture after explaining. In doing so, Agent B is also performing a *state tracking* role, summarizing the current state of the solution which can be carried forward. This technique mitigates issues of context window limits and helps both agents recall important points without re-reading everything. It’s similar to how the “Chain-of-Agents” approach by Google concatenates messages passed from one agent to the next as they handle different chunks of a task – each agent’s output becomes part of the next agent’s input, thereby accumulating information.

The loop repeats with Agent A using Agent B’s latest feedback to refine its reasoning in the next cycle. Over successive iterations:

* Agent A’s proposals should become more accurate and detailed, as it incorporates corrections and insights provided by B.  
* Agent B’s explanations might become more concise (as Agent A makes fewer mistakes to correct) and more focused on higher-level verification or exploring edge cases. The process stops when a termination condition is reached. This could be a predefined number of iterations, or a convergence criterion like “Agent B’s feedback indicates no significant issues and both agents agree on an answer.” For instance, if Agent A in one turn produces a fully consistent solution and Agent B responds with essentially *“That looks correct and I have no further corrections”*, then the system can conclude that an answer has been found along with a validated reasoning chain.

Throughout the EPE loop, two supporting mechanisms are at work: **semantic drift monitoring** and a **self-diagnosis protocol** for errors. These ensure the quality of the reasoning pathway does not degrade as the conversation lengthens. We detail these next.

### **3\. Semantic Drift Monitoring and Mitigation**

**Semantic drift** refers to the model’s tendency to gradually deviate from the original context or factual correctness as it generates more content. In a multi-turn setting, drift can be insidious: each turn might stray just a little, but after many turns the conversation could be addressing a substantially different question or containing fabrications that accumulate unnoticed. Monitoring drift in our N2E loop is critical, as we want the final reasoning to remain relevant and correct relative to the initial problem.

Our framework implements drift monitoring in a few ways:

* **Anchor Checks:** We use the semantic constraints set at initialization as anchors. At each iteration (or every few iterations), one of the agents – usually Agent B, given its verifier role – explicitly checks that those anchors still hold. For example, Agent B may include a sentence in its feedback like, *“We still have Premise A and B as given, and nothing we’ve reasoned contradicts them so far,”* or *“Recall that we must conclude within the scope of premise C.”* This reaffirmation serves two purposes: it reminds Agent A (and itself) of the original facts, and it tests if any of Agent A’s statements violated them. If a violation is found (say, Agent A made an assumption that conflicts with a premise), that is immediately flagged as a **SIC violation** (Semantic Integrity Constraint violation) and corrected. Catching these early prevents the error from propagating through subsequent steps.  
* **Trajectory Similarity (Embed and Compare):** As an optional analytical tool, we can track the semantic content of the dialogue by computing embeddings (vector representations) of each turn or each summary state. A drastic change in these vectors over turns can indicate drift away from initial topics. For instance, if the cosine similarity between the embedding of the current conversation state and the embedding of the initial problem statement drops below a threshold, it suggests the conversation might have wandered. In such a case, the system (perhaps an external wrapper, not the agents themselves) could trigger a *context refresh*. This might involve instructing Agent A on the next turn to explicitly summarize how the current discussion relates to the original question before continuing. Research shows that **re-injecting relevant information into a new prompt can mitigate drift** by resetting the context alignment. In practice, this could be done by having the system automatically prepend a condensed recap of the original question and what has been resolved so far if drift is detected.  
* **Multi-Turn Consistency Queries:** Another strategy is to occasionally have Agent B pose a direct question to Agent A that tests for drift. For example, “Are we still trying to solve *the original problem*? What is our current understanding of it?” If Agent A’s answer is off-base, it means drift occurred and they need to realign. This self-query approach forces the model to reflect on whether it’s still on topic – a kind of mini *attention check*.

By monitoring these signals, the system keeps a **drift map** – effectively a log or visualization of how much the conversation’s meaning has shifted at each step. A drift map could be as simple as a table listing each iteration and a measure of deviation (e.g., a “semantic drift score”). For instance, if using embeddings, one column could be similarity to initial context. If we see it dipping iteration by iteration, that alerts us that the agents might be going off course. In previous studies, such drift maps show that output quality can oscillate – sometimes staying stable, other times quickly degrading. Our goal is to flatten that curve as much as possible, keeping semantic similarity high until the end when an answer is produced.

It’s worth noting that drift isn’t always bad – sometimes the problem requires exploring tangents or subproblems. The key is *controlled divergence*: the agents can explore hypotheses, but they must ultimately circle back to the main question. The drift monitoring ensures they do circle back. Should a large drift occur (i.e., the agents clearly went down a wrong path), the system can employ a **self-repair**: they diagnose what point the divergence happened and either roll back (revisit a previous correct step) or explicitly correct the course. This leads into the self-diagnosis protocol.

### **4\. Self-Diagnosis and Semantic Self-Repair Protocol**

The **self-diagnosis protocol** is a structured approach the agents use to identify and fix errors or inconsistencies in their reasoning chain. Unlike drift monitoring (which is ongoing), self-diagnosis kicks in when a problem is detected – be it a contradiction, a violation of constraints, or simply failure to make progress. It’s essentially the agents taking a step back and asking: *“Where might we have gone wrong, and how do we fix it?”* This protocol can be initiated by either agent, but it will often be Agent B that triggers it, since B is scanning for issues.

Key components of the self-diagnosis protocol include:

* **Explicit Reflection Turns:** The agents may enter a special mode where instead of moving the solution forward, they discuss the reasoning *itself*. For example, Agent B might say, *“Let’s reflect on our solution so far: In Step 3 we assumed Z, but is that really supported? Perhaps we should revisit Step 2.”* This is analogous to an LLM performing *self-reflection*, a technique proven to improve problem solving by having the model critique its own output before finalizing an answer. In fact, researchers have found that LLM agents can significantly improve their performance by reflecting on mistakes and then trying again with that insight. Our two-agent setup naturally facilitates this: Agent B’s critique provides the reflection for Agent A, and then Agent A “tries again” next iteration using that guidance.  
* **Error Traceback:** If a specific step in the reasoning chain is identified as faulty, the agents isolate it. They practically mark the step as questionable and then either re-derive it or alter it. For instance, suppose they find that a calculated value was wrong in Step 4; the self-diagnosis would have Agent A redo Step 4 properly (or Agent B might do it and hand the correct value to A). The conversation at this point might produce an artifact like a **SIC violation report** – a short report enumerating which semantic integrity constraints were violated or where logical errors occurred. For example, “Violation: Step 4 result contradicted Constraint \#2 (factual error). Action: Recalculate Step 4.” Maintaining a list of such violations and resolutions ensures they don’t slip through the cracks.  
* **Meta-Reasoning Prompts:** During self-diagnosis, we may employ meta-prompts (prompts that encourage reasoning about the reasoning). An example meta-prompt to Agent A during self-repair could be: *“Critically examine each of your previous steps and list any assumptions that might be false.”* This essentially forces Agent A to generate an introspective analysis of its chain-of-thought. According to one study, giving models a meta-level prompt like this can help them dynamically choose better strategies. In our case, the strategy is fixing an error – a meta-reasoning prompt can help the agent systematically go through its prior logic like a checklist.  
* **Resetting with Learned Insight:** After identifying an error and hypothesizing a fix, the agents incorporate the fix and continue the normal EPE loop. If things had gone severely off track, they might even decide to restart the reasoning from a certain point using the corrected approach. For example, *“We will restart from step 2 with the corrected understanding that Y is true,”* and then proceed. This resembles how one might backtrack in a proof or in debugging code: go to the last known correct state and try a different path. The conversational format allows this to happen transparently – earlier steps can be revised in light of new insight, and all those steps (even the incorrect ones) remain in the transcript as a **causal record** of what was tried and learned.

**Outcome of Self-Diagnosis:** Ideally, after a self-diagnosis phase, the agents have a cleaner slate to continue from, and the reasoning chain is now more robust. They have effectively *repaired* the semantic or logical break. For example, if earlier a wrong intermediate conclusion was drawn, they’ve now replaced it with the correct one and will use that going forward. This process might repeat multiple times – especially on very hard problems, the agents could go through cycles of making an attempt, identifying flaws, and refining. Each cycle is akin to “trial, error, and improvement,” which is how humans often learn and develop expertise.

One can see parallels of this protocol in approaches like **ReAct (Reason \+ Act)** and other self-correction techniques. ReAct mixes reasoning statements with actions (which could be corrections) in a single agent. Here we distribute that across two agents and a dedicated reflection phase, but the spirit is similar: making the reasoning explicit and having mechanisms to verify and adjust it leads to more reliable outcomes.

Finally, note that self-diagnosis contributes to the **emergence of heuristics** in the system. Each time the agents diagnose a pitfall and solve it, they effectively gain a heuristic (“avoid doing that mistake next time”). Over many problems, this could lead to patterns: e.g., the agents might learn that *whenever* they see a certain type of sub-problem, they should double-check a particular condition (because in the past they got it wrong). While in our simulation they may not literally “learn” persistently (unless we fine-tune them on the transcripts), within the session they accumulate these mini-lessons. This is how the reasoning process becomes more expert-like: not only are specific errors fixed, but the **approach** is refined. For instance, an emergent heuristic might be *verifying arithmetic results with a reverse operation* (if they once caught a mistake by doing the inverse calculation, they might start doing that regularly).

The loop of prompting, explaining, monitoring drift, and self-correcting continues until the agents arrive at a conclusion that Agent B finds satisfactory and consistent with all constraints. At that point, the dialogue ends, yielding a number of **output artifacts** documenting the journey.

## **Output Artifacts of the N2E Process**

Throughout the multi-turn N2E process, the system produces several **output artifacts** – intermediate and final outputs that can be analyzed to understand and verify the reasoning pathway. These artifacts are valuable for researchers or developers to inspect how and **why** the system arrived at an answer, which is crucial for causal traceability and debugging. The expected output artifacts include:

* **Causal Reasoning Chains:** This is the ordered sequence of reasoning steps generated principally by Agent A (and refined by Agent B). It reads like a rationale or proof, showing how the solution was constructed step by step. Each link in the chain is either a premise from the input, an intermediate inference drawn by Agent A, or an explanatory clarification added by Agent B. The chain is *causal* in that one can follow the influence of each step on the next – for example, “From 1 and 2, therefore 3” style progression. Because Agent A has been encouraged to be explicit, and Agent B has elaborated on any implicit jumps, the final chain-of-thought should lay out a clear cause-and-effect narrative from problem to solution. An excerpt might look like:

  * Premise: A implies B.  
  * Premise: B implies C.  
  * Agent A: Assuming A is true, B must be true (by premise 1).  
  * Agent B: Correct, and since B is true, by premise 2, C is true.  
  * Agent A: Therefore, given A, we conclude C.  
* In this example, one can *diagnose* that if something went wrong (say C didn’t actually follow), you know exactly which step would be at fault. Such reasoning chains greatly enhance the **diagnosticity** of the process – observers can pinpoint the origin of any error or see the justification for each conclusion. In AI research, forcing models to produce rational chains (like with chain-of-thought or ReAct) is known to improve transparency and even performance. Our artifact here is a well-vetted chain that has passed Agent B’s scrutiny, making it even more reliable.  
* **Drift Maps and Logs:** As discussed, the system keeps track of semantic drift over the conversation. The *drift map* can be an explicit artifact output at the end, showing, for each turn or phase, how closely the discussion aligned with the original problem context. For instance, it might list: *Turn 1: no drift; Turn 2: slight drift – re-centered by Agent B; Turn 3: back on track; …*. It could include scores or qualitative notes. In some implementations, this might literally be a chart of similarity metrics over time. The drift map offers insight into the **semantic integrity** of the reasoning process by highlighting where the conversation risked going astray. For example, if we see a big dip halfway, we can look at that point in the transcript and see what caused it. This artifact is especially useful in research on long dialogues, confirming whether the measures (like anchor re-injection) were effective in maintaining context. Essentially, it documents the “health” of the semantic context throughout the interaction. If any points show major drift, they would correspond to the moments where SIC violations or corrections occurred, linking to the next artifact.  
* **SIC Violation Reports:** If any **Semantic Integrity Constraint** was violated during the reasoning (and subsequently detected), the system can produce a brief report of these incidents. A SIC violation report might list each occurrence where either agent said something that broke a rule or contradicted a known fact, along with the action taken. For example:

  * *Violation (Turn 4):* Agent A stated a fact inconsistent with Premise 2 (semantic mismatch).  
    * *Detected by:* Agent B in Turn 5\.  
    * *Resolution:* Agent B corrected the fact and re-aligned the discussion.  
  * *Violation (Turn 7):* The chain-of-thought ignored Constraint X about not introducing new entities.  
    * *Detected by:* Drift monitor (embedding similarity dropped).  
    * *Resolution:* Agent B requested clarification; Agent A restated without new entities.  
* Such a report serves as an audit trail for integrity. In complex tasks or long runs, there might be multiple small violations that were fixed; listing them ensures none are forgotten in the final answer and provides a sense of how challenging the problem was (many corrections might indicate a harder problem). It also demonstrates the system’s robustness in catching and fixing its own errors – a key aspect of trustworthiness. In database terms, this is analogous to integrity constraint violations being logged and handled – here we are doing it for a reasoning process instead of a database, but the concept is similar: don’t let the process continue on a corrupted state, fix it and log it.  
* **Emergent Heuristics and Strategy Notes:** Over the course of the dialogue, especially after going through self-diagnosis cycles, the agents might either explicitly or implicitly note certain strategies that helped. These could be output as a set of **heuristics or rules-of-thumb** that appeared during reasoning. For example, towards the end Agent A might summarize: *“Notably, to solve this, we broke the problem into smaller parts and verified each – a generally useful strategy.”* Or Agent B could note: *“We found it effective to double-check each assumption against known constraints.”* These comments can be collated as an artifact: a list of heuristic insights the agents generated. In a research context, this is fascinating because it shows *learning in action* (even if it’s not retained beyond the session). We essentially witness the agents inventing or adopting problem-solving strategies on the fly. For instance, in multiple problems of a similar type, you might observe the agents consistently start doing a particular useful thing that wasn’t explicitly told – that’s an emergent heuristic. A formal way to capture this might be to compare transcripts across different problems and identify common patterns the agents started using (like always summarizing after a few turns, or always verifying arithmetic). Since our framework is designed to encourage generalizable reasoning, spotting these emergent patterns is a success indicator. This artifact might be more qualitative. One could imagine presenting a small table of “Heuristic Emerged – Description – Where it was applied.” For example:

  * *Heuristic:* Check consistency after each major inference.  
    * *Description:* After drawing an inference, explicitly compare it against initial data.  
    * *First observed:* Problem 2, Turn 6, when Agent B started doing consistency summaries.  
  * *Heuristic:* Decompose complex queries into sub-queries.  
    * *Description:* Agent A began asking itself smaller questions that Agent B then answered, rather than tackling the big question in one go.  
    * *Observed:* Throughout Problems 3–5.  
* These emergent heuristics reflect the system’s move towards expert-like behavior; they are essentially the “knowledge” the agents gained through the conversation. Notably, systems like **HeurAgenix** (in a different context of optimization) leveraged multiple agents to **generate and evolve heuristics** for solving problems, showing that LLMs can autonomously come up with novel strategies given the opportunity. In our case, the heuristics might not be as domain-specific as in combinatorial optimization, but rather general reasoning tactics.  
* **Final Answer with Explanation:** Finally, of course, the system produces a **solution or answer** to the original task, accompanied by an explanation derived from the reasoning chain. The answer would typically be given by Agent A (with Agent B’s approval) in the last turn, or we might have a mechanism that one of the agents or the system extracts the answer from the final chain and presents it. The explanation part can be brief since we have the entire detailed chain as another artifact. It might read like: *“Answer: Yes, the proposition follows (Correct). Explanation: As shown in the reasoning steps above, assuming A led to C through B, which confirms the proposition.”* This final output is what an end-user would see if this were an application (the chain and other artifacts are more for internal use or research).

All these artifacts ensure that the outcome of the N2E process is **not just an answer, but a package of evidence**. Anyone can audit the reasoning chain, see where corrections happened, verify that no constraints were violated in the end, and even glean new strategies that emerged. This level of interpretability is crucial for trusting complex AI systems. It also provides rich data for analyzing how general reasoning emerges in such a setup.

## **Success Metrics for Emergent Reasoning**

To evaluate the performance of the N2E multi-agent system, we define several **success metrics**. These metrics go beyond simple accuracy of the final answer – they gauge the quality, robustness, and generality of the reasoning processes that emerged. The key success criteria include **causal diagnosticity**, **semantic integrity**, and **heuristic emergence**, among others. We detail each below, including how they can be measured:

* **Causal Diagnosticity:** This metric assesses how easy it is to diagnose causes of outcomes or errors from the reasoning chain – essentially a measure of how *traceable and explainable* the reasoning is. A highly causally diagnostic reasoning process is one where each conclusion is *clearly linked* to previous steps or premises, making it straightforward to answer questions like “Why did the system think this?” or “Where did it go wrong (if it did)?”. We can measure this by inspecting the **causal reasoning chain artifact**. For example, one could manually or automatically check if every statement in the chain either is an input fact or is derived from earlier statements (no non-sequiturs). Another approach is to see if an independent verifier (perhaps another LLM or a symbolic checker) can follow the chain and reach the same conclusion – if yes, the chain is logically sound and diagnostic. If the model made an error, causal diagnosticity is high if the exact step of the error is identifiable. This often correlates with the chain being explicit and well-structured. In contrast, if the model gave an answer with little explanation or with leaps of logic, then diagnosing would be hard (low diagnosticity). Success here means that **the reasoning pathway is so clear that one could debug or trust the answer by examining it**. It also means that if a different question is asked about the same reasoning (e.g., a hypothetical or a variation), one could trace how a change in input would propagate through the chain to affect the output – a hallmark of understanding causality. In practice, one might quantify diagnosticity by the proportion of steps that are justified or by using a scoring from human judges on how explainable the solution is. The goal is that the N2E process scores much better on this than a single-turn prompt solution would. Notably, multi-agent reflective systems like DPSDP (an actor-critic refinement approach) have shown that breaking reasoning into multi-turn processes makes it easier to pinpoint which part of the reasoning contributed to success or failure – essentially boosting diagnostic insight. We aim for the same benefit.  
* **Semantic Integrity:** This metric captures whether the content of the conversation remained consistent and on-topic – in other words, did the agents preserve the truthfulness and relevance of information throughout. It can be thought of as the absence (or low presence) of semantic drift and constraint violations. We measure this by looking at things like:

  * How many **SIC violations** occurred? Fewer (especially none uncorrected) means high integrity.  
  * The final output’s factual accuracy relative to the input (e.g., if the task was to derive a fact, is that fact correct given the premises?).  
  * The trend in the **drift map**: ideally, we see semantic similarity to the original context stay high, as opposed to plunging halfway through.  
  * An external factual evaluator could be used on each agent turn to flag hallucinations or incorrect statements. If the system’s own self-checks worked, those should be minimal.  
* Semantic integrity is high if *at no point did the reasoning introduce falsehoods without correction, and the final reasoning chain is fully consistent with the initial problem description*. For example, on a math problem, the system never treats a value improperly or if it does, it fixes it; on a logical inference, it never claims something that doesn’t logically follow from or outright contradicts the premises. We could assign a score based on the proportion of conversation where all semantic constraints hold. Another aspect is coherence – did the conversation stay focused on solving the problem given, without going off on irrelevant tangents or getting confused by irrelevant details? This can be judged by human evaluators or automated topic modeling. In research terms, we want to see that our drift mitigation strategies worked: prior studies show a \~39% drop in reliability from single-turn to complex multi-turn dialogue if no mitigation is applied. Our metric would reflect improvement, e.g., significantly less drop or even maintenance of reliability across turns. A concrete metric used by some works is the **semantic drift score** which quantifies correctness vs position in text. We could adapt that per turn – measuring, say, factual accuracy of statements as the dialogue progresses and ensuring it doesn’t degrade.  
* **Heuristic Emergence:** This metric evaluates whether the system has developed generalizable problem-solving strategies (heuristics) that were not explicitly given initially. Essentially, we look for evidence of **learning or adaptation** in the approach the agents take, especially patterns that could transfer to other tasks. This can be measured by analyzing the transcripts and artifacts for novel tactics. For example, did the agents spontaneously start summarizing each other’s points (even if not instructed to do so verbatim)? Did they invent a shorthand or a systematic way to handle a certain type of sub-problem? One way to test heuristic emergence is to give the system a new but related problem after a completed session and see if it solves it *faster or more efficiently*, perhaps by reusing a strategy from the previous problem. If yes, that suggests the heuristic learned earlier carried over. In a single session context, we rely on qualitative analysis: for instance, noticing that by the end, Agent A always does a particular check that it didn’t do in the beginning. We might designate success on this metric if **the agents demonstrate at least one clear instance of a self-discovered strategy that improves their performance**. Academic interest in this metric ties to the concept of *meta-learning* or *auto-curriculum*, where an AI agent in a loop with itself can bootstrap new skills. The multi-agent reflection study noted improved out-of-distribution generalization – meaning the refined reasoning policies helped on new problems, which implies heuristics were acquired rather than just specific answers. Another related metric could be the reduction in needed feedback over iterations: if at first Agent B had to correct A a lot, but over time A starts “pre-correcting” itself in anticipation (like it learned from B’s style), that’s an emergent heuristic (e.g., “I know B will check arithmetic, so I’ll do it carefully now”). We could measure this as a decline in the number of major corrections per turn as the dialogue progresses.  
* **Efficiency and Convergence:** While not explicitly requested, another practical metric is how efficiently the system arrives at a solution. Does it converge in a reasonable number of turns? Efficiency can be measured in terms of token count or iterations. If meta-prompts and self-reflection make the system more effective, we would expect it might solve problems in fewer cycles than a baseline approach. However, given the focus is on reasoning quality, some inefficiency is acceptable as long as the other metrics are strong. Still, tracking this is useful to ensure the method is tractable. For example, if we need 50 turns for a simple problem, that might indicate an issue; success might be solving typical tasks in, say, under 10 exchanges.  
* **Robustness and Generality:** Another qualitative metric is how well the system handles different types of tasks without task-specific tuning. If the framework truly fosters general reasoning, it should succeed across math problems, logical inference, commonsense reasoning, etc., possibly only needing changes in the initial context and constraints. Success here can be measured by testing the system on a suite of tasks (like benchmarks GSM8K for math, CSQA for commonsense, logic puzzles, etc.) and seeing consistent performance. The expectation is not necessarily to beat state-of-the-art on each, but to demonstrate *consistent reasoning integrity*. If a mistake is made, the metrics above (diagnosticity, integrity) ensure it’s recoverable or at least identifiable. Essentially, we want to avoid the situation where the system works well on one kind of task but falls apart on another – that would suggest it hasn’t really developed general reasoning but maybe overfit to a style. Meta-reasoning prompting approaches have shown that explicitly guiding model reasoning can increase adaptability to diverse tasks. Our framework’s success would align with those findings if it shows strong performance across various domains by virtue of its flexible reasoning process.

To encapsulate these metrics more succinctly, we could consider a scoring system or evaluation checklist. For example:

* **Traceability Score:** Do we understand the reasoning? (High if every step is justified) – *Target:* High.  
* **Integrity Score:** Did the conversation remain truthful and relevant? (High if no uncorrected drifts) – *Target:* High.  
* **Heuristic Score:** Did we observe new strategies being used? (High if yes and beneficial) – *Target:* Medium to High (even a few is good).  
* **Accuracy Outcome:** Was the final answer correct given the problem? – *Target:* High, although a correct answer with poor reasoning would not be considered truly successful in this framework; we value reasoning quality even if the final answer might be slightly off on rare occasions.  
* **Turn Efficiency:** Number of turns to converge – *Target:* Moderate (not too high, indicating not too much wandering).

Ultimately, the goal is a comprehensive framework that doesn’t just get answers, but gets them in a way that we can trust and learn from. If causal diagnosticity and semantic integrity are high, it means we have an **explainable and reliable reasoning process**. If heuristic emergence is present, it means the system is actually improving itself or at least demonstrating versatility – a sign of general intelligence. Meeting these metrics indicates success in simulating the emergence of generalizable reasoning within the N2E architecture.

## **Meta-Prompting Strategies for Enhanced Reasoning**

An important extension to this framework is the use of **meta-prompts** – prompts that encourage the agents to reflect on and improve their own prompting or strategy. Meta-prompting essentially means *using the model to better use itself*. In our two-agent setup, meta-prompt strategies can be applied to make each agent more effective at its role and at guiding its counterpart. Incorporating meta-prompts can significantly enhance the depth and adaptability of the reasoning process.

**What is Meta-Prompting in this context?** In general, meta-prompting involves an LLM generating or refining prompts, instructions, or questions for itself or another LLM. It’s like the agents not only talk about the problem, but occasionally talk about *how* they are talking about the problem. This self-referential prompting can guide the system to adjust its approach dynamically. For example, rather than always relying on a fixed initial prompt, the agent might create a new prompt mid-way that is better tailored to the sub-problem at hand.

In the N2E system, we can implement meta-prompting in various ways:

* **Agent-level Meta-Prompts:** Each agent’s role description can include meta-instructions about reasoning. For instance, Agent A’s prompt might say: *“If you are unsure how to proceed, formulate a sub-question for clarity.”* Agent B’s prompt might include: *“If the reasoning is stuck or looping, suggest a new angle or rephrase the question to Agent A.”* These instructions essentially prompt the agents to generate prompts (questions or rephrasings) for each other when needed. This way, the conversation contains not just problem content, but also strategic guidance produced by the agents themselves. It’s been noted that LLMs can improve outcomes by adjusting prompts on the fly; here the agents do that for each other.  
* **Mid-conversation Meta-Prompt Injections:** We might design certain turns to be explicitly meta. For example, after a few iterations, insert a turn (maybe triggered by the system or by Agent B) like: *“Let’s consider how we’re approaching this problem. Should we try a different strategy or structure our thoughts differently?”* This is akin to a planning or strategy meeting between the agents. During this turn, they are not advancing the solution, but discussing the best way *to* advance the solution. For instance, they might agree to break the problem into cases or to use a known reasoning method (like, “maybe we should draw a table of possibilities” – if the LLM can simulate something like that in text form). Meta-prompts can thus lead to a **strategy shift** if the initial approach wasn’t working well.  
* **Prompt Optimization by the Agents:** One agent can act as a **“prompt engineer”** for the other. For example, Agent B (the explainer) might notice that Agent A’s outputs are often too brief or missing detail. Rather than just fixing the detail every time, Agent B could meta-prompt Agent A: *“Please provide more detail in your next reasoning step, for example list out the criteria you’re considering.”* This is a direct meta instruction to improve Agent A’s next prompt (which is its own internal reasoning process). Likewise, Agent A could request Agent B to give hints in a certain format if that would help. Essentially, they negotiate the form of their communication to enhance clarity. This aligns with the idea that *prompts that write other prompts* can lead to clearer and more structured outcomes. Here the agents are crafting each other’s prompts: a collaborative meta-prompting.  
* **Dynamic Method Selection:** Drawing inspiration from **Meta-Reasoning Prompting (MRP)**, the system could allow agents to choose different reasoning methods on the fly. We could have a meta-prompt that lists possible tactics (such as “exhaustive search”, “divide and conquer”, “analogy”, etc.) and have Agent A decide which to use based on the task. For instance, at the start Agent A might evaluate: *“This problem looks like it might need a case-by-case analysis. I will proceed by examining two separate cases…”*. That decision was a meta-reasoning act guided by a prompt telling it to pick a strategy. Microsoft researchers have shown that giving models a menu of reasoning strategies and letting them pick can improve performance across tasks. In our dialogue, we can simulate that by an initial meta conversation or even an internal thought (some implementations allow an agent to have a private scratchpad).  
* **Self-Correction Meta-Prompts:** When errors are found (as part of self-diagnosis), meta-prompts can reinforce learning. For example, after correcting a mistake, Agent B could insert: *“Meta-note: In the future, remember to verify the units consistency, to avoid this kind of error.”* This looks like a note-to-self, which might influence how Agent A handles things a few turns later or on the next problem. Even if we don’t do continuous learning, within the conversation this note can act as a temporary rule. It’s similar to how a human might say “OK, note to self: next time check that earlier.” This could arguably be considered part of the normal conversation, but since it’s about the process, we label it meta.

**Benefits of Meta-Prompting:** By including these meta-level communications, we give our agents a form of **metacognition** – awareness of their own strategy and an ability to change it. This has several benefits:

* It **increases alignment and coherence**. If the agents can talk about how they talk, they can resolve misunderstandings and ensure they are on the same page regarding the plan. For example, if Agent B notices Agent A keeps misinterpreting a premise, a meta-prompt could be: *“Maybe we interpret the premise differently. Here’s how I see it… Could you try framing it this way?”* This can fix misalignment that would otherwise persist.  
* It **prevents ratholes and dead-ends**. If the dialogue isn’t making progress, a meta-discussion can reorient. Essentially, the agents can do what a good human problem-solver would: step back and rethink their approach. This might save time and also improve the chances of success on hard problems.  
* It can **inject domain knowledge or best practices** implicitly. For instance, an initial meta-prompt can come from the system giving a hint like: *“Consider using the principle of mathematical induction if appropriate.”* If the agents heed this, they incorporate a powerful problem-solving method they might not have otherwise used. In a sense, meta-prompts can be a vehicle for *few-shot demonstrations or tips* without explicitly showing how to solve the current problem, thus still forcing the agents to do the heavy lifting. They guide structure, not content. OpenAI’s developers describe meta-prompting as using prompts to structure other prompts for better outcomes – in our case, the outcome is improved reasoning structure.

One concrete example where meta-prompting shines is in **complex multi-step math**: Suppose the agents are stuck trying to directly solve an equation. A meta-prompt might lead them to adopt a heuristic like working out a simpler analogous problem first (a common human strategy). Agent A might say: *“Let’s solve a simpler version first as a hint,”* which is a prompt to itself to gain insight. This approach of analogical or simplified problems can indeed help and is something an LLM might not spontaneously do without a meta-level nudge.

**Ensuring Meta-Prompting is Controlled:** We have to ensure that meta conversations remain useful and don’t themselves lead to confusion. It’s possible for the agents to get too caught up in *how* to solve and forget to solve. Thus, we might impose some limits, e.g., a meta-discussion happens at most once every few turns, or if a solution is converging well, they skip it. Also, the design of the meta-prompts should be such that they clearly transition in and out of meta-mode (maybe by explicit markers like “Meta-thought:” so the agents know when they are in that mode). The system or the roles can enforce that after a meta turn, they summarize any decision (like “Alright, we agreed to try approach X next.”) and then resume normal reasoning.

**Relation to Emergent Reasoning:** Meta-prompting can actually accelerate the emergence of general reasoning. By having the agents effectively *learn how to prompt themselves better*, we’re potentially reducing the search space of trial-and-error they need. It’s like giving them a higher vantage point to see the problem landscape. Over time, the meta-level guidelines they formulate could become second nature (within a session, or across sessions if learning is retained). This means future problems might be handled with an improved internal playbook. We can look at meta-prompt usage as scaffolding that eventually leads to **self-sufficiency** – the agents might internalize those strategies and require fewer explicit meta interventions later on.

In summary, meta-prompts are an **integral part of the comprehensive N2E framework** as they empower the agents to shape their reasoning process actively. By guiding *how* they think and not just *what* to think, we tap into a form of meta-reasoning that is characteristic of human experts (who develop not just knowledge but also strategies to use knowledge). When implemented correctly, meta-prompting yields clearer, more reliable, and more **adaptive reasoning pathways**, thereby enhancing all the success metrics:

* Diagnosticity improves because the reasoning is more structured (thanks to prompts that enforce structure).  
* Semantic integrity benefits because meta-checks can catch drift or confusion early (the agents discuss when they feel off-track).  
* Heuristic emergence is practically built-in, as meta-prompts often encode or produce heuristics explicitly.  
* And overall, the capacity to handle diverse tasks increases, as the agents can choose or craft an approach suited for each new challenge rather than blindly applying one fixed method.

The end result is a **multi-agent N2E system with meta-cognitive capabilities** – a system that not only learns *within* a problem but can also abstract lessons *about* problem-solving and apply them, which is a stepping stone toward more general intelligence in AI reasoning systems.
