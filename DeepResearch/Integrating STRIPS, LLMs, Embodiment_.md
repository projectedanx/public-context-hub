

# **From Symbolic Atoms to Latent Worlds: A Cognitive Architecture for Interoperable Planning**

## **Part I: The Semantic Bridge: Reconciling LLM Fluency with Symbolic Rigor**

The advent of Large Language Models (LLMs) has introduced a paradigm shift in human-computer interaction, offering unprecedented capabilities in natural language understanding and generation. However, their integration into systems requiring logical precision and verifiable correctness, such as autonomous robotic planning, presents a formidable challenge. The probabilistic, associative nature of LLMs stands in stark contrast to the deterministic, logical foundation of classical planning formalisms like the Stanford Research Institute Problem Solver (STRIPS) and its descendant, the Planning Domain Definition Language (PDDL). This section architects a semantic bridge designed not merely to translate between these two worlds, but to create a robust, fault-tolerant interface that harnesses the semantic breadth of LLMs while preserving the logical integrity of symbolic planning. We move beyond simplistic pipelines to propose a sophisticated, feedback-driven validation architecture that systematically filters, verifies, and refines LLM outputs, ensuring that only high-fidelity symbolic representations inform the agent's deliberative processes.

### **The Probabilistic Action Compiler: A Semantic Validation Layer**

The core challenge in integrating LLMs with classical planners is the translation of high-level, often ambiguous, natural language instructions into the formal, unambiguous operators required by a PDDL-based planner.1 While recent work has demonstrated that LLMs can generate syntactically plausible PDDL from natural language prompts, these outputs frequently suffer from logical inconsistencies, factual inaccuracies (hallucinations), and semantic misalignments with the intended meaning.3 A simple, unidirectional pipeline from LLM to planner is therefore unacceptably brittle for any real-world application.

To address this, we ground our architecture in the **LLM-Modulo Framework**, a paradigm that treats LLMs as powerful but non-veridical "idea generators" or "universal approximate knowledge sources".5 This framework posits that the probabilistic, intuitive outputs of an LLM (akin to System 1 cognition) must be vetted by external, model-based verifiers or "critics" that perform rigorous, logical deliberation (System 2 cognition).4 Our proposed

**Semantic Validation Layer (SVL)** serves as this critical verification engine, functioning as a "Probabilistic Action Compiler" that iteratively refines LLM outputs into provably sound symbolic code. This bi-directional interaction, where the verifier provides feedback to the LLM for correction, is fundamentally more robust than a simple pipeline.5

The SVL operates as a multi-stage filtering and validation process. When a high-level instruction like "prepare the table for dinner" is given, the LLM, acting as a semantic parser, generates a candidate PDDL action operator.1 This candidate operator is then passed through the SVL's gauntlet of checks:

1. **Syntactic Check (Parsability):** The first and most basic gate uses a standard PDDL parser to validate the syntactic correctness of the generated code. This ensures the output conforms to the formal grammar of the planning language (e.g., PDDL2.1 or the Extended STRIPS Temporal Grammar proposed in Part II). While many modern LLMs perform well on this metric, smaller or less capable models often fail at this initial stage.1 An output that fails this check is immediately rejected, and feedback is sent to the LLM to correct the syntax.  
2. **Solvability Check:** A syntactically correct operator must also be logically integrable with the existing domain model. The SVL performs a "solvability" check by temporarily adding the new operator to the domain definition and invoking a fast, heuristic-based planner, such as Fast Downward 11, on a canonical test problem. If the planner fails to find a solution or the domain becomes inconsistent, it indicates the new operator has introduced a logical flaw (e.g., contradictory effects). This reflects the practical utility of the generated code.1  
3. **Semantic Equivalence Check (The Verifier):** This is the most crucial and challenging stage. The SVL must verify that the *meaning* of the generated PDDL operator aligns with the user's intent and the physical realities of the domain. This addresses the core problem of ambiguity and hallucination. We propose a multi-faceted verification approach:  
   * **Gold-Standard Comparison:** For actions where a trusted "gold standard" representation exists in a knowledge base, the SVL computes the semantic similarity between the generated operator and the standard. This is achieved by calculating the normalized difference between their respective precondition and effect sets, yielding a similarity score.1 The formula can be expressed as:Similarity=1−∣pregen​∪pregold​∣+∣effgen​∪effgold​∣∣pregen​Δpregold​∣+∣effgen​Δeffgold​∣​

     where pre and eff represent the precondition and effect sets, and gen and gold denote the generated and gold-standard versions, respectively.  
   * **Constraint-Based Verification:** The SVL leverages a library of domain-specific constraints and safety properties (e.g., "a robot cannot hold two objects at once"). It uses a formal verifier to check if the generated operator, when applied, could lead to a violation of these constraints. This approach is inspired by verification techniques in neuro-symbolic systems that prove properties over hybrid architectures.13  
   * **Back-Prompting for Clarification:** When the verifier detects ambiguity or underspecification, it does not simply fail. Instead, it formulates a "back-prompt" to the LLM, leveraging its conversational abilities to resolve the issue.9 For instance, if the LLM generates  
     (prepare-table) without specifying the number of place settings, the SVL might ask: *"The action (prepare-table) is underspecified. Should it be parameterized by the number of guests, or should it use a default value?"* This transforms the verification process into a collaborative dialogue, refining the operator until it meets the required level of precision.

The true architectural function of the Semantic Validation Layer is not merely to translate, but to act as a **semantic firewall**. It is an essential component that isolates two fundamentally different modes of reasoning. The LLM operates as a massive, probabilistic, associative memory, generating plausible outputs based on statistical correlations in its training data—a form of System 1 thinking.4 The symbolic planner, however, requires a deterministic, logically sound world model to perform deductive reasoning and guarantee plan validity—a form of System 2 thinking. The SVL stands between them, preventing the "semantic noise" of the generative model—its ambiguities, hallucinations, and logical fallacies—from corrupting the "logical signal" of the deductive planner. This reframes the problem from one of simple translation to one of epistemic protection, acknowledging the fundamental ontological differences between the two systems.

### **Modeling and Mitigating Latent Drift**

Even with a robust Semantic Validation Layer, a hybrid planning system operating over long horizons is susceptible to a more insidious form of error: **latent drift**. This concept, adapted from the study of semantic drift in LLM text generation 17, describes the gradual, cumulative divergence of the planner's symbolic world model from the actual state of the physical world. This divergence is not caused by a single, catastrophic error but by the accumulation of small, seemingly insignificant semantic approximations inherent in the LLM-to-symbol translation process. The root cause lies in the LLM's fundamental lack of a true, grounded internal world model, which leads it to generate locally coherent but globally inconsistent outputs.15

Latent drift manifests as a progressive decay in the plan's truthfulness and logical coherence.18 We can model and simulate several characteristic failure cascades that arise from this phenomenon:

1. **Vague Precondition Hallucination:** This occurs when an LLM generates an action with a precondition that is semantically plausible but lacks critical, quantifiable detail. For example, an LLM generates the action (:action boil-water :precondition (water-in-kettle)...) for a tea-making task. The SVL, checking for syntactic and logical correctness, approves it. However, the LLM has failed to specify the *amount* of water required. Later in the plan, a subsequent action (:action pour-water-into-pot :parameters (?pot)) requires a specific volume of water that is not present in the kettle. The plan fails at execution time, not because any single operator was overtly wrong, but because a crucial detail was hallucinated as being implicitly satisfied.  
2. **Operator Overloading and Action Aliasing:** This failure mode arises from the LLM's tendency to map semantically distinct real-world actions onto the same symbolic operator. For instance, a user asks the robot to "pick up the wrench." The LLM correctly generates the operator (grasp wrench-1). Later, the user asks the robot to "pick up the delicate orchid." The LLM, associating "pick up" with the grasp symbol, generates (grasp orchid-1). While syntactically valid, the semantics of "grasp" are critically different in these two contexts—one implies a firm, high-force grip, while the other requires a gentle, low-force pinch. The single STRIPS operator grasp is now dangerously overloaded. A plan that uses this operator to handle the orchid will likely result in its destruction. This is a form of action aliasing, where the richness of the latent semantic space is collapsed into an impoverished and ambiguous symbolic representation.  
3. **Cumulative Incoherence:** This is the most subtle form of latent drift, where a sequence of individually acceptable actions, when chained together, leads to a globally nonsensical or physically impossible state. An LLM's focus on local coherence—ensuring that each generated token is plausible given the immediately preceding tokens—blinds it to the long-range consequences of its outputs.17 For example, in a "tidy the room" task, a plan might consist of a series of  
   (move-object?obj?loc) actions. Each action, like (move-object book-1 bookshelf-3), might pass the SVL's checks in isolation. However, the full sequence of these moves could result in the planner's world model believing that a book has been placed inside a solid wall, or that the same space is occupied by two different objects. The accumulation of small semantic inaccuracies leads to a complete break with physical reality.

The existence of latent drift suggests that it is a fundamental property of such a hybrid architecture, akin to a form of **epistemic entropy**. The symbolic planner's world model is designed to be a low-entropy, highly structured, deterministic system, operating under a closed-world assumption.19 The LLM, in contrast, is an open-world, high-entropy source of probabilistic information. The Semantic Validation Layer acts as a "Maxwell's Demon," attempting to allow only well-formed, low-entropy information (logically sound operators) to pass into the symbolic system. However, the SVL is not perfect; every LLM-sourced operator carries a residual uncertainty. Over a long-horizon plan involving many such operators, this uncertainty accumulates, the entropy of the symbolic model increases, and its correspondence to the real world degrades. This drift is not a bug to be fixed but a thermodynamic tendency that must be actively and continuously managed.

Mitigating latent drift requires a shift from one-time validation to continuous management:

* **Probabilistic Predicates:** The planner's state representation can be augmented to move beyond simple Boolean predicates. Instead of (water-in-kettle) being true or false, it could be represented by a probability, P(water-in-kettle) \= 0.95. The SVL's output would then be a distribution over possible PDDL operators, and the planner would be required to reason under this explicit uncertainty.  
* **Grounding Confidence Score (GCS):** A confidence score, derived from perceptual evidence and model certainty, can be attached to each predicate. Actions that rely on low-confidence preconditions can be assigned a higher cost, incentivizing the planner to build plans on more reliable information. This concept will be formalized in Part IV.  
* **Periodic Re-Grounding and Verification:** The most robust defense is to break the open-loop nature of long-term planning. The system must periodically pause execution to re-ground its entire symbolic world model against fresh sensor data. This allows the system to detect and correct drift before it leads to a critical failure cascade, forming a closed-loop control system for epistemic integrity.

## **Part II: The Temporal Nexus: Synchronizing STRIPS Logic in Dynamic Worlds**

Classical planning formalisms, including STRIPS and early versions of PDDL, were designed for static, deterministic, and fully observable worlds where actions are treated as instantaneous, atomic events. This atemporal ontology is fundamentally incompatible with the reality of embodied agents, which operate in environments characterized by continuous time, actions with duration, and a constant stream of asynchronous events from external sources like human collaborators or independent physical processes.20 This part of the report addresses this critical gap by proposing an architectural upgrade to the core planning formalism itself. We first introduce an

**Extended STRIPS Temporal Grammar (ESTG)** that enriches PDDL with primitives for handling complex temporal phenomena. We then analyze the computational ramifications of this enhanced expressivity and outline an architecture for maintaining plan coherence through efficient temporal replanning.

### **An Extended STRIPS Temporal Grammar (ESTG)**

To enable robust planning in dynamic environments, the planning language must be capable of representing and reasoning about time, concurrency, and external events. Our proposed ESTG builds upon the solid foundations of PDDL2.1 and PDDL3, which introduced key temporal concepts. PDDL2.1 formalized durative-actions, which possess a duration and allow for conditions to be specified (at start), (at end), and (over all) the action's execution, with effects occurring at the start and end points.22 PDDL3 further enhanced this by adding state-trajectory constraints specified using temporal modal logic, such as

(sometime-after) and (hold-during), enabling more expressive goals and preferences.26

However, even these advanced formalisms are primarily agent-centric. They model a world where change is driven either by the planner's own actions or by predictable, continuous processes initiated by those actions (e.g., a battery level decreasing over time).28 They are less equipped to model and react to truly

**asynchronous events**—state changes that are triggered by external, independent actors or processes over which the agent has no control.20 The classical model of action sequencing breaks down when the agent's deliberative cycle is decoupled from the environment's perceptual cycles, a phenomenon known as perceptual asynchrony.29

To bridge this gap, the ESTG introduces a set of new primitives and structures into the PDDL syntax, designed to make the planner aware of and reactive to a dynamic, multi-actor world.

* **Timing\_Tick:** This introduces a reference to a global, real-world clock. While temporal planners often reason about relative time intervals, a Timing\_Tick allows actions and events to be synchronized with external time sources, which is essential for coordinating with humans or other systems that operate on their own schedules.  
* **(:event \<event\_name\>...):** This defines a new first-class entity in the domain, parallel to (:action). An event is a state transition that is not executed by the agent. It has a set of preconditions and a set of effects. The event is triggered automatically by the environment whenever its preconditions become true. This formalizes the concept of "exogenous events" discussed in the literature.20 For example:  
  Code snippet  
  (:event (human-hesitation)  
      :parameters (?r \- robot?h \- human)  
      :condition (and (approaching?r?h) (detects-retreat?h))  
      :effect (and (plan-is-paused) (increase (anxiety-level?h) 1))  
  )

* **Duration\_Flex(min, max):** This constraint on durative action duration allows for actions whose execution time is uncertain or dependent on external factors. For example, an action (wait-for-human-response) might have a Duration\_Flex(1, 30\) in seconds, capturing the unpredictability of human interaction.  
* **(at \<time\_offset\> \<effect\>):** Standard durative actions only allow effects at their start and end points. This primitive extends the :effect field to allow effects to occur at any specified time offset from the start of the action. This is critical for modeling complex processes with intermediate state changes, a known limitation of some temporal planners.30 For instance, a  
  (brew-coffee) action might have an effect (is-brewed) at an offset of 120 seconds, but the final effect (is-ready-to-pour) only at the action's end.  
* **(:sync-token \<token\_name\> \<modality\>):** This special predicate type acts as a bridge to the perceptual system. It becomes true when a specific, meaningful event is detected by a given sensor modality. For example, a condition (is-true (sync-token scene-change-detected vision)) would allow the planner to trigger a contingency plan (e.g., re-scan the environment) only when the vision system reports a significant change, rather than having to poll continuously.

The introduction of these primitives, particularly (:event) and (:sync-token), forces a fundamental shift in the conceptualization of a "plan." A plan is no longer a simple linear or even partially ordered sequence of agent-controlled actions. It becomes a **hybrid temporal network** comprising both controlled actions and potential, uncontrolled environmental events. The planner's task evolves from "finding a sequence of actions" to "constructing a robust policy that can navigate a web of possible events and state changes." This moves the planner's output closer to a structure like a Temporal Plan Network (TPN), which explicitly models choices and conditional branches 32, or a reactive policy that maps states and incoming events to actions. The ESTG, therefore, is not merely a syntactic extension; it is a language designed to describe a fundamentally different, more dynamic kind of planning problem, one that shares deep connections with control theory and reactive systems engineering.

### **The Complexity of Coherence: Analyzing Temporal Replanning**

The enhanced expressivity of the ESTG comes at a significant computational cost. Temporal planning is already a computationally demanding field. Research has shown that, when interpreted over dense time, PDDL2.1 planning can range from PSPACE-complete to EXPSPACE-complete, and can even be undecidable under certain assumptions about action self-overlap and the separation between mutually exclusive events.25 By introducing asynchronous events and more complex temporal constraints, our ESTG firmly places planning problems in this high-complexity space. Consequently, a strategy of brute-force replanning—generating a completely new plan from scratch every time an unexpected event occurs—is computationally intractable for any non-trivial application.33

The key to maintaining coherence in a dynamic world lies in efficient **plan repair** rather than full replanning.35 The objective is to surgically modify only the parts of the plan that have been invalidated by an unexpected event, while preserving the integrity of the remaining, still-valid portions.34 Advanced execution systems like IXTET-EXEC exemplify this approach. They maintain a flexible, partially ordered plan and, upon detecting a failure, use the planner's own reasoning capabilities to repair the invalidated segment, often while other independent parts of the plan continue to execute.36

At the heart of temporal plan management is a constraint network, typically a Simple Temporal Network (STN) or one of its more expressive variants like a Disjunctive Temporal Problem (DTP) or an STN with Uncertainty (STNU).35 An STN represents a plan as a graph where nodes are time points (e.g., the start or end of an action) and directed edges represent temporal constraints on the difference between them (e.g.,

end(A) \- start(A) \<= 10). Checking the consistency of a basic STN is a polynomial-time problem, often solved with an all-pairs shortest-path algorithm like Floyd-Warshall, which has a complexity of O(n3) where n is the number of time points.39

However, the introduction of asynchronous events creates complex temporal edge cases that stress this model and increase the complexity of repair:

1. **Delayed Effects and Invalidation:** An action A is initiated at time t1​ with an effect scheduled to occur at t1​+δ. An asynchronous event occurs at time t2​, where t1​\<t2​\<t1​+δ, which invalidates a precondition for A's effect. The repair mechanism must not only handle the invalidation of A but also trace all causal and temporal dependencies forward to identify and manage all subsequent actions that relied on A's now-cancelled effect.  
2. **Event Overlap and Race Conditions:** Two or more asynchronous events occur in close succession, for example, a (human-interferes) event and a (low-battery-warning) event. The order in which the system processes these events can lead to drastically different repaired plans. The architecture must include a policy for prioritizing or handling concurrent events. The computational problem of checking the controllability of a multi-agent temporal network in the presence of such uncertainty can be NP-complete or co-NP-complete.37  
3. **Rollback Race Conditions:** An event invalidates action A. The system initiates a plan repair. While the repair is being computed, action B, which depended on A in the original plan, begins execution based on the outdated schedule. This can lead to catastrophic failures. The execution system must be able to immediately halt all plan fragments that are causally or temporally dependent on an invalidated action.

These challenges dictate a critical architectural requirement: the system cannot rely on a simple plan-then-execute model. It necessitates a **Continuous Temporal Reasoner** or **Execution Monitor** that runs concurrently with the plan's execution.21 This component is responsible for:

* Maintaining the STN that represents the temporal constraints of the current plan.  
* Receiving and timestamping asynchronous event tokens from the environment and perceptual systems.  
* Updating the STN to reflect the consequences of these events (e.g., adding new constraints, invalidating nodes).  
* Continuously running temporal consistency checks on the updated network.  
* If an inconsistency is detected, triggering the planner in a "repair mode," providing it with the specific set of broken constraints and the current world state as the starting point for a localized search.

This architecture leads to a profound shift in perspective: the plan is no longer a static blueprint for the future. It is a **living document**, a dynamic data structure that is constantly being queried, updated, and revised in response to the continuous flow of real-world events. The "planner" is transformed from a one-shot, offline solver into an online "reasoning-as-a-service" component that is called upon by the execution system to perform the specific, bounded task of plan maintenance. This blurs the traditional distinction between planning and execution, merging them into a single, continuous, adaptive process.

## **Part III: The Embodied Ground: Anchoring Symbols in Sensorimotor Reality**

A symbolic planner operates in a world of abstract tokens like cup-1, is-hot, and (on table-1 cup-1). For an embodied agent, these symbols are meaningless unless they are robustly and persistently connected to the physical world as perceived through its sensors. This is the essence of the **symbol grounding problem**.40 This part of the report architects the critical bridge between the planner's abstract symbolic layer and the robot's continuous, noisy, and multimodal perceptual layer. We first design a "Multimodal Grounding Bridge" layer based on the principles of perceptual anchoring. We then analyze the distinct grounding requirements of deliberative (STRIPS) and reactive (Behavior Tree) control hierarchies, revealing a fundamental duality in the nature of grounding itself.

### **The Multimodal Grounding Bridge Layer**

The core task of the Multimodal Grounding Bridge (MGB) layer is to translate the continuous flux of sensorimotor data into the discrete, STRIPS-valid predicates that constitute the planner's world state, and, crucially, to maintain the integrity of this translation over time as the world and the robot's perception of it change.44

To achieve this, we adopt the **Perceptual Anchoring** framework as the architectural foundation for the MGB.45 Perceptual anchoring focuses specifically on creating and maintaining the correspondence between symbols that denote physical objects and the sensor data that refers to them.47 This is a natural fit for a STRIPS-based system, which reasons about the properties and relations of persistent objects. The central data structure in this framework is the

**anchor**. An anchor is an internal representation that binds a symbolic identifier (e.g., cup-1) to the stream of percepts associated with the corresponding physical object.46 A well-formed anchor contains:

* A unique symbolic identifier (e.g., cup-1).  
* A symbolic description, which is a set of predicates believed to be true of the object (e.g., (type cup-1 cup), (color cup-1 red)).  
* A perceptual signature, which may include feature vectors from various modalities (e.g., visual shape descriptors, thermal readings, haptic force profiles).  
* A history of perceptual states, such as a trajectory of past positions and property values, which is essential for tracking and reasoning about change over time.45

The MGB is the system responsible for creating and managing the "anchor space." Its operation can be broken down into a continuous pipeline:

1. **Sensor Fusion:** The MGB ingests raw data from the robot's diverse sensor suite (e.g., RGB-D cameras, LiDAR, tactile sensors, microphones). This data is fused to create a more complete and robust perceptual representation.48 Fusion can occur at different levels of abstraction: data-level (combining raw pixel or point cloud data), feature-level (combining extracted features like edges or textures), or decision-level (combining the outputs of separate classifiers).49 For example, a visual object detector might identify a bounding box, and a haptic sensor making contact within that region provides force and texture data for the same object.  
2. **Feature Extraction and Classification:** A suite of neural networks, including both modality-specific and cross-modal architectures 50, processes the fused sensor data to extract meaningful features. These features are then fed into classifiers that map them to symbolic predicates. Importantly, these classifiers are probabilistic. A visual classifier does not simply output  
   is-red; it outputs a probability, P(is-red∣image\_patch). Similarly, a haptic classifier might output P(is-heavy∣force\_profile).  
3. **Anchoring Management:** The MGB takes these probabilistic predicate groundings and uses them to update the anchor space through three core functions 45:  
   * **Acquire:** When a new object is perceived that does not match any existing anchor, a new anchor is created and initialized with the object's symbolic identifier, perceptual signature, and initial set of grounded predicates.  
   * **Re-acquire/Track:** When new sensor data arrives, the MGB must determine which existing anchor it corresponds to. This matching process is critical. Once a match is found, the anchor is updated with the new perceptual information and its symbolic description is revised. This is the key function for maintaining the symbol-percept link over time.  
   * **Find:** The planner can query the MGB with a symbolic description (e.g., "find an object that is a cup and is red"). The MGB searches the anchor space for an anchor whose symbolic description matches the query.  
4. **Predicate Generation for STRIPS:** The MGB exposes the current state of the anchor space to the planner. For each anchor, it generates a set of grounded predicates (e.g., (is-red cup-1), (is-heavy cup-1)) that form the planner's belief about the world state. Crucially, these predicates are not asserted as absolute truth. Each predicate is accompanied by a **Grounding Confidence Score (GCS)**, a value in $$ derived from the output probabilities of the underlying classifiers, the degree of consensus between different sensor modalities, and the history of observations.

This architectural design reveals a deeper truth about the nature of symbol grounding. The process is not a static, one-time translation from a perceptual pattern to a symbolic label. It is, in fact, a problem of **state estimation for a partially observable dynamic system**. The "state" in this context is the true set of symbolic predicates describing the world, which is hidden and can only be inferred through noisy observations (sensor data). The Multimodal Grounding Bridge, therefore, functions as a sophisticated **Bayesian filter** (such as a Kalman filter for tracking position or a particle filter for more complex state distributions). Each anchor is the filter's internal belief state for a specific object. The continuous process of re-acquiring and tracking is the filter's update step, where new observations are used to refine the belief state. The Grounding Confidence Score is a direct representation of the filter's certainty in its estimate (analogous to the covariance matrix in a Kalman filter). This reframing connects the problem of symbol grounding to the powerful and mathematically rigorous formalisms of control theory and probabilistic robotics, providing a rich set of tools for analyzing and improving its performance.

### **A Tale of Two Hierarchies: STRIPS vs. Behavior Trees**

In a sophisticated robotic architecture, task execution is typically not monolithic but is stratified into at least two hierarchical layers: a high-level deliberative layer and a low-level reactive layer. In our proposed system, these roles are filled by the STRIPS planner and a Behavior Tree (BT) executor, respectively. These two formalisms, while both dealing with action sequencing, have fundamentally different characteristics and place distinct demands on the Multimodal Grounding Bridge.

* **Distinct Roles and Characteristics:**  
  * **STRIPS Planner (Deliberative Layer):** The planner operates on an abstract, symbolic representation of the world. Its function is to perform goal-directed, long-horizon reasoning, searching through a vast state space to find a sequence of abstract actions (e.g., (pick-up cup-1), (move-to kitchen), (place-on-counter cup-1)) that provably leads from the initial state to the goal state. While powerful for complex, multi-step problem solving, classical planning is typically performed offline and is not inherently reactive to real-time environmental changes.53  
  * **Behavior Tree (Reactive Layer):** The BT's role is to execute the plan produced by the deliberative layer. It takes a single abstract action from the plan, such as (pick-up cup-1), and decomposes it into a fine-grained sequence of low-level conditions and motor commands. BTs are structured as hierarchical trees of tasks and are "ticked" at a high frequency, causing them to constantly re-evaluate the state of the world.54 This makes them highly reactive, modular, and more scalable and readable than traditional Finite State Machines (FSMs) for composing complex behaviors.55 Recent work has even explored using LLMs to generate BTs directly from natural language, a promising avenue for future integration.58  
* Integration and the Impact of Perceptual Ambiguity:  
  The MGB serves as the crucial interface for both hierarchies, but they consume its information in different ways. This interaction is stressed by perceptual ambiguity, which arises from sensor noise, conflicting data from different modalities 48, or partial observability due to occlusions.59 Such ambiguity is a pervasive challenge in real-world robotics and human-robot interaction.60  
  * **Impact on the STRIPS Planner:** The planner consumes a "snapshot" of the world state from the MGB to begin its deliberation. Perceptual ambiguity destabilizes this snapshot. If the MGB reports that the predicate (clear block-A) has a low GCS of 0.3 due to a cluttered view, the planner's symbolic world model becomes uncertain. It faces a difficult choice: assume the block is clear and risk generating a plan that will fail, assume it is not clear and be overly conservative, or generate an epistemic plan to actively reduce the uncertainty (e.g., by executing a (move-to-new-viewpoint) action). This highlights the necessity for planning under perceptual uncertainty, a key research area.59  
  * **Impact on the Behavior Tree:** The BT consumes a continuous, real-time stream of information from the MGB to evaluate its condition nodes. Ambiguity can cause a BT to fail or become stuck. Consider a BT for grasping an object that includes the sequence: (1) IsObjectVisible? \-\> (2) ApproachObject \-\> (3) IsGripperReady? \-\> (4) Grasp. If the IsObjectVisible? condition node queries the MGB and receives a flickering signal due to sensor noise, the BT will repeatedly fail at the first step and never progress. While BTs can be made more robust by using probabilistic conditions (e.g., succeeding if confidence is \> 0.8), they inherently lack the planner's global reasoning capability to decide *how* to resolve the underlying ambiguity.

This analysis reveals a **duality in the function of grounding**. The STRIPS planner and the BT executor have fundamentally different grounding requirements. The planner requires **state-level grounding**: a stable, coherent, and consistent symbolic snapshot of the world that can serve as the initial state for deliberation. The BT requires **transition-level grounding**: a continuous, low-latency stream of boolean or probabilistic evaluations of its condition nodes to drive reactive transitions between behaviors. A robust MGB must be architected to serve both of these masters simultaneously. This might imply that the MGB exposes two distinct APIs or data streams: one optimized for providing consistent, world-model snapshots to the planner, and another optimized for providing high-frequency, low-latency updates to the BT executor. Recognizing this duality is a critical design consideration for achieving seamless cross-hierarchy integration.

| Feature | STRIPS Planner | Behavior Tree (BT) Executor |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Primary Function** | Deliberative, goal-directed, long-horizon reasoning. | Reactive, task-oriented, short-horizon execution. |
| **Time Horizon** | Entire task duration. | Single action or sub-task. |
| **Reactivity** | Low. Typically non-reactive unless integrated in a replanning loop. | High. Continuously re-evaluates conditions via high-frequency "ticks". |

| **Role in Hybrid Architecture** | The "brain" that decides *what* to do and in what high-level order. | The "nervous system" that controls *how* each step is physically executed. |

## **Part IV: Synthesis and Recovery: Architecting a Fault-Tolerant Cognitive Loop**

A truly autonomous system cannot be merely proficient; it must be resilient. The preceding sections have designed the individual bridges between semantics, time, and perception. This part integrates them into a single, cohesive cognitive architecture. We first conduct an integrated failure mode analysis, tracing a single error as it cascades across the system's layers. We then design a **Recursive Recovery Engine**, a meta-level control loop that endows the system with fault tolerance, enabling it to detect, diagnose, and dynamically recover from such failures through adaptive planning and execution.65

### **An Integrated Failure Mode Analysis**

To understand the necessity of an integrated recovery system, we simulate a complex, realistic failure scenario involving a therapeutic robot tasked with comforting an anxious patient. This scenario is designed to stress all three architectural pillars simultaneously.

* **Scenario:** A robot in a clinical setting is tasked with achieving the high-level goal (patient-is-calm). The system uses an LLM to generate potential comforting strategies, a temporal planner to schedule actions, and multimodal sensors (vision, haptics, audio) to perceive the patient's state.  
* **The Failure Cascade:**  
  1. **Layer 1: Semantic Failure (LLM Ambiguity).** The system prompts the LLM for a comforting action. The LLM, drawing on its vast corpus of text, provides the semantically rich but operationally vague instruction: "Gently hold the patient's hand to provide comfort." The **Semantic Validation Layer (SVL)** attempts to compile this into a formal ESTG operator. It produces: (:durative-action apply-comforting-touch :parameters (?r \- robot?p \- patient) :duration (=?duration 10\) :condition (at start (is-receptive?p)) :effect (at end (is-calm?p))). The SVL's verifier, lacking a precise grounding for "receptive," flags the precondition (is-receptive?p) as a point of high semantic ambiguity. However, without a clear contradiction, it may allow the operator to pass with a warning, creating a latent vulnerability in the plan.  
  2. **Layer 2: Temporal Failure (Asynchronous Event).** The planner incorporates the new action and generates a plan that includes executing apply-comforting-touch at time T=5. As the robot's manipulator approaches the patient's hand, the patient, feeling anxious, instinctively hesitates and pulls their arm back. This is an **asynchronous event** detected by the robot's vision and proprioceptive sensors. The action's execution is unexpectedly delayed. This delay violates a downstream temporal constraint in the plan—for instance, a scheduled medication reminder (hold-during T+15 T+30 (medication-reminder-active)) is now missed. The **Continuous Temporal Reasoner** detects this temporal constraint violation and signals a plan failure.  
  3. **Layer 3: Perceptual Failure (Grounding Mismatch).** As the robot pauses and attempts to re-evaluate the situation, its **Multimodal Grounding Bridge (MGB)** receives conflicting sensor data. The vision system, analyzing the patient's facial expression, reports a high probability for the predicate (has-neutral-expression patient-1). Simultaneously, the haptic sensor on the robot's hand, making incidental contact, detects elevated muscle tension and skin conductance, leading to a high probability for (is-anxious patient-1). The MGB's classifiers are now in disagreement. The confidence in the plan's critical precondition, (is-receptive patient-1), plummets. The symbolic world state, which the planner relies on, is now unstable and contradictory.  
* **Diagnosis:** The system has experienced a systemic failure, cascading across all layers. The root causes are deeply intertwined: the LLM's ambiguous suggestion created a brittle, ungrounded precondition; the temporal plan was not robust enough to handle the asynchronous dynamics of human interaction; and the perceptual system generated ambiguous, contradictory data that destabilized the symbolic foundation of the plan. This demonstrates that failures are not isolated to a single module but are emergent properties of the entire hybrid architecture.

|  | SVL | Compiles instruction into an operator with an ungrounded, ambiguous precondition (is-receptive). |

| **Temporal (Asynchrony)** | Temporal Reasoner | Fails to anticipate the human hesitation event, leading to a detected constraint violation after the fact. |

### **The Recursive Recovery Engine**

To address these systemic failures, the architecture requires a meta-level control loop: the **Recursive Recovery Engine**. This engine orchestrates the system's components in a tight feedback loop, transforming the system from a linear executor into an adaptive, self-correcting agent. Its operation is predicated on the formalization of uncertainty through the Grounding Confidence Score (GCS).

* Formalizing the Grounding Confidence Score (GCS):  
  The GCS is the currency of certainty within the system. For any predicate p in the world model, GCS(p) is a value in the range $$ that represents the system's confidence in the truth of that predicate. This score is not arbitrary; it is computed by the MGB using a principled, probabilistic approach. It can be formulated as the Bayesian posterior probability P(p∣E), where E is the set of all relevant sensor evidence.41 The GCS synthesizes information from multiple sources:  
  * The output probabilities of the MGB's neural classifiers.  
  * The degree of agreement or disagreement between different sensor modalities.49  
  * The robot's familiarity with the perceptual input (i.e., confidence may be lower for novel objects or environments).64  
  * The temporal consistency of observations over time.  
* The Recovery Loop in Action:  
  Let us revisit the therapeutic robot scenario to see how the Recovery Engine functions:  
  1. **Detection:** The failure cascade generates multiple fault signals. The MGB reports a low confidence: GCS(is-receptive patient-1) \= 0.4. The Temporal Reasoner simultaneously reports a temporal constraint violation.  
  2. **Pause and Diagnosis:** The Recovery Engine immediately pauses the execution of the current plan. It diagnoses the fault by identifying the low-confidence precondition and the temporal inconsistency as the primary sources of failure.  
  3. Dynamic Goal and Cost Refinement: The engine intervenes at the deliberative level. First, it modifies the planner's goal. The original goal was simply (is-calm patient-1). The new, temporary goal becomes a conjunction that includes an epistemic objective: (and (is-calm patient-1) (\> (GCS(is-receptive patient-1)) 0.9)). The planner is now tasked not just with achieving a world state, but with achieving a state of high confidence. Second, the engine dynamically adjusts the cost of actions. The cost of any action a is modulated by the confidence in its preconditions:  
     Cost(a)=minp∈preconditions(a)​GCS(p)BaseCost(a)​

     This formula makes actions that rely on uncertain preconditions prohibitively expensive, naturally guiding the planner away from brittle plans.13  
  4. **Information-Gathering Replanning:** The planner, now driven by the new epistemic goal and cost function, searches for a plan to increase its confidence. It may query the LLM for information-gathering actions and find an operator like (:action ask-clarification :parameters (?q \- question) :effect (human-has-responded)). The planner generates a new, short-term repair plan: (1) execute-vocal-synthesis "Is it okay if I hold your hand?".  
  5. **Execution and Re-Evaluation:** The robot executes the question. The patient's verbal response ("Yes, that's fine") is captured by the audio sensors and processed by the MGB. This new, strong piece of evidence causes the GCS for (is-receptive patient-1) to jump to 0.95.  
  6. **Resumption:** With the critical precondition now confidently met, the Recovery Engine discards the temporary epistemic goal and directs the Temporal Reasoner to reschedule the original apply-comforting-touch action, which now proceeds successfully.

This recovery cycle transforms the entire planning process into a form of **scientific inquiry**. When the system is faced with uncertainty (low GCS), it does not simply halt or fail. It formulates a hypothesis about the world (e.g., H: the patient is receptive), recognizes that its evidence for this hypothesis is weak, and then generates and executes a plan (an *experiment*, such as asking a question) for the explicit purpose of gathering more data. The outcome of this experiment is used to update its belief (the GCS), and it proceeds only when its confidence crosses a desired threshold. This elevates the planner from a simple "doer" to a "knower," an agent that actively manages and seeks to reduce its own uncertainty. This epistemic loop is the cornerstone of robust, adaptive intelligence.

## **Part V: Reflexive Audit and Future Horizons: Probing the Epistemic Foundations of Hybrid Intelligence**

The architecture detailed in the preceding sections provides a functional blueprint for a new class of intelligent systems. However, constructing such a system requires more than just technical specifications; it demands a reflexive examination of the deep philosophical and long-term challenges inherent in creating hybrid minds. This final part steps back from the architectural details to probe the epistemic and ontological foundations of our proposed system, addressing the fundamental questions of truth, time, and meaning that emerge at the intersection of symbolic logic, latent semantics, and embodied perception.

### **The Arbitration of Truth: Cognitive Dissonance and Epistemic Authority**

A critical challenge arises when the system's different components produce contradictory "truths." Consider the conflict from our therapeutic robot scenario: the LLM, based on its semantic knowledge, might infer that a patient's verbal assent ("I'm fine") means the predicate (is-calm patient-1) is true. Simultaneously, the Multimodal Grounding Bridge (MGB), processing haptic data showing high muscle tension and a rapid heart rate, concludes that (is-anxious patient-1) is true. This creates a state of **cognitive dissonance** within the AI. The system holds two contradictory beliefs about the world. Which one is correct? Who, or what, arbitrates?

This problem cannot be solved by simply averaging signals or taking a majority vote. It requires a formal mechanism for **epistemic arbitration**—a principled process for resolving conflicts based on the nature and reliability of the knowledge source.68 We propose an architecture that explicitly defines a hierarchy of

**epistemic authority**, prioritizing information that is more directly and reliably connected to the physical world:

1. **Level 1 (Highest Authority): Grounded Multi-Sensor Consensus.** A belief supported by consistent, corroborating data from multiple, independent sensor modalities (e.g., vision, haptics, and thermal sensors all indicate an object is hot).  
2. **Level 2: Grounded Single-Sensor Data.** A belief supported by data from a single sensor. This is still directly grounded in physical measurement but is more susceptible to noise or sensor-specific failure.  
3. **Level 3: Inferred Symbolic Knowledge.** A belief derived logically from other high-authority beliefs within a validated plan or a trusted knowledge base.  
4. **Level 4 (Lowest Authority): Ungrounded Semantic Knowledge.** A belief generated by the LLM based on statistical patterns in its training data, without direct, real-time perceptual evidence.

To implement this hierarchy, the architecture can incorporate a **Truth Maintenance System (TMS)**, a classic AI technique for managing belief revision.71 In this scheme, every predicate asserted into the planner's world model is tagged with its epistemic source (e.g.,

source: LLM, source: HapticSensor). When a new piece of information from a higher-authority source contradicts an existing belief from a lower-authority source, the TMS automatically retracts the less reliable belief and, crucially, all other beliefs that were logically derived from it. This process, known as dependency-directed backtracking, ensures that the system's active belief set remains consistent and is always anchored to the most reliable available evidence.74

### **The Temporal Ontology Tension: Is Sequential Action an Illusion?**

Classical planning, and by extension STRIPS, is built upon a specific **temporal ontology**: the world evolves through a sequence of discrete, instantaneous, agent-controlled actions. A plan is a well-ordered path through a state-space graph. However, our proposed architecture fundamentally challenges this ontology. The inclusion of a Continuous Temporal Reasoner and the handling of asynchronous events and perceptual cycles mean that the agent's knowledge of the world is not guaranteed to arrive in a neat, synchronous sequence.

Consider the phenomenon of **perceptual asynchrony**, where different attributes of a single event are perceived at different times.29 A robot might receive haptic confirmation that its gripper has made contact with an object

*before* its vision system has finished processing the image to confirm the object's identity. In this case, the robot's action (grasp) and its perception of the consequences are out of sync. If the planner is waiting for a full, consistent world state before proceeding, it will be paralyzed.

This tension suggests that the classical ontology of sequential action may be an illusion—a useful simplification that breaks down in complex, real-time interactions. The underlying reality is not a clean sequence but a messy, interleaved web of perceptual, cognitive, and motor cycles operating on different timescales.75

To resolve this tension, we propose that future research must shift the planner's core representation away from a sequence of actions and towards an ontology based on **events and constraints**.20 In this view, a plan is not a sequence

A \-\> B \-\> C. Instead, it is a set of desired goal events (e.g., (event-achieved (door-is-open) at T1), (event-achieved (robot-at-location-X) at T2)) and a network of temporal and logical constraints between them (e.g., T2 \> T1). The planner's job is not to find a single path, but to find a set of actions that, when executed by the reactive layer, will most likely satisfy this network of event-based constraints, regardless of the precise timing of asynchronous perceptual feedback. This aligns with the principles of constraint-based temporal planning and provides a more robust and realistic foundation for reasoning in dynamic worlds.77

### **The Impermanence of Meaning: A Framework for Symbol Drift Control**

The final and perhaps most profound challenge for a long-term autonomous system is the **impermanence of meaning**. In a system designed for lifelong learning, where models are continuously updated with new data, the grounding of its symbols will inevitably change over time.80 The MGB's neural network classifier for the predicate

(is-hot) might be retrained on a new dataset of thermal images, causing its internal decision boundary to shift. The symbol (is-hot) still exists, but its meaning—its connection to the physical world—has drifted.

This **symbol drift** is a conceptual cousin to "model drift" in MLOps, where a model's performance degrades over time as the real-world data distribution changes 82, and to the semantic drift of words in natural language, where meanings evolve over centuries (e.g., "awful" changing from "full of awe" to "terrible").83 For a robotic system, the consequences can be severe. A plan that was verified as safe with version 1.0 of the

(is-hot) symbol might become dangerous when version 1.1 is deployed, if the new version has a higher temperature threshold.

Controlling symbol drift is paramount for ensuring long-term safety and reliability. We propose a **Framework for Symbol Drift Control** that treats the system's symbolic vocabulary as a managed software artifact:

1. **Symbol Versioning:** Every predicate and operator in the symbolic system is versioned. When a component of the MGB (e.g., a classifier) is retrained and updated, the versions of all symbols whose grounding depends on that component are automatically incremented.  
2. **Drift Monitoring and Detection:** The system continuously monitors the statistical distribution of the perceptual features associated with each grounded symbol. An automated process detects significant shifts in these distributions, flagging a potential drift event. This is directly analogous to monitoring for data distribution shifts in production machine learning systems.82  
3. **Automated Plan Re-validation:** A drift alert for a symbol p acts as a trigger. The system automatically retrieves all stored plans and policies that rely on p as a precondition, effect, or goal, and submits them for re-validation against the new, versioned symbol grounding. Plans that are no longer provably safe or valid are quarantined.  
4. **Human-in-the-Loop Governance:** For safety-critical symbols (e.g., (is-safe-to-approach), (is-human)), any significant, detected drift requires explicit confirmation from a human operator before the new symbol grounding is deployed into the live system. This provides a crucial layer of oversight for the most sensitive aspects of the robot's knowledge.82

This framework leads to a final, powerful conclusion about the nature of our proposed architecture. The symbolic component—the set of predicates and operators—is not a static, predefined logic handed down by a programmer. It is a **dynamic, evolving language**. Its vocabulary is born from perception, its syntax is governed by PDDL, and its semantics are continuously being learned, refined, and renegotiated through the agent's ongoing interaction with the physical world. A truly adaptive, long-term autonomous system cannot be built upon a fixed logic; it must be built upon a logic that has the capacity to learn and adapt its own meaning over time. The ultimate goal is not just to bridge the gap between symbols and the world, but to create a system where the symbols and the world are locked in a perpetual, co-evolving dance.
