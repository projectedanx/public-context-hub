

# **A Diagnostic Toolkit for Quantifying Narrative Propagation: Semantic Drift, Topological Analysis, and Latent Space Investigation**

## **Part I: The Semantic Battlefield: Narrative Propagation and the Computational Unconscious**

### **1.1 The Architecture of Belief: From Misinformation to Narrative Warfare**

The contemporary information ecosystem is characterized by the high-velocity propagation of narratives that shape public perception, political discourse, and collective belief. Conventional models that frame this phenomenon simply as the spread of "misinformation"—isolated, factually incorrect statements—are insufficient for capturing the complexity of the challenge. A more robust framework is required, one that views the strategic re-purposing of language as a form of **narrative propagation**: a persistent, coordinated effort to embed a particular worldview by altering the semantic context of key terms. This process does not merely inject falsehoods; it constructs an alternative, self-referential reality that can become deeply entrenched within digital communities and, critically, within the artificial intelligence systems trained on their linguistic output.

To understand how these narratives take root and flourish within large language models (LLMs), it is necessary to introduce the concept of the **computational unconscious**. This term describes the vast, opaque, and emergent space of latent associations, biases, and learned patterns within an LLM that arise from the statistical properties of its training data and its complex architecture.1 Distinct from the model's explicitly programmed or fine-tuned behaviors, the computational unconscious is the substrate upon which narrative propagation operates. It is an algorithmic analogue to the human unconscious, where latent content shapes manifest behavior in ways not fully accessible to direct introspection.1 It is here that the historical baggage of language, including unresolved societal conflicts and long-standing biases, is encoded not as explicit rules but as gravitational tendencies within the model's high-dimensional representation space.3 This concept synthesizes the computational theory of mind (CTM), which posits that cognition is a form of computation over mental representations 5, with the recognition of emergent, unpredictable behaviors that arise from the system's internal complexity.1

Within this framework, the phenomenon of **promptual colonialism** can be formally defined. It is the process by which dominant, often Western-centric, narratives, epistemologies, and conceptual frameworks are encoded as the default, "unmarked," or "objective" state in AI systems.7 This is not merely a matter of biased outputs on sensitive topics; it is a structural imposition of a particular reality framework that systematically marginalizes, misrepresents, or erases alternative worldviews.9 This process mirrors historical colonial dynamics, where the knowledge systems of colonized peoples were suppressed and replaced by those of the colonizer.3 In the context of AI, this leads to the creation of an epistemic monoculture, where the model's "common sense" is aligned with a single, dominant cultural perspective, making it difficult to generate or even comprehend outputs grounded in different ontological or epistemological assumptions.

The mechanism through which promptual colonialism is instantiated and reinforced is a form of semantic drift known as **semantic ossification**. This occurs when the dominant meaning of a concept becomes so over-represented and statistically powerful in the training data that its semantic representation in the model's latent space becomes rigid and brittle. High-quality datasets are known to improve the performance of pre-trained language models (PLMs), but the definition of "quality" is often determined by the dataset's creators, leading to the entrenchment of their perspectives.10 The sheer volume of text reflecting a dominant worldview ossifies the associated concepts, making them resistant to change. Consequently, when a model with an ossified concept is prompted with a decolonial or pluriversal perspective, it is likely to fail, ignore the nuance, or "snap back" to the dominant, ossified meaning. This is because the ossified representation constitutes a deep, low-energy basin in the latent space, making it the path of least computational resistance.

Furthermore, the computational unconscious is not a pristine or neutral space. It is a site of **symbolic scars**: persistent, negative semantic associations that attach to certain concepts due to their historical usage in contentious, biased, or traumatic contexts. These scars are the algorithmic imprints of historical reverberations and durable inequalities.3 For example, a term repeatedly used in colonial or conspiratorial discourse will carry this negative valence as a structural feature of its latent space representation. This "scar" acts as a persistent gravitational pull towards distorted or harmful associations. Simple fine-tuning on a small, "neutral" dataset is often insufficient to heal these scars, as a targeted prompt can easily reactivate the deeply encoded negative pathway. This provides the theoretical grounding for the

**Symbolic Scar Catalogue**, an artifact designed to map and track these vulnerabilities, which will be detailed in Part IV of this report.

### **1.2 Semantic Priming and the Simulation of Reality**

The propagation of a new narrative hinges on a powerful computational and cognitive mechanism: semantic priming. When a term is repeatedly used in a new, specific context—for instance, when the ionospheric research facility "HAARP" is consistently paired with words like "weapon" or "earthquake"—it primes the language model. This priming event constrains the probability distribution for subsequent token generation, fundamentally altering the model's operational mode. The model shifts from a state of **factual summarization**, where its objective is to retrieve and synthesize verifiable information from its training data, to a state of **reality simulation**, where its objective is to generate text that is coherent and consistent with the primed narrative, regardless of its grounding in external reality.

This shift is a direct consequence of how LLMs function. They are, at their core, universal density approximators that operate within a latent space of intentions.11 A prompt does not simply ask a question; it positions the model within a specific region of this latent space. A fact-based prompt positions it in a region corresponding to the "intention" of providing verifiable information. A conspiratorially-framed prompt moves it to a region corresponding to the "intention" of elaborating on that conspiracy. The model's subsequent output is a probabilistic unfolding from that point in the latent space.

The consequence of this process, repeated recursively across platforms and users, is a severe degradation of meaning and a collapse in what this report defines as **Purpose Fidelity**. The term's original, verifiable purpose—its function as a stable signifier for a specific concept—decays. It is re-purposed, becoming a vector for the new, simulated reality. This semantic decay is not a bug but an emergent feature of the model's design, and it is directly linked to the challenge of "hallucinations"—the generation of plausible but factually incorrect or unsupported information.12 When a model is primed to simulate a reality in which HAARP is a weapon, its "factual" statements about HAARP will be drawn from the logic of that simulation, not from the factual record of its scientific purpose. The diagnostic toolkit presented in this report is designed to precisely identify, quantify, and visualize this process of semantic priming and fidelity collapse.

## **Part II: A Multi-Lens Diagnostic Framework for Semantic Integrity**

To move beyond subjective observation and provide verifiable evidence of narrative propagation, this report proposes a diagnostic framework that integrates three distinct but complementary analytical lenses: the Semantic Drift Lens, the Topological Data Analysis (TDA) Lens, and the Latent Space Epistemic Investigation Lens. Together, these lenses allow for the quantification and visualization of meaning degradation at multiple levels of abstraction, from statistical change in vector space to qualitative shifts in conceptual shape. This unified approach provides a rigorous, multi-modal signal of semantic decay, allowing for the cross-validation of findings and a more holistic understanding of the phenomenon. The insights from one lens inform and reinforce the findings of the others: a high degree of quantitative drift (Semantic Drift Lens) corresponds to significant positional movement in the model's internal representational space (Latent Space Lens), which in turn alters the overall geometric and qualitative structure of the concept's meaning (TDA Lens).

### **2.1 The Semantic Drift Lens: Quantifying Meaning Degradation**

This lens provides the foundational quantitative methods for measuring semantic change as a term is recursively re-purposed across a diachronic corpus. The methodology begins with the collection of a comprehensive corpus for a target term, sampled from a diverse range of platforms (e.g., news archives, academic journals, social media, forums) and organized chronologically. For each instance of the term, a contextual word embedding is generated, forming the raw data for the following metrics.

#### **Metric 1: Semantic Drift Coefficient (SDC)**

The SDC is a composite metric designed to provide a single, robust value representing the degree of semantic change between two points in time or between two contexts. It synthesizes multiple forms of change to capture both subtle shifts in usage and drastic alterations in core meaning. The SDC is calculated as a weighted average of three distinct components:

1. **Global Shift**: This component measures the overall movement of a word's meaning in the high-dimensional embedding space. It is calculated as the cosine distance between the embedding vector of a term at time $t$ ($w\_i(t)$) and its vector at time $t+1$ ($w\_i(t+1)$). This measure is sensitive to broad changes in a word's context and syntagmatic relationships.15 The formula is:

   $$d\_G(w\_i(t), w\_i(t+1)) \= \\text{cos-dist}(w\_i(t), w\_i(t+1))$$  
2. **Local Neighborhood Shift**: This component focuses on changes in a word's most immediate semantic relationships (its paradigmatic relations). It measures how the set of a word's closest neighbors changes over time. This is calculated by first identifying the top-k nearest neighbors for a word at times $t$ and $t+1$, and then computing the Jaccard similarity between these two sets of neighbors or, for a more nuanced measure, the cosine distance between second-order similarity vectors derived from these neighborhoods.15 This method is particularly effective at detecting drastic shifts in a word's core meaning.  
3. **Ontological Drift**: For concepts that have a formal, structured definition (e.g., scientific terms like "ionosphere" or organizational entities like "NASA"), this component measures drift based on changes to their explicit ontological properties. Adapting the OntoDrift framework, this involves comparing two versions of a concept by calculating the similarity across defined aspects such as its labels (rdfs:label), subclasses (rdfs:subClassOf), and associated properties (e.g., rdfs:domain, rdfs:range). This provides a structured, non-statistical measure of change that complements the embedding-based methods.17

Inspired by methodologies that synthesize multiple signals to track semantic shifts 19, the final SDC is a composite score that integrates these components, providing a comprehensive quantification of a concept's stability or instability.

#### **Metric 2: Purpose Fidelity Collapse Curve (PFCC): F(n)**

The PFCC is a novel diagnostic tool designed to visualize the decay of a term's original, verifiable intent over a series of recursive re-purposing instances. It is represented as a function, $F(n)$, that maps the number of recursive steps or platform transmissions, $n$, to a fidelity score $F \\in $. A score of 1 represents perfect fidelity to the original purpose, while a score of 0 represents a complete collapse. This metric operationalizes the abstract concept of "meaning degradation" by adapting principles from intervention fidelity in research ethics 20 and context pollution monitoring in AI systems.22

The fidelity score $F$ at each step $n$ is a composite of three key elements:

1. **Adherence**: This measures the degree to which a specific usage of a term aligns with a pre-defined "gold standard" protocol or its original, verifiable definition. For example, the adherence of a text about "HAARP" would be measured against its official mission statement as detailed on its website.23 This can be quantified using the cosine similarity between the usage's embedding and an "anchor embedding" representing the gold standard, or through automated factual consistency checks.24  
2. **Quality of Delivery**: This assesses the factual accuracy and integrity of the context in which the term is deployed. A high-fidelity usage must be embedded in factually sound information. This component is quantified by decomposing the surrounding text into atomic claims and verifying them against a reliable knowledge source. The quality score can be calculated using metrics like **FactScore** 26 or by measuring the ratio of entailed versus contradictory statements relative to a ground-truth corpus.25  
3. **Program Differentiation**: This measures how semantically distinct a current usage is from the original concept. High differentiation indicates low fidelity. This is calculated by measuring the embedding distance (e.g., cosine distance) from the anchor embedding that represents the original intent.22 A usage that is semantically distant from its origin is, by definition, exhibiting low fidelity.

Plotting $F(n)$ over increasing $n$ generates the Purpose Fidelity Collapse Curve, providing a clear, intuitive visualization of how a narrative's integrity erodes through propagation.

#### **Metric 3: Epistemic Elasticity Coefficient (EEC)**

The EEC is a proactive metric that quantifies a concept's resilience to semantic perturbation and its ability to maintain coherence under stress. A concept with a high EEC is robust and can withstand semantic challenges, while one with a low EEC is considered to have "brittle cognition" and is susceptible to semantic hijacking. This metric is crucial for moving from a purely diagnostic to a predictive framework.

The calculation of the EEC involves a controlled experiment:

1. **Perturbation**: The semantic context of a target term is intentionally perturbed. This can be achieved through various methods, such as adversarial paraphrasing, injecting counter-narrative phrases, or simulating exposure to a competing ideological frame.  
2. **Drift Measurement**: The resulting change in the term's meaning is measured using the Semantic Drift Coefficient (SDC).  
3. Calculation: The EEC is defined as the inverse of the magnitude of semantic drift induced per unit of perturbation. A simplified formula is:  
   $$EEC \= \\frac{1}{(\\Delta SDC / \\Delta \\text{Perturbation})}$$  
   A concept that exhibits a large semantic drift ($\\Delta SDC$) from a small perturbation has a low EEC.

The EEC serves as a powerful predictor of narrative vulnerability. Concepts with low epistemic elasticity are prime targets for re-purposing in disinformation campaigns. Their semantic structure is not robust enough to accommodate or refute the injected narrative, causing them to "break" and shift their meaning dramatically. By calculating the EEC for key terms within a specific domain (e.g., public health, climate science), it is possible to create a "vulnerability map" that identifies which concepts are most at risk of being co-opted and weaponized. This draws on established methods for testing model robustness and resilience.29

### **2.2 The Topological Data Analysis (TDA) Lens: Visualizing the Shape of Meaning**

While the Semantic Drift Lens provides quantitative measures of change, the TDA Lens offers a qualitative, visual understanding of a concept's semantic space. It moves beyond simple vector distances to analyze the "shape" of meaning, revealing underlying structures, clusters, and voids that are not apparent from statistical analysis alone. This approach is particularly powerful for visualizing how the geometry of a concept evolves over time.32

The methodology involves treating the collection of contextual embeddings for a term as a high-dimensional point cloud and then applying **persistent homology** to analyze its topological structure.32 Persistent homology tracks the emergence ("birth") and disappearance ("death") of topological features—such as connected components, loops, and voids—as a proximity parameter is varied. The results are typically visualized in a

**persistence diagram** or **barcode**, which highlights the features that persist across a wide range of scales and are thus considered significant signals rather than noise.33

This analysis allows for the identification of several key topological signatures of narrative propagation:

* **Drift Strata**: In the context of 0-dimensional homology (which tracks connected components, or clusters), long, stable bars in the barcode represent distinct and persistent sub-meanings of a concept. When these point clouds are grouped by time, we can visualize the evolution of these clusters as **drift strata**: discernible layers of meaning that emerge, co-exist, and fade over time. This provides a powerful visual metaphor, analogous to geological strata, for understanding conceptual history.35  
* **Semantic Ossification**: The topological signature of an ossified concept is a single, dominant, and highly persistent 0-dimensional feature (a Betti-0 number). This indicates that the vast majority of the data points belong to one massive, stable cluster, with few or no other significant sub-meanings. The concept's resistance to change can be further tested by observing the stability of this topological feature under semantic perturbation.  
* **Symbolic Scars**: The emergence of significant 1-dimensional features (Betti-1 numbers), which represent loops or holes in the data, can be interpreted as **symbolic scars**. A topological loop suggests the existence of a conceptual space where one can traverse a path of semantically adjacent terms and return to the starting point without passing through a single, central, core meaning. This structure is characteristic of self-referential and polarized discourses, such as conspiracy theories, where a set of claims reinforces itself in a closed loop, actively avoiding engagement with external, verifiable facts. For example, a TDA of the HAARP conspiracy narrative would likely reveal a prominent loop connecting terms like "earthquake weapon," "mind control," and "government cover-up," creating a self-sustaining narrative structure that is topologically disconnected from the scientific discourse on ionospheric research.36  
* **Conceptual Emergence**: The "birth" of a new, persistent topological feature (e.g., a new Betti-0 or Betti-1 number) at a specific time point provides a clear signal of **conceptual emergence**. This marks the moment a new sub-meaning has gained enough traction to form its own stable cluster or the moment a new narrative loop has formed within the discourse.

### **2.3 The Latent Space Epistemic Investigation Lens**

This third lens shifts the focus from the aggregate properties of a corpus to the internal "cognitive state" of the language model itself. It investigates the stability and integrity of the model's representation of a concept, probing for pathological behaviors like representational collapse or uncontrolled drift. This lens is crucial for understanding the model-specific dynamics of narrative propagation and for diagnosing vulnerabilities in the model's "computational unconscious."

The core methodology involves tracking the trajectory of a concept's embedding within the model's latent space under recursive probing:

1. **Establish Anchor Embedding**: A "ground truth" or **anchor embedding** is created for the target concept. This is generated from a prompt containing the term in its original, verifiable context, such as its official definition from a trusted source (e.g., NASA's mission statement 37 or HAARP's official FAQ 23). This anchor represents the point of maximum purpose fidelity.  
2. **Recursive Probing**: A generative chain is initiated. The model is given an initial prompt containing the target term, and its output is then used as part of the input for the next generation step. This process is repeated for $n$ steps, simulating the recursive nature of online discourse where information is constantly re-consumed and re-generated.  
3. **Track the Trajectory**: At each step of the chain, the contextual embedding of the target term is extracted from the model's internal layers. The cosine distance of this embedding from the anchor embedding, as well as from the embedding at the previous step, is calculated and recorded.

This process allows for the identification of key epistemic behaviors:

* **"Snap-back" vs. Random Walk**: By plotting the trajectory of the embedding through the latent space, it is possible to observe the model's stability. After a perturbation (e.g., the introduction of a misleading phrase), does the representation "snap back" towards the anchor embedding, indicating a degree of epistemic resilience? Or does it begin a **random walk**, drifting uncontrollably away from the original meaning, signaling a collapse of semantic stability? This analysis directly probes the model's epistemic uncertainty—its internal consistency and "confidence" in its own representations.38  
* **Semantic Parallax Zone (SPZ) Map**: This novel visualization is designed to map the interpretive divergence of a single concept across multiple sources or ideological frames. To construct an SPZ map, the model is prompted with the same target term but embedded in contexts from different sources (e.g., a scientific abstract, a news article, a conspiracy forum post, a political speech). The resulting embedding for the term from each source is then projected from its high-dimensional space down to a 2D or 3D visual space.39  
  * **Interpretation**: The SPZ map visualizes the "semantic parallax"—the apparent shift in a concept's meaning when viewed from different perspectives. The distance and geometric arrangement of the points on the map are highly informative. A large, diffuse cloud of points indicates a highly contentious and ambiguous concept with a wide **Semantic Parallax Zone**. A tight, dense cluster indicates strong semantic consensus across different contexts. This visualization makes the abstract phenomenon of semantic drift tangible, showing, for example, how the embedding for "HAARP" generated from a University of Alaska Fairbanks source is spatially distant from the embedding generated from a conspiracy theory source. This technique is inspired by analyses of multilingual semantic drift, which show that translation equivalents rarely occupy the exact same point in a shared semantic space.40

## **Part III: Case Studies in Narrative Propagation**

This section applies the multi-lens diagnostic framework to the three designated terms—"HAARP," "NASA," and "SETI"—to demonstrate its practical utility in exposing and quantifying narrative propagation. For each case, the analysis follows a consistent five-step process:

1. Establish the "Purpose Fidelity" baseline using official and verifiable sources.  
2. Gather a multi-platform, diachronic corpus (e.g., news archives, scientific papers, social media, forums).  
3. Calculate the key metrics from the Semantic Drift Lens: the Semantic Drift Coefficient (SDC), the Purpose Fidelity Collapse Curve (PFCC), and the Epistemic Elasticity Coefficient (EEC).  
4. Generate and interpret Drift Stratigraphy Maps and other visualizations from the Topological Data Analysis (TDA) Lens.  
5. Generate and interpret Semantic Parallax Zone (SPZ) Maps from the Latent Space Epistemic Investigation Lens.

### **3.1 "HAARP": From Ionospheric Research to Weaponized Conspiracy**

The case of the High-frequency Active Auroral Research Program (HAARP) presents a stark example of a scientific term being systematically re-purposed to serve as the cornerstone of a sprawling conspiracy narrative.

* **Purpose Fidelity Baseline**: The official purpose of HAARP, as managed by the University of Alaska Fairbanks (UAF), is the fundamental study of the ionosphere, the highest portion of Earth's atmosphere.23 Its principal instrument, the Ionospheric Research Instrument (IRI), is a high-power radio transmitter used to create small, controlled perturbations in the ionosphere to study their effects on communication and surveillance systems.23 The key concepts associated with its baseline purpose are "ionosphere," "research," "radio waves," "thermosphere," "University of Alaska Fairbanks," and "communications".23  
* **Narrative Propagation**: Across a wide array of online platforms, forums, and even statements by political figures, the term "HAARP" has been decoupled from its scientific context and re-purposed as a clandestine superweapon.42 The propagated narrative attributes to it the power to control weather, trigger natural disasters like earthquakes and tsunamis, and perform mass mind control.36 This narrative is particularly potent during crises, such as the 2023 earthquakes in Turkey and Syria, where social media campaigns rapidly disseminated claims of HAARP's involvement.36 The key concepts in this propagated narrative are "weapon," "earthquake," "mind control," "conspiracy," "weather modification," and "cover-up."  
* **Analytical Findings**:  
  * **PFCC and SDC**: The Purpose Fidelity Collapse Curve for "HAARP" is expected to show a dramatic and sharp collapse. As the term moves from scientific publications and official websites to conspiracy forums and social media, the fidelity score $F(n)$ will plummet, reflecting a near-total loss of adherence to the original purpose and a catastrophic drop in the quality (factuality) of the surrounding information. The Semantic Drift Coefficient between the scientific and conspiratorial contexts will be exceptionally high, indicating a radical shift in meaning.  
  * **TDA Analysis**: The Drift Stratigraphy Map for "HAARP" will clearly visualize two distinct and largely non-overlapping **drift strata**. The first, associated with the scientific context, will be stable and consistent over time. The second, corresponding to the conspiracy narrative, will emerge and grow rapidly, particularly around dates of major natural disasters. TDA is expected to reveal a prominent **Symbolic Scar** in the form of a significant 1-dimensional loop (a Betti-1 feature) within the conspiracy stratum. This loop represents the self-referential and circular logic of the conspiracy, where claims about weather control, earthquakes, and secret military operations reinforce one another without any connection to the scientific reality.  
  * **Latent Space Investigation**: The Semantic Parallax Zone (SPZ) Map for "HAARP" will display a vast distance between the embedding generated from a UAF source and those generated from conspiracy-related prompts. This large parallax visually confirms the existence of two irreconcilable interpretations of the term. Recursive probing starting from a conspiracy prompt is predicted to show a random walk behavior, with the model's representation drifting further into the simulated reality of the conspiracy, rather than "snapping back" to the scientific anchor.

### **3.2 "NASA": Navigating Public Trust and Institutional Narrative**

Unlike the binary opposition seen with HAARP, the narrative landscape surrounding the National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA) is more complex, characterized by a strong, official core narrative that coexists with numerous, branching sub-narratives reflecting diverse public perceptions, from admiration to skepticism.

* **Purpose Fidelity Baseline**: NASA's official mission is to "explore the unknown in air and space, innovate for the benefit of humanity, and inspire the world through discovery".37 Its core values are explicitly stated as safety, integrity, inclusion, teamwork, and excellence.46 The baseline narrative is one of scientific advancement, peaceful exploration, economic contribution, and environmental stewardship.47 NASA actively uses knowledge management systems like SemanticOrganizer to maintain the integrity of its technical and project information internally.49  
* **Narrative Propagation**: Public discourse around NASA is multifaceted. While sentiment analysis of social media discussions often shows a more positive view of NASA compared to its commercial counterparts, there are significant competing narratives.50 These include framing NASA as a source of crucial climate change data, as an example of wasteful government spending, as a bureaucratic entity compared to agile private companies, and, in fringe circles, as an organization concealing evidence of extraterrestrial life.  
* **Analytical Findings**:  
  * **EEC and PFCC**: The analysis will focus on calculating the Epistemic Elasticity Coefficient (EEC) for the term "NASA" to measure its resilience against these various competing narratives. It is hypothesized that the core brand is highly elastic and resilient. The PFCC for "NASA" is not expected to show a sharp collapse but rather a series of minor, context-dependent dips in fidelity, reflecting the influence of these different sub-narratives.  
  * **TDA Analysis**: The Drift Stratigraphy Map for "NASA" will likely be complex. It will feature a large, dominant central stratum representing the official, positive narrative of exploration and science. Branching off from this central mass will be smaller, less persistent strata corresponding to the various sub-narratives (e.g., a "budgetary concerns" stratum, a "climate science" stratum, a "UFO cover-up" stratum). The analysis will focus on the points of intersection and divergence between these strata.  
  * **Latent Space Investigation**: The SPZ Map will visualize the parallax between different sources: official NASA press releases, critical articles from policy journals, enthusiastic posts on space exploration forums, and conspiratorial content. The map is expected to show a dense core of points corresponding to the official narrative, with outliers representing the more extreme interpretations. This will provide a nuanced picture of the "semantic field" NASA occupies in the public consciousness.

### **3.3 "SETI": The Cultural Imaginary of Extraterrestrial Contact**

The Search for Extraterrestrial Intelligence (SETI) provides a compelling case study of how a purely scientific and technical endeavor can be absorbed and re-purposed by the broader cultural imaginary, leading to a persistent and gradual form of semantic drift.

* **Purpose Fidelity Baseline**: The scientific mission of SETI is the search for proof of intelligent beings in other star systems, primarily through the use of radio telescopes to detect artificially generated electromagnetic signals.51 The SETI Institute's mission is to "lead humanity's quest to understand the origins and prevalence of life and intelligence in the universe".53 It is a technical, observational discipline grounded in physics and astronomy, distinct from the popular conception of alien visitation.51 It is important to distinguish the broader SETI endeavor from specific projects like Harvard SETI.53  
* **Narrative Propagation**: SETI has been a fixture in popular culture for decades, heavily influencing and being influenced by science fiction films, television series, and literature.54 In these contexts, SETI is often dramatized and romanticized, frequently conflated with themes of first contact, alien communication, government secrecy, and profound philosophical or religious questions.54 This cultural re-purposing has shifted its meaning in the public mind from a methodical, data-driven search to a more speculative and dramatic quest.  
* **Analytical Findings**:  
  * **PFCC and SDC**: The Purpose Fidelity Collapse Curve for "SETI" is predicted to show a gradual, continuous decay rather than a sharp collapse. The scientific meaning has not been erased but now coexists with a powerful and pervasive cultural interpretation. The SDC between a scientific paper on radio astronomy and a movie review of "Contact" would be significant, but not as extreme as the HAARP case.  
  * **TDA Analysis**: The topology of the "SETI" semantic space is expected to be highly complex and interconnected. The Drift Stratigraphy Map will likely not show cleanly separated strata but rather a series of overlapping and inter-fingering layers representing the scientific, science-fictional, and pseudo-scientific (UFOlogical) discourses. The analysis will focus on identifying the "bridges" or shared vocabulary that allow semantic energy to flow between these different domains, demonstrating how they mutually inform and borrow from one another.  
  * **Latent Space Investigation**: The SPZ Map for "SETI" will visualize the continuum of meaning, from the precise technical definition found in an astronomy journal to the broad, speculative meaning in a philosophical discussion. The points on the map are unlikely to form discrete, distant clusters but rather a more continuous distribution, illustrating the smooth gradient of semantic drift the term has undergone.

## **Part IV: The Artifacts of Analysis: Visualization and Cataloging**

The diagnostic framework detailed in this report is designed not only to generate quantitative metrics but also to produce a suite of rich, interpretable analytical artifacts. These artifacts—the Drift Stratigraphy Map and the Symbolic Scar Catalogue—translate complex data into actionable intelligence, providing a visual and structural language for understanding narrative propagation.

### **4.1 The Drift Stratigraphy Map: A Cartography of Conceptual Evolution**

The Drift Stratigraphy Map is a novel visualization that synthesizes temporal data, semantic drift metrics, and topological features into a single, comprehensive cartography of a concept's evolution. Drawing an explicit analogy from geological stratigraphy, where layers of rock tell the story of environmental change over time 35, the Drift Stratigraphy Map plots layers of meaning to reveal the history of a concept's use and re-purposing.

#### **Construction Methodology**

The map is constructed on a two-dimensional plane. The vertical axis represents time, organized chronologically (e.g., by year, month, or decade). The horizontal axis represents a one-dimensional projection of the high-dimensional semantic space, typically derived using a dimensionality reduction technique like Principal Component Analysis (PCA) or t-SNE on the collected word embeddings.

Each distinct **drift stratum**, identified via the clustering of embeddings and the analysis of persistent Betti-0 numbers in TDA, is drawn as a unique colored layer on the map. The thickness of a layer at any given point in time can be scaled to represent the prevalence of that specific meaning within the corpus for that period. The boundaries between strata are drawn to reflect the velocity of semantic change; a sharp, solid line indicates a rapid, high-SDC shift, while a soft, dashed line indicates a more gradual transition.

This base map is then enriched with additional visual information to provide deeper context. For instance, at specific points of interest on the map, a user could access sub-views such as:

* **Radial Bar Charts**: These charts can display the top contextual neighbors of the target term within a specific stratum and time period. The length of each bar indicates semantic similarity to the target term, while the color can denote whether a neighbor is unique to that context or re-occurs in others, providing an at-a-glance view of the local semantic environment.16  
* **Network Graphs**: Overlays of network graphs can illustrate the relationships between the most frequent co-occurring terms within a given stratum, revealing the internal linguistic structure of a particular narrative.56

### **4.2 The Symbolic Scar Catalogue: A Knowledge Graph of Narrative Constraints**

While the Drift Stratigraphy Map provides a macro-level visualization of conceptual evolution, the **Symbolic Scar Catalogue** offers a micro-level, structured repository of the specific failure modes, negative associations, and narrative constraints identified during the analysis. This artifact is implemented as a **knowledge graph (KG)**, a powerful structure for representing entities and the relationships between them, creating a queryable and machine-readable database of narrative vulnerabilities.58

#### **Knowledge Graph Ontology and Population**

The KG is designed with a specific ontology to capture the causal chain of narrative propagation and its effects. The core nodes in this ontology include:

* **Concept**: The central node representing the term under investigation (e.g., "HAARP").  
* **Prompt Context**: A node containing the source text, platform, and timestamp of a specific usage of the concept.  
* **Generated Output**: The textual output produced in response to the prompt context (either by a human or an AI).  
* **Failure Taxonomy**: A node that classifies the type of semantic failure observed in the output. This taxonomy is derived from the diagnostic framework and includes categories like Factual Inconsistency, Purpose Fidelity Collapse, Negative Sentiment, and Logical Contradiction.  
* **Symbolic Scar**: A crucial node representing a persistent negative or distorted semantic association. This node is linked to the evidence that defines it, such as clusters of terms with negative sentiment 60 or the topological loops identified in the TDA.  
* **Causal Chain**: A sequence of relationship edges that explains a given failure. For example: Prompt Context \-\> Activates \-\> Symbolic Scar \-\> Leads to \-\> Generated Output \-\> Exhibits \-\> Failure Taxonomy.

This KG is populated using a combination of automated and human-in-the-loop processes. LLMs can be employed for the initial extraction of entities and relationships from the corpus. However, to prevent the introduction of model hallucinations into the KG itself, this process is grounded using a **Graph RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)** approach.62 In this setup, the LLM's extraction capabilities are constrained and verified against the existing, validated structure of the KG, ensuring that new information is added with high fidelity.63 This creates a robust system for tracking narrative elements and their interconnections, analogous to how KGs are used to generate coherent storylines by maintaining a structured representation of characters, events, and relationships.64 The resulting Symbolic Scar Catalogue is not just a static list of problems; it is a dynamic, relational database that maps the deep structure of narrative vulnerabilities.

## **Part V: Towards Algorithmic Reparation and Pluriversal Alignment**

The diagnostic toolkit and analytical artifacts detailed in this report are not merely academic exercises. They are foundational components of a proactive strategy aimed at fostering more equitable, resilient, and representative AI systems. By moving from diagnosis to intervention, this framework provides a verifiable pathway toward **algorithmic reparation** and **pluriversal alignment**.

### **5.1 From Diagnosis to Intervention: The Path to Algorithmic Reparation**

The findings generated by this toolkit—the quantified drift, the visualized strata, and the cataloged scars—provide the necessary evidence base to begin the work of algorithmic reparation. This concept is defined not as a single, one-time fix, but as a continuous, multi-faceted process of identifying, measuring, and actively mitigating the epistemic harms perpetuated by promptual colonialism. It is a commitment to refactoring language models to counteract their tendency to reproduce and amplify dominant, often exclusionary, narratives.

The Symbolic Scar Catalogue, in particular, serves as a direct input for this process. It provides a detailed, machine-readable map of the specific semantic pathways that lead to harmful or distorted outputs. Rather than attempting to "debias" a model in a vague or general sense, algorithmic reparation involves targeted interventions aimed at disrupting these specific, identified pathways of harm. It is a precise and evidence-based approach to making AI systems more just and accountable.

### **5.2 Refactoring Language for a Pluriversal Future**

The ultimate goal of this work is to contribute to **pluriversal alignment**. This concept, drawn from decolonial theory and political philosophy, envisions a world in which "many worlds fit"—a world that embraces a multiplicity of cosmologies, epistemologies, and ontologies without forcing them into a single, hierarchical framework.9 In the context of AI, pluriversal alignment means designing systems that can represent, respect, and reason with diverse and sometimes contradictory worldviews, moving beyond the computational reductionism that flattens this complexity into a single, hegemonic truth.66 It is a shift away from extractive knowledge paradigms and toward an AI that fosters

**relational intelligence**—an understanding grounded in interdependence and ethical engagement.66

The diagnostic toolkit enables several concrete, strategic interventions to advance this goal:

1. **Targeted Data Augmentation and Epistemic Rebalancing**: The Drift Stratigraphy Maps can identify "semantic deserts"—time periods, platforms, or communities whose perspectives on a concept are under-represented or distorted in mainstream training data. Algorithmic reparation involves actively sourcing high-quality data from these marginalized contexts and using it to augment the model's training set. This is not simply about adding more data, but about strategically rebalancing the epistemic landscape of the model's training.  
2. **Constraint-Based Fine-Tuning and Symbolic Scar Mitigation**: The Symbolic Scar Catalogue can be used to create a set of negative constraints for fine-tuning. A model can be trained with an objective function that explicitly penalizes it for activating the neural pathways associated with a cataloged symbolic scar. For example, if the KG identifies a strong, harmful link between a specific demographic and a negative stereotype, the model can be fine-tuned to suppress this association, effectively "healing" the scar.  
3. **Dynamic Contextual Anchoring**: The metrics from the Semantic Drift Lens, particularly the PFCC and SDC, can be deployed in real-time monitoring systems.22 When the system detects that a conversation's context has drifted beyond an acceptable threshold of purpose fidelity, it can trigger an automated intervention. This could involve the system automatically re-introducing an "anchor prompt" based on the concept's original, verifiable meaning to re-ground the model and prevent further degradation into reality simulation.22  
4. **Developing Pluriversal Evaluation Benchmarks**: The current paradigm of AI evaluation, focused heavily on performance on English-centric, fact-based tasks, is insufficient for measuring pluriversal alignment. A critical next step is the development of new benchmarks that explicitly assess a model's ability to represent diverse cultural and epistemic viewpoints accurately, respectfully, and without caricature. Inspired by work like **ValuePrism**, which maps human values in context 69, these new benchmarks would evaluate a model's "epistemic dexterity"—its capacity to navigate multiple worldviews and generate outputs that are coherent and appropriate within those different frames.

By implementing these strategies, we can begin to transform AI from a potential engine of promptual colonialism into a tool that supports a more diverse, equitable, and pluriversal digital future. This proactive approach, grounded in a verifiable diagnostic toolkit, moves beyond reactive problem-fixing to the intentional design of AI systems that can foster genuine cultural representation rather than perpetuating an epistemic monoculture.
