The core pathology underlying this epistemic rupture is post-hoc rationalization.1 In this failure mode, the LLM determines a conclusion probabilistically through its opaque, high-dimensional latent space representations and subsequently fabricates a coherent—but non-causal—textual justification that mimics logical deduction. Correct final outputs are routinely preceded by fundamentally flawed or completely unfaithful reasoning traces.1 Furthermore, early attempts to measure this faithfulness relied heavily on contextual perturbations, such as removing or altering specific tokens within the prompt context and observing the resulting prediction changes.1 These contextual probes are inherently insufficient; erasing a step from the context window does not eradicate the underlying knowledge embedded within the model's parametric weights.1 Consequently, models can seamlessly reconstruct corrupted contextual information from their parameters, yielding false negative evaluations regarding the causal importance of specific reasoning steps.

The unlearning process is executed via Negative Preference Optimization combined with Kullback-Leibler divergence regularization (NPO+KL).\[5, 6\] This specific optimization strategy penalizes the model's probability of generating the forget sequences while simultaneously preserving its general fluency and capabilities across unrelated domains, ensuring that the intervention is highly specific to the targeted logic. \`\` The efficacy of this causal intervention is quantified using the Causal Perturbation Index (CPI).1

\`\` The CPI provides a singular, mathematically rigorous metric for causal faithfulness by calculating the exact magnitude of the shift in the model's prediction probability after the specific reasoning step has been parametrically erased.
