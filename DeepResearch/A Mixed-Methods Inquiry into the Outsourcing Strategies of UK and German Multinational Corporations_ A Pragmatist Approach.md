# **A Mixed-Methods Inquiry into the Outsourcing Strategies of UK and German Multinational Corporations: A Pragmatist Approach**

### **1.0 Introduction**

In an era of increasing global complexity, the strategic decisions of multinational corporations (MNCs) have profound implications for national competitiveness, supply chain resilience, and corporate performance. Outsourcing, in its various forms, has evolved from a tactical cost-cutting measure into a critical strategic tool for navigating this intricate landscape. This research proposes a timely and essential investigation into the outsourcing and offshoring strategies of leading MNCs in Europe's two largest economies: the United Kingdom and Germany. By comparing the approaches of firms rooted in these distinct institutional environments, this study seeks to illuminate the nuanced interplay of national economic models and firm-level capabilities in shaping global corporate strategy.

The core research problem stems from an intellectual "puzzle" triggered by empirical observation (Peirce, 1903). While the Varieties of Capitalism (VoC) framework predicts that UK firms, operating in a Liberal Market Economy (LME), will pursue cost-driven, arm's-length outsourcing, and German firms in a Coordinated Market Economy (CME) will favour long-term, coordinated partnerships, the empirical reality is far more complex and contradictory. We observe German engineering giants aggressively offshoring to third parties and UK airlines forming deeply embedded, long-term relationships that defy simple LME logic. These anomalies are not minor deviations; they represent a fundamental challenge to the predictive power of institutional theory in an era of globalized production and firm-level strategic agility. This study is therefore a novel inquiry designed to explain an empirical phenomenon that challenges the current state of knowledge (Mitchell, 2018).

This investigation is guided by one primary and two secondary research questions designed to systematically unpack this strategic puzzle:

* **Primary Question:** How and why do the outsourcing and offshoring strategies of leading UK and German multinational corporations diverge in practice?  
* **Secondary Questions:**  
  * In what ways do the distinct institutional frameworks of a Liberal Market Economy (UK) and a Coordinated Market Economy (Germany) shape corporate decisions regarding outsourcing location, ownership, and control?  
  * How do firm-level dynamic capabilities, as described by the Resource-Based View (RBV), interact with these institutional pressures to produce unique strategic outcomes?

The principal aim of this research is to generate a novel, empirically grounded conceptual framework that offers a superior explanation for the observed strategic differences between UK and German MNCs. To achieve this, the study will pursue three core objectives:

1. To empirically map and compare the outsourcing strategies of selected UK and German MNCs in the engineering and airline sectors.  
2. To analyze the interplay between institutional factors (VoC) and firm-specific capabilities (RBV) in shaping these strategies.  
3. To develop a set of evidence-based propositions by operationalizing an integrated cycle of abductive, deductive, and inductive reasoning.

This proposal is structured as follows: Section 2.0 synthesizes the relevant literature to build an initial theoretical framework. Section 3.0 details the pragmatist research philosophy and the cycle of inquiry that underpins the study. Section 4.0 presents the mixed-methods research design. Section 5.0 outlines the potential contributions, and Section 6.0 provides a projected timeline for completion.

To adequately address these complex questions and develop a more nuanced understanding of contemporary business strategy, it is first necessary to synthesize the key theoretical perspectives that inform this study.

### **2.0 Theoretical Framework and Literature Review**

The multifaceted nature of MNC outsourcing strategy defies explanation by any single theory. A comprehensive understanding requires a multi-theoretical perspective that integrates insights from institutional economics, strategic management, and economic geography. This section synthesizes key concepts from Varieties of Capitalism (VoC), the Resource-Based View (RBV), and Global Production Networks (GPN) to construct the initial conceptual framework that will guide the inquiry. This approach provides the essential starting point for an abductive research process (Mitchell, 2018).

#### **2.1 Varieties of Capitalism (VoC)**

The VoC framework provides the foundational institutional context for this study (Hall and Soskice, 2001). It differentiates between two ideal-typical models of developed economies. The United Kingdom is characterized as a **Liberal Market Economy (LME)**, where firms coordinate their activities primarily via hierarchies and competitive market arrangements. This model predicts corporate governance structures that prioritize shareholder value, fluid labour markets, and strategic priorities focused on cost-efficiency and short-term financial returns. In contrast, Germany represents a **Coordinated Market Economy (CME)**, where firms depend more heavily on non-market relationships to coordinate their endeavours. CMEs feature corporate governance that engages a wider group of stakeholders (including employees), labour relations that emphasize long-term employment and skill development, and strategic priorities geared towards consensus, niche production, and long-term stability.

#### **2.2 The Resource-Based View (RBV) and Dynamic Capabilities**

While VoC explains the institutional pressures on the firm, it offers a deterministic view that struggles to account for strategic innovation and firm-level agency. To understand this, we must turn to the firm-level perspective of the Resource-Based View (RBV), which offers a crucial counterpoint. The RBV posits that competitive advantage stems from a firm's unique and valuable internal resources and capabilities (Mitchell, 2018). From this perspective, outsourcing is not merely a reactive response to external institutional pressures but a proactive strategy to leverage unique assets, achieve operational agility, and build **dynamic capabilities**—the firm's ability to integrate, build, and reconfigure internal and external competences to address rapidly changing environments. This lens allows for an analysis of how firms actively shape their outsourcing strategies to enhance their competitive position, rather than being passively determined by their national context.

#### **2.3 Global Production Networks (GPN) and Embeddedness**

Finally, while RBV explains strategic choice, the concepts of Global Production Networks (GPN) and embeddedness illuminate the complex web of inter-firm relationships and socio-economic contexts that shape *how* those choices are implemented in practice. Scholars such as Gereffi have detailed the governance of GPNs, but it is Granovetter’s (1985) concept of embeddedness that is critical here, as it posits that economic transactions are suffused with social relationships, trust, and power dynamics that cannot be reduced to contractual terms. This explains why an LME firm might maintain a long-term, high-trust relationship with a supplier that appears economically suboptimal on paper, or why a CME firm might engage in an arm's-length transaction that defies its institutional logic. This perspective is vital for understanding the nuances of how UK and German firms manage their offshore and outsourced operations, highlighting that these decisions are not made in a social vacuum.

#### **2.4 An Initial Conceptual Framework**

Synthesizing these theoretical perspectives allows for the construction of a predictive taxonomy that outlines the expected strategic approaches for UK and German firms. This framework represents the "(0) prior theoretical knowledge" in the abductive research process (Kovács & Spens, 2005\) and serves as the initial benchmark against which empirical findings will be compared.

| Dimension of Strategy | Theoretical Underpinnings | Predicted UK (LME) Approach | Predicted German (CME) Approach |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **1\. Motivation** | VoC, RBV | Primarily cost-driven; outsource non-core activities. | Driven by expertise and cost; retain ownership in key markets. |
| **2\. Ownership** | VoC, RBV/DC | Preference for outsourcing to third parties. | Preference for offshoring but retaining ownership (subsidiaries). |
| **3\. Control/Coordination** | VoC, GPN | Loose control; significant role for procurement and contract management. | Tight control from headquarters, relaxing over time with trust. |
| **4\. Cultural Proximity** | VoC, GPN | Less sensitive to cultural differences; preference for offshore to locations like China and India. | Highly sensitive to cultural proximity; preference for near-shoring. |

While this synthesized framework is robust, it is a theoretical starting point. The potential for empirical reality to deviate from these predictions—creating the "surprising facts" that spark new insight—necessitates a research methodology that is not merely confirmatory. The study requires a design that is flexible, generative, and capable of producing novel explanations for the complex phenomena under investigation.

### **3.0 Research Philosophy and Methodology**

The selection of a research philosophy is a foundational decision that shapes the entire inquiry. The so-called "paradigm wars" between purely quantitative (positivist) and qualitative (interpretivist) approaches have proven unproductive for investigating complex, real-world business problems (Mitchell, 2018). Such challenges require a more flexible and problem-centred approach. Therefore, this research adopts **pragmatism** as its philosophical foundation, arguing it is the most suitable for a study focused on generating practical insights and useful knowledge about corporate strategy.

#### **3.1 Pragmatism as a Guiding Philosophy**

Pragmatism offers an attractive philosophical partner for mixed-methods research by sidestepping ontological and epistemological dogmatism in favour of a focus on "what works" (Tashakkori & Teddlie, 2010). Its core tenets include a commitment to methodological pluralism, an emphasis on the practical consequences of ideas, and the view that knowledge is produced through a continuous process of inquiry. By supporting the use of different research methods, pragmatism provides a coherent rationale for combining qualitative and quantitative techniques to gain a superior understanding of the research problem (Mitchell, 2018).

#### **3.2 The Integrated Cycle of Inquiry**

This pragmatist philosophy is operationalized through a unique cycle of inquiry that integrates three modes of reasoning: abduction, deduction, and induction.

##### **3.2.1 Limitations of Deductive and Inductive Reasoning**

A research design relying solely on one mode of reasoning would be inadequate. A purely deductive approach, which tests a pre-selected theory, is constrained by a "lack of clarity in terms of how to select the theory to be tested" from among many alternatives (Mitchell, 2018). Conversely, a purely inductive approach, which aims to build theory from data, faces the challenge that "no amount of empirical data will necessarily enable theory-building" (Mitchell, 2018). Neither approach, on its own, provides a complete logic for scientific discovery and justification.

##### **3.2.2 Abductive Reasoning as the Engine of Discovery**

This research champions **abductive reasoning** as the primary engine of discovery. Defined as "inference to the best explanation" (Psillos, 2000), abduction is, in the words of its originator, "the only logical operation which introduces any new idea" (Peirce, 1903). The process begins not with a theory to test or data to generalize, but with an incomplete set of observations or a "surprising fact" that existing theories fail to explain. From this starting point, the researcher seeks to generate the most plausible hypothesis that would, if true, best explain the phenomenon. The choice of the "best" explanation is a subjective act based on principles of parsimony and explanatory power, a challenge this research design manages systematically. The utility of this approach is powerfully illustrated by medical diagnosis, where a physician observes a patient's symptoms (the surprising facts) and infers the most likely underlying condition (the best explanation) from among many alternatives (Mitchell, 2018).

##### **3.2.3 The Proposed Reasoning Cycle**

The research will proceed not linearly but through a continuous, iterative cycle of reasoning adapted from the model proposed by Kovács & Spens (2005). This cycle ensures that theoretical development is rigorously grounded in empirical evidence and that data collection is purposefully guided by emerging insights.

1. **Abduction:** The inquiry begins by mapping empirical data from the case studies against the initial theoretical framework (Section 2.4). Any phenomena that deviate from or are not adequately explained by this framework are identified as "surprising facts." The abductive stage involves generating the most plausible explanatory hypothesis for these anomalies.  
2. **Deduction:** The newly generated hypothesis is then treated as a premise. Its logical consequences are explored to determine what other empirical evidence should be observable if the hypothesis were true. This deductive step refines the hypothesis and makes it testable.  
3. **Induction:** The research then moves to a phase of empirical substantiation, seeking further data to systematically test the hypothesis. This inductive stage aims to verify or falsify the abductively-generated proposition, moving from a plausible explanation toward a robust, evidence-based conclusion.

This philosophical and logical framework is operationalized through a specific, multi-phase mixed-methods research design, which is detailed in the following section.

### **4.0 Research Design and Methods**

This section provides the concrete operational plan for executing the research. It details the mixed-methods design that brings the pragmatist philosophy and the abductive-led cycle of inquiry to life, ensuring a rigorous and systematic approach to answering the research questions. Combining qualitative and quantitative methods allows for **triangulation**, a powerful technique for overcoming the intrinsic biases and weaknesses of single-method studies (Mitchell, 2018). This design will leverage the depth of qualitative case studies to generate novel hypotheses and the breadth of quantitative analysis to substantiate them, thereby producing a more complete and robust understanding of the phenomenon.

#### **4.1 Qualitative Phase: Case Study and Data Collection**

The initial phase of the research will focus on in-depth qualitative exploration to identify the "surprising facts" that fuel the abductive process.

##### **4.1.1 Case Study Selection**

The research will employ a **comparative case study design**, a method well-suited for exploring complex phenomena in their real-world context. Four MNCs will be selected: two based in the United Kingdom (LME) and two in Germany (CME). To ensure analytical depth, these firms will be drawn from two representative sectors—**engineering and airlines**—facilitating both cross-national (UK vs. Germany) and cross-sectoral comparison.

##### **4.1.2 Data Collection**

Data will be collected from multiple sources to enable triangulation and construct a rich, detailed account of each firm's strategy. Primary methods will include:

* **Semi-structured interviews** with senior executives and managers responsible for corporate strategy, operations, and procurement.  
* **Documentary analysis** of corporate materials, including annual reports, strategic plans, investor briefings, and press releases.  
* **Review of secondary data sources**, such as industry reports, financial news archives, and existing academic literature pertaining to the selected firms.

#### **4.2 Abductive Analysis and Proposition Development**

The analytical process is designed to move systematically from data to novel theory. First, the collected qualitative data from each case will be mapped against the predictive taxonomy developed in Section 2.0. Any findings that contradict, extend, or are not explained by the initial LME/CME framework will be isolated as "surprising facts" or empirical puzzles that require a new explanation. The core abductive step involves generating and evaluating competing hypotheses to find the "best explanation" for these anomalies. This process of moving from surprising findings to robust propositions, modelled on the methodology outlined by Mitchell (2018), will result in a new, more nuanced conceptual framework about MNC outsourcing strategy. This process directly operationalizes the abductive stage of the inquiry (Kovács & Spens, 2005), moving from anomalous empirical data ('deviating real life experiences') to the generation of a new, plausible hypothesis ('theory suggestion').

#### **4.3 Quantitative Phase: Inductive Substantiation**

The propositions generated through abduction in the qualitative phase will be operationalized as testable hypotheses and substantiated through an inductive analytical phase. This will be achieved through a targeted survey distributed to a larger sample of senior managers in UK and German firms to test the generalizability of the propositions, supplemented by statistical analysis of existing business databases to find corroborating patterns. This phase provides the "empirical substantiation" called for in the inquiry cycle (Kovács & Spens, 2005).

#### **4.4 Research Rigour and Ethical Considerations**

Rigour will be maintained throughout the research process via the systematic triangulation of data sources (interviews, documents, secondary data) and methods (qualitative and quantitative). All research activities will adhere to the highest ethical standards. Informed consent will be obtained from all participants prior to interviews, and measures will be taken to ensure the anonymity and confidentiality of both individuals and their organizations.

This phased and rigorous design ensures a robust process for both generating and validating novel, theoretically significant, and practically relevant insights into international business strategy.

### **5.0 Potential Contributions and Dissemination**

The value of this study extends beyond its specific findings to make significant and distinct contributions to academic theory, business practice, and research methodology. By addressing a clear gap in the literature through an innovative methodological approach, the research is positioned to have a lasting impact.

#### **5.1 Theoretical Contribution**

By focusing on the empirical anomalies that are not fully explained by the Varieties of Capitalism framework, this research delivers a necessary refinement to theories of comparative capitalism and international business. The primary theoretical contribution will be a novel conceptual model that offers a more dynamic and nuanced understanding of how macro-level institutional forces and firm-level strategic capabilities interact to shape global corporate strategy.

#### **5.2 Practical and Managerial Contributions**

This research will equip managers and policymakers with an empirically-validated framework for strategic decision-making. For managers, the findings will yield a more sophisticated framework for designing and implementing global outsourcing strategies, enabling them to better navigate the trade-offs between cost, control, and capability development. For policymakers, the study will offer valuable insights into the institutional factors that foster distinct national competitive advantages and how these are leveraged by firms in a globalized economy.

#### **5.3 Methodological Contribution**

This research serves as a methodological corrective that demonstrates how to rigorously operationalize abductive reasoning in international business research, moving beyond the field's often simplistic deductive or inductive stances. By systematically documenting the integration of abduction, deduction, and induction within a pragmatist, mixed-methods design, the study provides a valuable and replicable model for future researchers seeking to generate novel and empirically grounded theory.

#### **5.4 Dissemination Plan**

The findings and contributions of this research will be shared through multiple channels to reach both academic and professional audiences. The dissemination strategy includes:

* Publication of findings in top-tier, peer-reviewed academic journals in the fields of international business, strategic management, and economic geography.  
* Presentation of emerging results and final conclusions at major international academic conferences.  
* Completion and successful defence of a doctoral thesis that consolidates the full scope of the research.
