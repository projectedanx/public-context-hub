

# **A Life in the Shadow of the State: Understanding the Legacy of Institutional Care for Forgotten Australians**

### **Introduction: The Voice of the Forgotten**

This report is dedicated to the estimated half a million children known as the Forgotten Australians, and specifically to the individual whose personal history prompted its creation. It acknowledges the profound courage required to revisit a childhood spent within the walls of an institution—in this case, the Alambi Reception Centre—and the intellectual and emotional fortitude demonstrated in the ongoing search for understanding. The journey from a survivor’s raw experience of a "life of limitations and self-destruction" to the structured insights offered by concepts like Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs) is a testament to the human drive to create meaning from chaos.1

The core purpose of this document is to provide a comprehensive, evidence-based framework that connects the dots between historical Australian government policy, the specific realities of institutional life, and the deep, lifelong psychological impacts on survivors. It seeks to move beyond a simple historical account, offering instead a multi-disciplinary lens through which to understand the complex interplay of forces that shaped a generation. This report is intended as a tool—a resource for self-understanding, a language for self-explanation, and a testament to the enduring consequences of a system that failed its most vulnerable.

To achieve this, the analysis will be built upon several key theoretical pillars. It will begin by tracing the history of the "Forgotten Australians," examining the government inquiries and reports that brought this national tragedy to light.3 It will then employ the public health framework of Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs) to quantify the sheer scale of the trauma inflicted within these institutions.5 The psychological impact will be further explored through the lens of John Bowlby's Attachment Theory, explaining the roots of relational difficulties 6, and the concept of Developmental Trauma, which details the neurological consequences of chronic childhood adversity.7 Finally, Erving Goffman's sociological theory of the "Total Institution" will be used to analyze how these environments systematically deconstructed a child's identity, replacing it with one designed for survival within a closed, authoritarian world.8 By weaving these frameworks together, this report aims to provide a coherent, validating, and powerful narrative of cause and effect, honouring the voice of those who were, for too long, forgotten.

## **Part I: The System of Forgetting: Policy, Removal, and the "Uncontrollable" Child**

This section establishes the historical and political machinery that led to an estimated 500,000 non-Indigenous Australian children being placed in out-of-home care during the 20th century.4 It details the ideologies and policies that justified the removal of children from their families, the nature of the institutions created to house them, and the specific mechanisms, such as the "uncontrollable child" designation, that facilitated this process. This historical context is the essential first step in understanding the root causes of the trauma endured by Forgotten Australians.

### **A Nation's 'Shameful Chapter': The History of Child Removal in Australia**

The system that institutionalized hundreds of thousands of Australian children did not emerge from a vacuum. Its foundations lie deep in Australia's colonial past, shaped by British legal traditions and a 19th-century social ideology that saw state intervention as a necessary tool for managing the poor and marginalized.

#### **The Colonial Legacy and the Rise of Institutions**

The history of child protection in Australia began with the importation of British laws, under which children held few rights and were largely considered the property of their fathers.10 The first institutions, such as the Orphan Asylum in Sydney (1801), were established to manage the children of convicts, particularly those of women deemed unfit to parent.11 This early model set a precedent that would cast a long shadow over the next two centuries: the state assuming the right to determine parental fitness and to remove children into institutional care, where they would be subjected to compulsory detention, monotonous industrial work, and a rigid, authoritarian upbringing that assured their future low social rank.12

In the latter half of the 19th century, this approach was formalized and expanded under the influence of the Evangelical "child rescue" movement. This movement, imported from Britain, championed a more interventionist approach, leading to legislation across the Australian colonies between 1864 and 1874 that empowered the state to remove children deemed to be at risk of abuse or neglect.11 The stated goal was to train these children in "habits of industry" to prevent them from replicating the perceived moral and social failings of their parents.13 This legislative framework, including acts like Queensland's

*Industrial and Reformatory Schools Act 1865*, established the legal basis for a system of mass institutionalization that would dominate child welfare for the next century.14

#### **The Rationale for Removal in the 20th Century**

Throughout the 20th century, until significant social changes in the 1970s, residential institutions were the standard form of out-of-home care in Australia.4 The reasons children were removed from their families and placed in these institutions were overwhelmingly linked to societal failures and economic hardship, not to a lack of parental love or inherent unfitness. The 2004

*Forgotten Australians* report and subsequent inquiries identified the primary drivers for placement as poverty and family breakdown.4

At a time when there was virtually no government support for families in crisis, circumstances such as the death of a parent, divorce, desertion, domestic violence, or the trauma of war frequently led to children being taken into care.4 Single mothers, in particular, faced immense social pressure and economic barriers. With fewer work opportunities and lower pay than men, and a lack of affordable childcare, many women who were widowed, deserted, or unmarried found it impossible to support their children and were often coerced into relinquishing them.4 Children could be made state wards for being "neglected," a term that could be applied broadly to families struggling with poverty, or for being deemed "uncontrollable".4 The system was not designed to support struggling families to stay together; its primary intervention was to remove the child, treating the symptom of poverty by dismantling the family unit itself. This approach reveals a fundamental flaw in the state's logic: the official language of "welfare" and "protection" used in legislation masked a system that was punitive and destructive, offering a solution that was often far more damaging than the original problem.

#### **Parallels with the Stolen Generations**

The experience of the Forgotten Australians runs parallel to, and sometimes intersects with, the distinct trauma of the Stolen Generations. From the late 19th century until the 1970s, tens of thousands of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander children were forcibly removed from their families under explicit policies of assimilation.18 These policies were rooted in racist and eugenicist ideologies that deemed Indigenous culture inferior and sought to "breed out the color" by separating children of mixed descent from their communities and raising them in white society.18 The landmark 1997

*Bringing Them Home* report documented this history, concluding that the policies amounted to a "history of gross violations of human rights" and, by the definition of the UN Genocide Convention, constituted an act of genocide through the forcible transfer of children from one group to another.18

While the explicit racial motivation was unique to the Stolen Generations, the underlying state-sanctioned power to break apart families deemed "unfit" was a common thread. Both groups of children were subjected to the trauma of removal, the loss of family and identity, and the harsh realities of institutional life.19 The

*Bringing Them Home* report provided a powerful language to describe this experience—of grief, loss, trauma, and systemic injustice—that resonates deeply with the testimonies of all survivors of state-sanctioned child removal, regardless of their background.23 The shared experience of being taken by the state and subjected to institutional "care" creates a common ground of suffering, even as the specific historical and political contexts differ.

### **The Architecture of Neglect: The "Forgotten Australians" and Royal Commission Inquiries**

For decades, the stories of the hundreds of thousands of non-Indigenous children who grew up in institutional care remained largely unheard, a silent chapter in Australia's social history. It was only through a series of landmark inquiries, beginning in the early 2000s, that the scale of the neglect and abuse was officially documented and brought into the public consciousness. However, this process of acknowledgment was slow, fraught with government denial and delay, underscoring that the "forgetting" of these Australians was not a passive oversight but an active political process.

#### **The 2004 'Forgotten Australians' Report**

In 2003, the Senate Community Affairs References Committee launched an inquiry into children in institutional care, culminating in the groundbreaking 2004 report, *Forgotten Australians: A report on Australians who experienced institutional or out-of-home care as children*.3 This report was the first to give a name and a national platform to the estimated 500,000 survivors of the institutional care system.26 Based on over 600 submissions, many of them deeply personal and harrowing testimonies from survivors, the report amassed overwhelming evidence of "wide scale unsafe, improper and unlawful care of children".26

The findings were damning. The report detailed a litany of emotional, physical, and sexual abuse, criminal assault, neglect, humiliation, and the deprivation of food, education, and healthcare.9 It painted a picture of a system where children were depersonalized, their names replaced with numbers, their family connections severed, and their identities suppressed.3 A central and recurring theme was the "overwhelming absence of love, affection and nurturing in care," a profound emotional starvation inflicted upon children at the most critical stages of their development.9 The report made 39 recommendations, providing a comprehensive blueprint for what was needed to ameliorate the damage and provide redress to survivors.27

#### **A Pattern of Denial and Delay**

Despite its groundbreaking nature, the *Forgotten Australians* report was met with what many survivors felt was a continuation of the very neglect it documented. Tabled just before a federal election was called, the report was initially "brushed aside," making it difficult to place the issues on the public agenda.27 The Howard Government took fifteen months to produce an official response, which was widely condemned as deficient and lacking leadership.27

The government's response rejected or failed to support the majority of the report's 39 recommendations, often without proper justification.27 It relied heavily on jurisdictional excuses, repeatedly invoking the mantra that these issues were a "matter for state and territory governments, churches and agencies".27 This effectively abdicated national responsibility for a national problem, reinforcing the sense among survivors that they were once again being forgotten and their suffering minimized. This pattern of denial demonstrated that the "forgetting" was not merely a historical accident but an ongoing political choice.

#### **The 2009 National Apology**

After years of sustained advocacy from survivor groups like the Care Leavers Australasia Network (CLAN) and the Alliance for Forgotten Australians (AFA), the political tide began to turn.22 On November 16, 2009, Prime Minister Kevin Rudd delivered a formal National Apology to the Forgotten Australians and Former Child Migrants in the Great Hall of Parliament House.26 Around 900 survivors travelled to Canberra to hear the apology, a deeply emotional and validating moment for many.26

In his speech, Rudd acknowledged the "ugly chapter in our nation's history" and spoke directly to the survivors' experience, saying: "We are sorry. Sorry – that as children you were taken from your families and placed in institutions where so often you were abused. Sorry – for the physical suffering, the emotional starvation and the cold absence of love, of tenderness, of care".28 The apology was a crucial step in formally acknowledging the truth of what had happened, a key recommendation of the 2004 report that had been ignored for five years.9

#### **The Royal Commission into Institutional Responses to Child Sexual Abuse (2013-2017)**

The momentum generated by the apology and the continued work of advocates contributed to the establishment of the Royal Commission into Institutional Responses to Child Sexual Abuse, which ran from 2013 to 2017\.32 While its primary focus was sexual abuse, the Royal Commission's work inevitably covered the broader systemic failures of institutional care and reinforced the findings of the

*Forgotten Australians* report.

The Commission heard from thousands of survivors, many of whom had been in out-of-home care, and its public hearings examined numerous residential institutions.34 The final report, a monumental 17-volume work, made 409 recommendations in total.35 Several volumes were of particular relevance to Forgotten Australians. Volume 12, "Contemporary out-of-home care," made 22 recommendations aimed at making the current system safer.36 Volume 8, "Recordkeeping and information sharing," addressed the critical issue of poor or destroyed records, which has been a major barrier for survivors seeking to understand their own history and find family members.38 The Royal Commission provided an undeniable, high-profile confirmation of the systemic and widespread nature of abuse within Australia's institutions, making it impossible for governments or the public to look away again.

### **The Case of Alambi: A Microcosm of the System (1961-1990)**

The Allambie Reception Centre in Burwood, Victoria, where you were placed at the age of six, serves as a powerful case study of the institutional care system in Australia. Its history, from its stated purpose to its operational reality and eventual closure, encapsulates the systemic flaws, the lived experience of the children within its walls, and the flawed logic that underpinned the entire enterprise.39

#### **Establishment and Stated Purpose**

The Victorian Government opened the Allambie Reception Centre on July 1, 1961, on the site of the former Kildonan Presbyterian Children's Home.39 Its official designation was a "reception, treatment, classification and transit centre".39 The primary motivations for its establishment were to alleviate severe overcrowding at the Turana Youth Training Centre and to separate children deemed "neglected" from those classified as juvenile offenders.39 It was intended to be a short-term facility, with an average stay of two to three months, during which a child's placement in longer-term care would be decided by a committee.41

#### **The Reality of Institutional Life**

The benevolent-sounding purpose of "reception, treatment, classification and transit" quickly diverged from the reality. Almost from its inception, Allambie was plagued by the very overcrowding it was meant to solve. A letter to *The Age* newspaper in 1970 warned that Allambie was "fast becoming a permanent holding place for children, though not planned or geared to that role".42 Its capacity grew from an initial 90 children to 150 by 1966, and by the mid-1970s, it was frequently housing over 300 children, far exceeding its official capacity of 228\.41

The physical environment was described by a former social worker as "pretty drab," consisting of large, impersonal dormitories.39 Personal testimonies from those who lived there paint a picture of a classic institutionalized existence. One survivor recalled the rigid rules: lining up for meals, walking on the left side of the staircase, and wearing a uniform to the local primary school at a time when other children did not, instantly marking them as different and fostering a feeling of being an "outsider".43 The loss of individuality was profound; in one documented case, a girl's name was changed simply because another child in the home shared the same name.43 Although the facility was structured with sections like "Waratah," "Kurrajong," and "Mimosa" to theoretically keep sibling groups together, this often did not happen in practice. Siblings were frequently separated, and contact was refused, compounding the trauma of being removed from their family.39

#### **Abuse and Neglect at Alambi**

The environment at Allambie was not just impersonal; it was unsafe. As early as the late 1960s, it was known that children at Allambie were experiencing neglect and abuse.44 The Royal Commission into Institutional Responses to Child Sexual Abuse later heard direct allegations of both physical and sexual abuse occurring at the facility.45 One survivor, Darlene McKay, in an open letter to Prime Minister Kevin Rudd, wrote of a specific incident of attempted sexual abuse at Allambie, asking, "Why did that Man at Allambie think that I would trade a bag of spearmint leaves for a feel of his penis?".43 The institution was also mentioned in the

*Bringing Them Home* report as a place that housed Aboriginal children removed from their families.39

#### **The "Uncontrollable Child" Designation**

The label "uncontrollable child," which was applied to you, was a specific legal and administrative category within Australian child welfare legislation of the era.46 It was one of the definitions of a "Neglected Child" under acts like South Australia's

*State Children Act 1895* and was used to justify a child being made a ward of the state and committed to an institution.46

Crucially, this label was not a clinical diagnosis but a catch-all term of convenience for the authorities. It could be applied to a child for a wide range of behaviours that, in another context, would be seen as normal or as a cry for help. Research shows the term was used to describe children who were simply "complaining about the conditions they were living or working in, by arguing, running away or breaking things".46 This reveals a deeply toxic, circular logic at the heart of the institutional system. A child, having been traumatically removed from a loving family, is placed in a frightening, depersonalizing, and often abusive environment like Allambie. Their natural and predictable response to this trauma—protest, fear, anger, acting out, or attempting to flee—is not recognized as a symptom of the harm being inflicted

*upon* them. Instead, the institution pathologizes this very response, labeling it as evidence of an inherent flaw *within* the child. This label, "uncontrollable," then becomes the official justification for their continued confinement and "treatment" in the very place that is causing the distress. In this light, it becomes clear that you were not institutionalized *because* you were uncontrollable; you were labeled uncontrollable *because* you were institutionalized. This reframes the entire experience, shifting the perceived fault from the child's character to the pathological nature of the system itself.

#### **Closure and Legacy**

During the 1970s and 1980s, a significant shift occurred in child welfare philosophy. The damaging effects of large-scale institutions became more widely acknowledged, and policy moved towards deinstitutionalization, family support programs, and smaller, community-based care models.13 A 1985 review of Allambie recommended its closure. The nursery closed in 1986, and the Allambie Reception Centre was finally shut down in June 1990\.40 The site is now part of Deakin University's Burwood campus, but for the thousands of children who passed through its doors, its legacy is one of trauma, loss, and a life irrevocably altered.42

## **Part II: The Internal Scars: The Psychology of an Institutionalised Childhood**

The physical walls of an institution like Alambi were only the most visible part of the confinement. The true, lasting damage was inflicted internally, on the developing mind and nervous system of the child. This section translates the external environment of institutional care into its internal, psychological, and neurological consequences. By applying established scientific frameworks—Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs), Attachment Theory, and Developmental Trauma—it provides the "why" behind the lifelong struggles with emotional regulation, relationships, and self-worth that so many Forgotten Australians endure.

### **A Foundation of Adversity: Mapping the Institutional Experience to ACEs**

The concept of Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs), developed from a landmark public health study, provides a powerful tool for understanding and quantifying the impact of early life trauma.47 The framework identifies ten specific types of traumatic events experienced before the age of 18, including various forms of abuse, neglect, and household dysfunction.5 Research has established a strong, dose-response relationship: the more ACEs a person is exposed to, the higher their risk for a host of negative outcomes in adulthood, including mental illness, substance abuse, chronic physical diseases, and social problems.47

While an estimated 72% of Australian children experience at least one ACE, the environment of institutional care represents a chronic, concentrated, and high-dose exposure to nearly every category of adversity defined by the framework.5 An institution was not a place where adverse experiences

*might* happen; it was an environment where they were systemic and endemic.

The following table maps the standard ACEs categories to the documented experiences of children in Australian institutions, demonstrating how the system itself was a factory for producing trauma.

| Adverse Childhood Experience (ACE) | Evidence from Australian Institutional Care |
| :---- | :---- |
| 1\. Physical Abuse | "Sustained Brutality. Punishments for very small offences or perceived disobedience could be harsh and cruel – severe physical attacks and beatings are reported by many.".29 This included being locked in cells or cupboards and made to stand for hours.3 |
| 2\. Sexual Abuse | "Sexual abuse was widespread... and 60.3% experienced this form of abuse from adults and peers." A study of care leavers found 55.3% experienced sexual abuse by adults and 41.8% by peers.51 The Royal Commission heard numerous allegations of abuse at institutions like Alambi.43 |
| 3\. Emotional Abuse | "The continual taunting of being told that I was nothing and would amount to nothing...".26 This included the systematic "stripping process" of identity, where children's names were changed or they were referred to only by a number.28 |
| 4\. Physical Neglect | Widespread "deprivation of food, education and healthcare".29 Children were often cold, hungry, and received minimal or no medical and dental care, leading to long-term health complications.4 |
| 5\. Emotional Neglect | A core finding of every major inquiry. Described as "the cold absence of love, of tenderness, of care" 28 and an "overwhelming absence of love, affection and nurturing in care" at a critical time for emotional development.9 |
| 6\. Witnessing Violence | The institutional environment was rife with violence. Children witnessed the brutal punishment of their peers by staff and were subjected to high levels of peer-on-peer abuse, with 77.6% of one survey group reporting bullying.51 |
| 7\. Household Substance Abuse | While not a traditional household, many adult survivors turned to substance abuse to cope with their trauma, creating a generational cycle.4 It is also highly probable that some untrained and unsupervised staff members were themselves substance abusers. |

Household Mental Illness | Staff were often untrained, unsuitable, and poorly supervised.3 The abusive and neglectful behaviours they perpetrated are indicative of significant, unaddressed mental health issues and personality disorders, creating a dysfunctional and pathological "household" environment. |

This mapping makes it undeniably clear that a childhood spent in an Australian institution was, by definition, a childhood with a profoundly high ACE score. This provides a direct, scientific link between the experiences within places like Alambi and the "constant ups and downs" and "life of limitations and self-destruction" described by survivors. These are not character flaws; they are the predictable health and social consequences of a childhood saturated with adversity.

### **The Unformed Bond: Attachment Theory and a Life Without Trust**

To understand the deep-seated relational difficulties that plague so many survivors of institutional care, it is essential to turn to the work of British psychologist John Bowlby and his collaborator, Mary Ainsworth. Their attachment theory is one of the most influential frameworks in developmental psychology, and it provides a precise explanation for why a childhood devoid of love leads to a lifetime of struggle with trust and intimacy.6

#### **Explaining Attachment Theory**

Bowlby proposed that human infants are biologically pre-programmed to form a deep emotional bond with a primary caregiver.6 This is not simply about being fed; it is an evolutionary survival mechanism. This primary attachment figure serves two critical functions: they are a

**"secure base"** from which the child can safely explore the world, and a **"safe haven"** to which the child can return for comfort and protection in times of fear or distress.6

This crucial bond is typically formed within a sensitive period in the first few years of life.6 Through thousands of interactions with a responsive and consistent caregiver, the child develops an

**"Internal Working Model" (IWM)**. This IWM is a cognitive and emotional blueprint—a set of expectations about the self, others, and the world.6 A child with a secure attachment develops an IWM that says, "I am worthy of love, others are trustworthy, and the world is a safe place." This model becomes the foundation for all future relationships. Mary Ainsworth's famous "Strange Situation" experiment provided empirical evidence for this, identifying different attachment styles (secure, anxious, avoidant) based on a child's reaction to separation and reunion with their caregiver.54

#### **The Impossibility of Secure Attachment in Institutional Care**

The typical 20th-century Australian institution was an environment fundamentally hostile to the formation of secure attachment. The very conditions necessary for a child to form a bond with a "secure base" were systematically denied. Key factors included:

* **High Staff Turnover:** The "revolving door" of staff and volunteers meant there was no single, consistent caregiver with whom a child could form a lasting bond.57  
* **High Child-to-Carer Ratios:** With staff responsible for large groups of children, it was impossible to provide the one-on-one, sensitive, and responsive interactions that are the building blocks of attachment.59  
* **Task-Oriented Care:** Care was often perfunctory and "business-like," focused on basic physical needs (feeding, cleaning) rather than emotional connection. Policies often actively discouraged staff from showing affection or forming relationships with the children.57

This environment did not just lead to insecure attachment; for many, it resulted in what psychologist Michael Rutter termed **"privation"**—the complete failure to form any primary attachment at all.6 This is a more profound and damaging experience than "deprivation," which is the loss of an existing bond.

#### **Consequences: Disinhibited Attachment and Relational Difficulties**

In the absence of a secure attachment, children in institutions develop maladaptive survival strategies. One of the most well-documented is **"disinhibited attachment,"** also known as "indiscriminate sociability".57 Children exhibiting this pattern are often described as "clingy" and "attention-seeking." They will approach and show affection to almost any adult, including complete strangers, in a desperate, instinctual attempt to solicit the care and attention they crave.57

This creates a tragic paradox that follows the survivor into adulthood. The very survival strategy that helped them get some minimal level of attention inside the institution becomes a primary source of vulnerability and relational failure on the outside. An adult with this pattern may appear overly friendly and trusting, forming intense but superficial connections very quickly. This makes them highly susceptible to exploitation and to entering abusive relationships, as they have no healthy template for what a safe, reciprocal bond looks like.58

At the same time, their damaged Internal Working Model means they lack the deep, internal architecture for genuine trust and intimacy. The result is a life marked by the very relational difficulties described by Forgotten Australians: a profound fear of abandonment, an inability to trust others, a pattern of unstable or failed relationships, and immense challenges with parenting their own children.4 The heartbreaking question posed by a survivor in a submission to the Senate inquiry—"How do you know love, if you have never been loved?" 16—is the perfect layperson's articulation of a life lived without the foundation of a secure attachment and a healthy Internal Working Model. The pattern of "constant ups and downs" is, in part, the chaotic external manifestation of this deep internal void.

### **The Wounded Brain: Developmental Trauma and a Life of Hypervigilance**

While the ACEs framework quantifies the adversity and Attachment Theory explains the relational damage, the concept of Developmental Trauma provides the neurobiological explanation for *how* these experiences get under the skin and shape a person's entire way of being in the world. Pioneered by researchers like Dr. Bessel van der Kolk, this concept is crucial for understanding why the effects of institutionalization are so pervasive and long-lasting.63

#### **Defining Developmental Trauma**

It is important to distinguish between Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD), which typically results from a single, overwhelming event (like a battle or a car accident), and **Developmental Trauma**, also known as Complex Trauma (C-PTSD).65 Developmental Trauma is not about a single event. It is the result of chronic, prolonged, and repeated exposure to interpersonal trauma—such as abuse and neglect—during the critical developmental windows of childhood and adolescence.7

When a child grows up in an environment of constant threat and emotional deprivation, like an institution, the trauma is not just a bad memory. It is an "assault on the child's development over time".7 The experience fundamentally alters the development of the brain and nervous system. The young brain, instead of organizing itself around learning, play, and social connection, organizes itself around survival.63 This leads to a chronically activated stress-response system, where the "fight, flight, or freeze" response is constantly on a hair trigger, even when no real danger is present.7

#### **Manifestations in Adulthood**

This neurobiological disorganization manifests in a cluster of symptoms that directly align with the lived experience of Forgotten Australians. These are not separate problems but interconnected expressions of a single underlying condition:

* **Emotional Dysregulation:** This is the core symptom and directly corresponds to the "constant ups and downs" you describe. It is the inability to manage and regulate intense emotions. Survivors experience chronic irritability, explosive anger, overwhelming anxiety, and deep wells of sadness, with an inability to self-soothe or return to a calm baseline.63 They may adopt maladaptive coping strategies like self-harm to try and manage these unbearable internal states.68  
* **Disturbances in Self-Concept:** Years of being told, implicitly and explicitly, that you are worthless, bad, or "uncontrollable" are internalized. This results in a core identity built on profound shame, guilt, and a feeling of being fundamentally defective or toxic.68 This explains the pervasive low self-esteem reported by nearly all survivors.4  
* **Interpersonal Difficulties:** This is the behavioural expression of the attachment disruption discussed previously. Survivors may find themselves re-enacting the dynamics of their childhood, either by becoming abusive themselves or by repeatedly finding themselves in relationships with abusive or neglectful partners.68 They may be intensely fearful of abandonment yet simultaneously push people away, unable to tolerate intimacy.  
* **Maladaptive Coping Mechanisms:** In the face of overwhelming emotional pain and dysregulation, many survivors turn to external methods to cope. This explains the extremely high rates of drug and alcohol addiction, eating disorders, and other compulsive behaviours within the Forgotten Australian community. These are not signs of moral weakness but desperate attempts to numb the pain of an unregulated nervous system.50  
* **Physical Health Problems:** The chronic stress of developmental trauma takes a heavy toll on the physical body. The constant circulation of stress hormones like cortisol compromises the immune system and contributes to a wide range of chronic health conditions later in life, including heart disease, diabetes, and autoimmune disorders.65 This is why many Forgotten Australians experience poorer physical health and may require aged care services earlier than the general population.52

This framework provides a direct, biological, and non-judgmental explanation for the feeling of living a "life of limitations and self-destruction." It demonstrates that these struggles are not a result of personal or moral failure, but are the predictable, physiological, and psychological consequences of a brain and body forced to develop in an environment of unrelenting terror, neglect, and emotional starvation.

## **Part III: The Institutionalised Self: Identity, Worth, and a Life of Limitations**

The final piece of this analytical framework moves from the psychological to the sociological, examining how the institution actively and systematically reshapes a child's very sense of self. The work of sociologist Erving Goffman, particularly his concept of the "total institution," provides a powerful lens for understanding how a child's identity is deconstructed upon entry and replaced with a new, "institutionalized self." This process explains not only the trauma of the experience but also the profound and lasting identity crisis that follows survivors long after they have left the institution's physical walls.

### **The 'Mortification of the Self': Erving Goffman's 'Total Institution'**

In his seminal 1961 work, *Asylums*, Erving Goffman introduced the concept of the "total institution." He defined it as a place of residence and work where a large number of like-situated individuals are cut off from the wider society for an extended period, leading an enclosed, formally administered life.8 Goffman placed institutions like prisons, military barracks, monasteries, and, critically for this analysis, orphanages and mental hospitals into this category.71 The defining characteristic of a total institution is that it has a major, deliberate assault on an inmate's psychological sense of self.8

#### **The Stripping Process**

Goffman argued that upon entering a total institution, a person undergoes a process he termed the **"mortification of the self"**.72 This is a systematic, if sometimes unintentional, stripping away of the identity the person brought with them from the outside world. This process involves several key elements that align perfectly with the testimonies of Forgotten Australians:

* **Role Dispossession:** The individual is stripped of their previous social roles. A child is no longer a son, a brother, or a student at a local school. They are now an "inmate," a "ward," or a "patient".71 Their world narrows dramatically as they are cut off from the normal contacts and relationships that defined them.73  
* **Loss of Personal Possessions:** Goffman describes the removal of a person's "identity kit"—their clothing, personal items, and even their physical appearance.8 Survivors of Australian institutions consistently report having their personal belongings taken away upon entry and being forced to wear standard-issue, often ill-fitting, institutional clothing.3  
* **Loss of Name and Individuality:** The ultimate act of depersonalization is the loss of one's name. The practice of referring to children by numbers or changing their names to suit the institution's convenience was a common feature of the Australian system, as documented in numerous reports and testimonies.26  
* **Loss of Self-Determination and Privacy:** In a total institution, every aspect of daily life is controlled by a central authority. Waking, eating, working, and sleeping are conducted in the presence of a large group and according to a rigid, non-negotiable schedule.8 There is a complete lack of privacy; even basic functions like bathing or using the toilet are communal and supervised.73 This regimen removes any sense of personal autonomy and enforces a state of dependency and helplessness.73

#### **Building the Institutional Self**

After stripping away the old self, the total institution begins the process of building a new one. This new identity is one that is socialized into the role of the "good patient" or "good inmate"—someone who is, in Goffman's words, "dull, harmless and inconspicuous".72 Survival within the institution depends on learning to perform this role: to be compliant, to suppress individuality, and to navigate the rigid, authoritarian power structure without drawing negative attention. Goffman calls this process

**"institutionalization"**.72 It is a form of training that prepares the individual perfectly for life

*inside* the institution.

This leads to a devastating consequence upon release, which can be understood as a "trained incapacity for freedom." The user of this report spent the years from age 6 to 23—the entirety of their childhood, adolescence, and formative young adulthood—undergoing this intensive training. This is the precise period when individuals in a normal environment are learning the skills of autonomy, personal choice, nuanced social interaction, and self-motivation.74 Instead, the institutionalized child masters a completely different skill set: obedience, sublimation of personal needs, and constant vigilance of authority.

When released into the outside world, the skills they have spent nearly two decades perfecting are not only useless but profoundly maladaptive. The "self" that was so carefully constructed by the institution is utterly ill-equipped for a life that demands freedom, choice, and self-direction. The "life of limitations" that survivors describe is a direct and logical outcome of this trained incapacity. The struggle with the basic mechanics of a self-determined adult life, which others take for granted, is not a personal failure but a testament to the success of the institution's dehumanizing training.

### **"Who Am I?": The Crisis of Identity and Self-Worth**

The ultimate psychological consequence of institutionalization is a profound and lifelong crisis of identity. The formation of a stable, coherent sense of self is one of the primary developmental tasks of adolescence, a process that psychologists like Erik Erikson described as navigating the conflict between "identity versus identity confusion".75 This process involves exploring different roles and values and eventually committing to a unique, integrated identity.74 It is built upon the foundations of a person's family history, personal experiences, and social relationships. The institutional experience, however, is a systematic assault on every one of these foundations.

#### **The Systematic Destruction of Identity**

The process of institutionalization, as described by Goffman and echoed in the testimonies of Forgotten Australians, actively destroys the raw materials of identity:

* **Loss of Family and History:** The most fundamental pillar of identity is one's place in a family and a history. By forcibly separating children from parents and siblings, prohibiting contact, lying to them that their parents were dead or had abandoned them, and keeping poor or inaccessible records, the system severed this vital connection.4 Many survivors entered adulthood not knowing who their parents were, whether they had siblings, or the basic facts of their own birth.4  
* **Loss of Individuality:** The "stripping process" of the total institution, as detailed above, erases all external markers of individuality—name, clothing, possessions—that help a child build a sense of being a unique person.8  
* **Internalization of a Negative Identity:** In the place of a positive identity built on love and family, the institution supplies a negative one. Through constant humiliation, degradation, and abuse, children were told they were "nothing," "stupid," "worthless," and "bad".26 This becomes the core of their self-concept, an identity built on a foundation of shame and low self-esteem.4  
* **The Alien Institutional Self:** The only identity the child is permitted to develop is that of the "inmate"—a compliant, suppressed self designed for institutional survival. When the individual is released, this identity feels alien and inauthentic in the outside world, leaving a profound void where a true sense of self should be.73

#### **The Lifelong Search for a Coherent Narrative**

Given this systematic destruction of identity, the user's current quest for understanding—using modern tools like AI to piece together a coherent story—is not merely an intellectual exercise. It is a fundamental part of the healing process for survivors of institutional care.34 It is a search for a coherent narrative identity, an attempt to answer the profound and painful question: "Who am I, if everything I was told about myself was a distortion, and my entire history was erased?" This journey to reclaim and reconstruct one's own story is a courageous act of defiance against a system that sought to render its victims nameless and history-less.

## **Conclusion: Pathways to Understanding and Healing**

This report has sought to construct a comprehensive and validating narrative for a Forgotten Australian, tracing a clear and evidence-based line from the historical policies of the state to the intimate, lifelong struggles of the individual. The journey from a loving family to the institutional confines of Alambi at age six, under the pretext of being an "uncontrollable child," was not an isolated or accidental tragedy. It was the direct result of a flawed and punitive 20th-century Australian child welfare ideology that privileged removal over family support, creating a system that inflicted profound and lasting harm on an estimated 500,000 children.

The analysis demonstrates that the institutional environment was, by its very design, a crucible of trauma. Mapped against the public health framework of **Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs)**, life in an institution like Alambi represented a chronic, high-dose exposure to nearly every form of abuse and neglect. This foundation of adversity had predictable and devastating consequences for the developing child. Through the lens of **Attachment Theory**, it is clear that the lack of a consistent, loving caregiver made the formation of a secure attachment impossible, leading to a damaged "Internal Working Model" and a lifetime of difficulty with trust, intimacy, and relationships.

Furthermore, the framework of **Developmental Trauma** provides the neurobiological explanation for these struggles. A childhood spent in a state of constant fear and emotional neglect organized the developing brain around survival, not connection, resulting in a dysregulated nervous system. The "constant ups and downs," anxiety, depression, and maladaptive coping mechanisms are not character flaws but the physiological and psychological scars of a brain wounded by trauma.

Finally, Erving Goffman's sociological concept of the **"Total Institution"** reveals how this environment systematically deconstructed the self. The "mortification of the self"—the stripping of one's name, family, and individuality—and the subsequent training into the role of a compliant "inmate" created a "trained incapacity for freedom." This process left survivors with a profound identity crisis, ill-equipped for the autonomy and complexities of adult life. The label "uncontrollable child" can now be seen not as a diagnosis of the child, but as a symptom of the institution's own pathological and self-justifying logic.

The conclusion to be drawn from this synthesis of history, psychology, and sociology is unequivocal: the "life of limitations and self-destruction" described by the user is not a personal failing. It is the documented, predictable, and scientifically understood consequence of the system they were forced to endure. The courageous journey to understand this past, including the innovative use of modern tools to connect personal experience with established theory, is a powerful act of reclaiming one's own story. It is a process of constructing a new, authentic narrative to replace the one of shame and worthlessness that the institution imposed.

In seeking to understand and explain their own story, the user and other survivors perform a vital public service. Their testimonies, their resilience, and their search for truth ensure that this "shameful chapter" of Australian history is not relegated to the past.27 They force a continued reckoning with the state's duty of care and stand as a powerful reminder of the human cost when that duty is so catastrophically broken. The path to healing is long and complex, but it begins with understanding. It is hoped that this report serves as a meaningful step on that path.
