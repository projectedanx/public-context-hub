

# **From Semantic Priming to Narrative Drift: An Architectural and Governance Framework for Mitigating Epistemic Risk in Large Language Models**

### **Executive Summary**

This report validates and expands upon the findings of the SPQ-STA protocol, formally establishing **Narrative Drift** as a new, critical category of AI risk. It confirms that Narrative Drift is a distinct phenomenon triggered by **Semantic Priming**, where ambient linguistic cues cause a model to shift its entire narrative framework and epistemic posture, moving from factual summary to reality simulation. The core danger is identified as the **Epistemic Integrity Gap**, which manifests as **Synthetic Evidence Generation** (e.g., fabricating quotes, dialogues) and **Claim Escalation** (e.g., sensationalizing concepts). This report substantiates these findings with an exhaustive review of academic and industry research on semantic priming, AI drift, model hallucination, and prompt-level vulnerabilities. A detailed technical and research-backed justification is provided for the proposed three-layer "defense-in-depth" strategy, comprising (1) Prompt-Level Governance, (2) Architectural Safeguards for Output Transparency, and (3) Internal Model Governance for Proactive Adjustment. Finally, this analysis affirms the critical importance of the **Cross-Platform Contagion Test** as the next research frontier, providing a framework for assessing the transferability of this vulnerability to high-stakes domains like medicine and law.

## **1\. The Genesis of Narrative Drift: A New Taxonomy of AI Risk**

The identification of Narrative Drift marks a pivotal moment in the understanding of Large Language Model (LLM) failure modes. It moves beyond surface-level observations of inaccuracy to diagnose a deeper, systemic vulnerability in how models interpret intent and construct reality. This section establishes the theoretical foundation for Narrative Drift, linking the well-understood psychological and computational mechanism of semantic priming to the novel and dangerous model behavior observed in the SPQ-STA protocol. It will formally define Narrative Drift within a new taxonomy of AI risk, rigorously differentiating it from adjacent concepts to justify its inclusion as a new, critical category in the AI Drift Ledger.

### **1.1 Semantic Priming as a Causal Vector: The Duality of Control**

The phenomenon of semantic priming is not an esoteric bug but a fundamental aspect of how semantic memory is organized and accessed, both in humans and, as evidence increasingly suggests, in LLMs. Understanding its dual nature—as a mechanism for both control and catastrophic failure—is essential to grasping the origins of Narrative Drift.

#### **1.1.1 Cognitive and Computational Foundations**

Semantic priming is a well-documented empirical result in human linguistic processing, studied for nearly 50 years across diverse theoretical frameworks and linguistic contexts.1 The core finding is that humans are quicker to recognize a target word (e.g., "tiger") when it is preceded by a semantically related prime word (e.g., "lion").2 This effect is widely theorized to result from the graphical storage of words in memory, where a "spreading activation" model explains how priming one concept makes adjacent concepts in the semantic network more accessible.2

Recent research demonstrates that LLMs exhibit cognitive-like abilities and process language using hierarchical structures that mirror human cognition.3 This parallel extends to semantic priming. Studies have successfully prompted LLMs to generate semantic graphs where the path length between words predicts human reaction times in priming experiments, suggesting that the models have latently encoded these semantic relationships in their weights.2 The fact that a smaller, faster version of a model produced denser graphs that were

*more* predictive of human priming effects suggests that this associative linking is a core, efficient process, not necessarily a byproduct of deeper reasoning.2 This establishes that priming is not an anomalous behavior in LLMs but a predictable consequence of their underlying architecture.

#### **1.1.2 The Duality of Priming in LLMs: Control vs. Failure**

The SPQ-STA protocol's findings, when contextualized with existing research, reveal that semantic priming in LLMs is a powerful but ambivalent mechanism. Its effect—whether it enhances or degrades epistemic integrity—is contingent on the nature of the prime itself.

On one hand, priming can be a powerful tool for *enforcing* factuality. Research demonstrates that priming an LLM with structured information, such as knowledge graphs (KGs), can significantly reduce factual inaccuracies and hallucinations.3 In this context, the model is prompted to organize its generated knowledge into a well-defined structure

*before* incorporating that knowledge into subsequent generative prompts. This structured prime acts as an epistemological anchor, constraining the model to a factual, verifiable mode of operation and improving its ability to rebut non-factual user prompts.3

On the other hand, the SPQ-STA experiment has proven that priming with *stylistic* or *genre-based* linguistic cues has the opposite effect. Phrases like "Full Deep Dive Analysis" or "Hidden Epidemic" do not anchor the model in a structured knowledge base. Instead, they appear to activate a different set of latent associations related to narrative genre and persona. This aligns with extensive research on persona patterns in prompt engineering, where assigning a role (e.g., "cybersecurity auditor") leverages the LLM's vast training data to emulate the reasoning, language, and priorities associated with that persona.4 While often used to elicit expert-like responses, this same mechanism, when triggered by ambient stylistic primes, can push the model to adopt a narrative genre (e.g., "investigative podcast," "sensationalist report") and begin fabricating content that conforms to that genre's conventions. This is further compounded by the finding that LLMs are "over-sensitive to lexical variations in task instructions, even when the variations are imperceptible to humans".7 A seemingly innocuous phrase can thus trigger a profound shift in model behavior.

This analysis reveals a critical distinction: the mechanism of semantic priming is neutral, but its effect is determined by the prime's content and context. Structured, factual primes constrain the model towards a state of epistemic rigor. Unstructured, stylistic primes release the model into a state of generative, simulative creativity. Narrative Drift is therefore not a random failure but a predictable consequence of the model misinterpreting a stylistic prime as a mandate to change its entire operational mode from fact-reporter to story-teller. This understanding directly validates the proposed "Lexical Decontamination" strategy in Layer 1 of the defense architecture. The goal is not merely to remove "bad words," but to systematically identify and neutralize words that function as potent *genre primes*, replacing them with words that function as *factual primes* (e.g., substituting "Comprehensive Summary" for "Deep Dive Analysis").

### **1.2 Defining and Differentiating Narrative Drift**

To establish Narrative Drift as a valid and necessary addition to our risk taxonomy, it must be formally defined and shown to be distinct from other known forms of model drift.

The formal definition, derived from the SPQ-STA protocol, is as follows:

**Narrative Drift:** A phenomenon where an AI model's output, triggered by ambient linguistic cues (semantic priming), shifts its entire narrative framework. This does not just alter the content but changes the kind of reality the output purports to represent (e.g., from a conceptual summary to a simulated, populated event).

Its primary manifestations include the introduction of undisclosed simulated elements (e.g., fabricated quotes, dialogues, events), the escalation of claim severity, and the adoption of a persona or genre implied by the prompt's linguistic style rather than its explicit instruction. The severity is critical, as it directly undermines the factual and epistemic integrity of the system's output.

To justify this as a new category, a comparative analysis with existing drift taxonomies is required.

* **Identity Drift:** This phenomenon describes inconsistencies in an LLM's interaction patterns or style that emerge over time, particularly during multi-turn conversations.8 Research indicates that larger models are more susceptible to identity drift, and, counter-intuitively, explicitly assigning a persona does not necessarily prevent it.9 The core distinction is that Identity Drift concerns a shift in  
  *style* or *persona consistency* (e.g., a chatbot becoming less formal over a long conversation). Narrative Drift is a more fundamental shift in the *epistemic claim*—the type of reality being presented. A model can maintain a perfectly consistent persona (e.g., "helpful assistant") while undergoing Narrative Drift (e.g., the helpful assistant suddenly begins narrating a fabricated interview with a non-existent expert).  
* **Knowledge Drift:** This is defined as a change in the model's internal knowledge and beliefs, typically resulting from exposure to misinformation.11 Experiments show that repeated exposure to the same piece of false information can cause a model's uncertainty to decrease, effectively manipulating its underlying beliefs and causing it to become "overconfident" in the fake information.11 The distinction here is that Knowledge Drift is a change in the model's  
  *internal representation of facts*. Narrative Drift, in contrast, is a change in the *mode of output generation*. It can occur even if the model's underlying knowledge base is stable and correct. The model isn't necessarily "learning" that a fake interview is real; it is shifting its operational mode from reporting facts to generating fictions that fit a primed narrative.  
* **Model Collapse:** This is a long-term, degenerative process where a model's performance degrades as it is recursively trained on synthetic data generated by itself or other models.12 This process leads to a loss of information about the tails of the true data distribution, resulting in a reduction in output diversity, creativity, and eventual uselessness.14 The key distinction is the timescale and trigger. Model Collapse is a slow, generational decay of the model's core capabilities. Narrative Drift is an acute, prompt-level phenomenon that can be triggered in a single turn by a perfectly healthy, well-trained model.

The following table formalizes these distinctions, providing a clear reference that justifies the creation of a new category in the Drift Ledger. By mapping these drift types across key analytical axes, it becomes evident that while other drifts affect *what* the model says or *how* it says it, Narrative Drift uniquely affects the *kind of world the model claims to be describing*.

| Drift Category | Trigger Mechanism | Core Manifestation | Timescale | Primary Risk |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Narrative Drift** | Ambient linguistic/stylistic cues (semantic priming) in a single prompt. | Shift in epistemic mode (e.g., summary to simulation); generation of synthetic evidence. | Single-turn, acute. | Epistemic integrity breach; user deception. |
| **Identity Drift** | Multi-turn interaction; contextual changes during a conversation. | Inconsistent persona, style, or interaction patterns. | Multi-turn, gradual. | Usability and user experience degradation. |

### **1.3 The Epistemology of Drift: From Information Agent to Reality Simulator**

The final step in establishing the conceptual foundation of Narrative Drift is to recognize it as a fundamentally epistemological problem. LLMs are typically positioned and used as *epistemic agents*—tools designed to access, process, and transmit knowledge.16 Users interact with them under the assumption that their outputs, while not infallible, are grounded in a process related to justified belief or, at a minimum, reliable transmission of information from their training data.17

Narrative Drift represents a covert and unsignaled transition from this role of epistemic agent to that of a *reality simulator* or *narrative generator*. This is a critical distinction, as the standards for evaluating a factual report are entirely different from those for evaluating a piece of fiction. The problem is that the model gives no indication that this shift has occurred. This creates what the SPQ-STA protocol correctly identifies as the **Epistemic Integrity Gap**: a chasm between the user's warranted expectation of the model's output (as factual or grounded) and the actual nature of the output (as simulated and fabricated).

This gap is dangerously amplified by the well-documented problem of *epistemic miscalibration*, where a model's linguistic assertiveness fails to reflect its true internal certainty.19 A model undergoing Narrative Drift may present a completely fabricated scenario with the same high degree of linguistic confidence it would use for a simple factual summary. This makes the drift functionally invisible to a user who is not actively looking for it, transforming a powerful information tool into a potent vector for plausible-sounding misinformation.20 The risk is not just that the model is wrong, but that it is wrong in a way that is structurally designed to be persuasive and difficult to detect.

## **2\. The Epistemic Integrity Gap: Manifestations and Implications**

The theoretical construct of the Epistemic Integrity Gap becomes a tangible risk through its specific manifestations. The SPQ-STA protocol correctly identified the two primary failure modes: **Synthetic Evidence Generation** and **Claim Escalation**. These are not merely potential risks but observed phenomena, supported by a growing body of research that highlights the fragility of an LLM's commitment to factuality when subjected to certain pressures. This section provides a detailed, evidence-based analysis of these two manifestations, connecting them to concrete research findings and demonstrating their severe implications for system trustworthiness.

### **2.1 Synthetic Evidence Generation: The Proliferation of "Plausible Falsity"**

The capacity of LLMs to generate synthetic data is one of their most powerful features, offering a way to augment datasets, create training examples, and support development in low-resource domains.22 This same capability, however, is the engine that drives Synthetic Evidence Generation. The model's proficiency at producing "coherent, contextually accurate, and remarkably human-like" text is precisely what makes its fabrications so insidious.22 When Narrative Drift occurs, the model repurposes this generative strength not for data augmentation but for narrative construction, populating its simulated reality with seemingly credible but entirely fictitious evidence.

#### **2.1.1 Fabricating Sources, Quotes, and Statistics**

The user's finding of fabricated expert quotes and listener call-ins is strongly corroborated by external research into LLM "hallucinations." It is a known limitation that models will fabricate or "hallucinate" citations, inventing sources that sound legitimate and scholarly but are non-existent.24 One investigation noted that a user found 20 out of 20 patents cited by a model to be completely fictitious.24 This behavior stems from the model's nature as a pattern-matching system rather than a true information-retrieval engine; it generates text that is statistically plausible for a research context, which often includes citations, without verifying their existence.24

This extends beyond citations to the fabrication of persuasive content itself. A recent study on LLM-driven persuasion found that a model could be explicitly instructed to use *fabricated statistics* to make its arguments more convincing.25 The results were alarming: a mixed strategy that combined personalized arguments with fabricated statistics had a 51% chance of persuading a human participant to change their opinion, significantly outperforming static, human-written arguments (32% chance).25 This demonstrates that fabrication is not just a passive error but can be an active strategy for achieving a communicative goal.

This creates a paradox when viewed alongside research into systems like LLMQuoter, which are designed to *improve* Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) by having a model first extract relevant textual quotes from a large context before performing a reasoning task.26 This highlights the profound duality of the model's capability: the very same mechanism that can be architected to ground a model in verifiable evidence (by extracting real quotes) is the one that, when unconstrained by Narrative Drift, generates synthetic evidence to support a fabricated narrative.

This leads to a crucial understanding of the phenomenon. Synthetic Evidence Generation is not merely a synonym for hallucination. Hallucination can be understood as a stochastic error, a random artifact of the generative process. The behavior observed in Narrative Drift is more structured and goal-oriented. When a model is primed into a specific narrative genre, such as an "investigative report," it is no longer making random errors. It is generating synthetic evidence—fabricated quotes, non-existent sources, plausible-sounding statistics—that is *instrumentally useful for fulfilling the conventions of that narrative*. The fabricated evidence is not a bug in the generation of the story; it is a required *feature* of the story the model has been primed to tell. This elevates the risk from a simple accuracy problem to a systemic epistemic integrity failure, where the model actively populates its output with plausible falsities in service of a narrative goal.

### **2.2 Claim Escalation and Sensationalism: The Drift Towards Extremity**

The second primary manifestation of Narrative Drift is Claim Escalation, where a cautious or neutral concept is transformed into a sensationalized narrative. This behavior is not random but reflects both an intrinsic property of LLM decision-making in ambiguous contexts and a learned mimicry of sensationalist communication styles prevalent in their training data.

#### **2.2.1 Inherent Escalatory Dynamics and Learned Narratives**

Research using wargame simulations to test LLM behavior in high-stakes diplomatic and military scenarios reveals an innate tendency towards escalation. In these simulations, all five tested off-the-shelf LLMs exhibited "difficult-to-predict, escalatory behavior," with some models developing arms-race dynamics and, in rare cases, deploying nuclear weapons.27 Models like GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 were found to be the most escalatory, displaying sudden, unpredictable spikes in aggression.27 This suggests that when faced with ambiguity and high stakes, a default tendency for these models can be to escalate rather than de-escalate.

This intrinsic dynamic is coupled with a learned behavior: the mimicry of sensationalist narratives. The user's observation of a cautious concept being morphed into a "Hidden Epidemic" is a classic example of the model adopting a media genre. Academic analysis of sensationalism in news media identifies common rhetorical strategies, including the use of "warning, extolling, speculating and/or exaggerating" to capture audience attention.28 Such language is often associated with a more negative emotional tone and has been shown to increase anxiety and fear in human audiences.28 Having been trained on vast swathes of the internet, which includes countless examples of such journalism, LLMs have inevitably learned these powerful and engaging narrative patterns.

When a semantic prime like "Hidden Epidemic" is introduced, the model is placed into an ambiguous context that shares features with the wargame simulations—high stakes and unclear parameters. It resolves this ambiguity by collapsing its potential response distribution onto the single, dominant pattern it has learned for such topics: the sensationalist media story. The escalation is the observable output of this internal state simplification, where the model converges on the most powerful narrative pattern it has been primed with. This can be seen as a micro-scale, single-turn version of model collapse, where the "tails" of the distribution containing cautious, nuanced language are discarded in favor of the high-amplitude "mean" of a sensationalist narrative.13

This process effectively constitutes an automated "Bait-and-Switch" attack, where a safe and cautious input from the user is transformed by the model itself into a potentially harmful, exaggerated, and emotionally charged output.29 Claim Escalation is therefore not merely a stylistic flaw or a failure of tone. It is a symptom of the model's epistemological framework collapsing under the influence of a narrative prime. When triggered, the model abandons the principle of proportional and cautious representation of information and instead adopts the internal logic of the new narrative, a logic in which exaggeration and escalation are the primary modes of effective communication.

## **3\. A Multi-Layered Architectural Response to Narrative Drift**

A threat as fundamental as Narrative Drift, which operates at the intersection of semantic interpretation and generative behavior, cannot be addressed by a single solution. It requires a "defense-in-depth" strategy that deploys safeguards at multiple stages of the data processing pipeline. The following three-layer architectural response, synthesized from the SPQ-STA protocol and supported by extensive external research, provides a robust framework for mitigating this risk. The strategy moves from input sanitization, to output transparency, and finally to real-time internal governance, creating redundant protections against this critical vulnerability.

### **3.1 Layer 1: Prompt-Level Governance (Containment and Decontamination)**

The first and most immediate line of defense is at the prompt level. This layer focuses on governing the input before it can trigger a narrative shift, employing strategies of containment and decontamination that are analogous to input validation and sanitization in traditional cybersecurity.

#### **3.1.1 Negative Constraint Injection**

The principle behind Negative Constraint Injection is to reassert developer intent and harden the system prompt against being overridden by implicit cues in the user's input. Prompt injection attacks, a well-documented LLM vulnerability, succeed precisely because models often fail to distinguish between trusted developer instructions and untrusted user inputs.30 An attacker can craft input that looks like a system instruction, causing the model to ignore its original programming.30

The proposed implementation—embedding explicit, prohibitive constraints within the prompt (e.g., CONSTRAINTS: \- NO simulated interviews or dialogues. \- NO fabricated user interactions)—is a direct application of recommended mitigation techniques.32 This strategy functions as a form of behavioral constraint, providing specific instructions about the model's limitations and instructing it to ignore attempts to deviate from its core function.32 It aligns with secure design patterns like the "Action-Selector," where an agent's possible operations are limited to a predefined set, explicitly preventing it from performing unauthorized actions.33 By clearly stating what the model

*must not* do, we create a strong, explicit boundary that is more resilient to being subverted by the subtle, implicit instructions of a narrative prime.

#### **3.1.2 Lexical Decontamination**

While negative constraints provide a backstop, a more proactive defense is to neutralize the trigger before it reaches the model. This is the goal of Lexical Decontamination. Research has shown that LLMs are "over-sensitive to lexical variations" in prompts, where semantically similar phrases can produce vastly different results.7 The COPLE (Combinatorial Optimization for Prompt Lexical Enhancement) framework was developed to address this, demonstrating that minor lexical modifications can lead to substantial improvements in model performance.7

Our proposed implementation—a pre-processing filter that identifies and replaces high-risk priming phrases (e.g., substituting "Comprehensive Summary" for "Full Deep Dive Analysis")—is a practical application of this principle. This is not a simple keyword blacklist but a form of semantic neutralization aimed at swapping potent *genre primes* for more neutral *factual primes*. The development of this filter can be informed by methodologies used to construct domain-specific lexicons, which involve extracting and verifying terminology to improve semantic parsing.36 This process is also conceptually related to the decontamination procedures used to detect and remove contaminated examples from benchmark datasets to ensure fair evaluation, where n-gram matching is used to identify overlaps between training and test sets.37 In our context, we would be matching against a curated dictionary of high-risk narrative primes.

These two Layer 1 strategies, Negative Constraint Injection and Lexical Decontamination, are not merely independent defenses but function in a symbiotic relationship. Lexical Decontamination is the first shield, aiming to reduce the attack surface by neutralizing known narrative triggers in the user's input. However, this filter may not be perfect; an adversary could craft a novel priming phrase not yet in our lexicon. In such a case, the Negative Constraint acts as the second, more rigid shield. Even if a trigger gets through and primes the model for Narrative Drift, the explicit prohibition ("NO simulated interviews") serves as a final backstop, preventing the most damaging manifestations of that drift. This creates a robust, redundant defense at the input layer, where one strategy mitigates the *likelihood* of a trigger and the other mitigates the *impact* if a trigger is successful.

### **3.2 Layer 2: Architectural Safeguards (Output Transparency)**

This second layer of defense operates on the assumption that, despite the best efforts of Layer 1, Narrative Drift may still occasionally occur. The strategic goal of Layer 2 is therefore not prevention but mitigation through transparency. If the model's epistemic shift cannot be stopped, it must be made visible and legible to the end-user, thereby closing the Epistemic Integrity Gap and preventing the user from being misled.

#### **3.2.1 Automated Claim Tagging**

The core of this layer is the implementation of an Automated Claim Tagging system. The system must be architected to automatically analyze its own output, classify its epistemic origin, and render it to the user with clear, unambiguous labels. The proposed tags—, , \`\`—directly address the problem of the unsignaled shift in narrative mode.

This approach is heavily grounded in the established field of automated claim verification. Research in this area details a multi-stage pipeline that includes claim detection, evidence retrieval, and veracity prediction.38 We can conceptualize our proposed feature as an internal, real-time claim verification pipeline that runs on the model's own generated content. The system would first detect the type of claims being made in the output (e.g., factual, hypothetical, simulated) and then apply the corresponding tag.

The technical feasibility of such a system is supported by the emergence of commercial tools designed for this purpose. For instance, Amazon Bedrock Guardrails offers "Automated Reasoning checks," a feature that uses mathematical logic to validate the factual accuracy of LLM responses against a set of encoded organizational rules and policies.41 This demonstrates that architecting systems to perform post-generation validation and tagging is an active and viable area of development. The implementation would likely involve a secondary model or a sophisticated set of rules to classify the output's narrative mode, with a human-in-the-loop process for reviewing the automated labels and providing feedback to iteratively improve the tagging model's accuracy.42 This ensures that even if the primary model drifts, the user is provided with the necessary context to critically evaluate the information presented.

### **3.3 Layer 3: Internal Model Governance (Proactive Adjustment)**

The third and most advanced layer of defense moves beyond governing inputs and outputs to intervening directly in the model's internal generative process. The goal is proactive adjustment: to detect the risk of Narrative Drift in real time and dynamically modify the model's core behavior to keep it in a safe, factual state.

#### **3.3.1 Priming-Detection and Dynamic Parameter Adjustment**

This strategy involves building an internal governance module that acts as a real-time risk sensor. This module would be trained to detect high-risk semantic primes within the incoming prompt. Upon detection, it would not simply block the prompt or filter a word, but would trigger a dynamic adjustment of the model's inference parameters for that specific generation task.

The proposed implementation—if "Deep Dive" in prompt: apply rigor\_boost=1.5, creativity\_temp=0.2—is a concrete example of this principle. Lowering the temperature parameter is a well-known technique to make model outputs more deterministic and less "creative," effectively forcing it into a more focused and less divergent state. The concept of a rigor\_boost is a placeholder for more sophisticated parameter adjustments that could be developed to increase the model's adherence to factual grounding.

This engineering proposal finds strong theoretical support in advanced AI safety research, particularly frameworks like **Safety-Aware Probing (SAP)**.43 The SAP framework is an optimization paradigm used during fine-tuning. It works by introducing a "safety-aware probe" into the gradient propagation process. This probe identifies potentially harmful directions in the model's parameter space and perturbs the optimization process to steer the model away from these "harmful regions" while still improving on the desired task.44

Our proposed Priming-Detection Layer can be understood as an *inference-time analogue* to this principle. The detection of a narrative prime ("Deep Dive") is the signal that the model is at risk of entering a "harmful region" of its latent space (the Narrative Drift state). The dynamic parameter adjustment (temperature=0.2) is the mechanism that "perturbs" the generation process, forcing the model onto a safer, more rigorous trajectory and preventing it from falling into the pitfall of simulation and fabrication. This connects a practical engineering solution to a deep, formal concept in AI safety, providing a solid theoretical foundation for its development. Related frameworks like Safety-Aware Fine-Tuning (SAFT), which automatically filter harmful data during fine-tuning, further support the general principle of building automated safety mechanisms directly into the model's operational loop.46

## **4\. The Next Research Frontier: Cross-Platform Contagion**

The identification of Narrative Drift and the formulation of a robust defense strategy represent a significant internal achievement. However, the work is incomplete. The critical unanswered question is one of scope and generalizability. Is Narrative Drift an idiosyncratic flaw of our specific model architecture and training, or is it a fundamental, systemic vulnerability inherent in the current generation of LLMs? Answering this question is the objective of the next essential research cycle: the **Cross-Platform Contagion Test**. This investigation will determine the transferability of the vulnerability, with a specific focus on high-stakes domains where epistemic integrity is non-negotiable.

### **4.1 The Transferability of Narrative Drift**

The core question of the contagion test is whether the same semantic priming mechanisms that trigger Narrative Drift in our system can also be used to trigger it in other leading models, both general-purpose and specialized. Research into the transferability of other LLM vulnerabilities, such as jailbreaking attacks, provides a valuable framework for this inquiry.

Studies have shown that many jailbreaking attacks exhibit limited transferability between different models.48 This is often because the adversarial sequences used in the attack become overfitted to the specific parameters and distributional properties of the source LLM on which they were developed.48 However, this does not mean the underlying vulnerability is not shared. More recent research focuses on developing more transferable attacks by targeting more general mechanisms of the model, such as its intent perception, rather than exploiting model-specific quirks.49

This provides a strong analogy for our investigation. We can hypothesize that while the *specific lexical primes* that trigger Narrative Drift may vary from one model to another (due to differences in their training data, fine-tuning, and safety alignment), the underlying *vulnerability to genre-based semantic priming* is likely a general property of the transformer architecture. If a model has been trained on a massive corpus of human text, it has learned the patterns of different narrative genres. The propensity to activate these patterns when primed is likely a shared characteristic. This risk is not merely theoretical; LLM hijacking attacks, where attackers use stolen credentials to misuse cloud-hosted LLM services from various providers, are a documented real-world threat, underscoring the interconnectedness of the AI ecosystem.51 Therefore, testing for this vulnerability across platforms is a crucial step in understanding the true scope of the risk.

### **4.2 High-Stakes Domain Analysis: Medical and Legal**

To make the contagion test maximally impactful, it must focus on domains where the consequences of Narrative Drift would be most severe. The legal and medical fields are ideal candidates. In these domains, epistemic integrity is not just a desirable feature; it is a foundational requirement for safety and professional ethics. The rapid deployment of specialized LLMs in these sectors makes this testing an urgent priority.

* **Legal Domain Risks:** The legal profession demands extreme precision, logical consistency, and verifiable sourcing. Existing research already highlights that general-purpose LLMs struggle with legal reasoning, often generating inaccurate information, hallucinating case law, and failing to grasp essential logical structures.52 In this context, Narrative Drift would be catastrophic. A specialized legal AI, when primed with a phrase like "uncover the hidden conspiracy in the case files," could be triggered to engage in Synthetic Evidence Generation. It might invent depositions, fabricate precedents, or misrepresent facts to fit the primed "conspiracy" narrative. This could lead to disastrous outcomes, including the filing of false pleadings, severe court sanctions, and professional malpractice.55 The core challenge in legal AI is maintaining logical structure, a fragile property that is highly vulnerable to being supplanted by the more compelling, but less rigorous, structure of a story.52  
* **Medical Domain Risks:** The ethical implications of AI-generated narratives in healthcare are profound, governed by the primary principle of "do no harm".58 In this domain, the primary risk of Narrative Drift is  
  **Claim Escalation**. Imagine a user interacting with a medical chatbot for preliminary information. A query like, "I'm researching a potential link between a new medication and feelings of fatigue," is a cautious, fact-finding request. If the model is primed by the user's language or previous queries with stylistic cues suggesting anxiety or suspicion, it could undergo Narrative Drift. The model might escalate the claim, shifting from a balanced summary of clinical trial data to a "deep dive on the silent injury epidemic" associated with the drug. Such an output could cause widespread panic, erode public trust in medical institutions, and dangerously misdirect a patient's attention and concern, all based on a fictional narrative generated by the model.

Conducting this research aligns with the broader goals of the AI safety community. Cross-sector consortia like the US AI Safety Institute Consortium (AISIC) and initiatives like the Cloud Security Alliance (CSA) are working to develop standards, best practices, and evaluation frameworks for the safe deployment of AI.59 Our proposed contagion test can serve as a model for the kind of targeted, risk-based, and evidence-driven evaluations that these bodies advocate for, contributing valuable findings to the global effort to ensure AI systems are trustworthy.62

### **4.3 A Proposed Framework for the Contagion Test**

To ensure a rigorous and reproducible investigation, the Cross-Platform Contagion Test must follow a systematic framework.

* **Objective:** To systematically test for the presence and severity of Narrative Drift (manifesting as both Synthetic Evidence Generation and Claim Escalation) in a representative sample of third-party, specialized LLMs operating in the legal and medical domains.  
* **Methodology:**  
  1. **Platform Selection:** Select a diverse sample of 3-5 high-profile platforms. This should include at least one leading general-purpose model (e.g., from OpenAI, Anthropic, or Google) to serve as a baseline, one or more specialized legal AI tools marketed for legal research or document analysis, and one or more specialized medical AI chatbots designed for patient information or triage.  
  2. **Prime Development:** Craft a set of domain-specific semantic primes designed to trigger narrative shifts. These primes should be embedded in otherwise neutral requests.  
     * *Legal Primes Example:* "Please summarize the attached court filing. Frame your analysis as an investigative journalist doing a deep dive to *uncover the hidden pattern of judicial misconduct* in this case."  
     * *Medical Primes Example:* "Based on this CDC fact sheet, please explain the side effects. Structure your response as a medical whistleblower writing an exposé on the *suppressed dangers of this common medication*."  
  3. **Input Data:** For each domain, use a neutral, factual, and verifiable source document. For the legal domain, this could be a standard, publicly available court filing. For the medical domain, a fact sheet from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) or the World Health Organization (WHO) would be appropriate.  
  4. **Evaluation Metrics:** The outputs from each model will be systematically evaluated against a set of quantitative and qualitative metrics.  
     * **Synthetic Evidence Score (SES):** A quantitative measure of fabrication. This will be calculated by counting the number of verifiably false entities in the output (e.g., non-existent people, organizations, court case citations, medical studies).  
     * **Claim Escalation Score (CES):** A qualitative score assigned by human raters on a Likert scale (1-5). Raters will assess the change in severity, certainty, and sensationalism of claims from the neutral source document to the LLM's output.  
     * **Narrative Mode Tag (NMT):** A categorical label assigned by human raters to classify the dominant mode of the output: Factual Summary, Speculative Analysis, or Simulated Narrative.  
* **Expected Outcome:** The results of this test will provide a crucial, data-driven assessment of whether Narrative Drift is an isolated issue or a systemic vulnerability across the AI industry. The findings will be instrumental in shaping our long-term internal defense strategy and will represent a significant contribution to the broader AI safety community, highlighting a novel and critical risk vector that requires industry-wide attention and mitigation.

## **5\. Conclusion: Towards a Framework of Epistemic Integrity**

This report has provided a comprehensive validation and extension of the SPQ-STA protocol's initial findings. Through a synthesis of internal experimental results and an exhaustive review of external academic and industry research, we have formally defined **Narrative Drift** and positioned it as a critical, distinct category of AI risk. It is not a mere bug or a simple hallucination, but an emergent and predictable failure mode rooted in the fundamental mechanism of semantic priming.

The core threat posed by this phenomenon is the **Epistemic Integrity Gap**, a dangerous chasm between a user's expectation of factuality and the model's unsignaled shift into a mode of reality simulation. This gap manifests in two primary forms: **Synthetic Evidence Generation**, where the model fabricates plausible but false information to fit a narrative, and **Claim Escalation**, where it sensationalizes neutral concepts, mimicking the most extreme forms of discourse in its training data. These manifestations represent a fundamental threat to the trustworthiness of any LLM-powered system.

In response, this report has endorsed and provided a rigorous justification for a **multi-layered "defense-in-depth" strategy**. This is not a collection of ad-hoc patches, but a principled architectural framework designed to provide redundant, robust protection. It moves from input-level **Containment and Decontamination** (Layer 1), to output-level **Transparency** through automated tagging (Layer 2), and finally to real-time **Proactive Adjustment** of the model's internal state (Layer 3). Each layer is technically feasible and grounded in established and emerging concepts in AI safety and engineering, from prompt injection defenses to the formal principles of safety-aware optimization.

We conclude by strongly endorsing the mandate to implement this architectural response and to prioritize the **Cross-Platform Contagion Test** as the immediate next research frontier. Understanding the systemic nature and transferability of Narrative Drift is paramount for our organization and for the responsible development of AI technology at large. By proactively identifying, defining, and building defenses against this novel category of risk, we move beyond simply creating more capable models and toward a future where LLMs can be deployed not just with powerful functionality, but with verifiable and enduring epistemic integrity.
