

# **Ritual Baseline: The Choreography of Invisible Structure**

## **Part I: Theoretical Foundations: The Basement of Culture**

This report outlines the theoretical and curatorial framework for *Ritual Baseline: The Choreography of Invisible Structure*, an exhibition concept that stages the daily, repetitive rituals of organizational and cultural life as works of performance art. The project synthesizes two fundamental concepts: "foundation," understood as the habitual, often unseen acts that provide structural support, and "exhibition," the deliberate and performative act of display. By choreographing the mundane—corporate meetings, safety checklists, hospitality routines, athletic warm-ups—the exhibition aims to render visible the invisible labor that constitutes the true structural work of society. It elevates maintenance to the level of creation and exposes the foundational routines that underpin all social structures. This first part establishes the core conceptual framework, arguing that these mundane rituals are not merely functional but are the foundational, often unconscious, structures that support our social reality. It blends architectural metaphor, psychoanalytic theory, anthropology, and feminist art history to construct a robust intellectual "basement" for the project.

### **Section 1: Excavating the Ritual Baseline**

The central metaphor of the exhibition is that of the "basement" as a site of foundational, yet often hidden, activity. This section connects the physical architecture of a foundation to the psychological structure of the unconscious and the sociological function of ritual, thereby excavating the conceptual ground upon which the exhibition is built.

#### **The Architectural & Psychological Metaphor of the Subterranean**

The title *Ritual Baseline* invokes a dual meaning of "basement" and "foundation" that is both architectural and psychological. Architecturally, a basement is a structural necessity, an integral part of the foundation that "helps keep the structure stable and safe to live in".1 It is the subterranean support system upon which the visible edifice of a building rests. Psychologically, this physical structure serves as a powerful metaphor for the deeper, often hidden, layers of the human psyche. The basement represents a space of unspoken emotions, hidden truths, and formative experiences that, while not always in conscious view, fundamentally shape the life lived in the rooms above.2

This metaphorical connection was most profoundly articulated by the psychoanalyst Carl Jung, who used a dream of a multi-storey house to develop his theory of the collective unconscious.3 In Jung's dream, the salon, or ground floor, represented "ego-consciousness"—the space of our daily lives, populated with the objects and ideas we wish to present to the world. However, a descent into the basement revealed progressively older layers: a medieval level, then a Roman vault, and finally a prehistoric cave cut into the bedrock, filled with ancient remains. Jung interpreted this architectural journey downward as a journey inward, demonstrating that our conscious life is "built upon older layers" of intellectual history, cultural memory, and, at its deepest level, a shared, primal psychic foundation.3 The lowest level, the cave, symbolized the "collective unconscious," the impersonal and universal stratum of the psyche that underpins all individual and cultural expression.3

The exhibition's title, *Basement of Rituals*, deliberately positions the mundane acts of modern life within this Jungian framework. The daily stand-up meeting, the pre-flight checklist, the hotel turndown service—these are the collective, often un-theorized, "prehistoric" foundations of our contemporary organizational and cultural lives. They are the rituals that reside in the basement of our social structures, operating largely outside of conscious analysis yet providing the essential stability for the "house" of society to stand. This concept is further reinforced by the architectural metaphor of a house as a life, supported by pillars of resilience; the strength of these unseen supports is critical for withstanding strain and maintaining structural integrity.4 Literary explorations, such as Graham Greene's short story "The Basement Room," enrich this metaphor by portraying the basement as a "vessel brimming with unspoken emotion" and a "crucible of innocence lost and secrets unearthed," highlighting the potent emotional and sometimes transgressive power that resides in these hidden, foundational spaces.2

#### **Ritual as Social Foundation: From Durkheim to the Modern Organization**

Transitioning from the metaphorical basement to the sociological "foundation" it contains, the exhibition focuses on ritual as the primary mechanism of social construction. The analysis of ritual in this context draws upon a century of anthropological and sociological scholarship, beginning with the foundational work of Émile Durkheim. In *The Elementary Forms of Religious Life*, Durkheim explained the creation of "mass social consensus through religious ritual and ceremony".5 For Durkheim, ritual is not merely a set of symbolic gestures; it is the very process through which a society affirms its collective values and integrates individuals into a cohesive whole. Ritual mediates between the individual and the social, bringing potentially opposing forces together and reinforcing shared norms.5

While Durkheim argued that modern, secular life was less able to establish this symbolic consensus than "primitive" societies, subsequent scholars have demonstrated the enduring power of ritual in contemporary contexts.5 The anthropologist Arnold van Gennep, in his seminal work

*The Rites of Passage*, contested Durkheim's claim, arguing that rites were "equally important in social transitions in the ‘urban world’".5 Van Gennep's most crucial contribution was his identification of a universal, tripartite structure underlying all rituals of transition. This structure consists of three phases:

1. **Pre-liminal (Separation):** The individual is removed from their previous social role or status.  
2. **Liminal (Transition):** The individual exists in an ambiguous, in-between state, temporarily devoid of a socially accepted identity.  
3. **Post-liminal (Incorporation):** The individual is formally integrated into their new role, endowed with a new identity and status.5

This powerful analytical framework is not limited to traditional ceremonies like marriage or initiation. Organizational scholars have explicitly applied it to the secular rituals of modern institutions, identifying equivalents such as "selection, promotion, turnover" as contemporary rites of passage.5 The fundamental purpose of these rituals, whether sacred or secular, is to manage change and "restore equilibrium to the social order" in the face of constant flux.5 For the purposes of this exhibition, ritual is therefore defined, following the work of Harrison M. Trice and Janice M. Beyer, as "a form of social action in which a group's values and identity are publicly demonstrated or enacted in a stylized manner, within the context of a specific occasion or event".5 This definition allows for the inclusion of seemingly non-symbolic acts like formal speeches, new employee dinners, or daily team meetings, recognizing them as potent cultural performances. The study of these seemingly mundane organizational rites provides a unique window into the culture, offering access to "compressed versions of cultural forms" that would otherwise be difficult to observe.5

The seemingly banal rituals of modern corporate life, such as employee onboarding processes or daily stand-up meetings, are not simply functional procedures designed for efficiency. A deeper analysis reveals them to be direct, secularized descendants of the sacred, tripartite "rites of passage" identified by Van Gennep. Their primary function extends far beyond mere task management; it is fundamentally about the reinforcement of social order, the management of individual transitions within a collective, and the creation of shared meaning. Van Gennep's model describes a universal structure for managing "life crises" or role transitions, composed of separation, a liminal state, and re-incorporation, with the ultimate purpose of restoring social equilibrium.5 Organizational scholars have explicitly mapped this anthropological structure onto corporate processes: the hiring process acts as a pre-liminal phase of separation from a previous identity (e.g., "job seeker"); the training period functions as a liminal phase of transition and ambiguity; and the full delegation of responsibility marks the post-liminal phase of incorporation into a new role (e.g., "employee").5

Even a micro-ritual like the daily Agile stand-up meeting can be understood through this lens.7 Each day, team members ritually separate from their individual, isolated tasks. They enter a brief but potent liminal state of collective reporting, answering the prescribed three questions—"What did I do yesterday? What will I do today? What blocks my progress?"—which temporarily suspends their individual work identities in favor of a collective one. Following this, they are re-incorporated into the team with a renewed sense of shared purpose and alignment for the day's work. Therefore, to choreograph and exhibit a daily stand-up meeting is not merely to stage a scene from office life; it is to stage a modern, compressed, and daily enactment of a fundamental anthropological structure designed to maintain social cohesion. The exhibition, through aesthetic means, makes this hidden sociological function visible, revealing the deep ritual grammar that underlies even the most apparently pragmatic corporate routines.

### **Section 2: The Maintenance Manifesto: Art in the Unseen**

Having established the sociological and psychological foundations of ritual, this section introduces the core artistic and political thesis of the exhibition. This thesis is grounded in the pioneering work of artist Mierle Laderman Ukeles and her concept of "Maintenance Art," which is here connected to a broader sociological critique of invisible and devalued labor. This framework provides the critical lens through which the exhibition re-evaluates the cultural significance of mundane, repetitive work.

#### **Development vs. Maintenance: Ukeles's Revolutionary Dichotomy**

The central artistic and political argument of *Ritual Baseline* is articulated most forcefully in Mierle Laderman Ukeles's "Manifesto for Maintenance Art 1969\!".9 Written in a single sitting shortly after the birth of her first child, the manifesto was an act of profound intellectual and personal integration, born from the frustration of her life being "divided in half by the opposing roles of mother and artist".11 Ukeles's revolutionary gesture was to propose a fundamental dichotomy that structures all human activity: the division between "Development" and "Maintenance."

"Development," in her schema, represents everything that is culturally valorized: "pure individual creation; the new; change; progress, advance, excitement, flight or fleeing".9 It is the realm of the "artist-genius," the "Avant-Garde par excellence," whose defining characteristic is originality and the refusal to repeat oneself.10 In opposition to this, Ukeles posits "Maintenance," the vast, necessary, and culturally devalued sphere of activity that supports development: "keep the dust off the pure individual creation; preserve the new; sustain the change; protect progress... repeat the flight".9 Maintenance is the work of perpetuation, of keeping things going, of what she terms the "Life Instinct" as opposed to the "Death Instinct" of pure, individualistic change.9

Ukeles's radical act was not simply to identify this dichotomy but to collapse it by declaring that her own maintenance work *is* art. In her proposal for an exhibition titled "CARE," she stated her intention to perform her everyday domestic chores within the museum as public art activities: "I will simply do these maintenance everyday things, and flush them up to consciousness, exhibit them, as Art... My working will be the work".10 This declaration directly challenges the foundational tenets of the avant-garde, which valorize originality and freedom from repetition.11 By elevating the repetitive, the mundane, and the necessary to the status of art, Ukeles performs a radical inversion of cultural value. Her proposed exhibition, with its three parts—personal (performing her own domestic labor), general (interviewing maintenance workers), and earth maintenance (exhibiting refuse and polluted materials)—provides a direct and powerful curatorial model for

*Ritual Baseline*.10

#### **Making the Invisible Visible: Maintenance Art as Sociological Intervention**

Ukeles's manifesto is not only an artistic statement but also a sharp sociological critique. She explicitly identifies the social and economic devaluation of maintenance labor: "Maintenance is a drag; it takes all the fucking time (lit.)... The culture confers lousy status on maintenance jobs \= minimum wages, housewives=no pay".9 Her work seeks to "flush up to consciousness" the vast amount of labor that is systematically rendered invisible precisely because it is associated with the domestic sphere, with women, and with low-status occupations.10

This artistic critique aligns perfectly with the sociological concept of "invisible work." The term, originally coined by sociologist Arlene Daniels to describe unpaid household and volunteer labor, has since been expanded to encompass any activities within the context of paid employment that go "unmarked, unnoticed, and uncompensated".12 The pioneering research of scholars like Arlie Russell Hochschild on "emotional labor"—the management of feeling to create a publicly observable facial and bodily display—and Evelyn Nakano Glenn on the racial and gendered dimensions of paid reproductive labor provides a robust theoretical framework for understanding this phenomenon.13 Their collective work demonstrates a crucial social principle: "work that is not seen is not valued, either symbolically or materially".12 This invisibility has profound consequences, leading to poor working conditions, low or no pay, and a lack of political recognition or support for the workers who perform these essential tasks.12

Ukeles's artistic practice can therefore be understood as a series of powerful political and sociological interventions. Her performances, such as *Washing/Tracks/Maintenance: Outside* (1973), in which she meticulously scrubbed the exterior steps and plaza of the Wadsworth Atheneum museum, are not merely aesthetic gestures.17 They are acts of making visible. By performing this devalued, feminized labor in the highly visible and sanctified context of the art museum, she forces a confrontation with the hidden labor that maintains not only our homes and cities but also our most revered cultural institutions. Her decades-long, unpaid residency with the New York City Department of Sanitation further underscores her commitment to embedding her artistic practice within the very systems of maintenance she seeks to illuminate.9

*Ritual Baseline* is conceived not merely as an observational or aesthetic project, but as a fundamentally corrective one. By applying the focused lens of artistic creation and exhibition to the mundane rituals of maintenance, the project actively intervenes in the cultural and economic systems of valuation that systematically render such labor invisible. It performs a "re-valuation" by transposing acts of maintenance from the devalued sphere of what Ukeles called the "Life Instinct" (unification, perpetuation, equilibrium) into the celebrated sphere of "Development" (creation, the new, progress).9 In doing so, the exhibition seeks to collapse the very dichotomy that Ukeles so brilliantly identified, arguing that maintenance

*is* a creative and structurally vital act.

The logic of this intervention is straightforward. Ukeles's manifesto and subsequent sociological research establish a clear cultural hierarchy: Development, associated with art and progress, is highly valued, while Maintenance, associated with cleaning and repetition, is devalued.9 This devaluation has tangible material consequences, as sociologists have shown, linking the invisibility of certain forms of labor—often performed by women and people of color—to low pay, poor conditions, and a lack of political power.12 The art exhibition, particularly within a major museum, is one of society's primary mechanisms for conferring value. An object, an idea, or an action, when placed within this institutional frame, is elevated, analyzed, and designated as culturally significant.

*Ritual Baseline* proposes to take acts of pure maintenance—a safety checklist, a corporate meeting, a cleaning routine—and frame them as choreographed artworks within this very space of cultural valuation. This act of transposition forces the audience to see a "maintenance job" not as a low-status necessity but as a complex, choreographed, and structurally vital performance. The exhibition thus uses the mechanism of cultural valuation (the exhibition) to critique and correct the cultural devaluation of maintenance. It compels a fundamental reconsideration of where cultural value is located, where it should be placed, and how the invisible labor of repetition forms the true foundation of all creative and social progress.

## **Part II: Artistic Precedents: A Vocabulary of the Mundane**

This part of the report builds the aesthetic and historical art context for the exhibition. It moves from the broad theoretical foundations established in Part I to the specific artistic languages and strategies that provide the necessary tools to translate mundane rituals into compelling artworks. It identifies key movements and practitioners—from the pioneers of postmodern dance who first aestheticized everyday movement, to the critical artists who turned the museum itself into a site of performance and critique—that together form a coherent artistic lineage for *Ritual Baseline*.

### **Section 3: The Neutral Body: Choreographing Pedestrian Acts**

This section establishes the essential choreographic language for the exhibition by looking to the revolutionary work of the Judson Dance Theater in the 1960s. The artists associated with this movement were the first to systematically strip dance of its theatrical and expressive conventions, turning instead to the movements of everyday life and the logic of task-based action. Their work provides a rich and directly applicable vocabulary for staging the functional, non-expressive rituals of organizational life.

#### **The Judson Dance Theater Revolution**

The performances and workshops that took place at the Judson Memorial Church in New York City in the early 1960s represent a pivotal moment in the history of performance. Artists like Yvonne Rainer, Steve Paxton, Trisha Brown, and Simone Forti instigated a radical break from the traditions of both classical ballet and expressive modern dance.19 They sought to move away from formal stylization, emotional narrative, and technical virtuosity, turning their attention instead to what Rainer termed the "'factual' quality of manual labour and task, and an aspiration to 'neutral doing'".19

The core of this revolution was the incorporation of "'found' movements from daily life" into the choreographic field.19 Simple, pedestrian actions such as walking, running, standing, sitting, eating, and lifting objects became the fundamental building blocks of their dances. By staging these mundane movements in non-traditional spaces like gymnasiums, galleries, and the church itself, these artists deliberately tested and blurred the boundary between ordinary behavior and art.19 This radical redefinition of what could constitute a dance provides a direct historical and aesthetic precedent for the project of choreographing the rituals of contemporary organizational life. The Judson artists created a new conception of choreography as "ordinary dance," a language perfectly suited to exploring the ordinary rituals that are the subject of this exhibition.19

#### **Rainer and Paxton: A Vocabulary for Task-Based Performance**

Within the Judson collective, the work of Yvonne Rainer and Steve Paxton is particularly crucial for developing a vocabulary for *Ritual Baseline*. Rainer's choreography, in particular, provides a sophisticated model for structuring task-based performances. In her 1963 piece *We Shall Run*, she used a group of twelve dancers and non-dancers who simply ran in various patterns for seven minutes, performing tasks without a single leading figure.20 The focus was on the collective execution of a simple, sustained action. Her most famous work,

*Trio A* (1966), is a complex, four-and-a-half-minute sequence of distinct, un-repeated movements performed with a continuous and unaccented flow of energy. In this piece, "no part is more important than the other," creating a "continuous stream" of action that deconstructs the traditional choreographic emphasis on climax and dynamic variation.20 This aesthetic of non-hierarchical, continuous, and task-like action is perfectly suited to representing the endless, repetitive, and functional nature of maintenance rituals.

Steve Paxton's work, developing in dialogue with Rainer's, was similarly focused on dismantling the hierarchies inherent in traditional dance. He did this by presenting overtly everyday activities as the dance itself. In his 1961 piece *Proxy*, the performers, including Rainer, engaged in legibly ordinary actions such as eating a pear, drinking a glass of water, and, for the most part, simply walking.21 Paxton's stated goal was to make the "ordinary," which is normally "invisible" because our senses tune it out, visible and available for examination.21 He sought to bring attention to the vast majority of our bodily experience that occurs outside the heightened focus of formal dance practice.

While both artists utilized everyday movement and a neutral facial expression, their approaches offered a subtle but important spectrum of possibilities.21 Rainer's choreography, while focused on tasks, often revealed a "softer body... simultaneously confident and vulnerable," allowing for moments of involuntary movement and the quiet emotionality of a body engaged in an activity.21 Paxton, by contrast, aimed for a more "resolute, boring one," stripping the actions of emotional content to present a more conceptual, minimalist vision of the body as a neutral vehicle for ideas.21 This range, from the subtly human and vulnerable to the purely mechanical and conceptual, provides a rich palette of choreographic strategies for the various rituals to be staged in the exhibition.

The aesthetic of "neutral doing," as developed by Yvonne Rainer and her peers at the Judson Dance Theater, is the essential artistic key to unlocking the performative potential of organizational rituals.19 This approach allows the exhibition to present these acts without imposing an artificial or external dramatic narrative, thereby focusing the audience's attention squarely on the pure form, rhythm, and structural function of the ritual itself. Organizational rituals, such as a daily safety check or a corporate status meeting, are typically performed without expressive intent; their purpose is functional and task-oriented, aimed at completion rather than emotional communication. Applying the conventions of traditional dance or theatre, which rely on expressivity, narrative arcs, and climactic moments, would feel inauthentic and would obscure the very nature of the ritual being examined.20

Rainer's choreography provides a powerful alternative. She explicitly rejected these theatrical elements in favor of the "factual" quality of tasks. Her concept of the "neutral" performer—one who avoids direct eye contact with the audience and strives to be a "neutral mover"—is a radical departure from the charismatic presence of the traditional dancer or actor.19 This aesthetic of "neutral doing" perfectly mirrors the psychological and physical state of a person performing a routine, habitual task. The focus is internal, on the correct execution of a sequence of actions, not on external projection. By adopting this aesthetic, the exhibition can present a ritual like a daily scrum meeting not as a piece of theatre

*about* an office, but as a direct transposition of the ritual's own inherent, embodied choreography. The artistry lies in the acts of framing, focusing, and repetition, not in the addition of external drama. This strategy allows the invisible structural work of the ritual itself to emerge as the primary subject and aesthetic object of the performance.

### **Section 4: The Museum as a Stage: Performance and Institutional Critique**

Having established a choreographic language for the mundane, this section situates the exhibition within its proposed venue: the museum. This context is not neutral. The act of placing live, ephemeral, and ritualistic performances within an institution historically dedicated to the preservation of static objects is a significant gesture. This section argues that *Ritual Baseline* functions not just as a display of art but as a sophisticated act of Institutional Critique, a mode of artistic practice that turns a critical lens onto the museum itself, exposing its own hidden rituals, power structures, and economies of labor.

#### **Performance Art's Entry into the "White Cube"**

The history of performance art is, in many ways, a history of its complex and often antagonistic relationship with the museum. Throughout the twentieth century, performance was often seen as a radical, non-traditional way of making art, offering artists alternatives to the static permanence and commodity status of painting and sculpture through its emphasis on liveness, physical movement, and impermanence.22 Early avant-garde movements like Futurism and Dada incorporated provocative performances intended to scandalize the public and encourage audience interaction.22 However, it was not until the post-war period that performance began to systematically enter the institutional space of the "white cube."

Institutions like the Museum of Modern Art (MoMA) and the Tate Modern gradually began to accommodate and even commission live, time-based works.23 MoMA showed performances intermittently through the 1960s, while the Tate's engagement with performance began in earnest in the early 2000s, emerging organically from the museum's work with a younger generation of artists interested in choreographing social relations.23 This historical trajectory has established the museum as a contested but ultimately viable and potent stage for performance. Exhibitions like MoMA's "Performing Histories (1)" (2012-13) demonstrate how contemporary museums now use performance as a critical tool to "reflect on interpretations of history," "deconstruct histories," and examine "how the past is constructed".24

*Ritual Baseline* extends this critical function, using performance to deconstruct not only the past but also the construction of our present social and organizational realities.

#### **Institutional Critique: Turning the Lens Inward**

Parallel to the rise of performance art, another critical artistic movement emerged in the late 1960s and 1970s: Institutional Critique. Artists associated with this movement began to "systematically inquire into the workings of art institutions," such as galleries and museums, which had previously been accepted as neutral backdrops for the display of art.25 Artists like Hans Haacke, Daniel Buren, Michael Asher, and Andrea Fraser developed innovative strategies to expose the hidden power structures, economic underpinnings, and ideological biases of these institutions.25

Their methods were diverse, including site-specific installations, architectural alterations, text-based works, and performative gestures intended to disrupt the "transparent operations of galleries and museums".26 Hans Haacke, for example, created works that detailed the controversial financial and political histories of museum trustees and donors, leading to the cancellation of his 1971 exhibition at the Guggenheim.27 Andrea Fraser's performances are a particularly relevant precedent; she often adopts the roles of various art world actors, such as museum docents or collectors, using their own language and rituals to expose the power dynamics, class biases, and internal contradictions of the art world from within.25 These artists demonstrated that the institution is not just a container for art but is itself a subject for art, a complex system of rituals and power relations worthy of critical investigation.

#### **"Ritual Baseline" as Institutional Critique**

*Ritual Baseline* is conceived as a powerful act of what might be termed second-wave Institutional Critique. It builds directly on the legacy of the movement's pioneers, particularly Mierle Laderman Ukeles, a key figure for this exhibition who is explicitly associated with Institutional Critique.26 Her work consistently blurred the lines between maintenance labor and artistic practice, often using the museum itself as her primary site and subject.

Her 1973 performance at the Wadsworth Atheneum, *TRANSFER: THE MAINTENANCE OF THE ART OBJECT*, provides a direct and profound model for *Ritual Baseline*. The piece involved the ritual cleaning of a glass case containing an ancient Egyptian mummy. Ukeles choreographed a sequence of three cleansings: first by the museum's regular maintenance person, then by the artist herself (who stamped the case with her "Maintenance Art Original" stamp, transforming it into art), and finally by a trained conservator. This simple act brilliantly exposed the different systems of value, labor, and authority that coexist, often invisibly, within the museum. The work of the maintenance person is necessary but devalued; the touch of the artist confers aesthetic and market value; the work of the conservator confers historical and scientific value.11

The exhibition can extend this critical strategy by staging not only rituals from the external corporate or industrial world but also the museum's own foundational maintenance routines. This would involve choreographing and presenting the prescribed paths of security guards, the systematic tasks of the nightly cleaning crew, the procedural checklists of the art handlers and preparators, or even the administrative rituals of curatorial budget meetings. By turning its lens inward, the exhibition implicates the institution in the very economies of invisible labor it seeks to analyze.

The most potent critical gesture of *Ritual Baseline* is its capacity for self-implication. By staging the museum's own foundational rituals alongside those from other sectors, the exhibition can avoid a significant potential pitfall of Institutional Critique: the tendency to position the artist and the art world as neutral or superior observers of external cultures (corporate, industrial, etc.). This move directly addresses the criticism that artists engaged in institutional critique often benefit from the very institutions they purport to critique, creating a comfortable and ultimately toothless feedback loop.26 Mierle Laderman Ukeles provided a powerful model for overcoming this dilemma. By becoming the official, unpaid artist-in-residence at the New York City Department of Sanitation, she embedded herself within the system she was studying.17 Similarly, by performing the act of cleaning the museum herself, she implicated the art institution directly in her critique of devalued labor.11

*Ritual Baseline* can adopt this strategy of self-implication as a core curatorial principle. For example, if a durational performance of a corporate "daily stand-up" meeting is staged in Gallery A, a precisely choreographed performance of the museum's own security team's morning briefing could be staged in Gallery B. If an installation documents the ritual of a hotel turndown service in one space, another could feature a video piece documenting the nightly routine of the museum's cleaning staff. This creates a powerful self-implicating loop. The audience is prevented from viewing the corporate or industrial ritual as something "other" or exotic, because the exhibition forces them to recognize that the very ground they stand on—the institution they currently inhabit—is supported by identical forms of invisible, repetitive, and ritualized labor. The critique is not directed "out there" at a separate world; it is located "in here," within the walls of the museum itself. This makes the exhibition's political and social argument more profound, unavoidable, and honest.

## **Part III: Curatorial Framework: Staging the Baseline**

This final part of the report moves from theory and history to practice. It outlines the specific curatorial philosophy that will guide the exhibition's relationship with its audience and provides concrete examples of how the "raw material" of contemporary rituals can be translated into compelling artworks. This section serves as a practical blueprint for realizing the conceptual and critical ambitions of *Ritual Baseline*, transforming it from a theoretical proposition into a tangible and impactful exhibition experience.

### **Section 5: The Social Encounter: A Relational Aesthetic**

This section defines the exhibition's mode of address to its audience, framing the gallery not as a space for passive contemplation of objects but as an active, social environment for shared experience and collective meaning-making. This approach is essential for an exhibition centered on live, durational, and participatory forms.

#### **Art as Social Context**

The curatorial approach for *Ritual Baseline* will be grounded in the principles of "Relational Aesthetics," a theoretical framework developed by French curator Nicolas Bourriaud in the 1990s. Bourriaud defined this tendency in contemporary art as "a set of artistic practices which take as their theoretical and practical point of departure the whole of human relations and their social context, rather than an independent and private space".29 This framework shifts the focus of art away from the production of autonomous objects and towards the creation of situations, encounters, and social events.

In this model, the role of the artist is not that of a solitary maker but of a "facilitator" or "catalyst" who initiates a process or creates a platform for interaction.29 The artwork itself is not a finished product but a social environment, a "model of action within the existing real" where people come together to participate in a shared activity.29 The meaning of the work is therefore not located in a discrete object to be interpreted by an individual viewer, but is instead "elaborated collectively" through the encounters and interactions that take place between people within the context the artist has created.29 This approach is perfectly suited to an exhibition of rituals, which are themselves inherently social and collective phenomena.

#### **From Spectator to Witness**

Adopting a relational framework fundamentally redefines the role of the audience, shifting it from that of the passive spectator to that of the active participant or witness. The exhibition will not be a collection of static representations *of* rituals, but rather the live enactment *of* them. This aligns with the core principles of Social Practice art, a related field that "focuses on community engagement through... human interaction and social discourse".31 In this mode of practice, the work is often co-created with its participants, even if that participation is as simple as being physically present in the space and sharing in the experience of a live event.

The durational nature of many of the proposed performances is key to this shift in the audience's role. For example, a ritual like a daily stand-up meeting might be performed on a continuous loop for the entire gallery day. This means that a visitor will never "see" the work in its entirety from a single, privileged vantage point. Instead, they will encounter a slice of an ongoing process, dipping in and out of a continuous flow of action. This mode of viewing mirrors how we experience rituals in our own everyday lives: not as discrete, framed performances, but as recurring patterns embedded in the fabric of our daily routines. The visitor becomes a witness to a process that seemingly existed before their arrival and will continue after their departure, reinforcing the sense that they are encountering a fundamental and ongoing structural activity rather than a self-contained theatrical presentation.

By applying a relational framework to the staging of rituals, the exhibition transforms the gallery space into a large-scale "liminal zone," a concept directly analogous to the transitional phase in Arnold van Gennep's rites of passage. The audience, upon entering the exhibition, is ritually separated from their everyday routines and enters a space where those very routines are defamiliarized, slowed down, and re-presented as aesthetic and social phenomena. This process begins with a simple but significant gesture, borrowed from the practice of performance artist Marina Abramović, of asking visitors to divest themselves of their coats, watches, and, most importantly, their personal electronic devices before entering the main galleries.32 This act serves as a clear, pre-liminal moment of separation from the outside world and its constant flow of information and distraction.

Once inside, visitors encounter familiar rituals—meetings, cleaning, safety checks—but stripped of their normal context and function. A stand-up meeting is being performed with meticulous precision, but it produces no actual product or outcome. A hotel bed is being turned down with ritualistic care, but no guest will ever sleep in it. A security guard's patrol is made hyper-visible, but the object of surveillance is the act of surveillance itself. This creates a state of productive ambiguity, a liminal space where the familiar rules and functions of the everyday are suspended. In this zone, the underlying structures, rhythms, and choreographies of these routines are made available for focused contemplation. The intended "post-liminal" effect occurs when the visitor leaves the exhibition and re-enters the world. The goal is that they can no longer see their own daily rituals as merely functional or invisible, but will now recognize them as performative, meaningful, and structurally significant acts that actively shape their social reality. The exhibition, in this sense, is a rite of passage for perception itself.

### **Section 6: A Typology of Performance: From the Daily Stand-Up to the Turndown Service**

This section provides the most concrete and speculative vision of the exhibition's content, moving from the curatorial philosophy to a detailed exploration of how specific, real-world rituals can be translated into performance pieces. It serves as a practical guide to the artistic possibilities inherent in the exhibition's concept, demonstrating the breadth of source material and the variety of artistic strategies that can be employed.

#### **Translating Ritual into Performance**

The artistic strategy of *Ritual Baseline* is one of translation, not simple re-enactment. The goal is to choreograph these found rituals in a way that highlights their formal, aesthetic qualities: their inherent repetition, rhythm, gestural vocabularies, spatial organization, and scripted language. The process involves isolating a ritual from its functional context and reframing it within the gallery to reveal its underlying structure and symbolic weight.

The raw material for these translations will be drawn from the diverse examples identified in the research, spanning multiple sectors of contemporary life:

* **Corporate Rituals:** The highly structured Agile "daily stand-up" or "daily scrum" meeting, with its rigid three-question script ("What did you accomplish yesterday? What are you planning to accomplish today? Are there any obstacles or challenges?") and strict 15-minute time limit, serves as a prime example of a modern corporate rite of integration and accountability.7 Other examples include team-building exercises, the ceremonial ringing of a sales gong, and the formal induction of new employees.33  
* **Hospitality Rituals:** The hotel "turndown service" is a quintessential ritual of invisible care. It is a highly scripted, discreet, and sequential performance that transforms a room from a functional space into a sanctuary for rest, often involving classic gestures like turning down the bed linens and placing a chocolate on the pillow.34  
* **Safety & Emergency Rituals:** The daily routines of industrial workers and emergency services are rich with ritual. These include rigorous, checklist-driven equipment inspections, comprehensive daily job briefings to mitigate hazards, and even collective physical warm-ups like a "Stretch & Flex" routine, which disciplines and prepares the body for labor.37 Ceremonies such as the "wet down" for a new fire truck also serve to bless and integrate new tools into the community.38  
* **Creative Rituals:** Even the supposedly unstructured world of creative industries is governed by ritual. Brainstorming sessions are often preceded by highly structured warm-up exercises or games, such as "Alternate Uses" or "Bad Ideas," which function as micro-rituals designed to manage the transition from analytical to creative thinking and to create a state of psychological safety.40

The artistic translation of these rituals will draw on the full spectrum of contemporary time-based media. This includes durational live performance, following the legacy of artists like Marina Abramović who use extreme duration and physical presence to create powerful experiences.32 It also includes video art, drawing on the work of artists like Bruce Nauman who used the camera to document and analyze monotonous, repetitive actions performed in his studio.44 Sound art can be used to translate the sonic architecture of a ritual—the clicks, beeps, and verbal confirmations of an equipment check, for example—into an immersive auditory environment.45 Finally, sculptural installations can be created from the artifacts and residues of ritual actions, transforming the mundane detritus of maintenance into aesthetic objects.47

### **Section 7: Conclusion: Surfacing the Structural Work**

This report has laid out the theoretical, historical, and curatorial foundations for *Ritual Baseline: The Choreography of Invisible Structure*. The exhibition is conceived as a critical intervention that synthesizes insights from anthropology, sociology, performance studies, and art history to achieve several key objectives. By moving from the conceptual "basement" of our social structures to the practical staging of their constituent rituals, the project aims to fundamentally alter our perception of the mundane.

First and foremost, the exhibition renders visible the vast and vital sphere of "invisible work" and "maintenance" that underpins all social and cultural production. Drawing directly from the legacy of Mierle Laderman Ukeles, it challenges a cultural value system that overwhelmingly privileges "development," "originality," and "creation" over the repetitive, necessary labor of preservation and support.9 By placing these maintenance rituals within the institutional frame of the art museum, the exhibition performs a radical re-valuation, arguing that the work of keeping things going is as complex, meaningful, and worthy of aesthetic consideration as the work of making things anew.

Second, the project provides a new and critical lens through which to view our own daily lives. It encourages a heightened awareness of the myriad rituals that structure our behavior, produce social cohesion, and manage our transitions through institutional life. By staging secular rites from the corporate, industrial, and service sectors, the exhibition draws a direct line to the foundational anthropological theories of Durkheim and Van Gennep, revealing the deep, often unconscious, ritual grammar that governs modern society.5 The visitor is invited to recognize the performative and structural significance of their own routines, transforming their perception of the everyday.

Third, *Ritual Baseline* utilizes the sophisticated aesthetic language of postmodern performance and the participatory framework of relational art to create a unique and compelling visitor experience. It moves beyond simple representation to create a live, durational, and social environment. Drawing on the "neutral doing" of Yvonne Rainer and the social encounters of Nicolas Bourriaud's relational aesthetics, the exhibition is designed to be both intellectually rigorous and sensorially engaging, immersing the audience in the rhythms, gestures, and sounds of the foundational work of our world.19

Finally, the exhibition performs a sophisticated and necessary act of Institutional Critique. By turning its lens inward to stage the museum's own maintenance and security rituals, it implicates itself in the very economies of invisible labor it seeks to explore. This self-reflexive gesture, following the critical strategies of artists like Andrea Fraser and Hans Haacke, avoids a didactic or judgmental tone, instead creating a powerful statement about the universal and often unacknowledged dependence of all institutions—including the art world—on foundational maintenance.25

In conclusion, by choreographing the mundane, *Ritual Baseline* does more than just stage routines. It surfaces the very grammar of our social existence. It reveals the profound and complex structural work performed by the small, ordinary, and endlessly repeated practices that keep our world standing. It takes what resides in the "basement" of culture—the overlooked, the repetitive, the necessary—and places it on the main exhibition floor, forcing a radical and urgent re-evaluation of what we see, what we value, and what we consider to be the proper subject of art.
