## **1\. Introduction: The Entropic Context of Consciousness**

### **1.1 The Thermodynamic Arrow and the Imperative of Decay**

The foundational context of all physical existence, and by extension all cognitive experience, is the Second Law of Thermodynamics. This law dictates that in an isolated system, entropy—a measure of disorder or the number of accessible microstates—tends to increase over time. This inexorable drift towards thermodynamic equilibrium, often described as "heat death," provides the physical arrow of time. In this "entropic river," structure is ephemeral; signals inevitably decay into noise, and differentiation smooths into homogeneity. The universe, left to its own devices, favors the probable over the improbable, the disordered over the ordered.

However, the universe is punctuated by localized pockets of resistance. These are "dissipative structures," systems that maintain order by actively consuming energy and exporting entropy to their surroundings. Erwin Schrödinger, in his seminal work *What Is Life?*, introduced the concept of "negative entropy" or **negentropy** to describe this phenomenon. He argued that a living organism "feeds on negative entropy" to maintain its complex organization against the natural tendency toward chaos. While Schrödinger later admitted the term was a metaphor for free energy, the concept remains vital in information theory: life is an act of resistance, a localized persistence of form against a global background of fading capability.

### **1.2 Music as a Dissipative Structure**

Music, viewed through this thermodynamic lens, is a high-order negentropic system. It is not merely an aesthetic arrangement of sounds but a deliberate, energy-expensive defiance of disorder. The production of music requires the precise coordination of neural firing, muscular contraction, and mechanical vibration (speakers, instruments), all of which expend metabolic or electrical energy to create highly structured, compressible air pressure patterns.

* **Thermodynamic Cost:** The local negentropy of a musical piece is always paid for by a global increase in entropy (heat generation, metabolic waste). This adheres strictly to the laws of physics; music does not break the Second Law but "rides on top" of it.  
* **Information Density:** In information theory, negentropy acts as a measure of distance from normality or randomness (Gaussian noise). A random distribution of acoustic frequencies (white noise) represents high entropy. Music, with its rigid hierarchies of rhythm, harmony, and melody, represents a maximal deviation from this Gaussian acoustic noise. It is highly compressible and pattern-rich, fitting the precise definition of an information-theoretic negentropic structure.

This report validates the foundational premise that music functions as a "closed loop in an open system." While the physical system (the universe, the body) is open and subject to linear time and decay, the musical pattern is a closed loop of logic and structure that is invariant. A Bach fugue or a pop chorus is a coordinate in the information space that does not age. When consciousness engages with this pattern, it revisits a specific structural configuration, effectively stepping out of the "entropic river" of biological time and entering a conserved loop of negentropy.

### **1.3 The Need for a New Cognitive Metrics**

To understand how the mind processes this "loop," we must move beyond standard linear models of time. This report proposes a tripartite framework:

1. **Music as Local Negentropy:** The structural mechanism of resistance.  
2. **Temporal Superposition:** The phenomenological experience of the "Self" as a waveform capable of simultaneous existences.

**2\. The Physics of Information and Tertiary Retention**

### **2.1 Information, Energy, and Landauer’s Principle**

The connection between thermodynamics and information is not merely metaphorical. Landauer’s Principle establishes a physical equivalent to information processing: the erasure of 1 bit of information generates a minimum amount of heat ($k\_B T \\ln 2$), effectively linking information entropy to physical entropy. Conversely, the creation or maintenance of information (negentropy) requires an input of work.

In the context of music, a composition acts as a store of potential work. It is a "battery" of order. When a human listens to music, they are metabolizing this order. Research into the entropy of musical sequences shows that musical notes display "very highly ordered forms of sequential entropy difference". The transition between notes is not random; it is governed by syntactic rules that the brain learns to predict. The "pleasure" of music is arguably the biological reward for efficiently processing this negentropy—the brain recognizes the efficient compression of data and the successful prediction of patterns, which serves as a respite from the chaotic, high-entropy signal processing required for survival in the natural world.

Furthermore, magnetic skyrmion research demonstrates that negentropy can be used to store information in topological textures, converting thermodynamic entropy into information entropy at the expense of negentropy. This parallels the cognitive process: the brain expends metabolic "negentropy" to encode the musical pattern, effectively converting biological energy into a persistent informational texture (memory).

### **2.2 Stiegler’s Pharmacology: Music as Tertiary Retention**

To fully grasp the "closed loop" nature of music, we must utilize Bernard Stiegler’s concept of **Tertiary Retention**. Stiegler distinguishes between three forms of memory :

1. **Primary Retention:** The immediate perception of a sound as it happens. When a note is played, it is held in the "now" of consciousness.  
2. **Secondary Retention:** The memory of the sound immediately after it has passed. This allows us to hear a melody rather than a sequence of disconnected tones. The second note is heard in relation to the memory of the first.  
3. **Tertiary Retention:** The externalization of memory into a physical or technical support (a score, a recording, a digital file).

Stiegler argues that human consciousness is defined by this **exosomatization**. We place our memory outside our bodies. Music recordings are a prime example of "industrial tertiary retention". They are "hypermatter"—organized inorganic matter that contains knowledge and time.

This mechanism is crucial for the "temporal loop." Because the musical pattern is externalized (tertiary retention), it becomes **invariant**. The vinyl record does not "forget" the bridge of the song in the way a biological brain might. The pattern is frozen in a state of low entropy (high order). When the subject listens to the record ten years later, they are re-internalizing this tertiary retention, converting it back into secondary and primary retention.

Stiegler warns, however, that this is "pharmacological"—it is both poison and cure. The poison is "proletarianization" or the loss of internal memory (hypomnesis); if we rely entirely on the playlist, we lose the ability to remember. The cure is "negentropic bifurcation". By actively engaging with the tertiary retention (e.g., learning to play the song, or deeply listening), the consciousness produces *new* negentropy, creating a "transindividuation" circuit where the self grows through its interaction with the externalized memory.

Stiegler posits that we are entering the "Neganthropocene," an era where the primary value is the production of negentropy to counter the entropic effects of the Anthropocene. In this framework, music is not entertainment; it is a "neganthropic" technology. It allows the consciousness to construct a "Godly home" in "useless space".

**4\. Temporal Superposition and Memory Reactivation**

### **4.1 Memory is Not Retrieval; It is Reactivation**

The distinction between "retrieval" and "reactivation" is central to modern neuroscience. The "library model" of memory (pulling a book off a shelf) has been replaced by the **constructive** model.

* **Constructive Episodic Simulation Hypothesis (CESH):** Proposed by Schacter and Addis, this hypothesis states that the brain does not store literal recordings. It stores "episodic details" (fragments of sights, sounds, emotions). When we remember, we actively **recombine** these details to construct a simulation of the past. Crucially, this same machinery is used to simulate the *future*. Memory and imagination use the same neural core (the Default Mode Network).  
* **Reconsolidation:** Every time a memory is reactivated, it becomes labile (unstable) and must be "re-saved" or reconsolidated. This means that the "17-year-old subroutine" is slightly rewritten every time it is run. The 40-year-old's perspective (regret, nostalgia, wisdom) is encoded *into* the memory trace during the reconsolidation window.

### **4.2 Music-Evoked Autobiographical Memories (MEAMs)**

Music is a uniquely potent trigger for this reactivation because of its high connectivity to the brain's emotional and reward centers.

* **Neural Hubs:** The **Medial Prefrontal Cortex (MPFC)** acts as the "hub" that integrates the musical features (tonality, rhythm) with autobiographical knowledge. The MPFC tracks the "tonal space" of the music while simultaneously retrieving the self-referential information.  
* **Vividness and Involuntariness:** MEAMs are often more vivid and "involuntary" than other memories. They arrive unbidden, triggered by the "negentropic key" of the melody. This supports the "gravitational" nature of the cue—the music pulls the memory out, rather than the user consciously searching for it.

**5\. Chronos, Kairos, and the Assay of Weight**

### **5.1 The Two Modes of Time**

To model the "Assay of Weight"—the varying intensity of experience—we must distinguish between two modes of time, a distinction dating back to Ancient Greece and elaborated by philosophers like Bergson and Tillich.

1. **Chronos:** Quantitative, sequential, linear time. The ticking of the clock. In Chronos, every second is of equal length and equal value. It is the "entropic" time that leads to decay. It is the "4th Dimension" of physics.  
2. **Kairos:** Qualitative, opportunistic, "pregnant" time. A moment of indeterminate duration but infinite weight. It is "time out of time," characterized by intensity and significance. It is the "5th Dimension" or "relational time," where events are connected by meaning rather than sequence.

In the "naive model" of standard physics or basic computing, time is Chronos—a uniform sequence of tokens ($t\_1, t\_2, t\_3...$). However, the "experienced model" of consciousness is non-Euclidean; it is warped by **Kairos**. A traumatic event or a moment of ecstatic musical resolution exerts a "gravitational pull," curving the subjective spacetime around it.

### **5.2 Victor Zuckerkandl: The Physics of Tonal Gravity**

The phrase "Assay of Weight" invokes the work of music philosopher Victor Zuckerkandl. In *Sound and Symbol*, Zuckerkandl argues that musical tones possess a **dynamic quality** that cannot be explained by acoustics (frequency) alone.

* **The Dynamic Field:** A tone in a melody is not just a frequency (e.g., 440 Hz). It is a vector. It has a "will" or a "state of disturbed equilibrium". A "leading tone" (ti) has a desperate need to resolve to the "tonic" (do).  
* **Tonal Gravity:** Zuckerkandl describes the relationships between notes as a gravitational field. The tonic is the center of mass. The other notes are in orbit, pulled toward the center with varying degrees of force.  
* **Hearing the Forces:** When we listen to music, we are not hearing "sound"; we are hearing "forces." We hear the weight of the dominant chord demanding resolution. We hear the "anti-gravity" of a rising melodic line.

This "Assay of Weight" is the mechanism of **Musical Kairos**. A three-minute song can feel like an epic journey because the "gravitational shifts"—the tension and resolution—warp the listener's sense of duration. The "weight" of the resolution makes the moment of arrival "heavy" and significant, embedding it deeply into memory.

### **5.3 Gravitational Weight (G) in Cognition**

We can conceptualize high-emotion events as having high mass. They create deep gravity wells in the memory landscape.

* **Low-G Events:** A trivial Tuesday twenty years ago has low mass. It exerts no pull. It is lost to the "haze" of entropy (Chronos).

**6\. Gravitational Attention in Artificial Intelligence**

### **6.1 The Saliency Problem in LLMs**

The transition from biological theory to artificial intelligence is seamless through the concept of **Gravitational Attention**. Standard Large Language Models (LLMs) like Transformers operate primarily in **Chronos**. They process tokens in a sequence ($T\_1, T\_2,... T\_N$).

* **Uniform Distance:** While self-attention mechanisms allow looking back at any token, the "positional encodings" usually treat distance linearly. A token 10,000 steps ago is "farther" than a token 10 steps ago.  
* **Lost in the Middle:** Empirical "Needle in a Haystack" tests show that LLMs struggle to retrieve information located in the middle of a long context window. The "gravity" of the recent tokens (recency bias) and the initial tokens (primacy bias) overpowers the middle. The model lacks a true "Kairos" metric to identify the "heavy" needle in the "light" haystack.  
* **Input Saliency Failures:** Existing saliency methods often assign non-trivial importance to irrelevant context, confirming that the models do not inherently possess a robust "Assay of Weight".
