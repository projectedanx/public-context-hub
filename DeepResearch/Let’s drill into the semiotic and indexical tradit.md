Directly: the semiotic/indexical tradition that best illuminates AI image epistemology runs from Peirce’s icon–index–symbol triad, through Barthes and Bazin on photographic trace and “that‑has‑been,” into post‑photography accounts of an **indexical crisis** in the AI age.[^1][^2][^3][^4]

This tradition doesn’t just tell us “AI images aren’t indexes”; it shows how indexicality fractures into causal, affective, and practice-based dimensions—and which of those AI images can still participate in.

***

## 1. Peirce: icon–index–symbol and photographs as “forced” signs

Peirce’s semiotics defines three basic sign types: **icons** that represent by resemblance, **indexes** that are “really affected” by their objects, and **symbols** that work by habit or convention. In a canonical formulation, he writes that an index “refers to the object that it denotes by virtue of being really affected by that object,” emphasizing a dyadic relation of existential contiguity between sign and object.[^5][^1]

Photographs become Peirce’s paradigm case of the index: they are “physically forced to correspond point by point to nature” by light, such that, in argentic photography, “it is not possible to take a photograph of a non‑existent object.” A Peircean visual semiotics paper notes that this makes photographic indexicality very strict: the photograph is existentially linked to an individual (or group), a unique time, and a unique place—it can only ever say, in Peirce’s phrase, “There!” about some concrete configuration.[^6][^1]

Crucially, Peirce also insists that signs are rarely “pure”: an index like a photograph always nests an icon within it (we only understand the index by recognizing likeness), and a symbol (like a caption or genre code) can contain an index. Recent work such as “The Art of Pointing” leans on this to argue that photography’s epistemic force depends not only on its causal trace but on how we use photos as *dicent* signs—assertive signs that “tell” us something about the world without needing supplemental indices.[^7][^8][^9][^10][^1]

For AI images, this Peircean framework gives you three leverage points:

- They are **icons** (resemblance) by design.
- They lack classical **indexicality** because there is no direct causal trace from a photographed scene.
- They can still participate in **symbolic** and practice-based indexical structures (e.g., when we treat a logged model ID or prompt as pointing to a particular generation event).

So the question is not “Indexical or not?” but “What *kind* of indexicality, if any, can a computational image sustain?”

***

## 2. Barthes, Bazin, and the affective/temporal index

If Peirce gives the logical form of indexicality, Barthes and Bazin give you its affective and temporal phenomenology. Bazin’s classic “Ontology of the Photographic Image” casts the photograph as sharing “in the existence of the model, like a fingerprint,” a metaphoric way of saying that the photograph’s being is tied to that of its referent. Commentators note that, retrospectively, Bazin is often read through Peirce: the fingerprint analogy is a textbook example of indexical trace.[^4][^1]

Barthes crystallizes this into the famous “that‑has‑been” (*ça a été*): the essence of photography is not that it shows what no longer is, but that it *certifies* that what is shown once existed before the camera. Later work on photographic temporality uses examples like Alexander Gardner’s 1865 execution photograph to show how this that‑has‑been entails a paradoxical temporal structure: from the viewer’s standpoint, the depicted death is both future (in the photo’s moment) and past (for us), and this temporal punctum is inseparable from indexicality.[^11][^3][^12]

Contemporary theorists complicate this by emphasizing that “indexicality as pointing, rather than a pure indicator of material connection, allows for a more nuanced articulation” of photographic experience. For example:[^13][^11]

- Rephotography and “time bridges” show how old analog photos become re‑embedded in contemporary physical spaces, making indexicality “the result of a performance whereby the photograph is recycled into the physical environment.”[^11]
- Work on “indexicality as symptom” reads Barthes through Žižek to argue that discourses of indexicality themselves are symptomatic of a desire for the real, rather than transparent descriptions of how photographs function.[^13]

For AI image epistemology, this suggests:

- The **affective** sense of index (“this really happened to someone”) is separable from the **causal** one; AI images can trigger the first while lacking the second.
- Indexicality can be enacted in practices (re‑situating, annotating, curating), not just in the moment of capture—opening space for *secondary* or *relational* forms of indexicality around synthetic images.

***

## 3. Post‑photography and the “indexical crisis” in the AI age

Recent work explicitly names an **“indexical crisis” at the dawn of the AI age**. A 2024 thesis titled *The End of Photography? An Indexical Crisis at the Dawn of the AI Age* argues that what is in crisis is not the medium per se but the semiotic category of indexicality as applied to images: digital and AI technologies make it impossible to assume that a visually photographic image is a physical trace of what it depicts.[^2][^14]

This “indexical crisis” literature does three important things:

1. It **historicizes** indexicality: early photography made strong indexical claims, but digital compositing, in‑game photography, XR, and AI gradually turned photography into a practice that can “create and depict non‑existent pasts and experiences.”[^15]
2. It **dematerializes** the image: computational images exist primarily as data structures and algorithmic outputs, not as light‑inscriptions; one article stresses that modern photographic technologies now generate images within computer games and AI systems, inaugurating a “new visual regime” where images no longer need any referent outside code.[^16][^15]
3. It **relocates** indexicality: instead of being a property of the image-object, indexicality becomes a property of the whole semiotic performance—devices, software, platforms, metadata, geospatial and temporal anchoring.[^10][^14][^11]

Essays on “the erosion of photography’s indexical power” in the era of AI filters give a more popular version of this: tools like Bold Glamour and AR filters mean that digital “photos” no longer record how someone looked at a moment, but instead align faces with algorithmically idealized templates. Rodowick’s earlier claim that digital capture weakens the indexical link because light is converted into discrete symbols is taken a step further: AI filters collapse the difference between capture and post‑processing, so the camera becomes a generator of *possible* appearances more than a recorder of actual ones.[^4]

From this vantage, AI images are not an abrupt break but the **culmination** of a trajectory: indexicality is hollowed out at the level of the pixel, while surviving—if at all—as a higher‑level property of provenance, use, and institutional embedding.

***

## 4. Reconstructing indexicality for AI images: layered and relational views

Given all this, what does the semiotic/indexical tradition tell you to do with AI images?

1. **Abandon “pure” indexicality, keep mixed signs**
Peirce already denies that we ever get “absolutely pure” indexes; every photograph is also iconic and embedded in symbolic practices. AI images simply push us to accept that *any* epistemic evaluation must account for this hybridity: their pixels are iconic, their prompts and manifests are symbolic, and whatever indexicality they have lies not in the light-path from object to sensor, but in:[^9][^1][^7]
    - logs that tie an image to a specific model, time, and seed;
    - chains of signatures (C2PA-style) that tie files to devices or generators;
    - contextual performance (e.g., an AI image used as part of a documented simulation study).[^17][^18][^10]
2. **Differentiate levels of indexical reference**
Building on Peirce’s idea that an index can “take hold of our eyes… and forcibly direct them to a particular object,” we can say that classical photographs are **token‑indexical** with respect to scenes: they point to a specific event. AI images, by contrast, can at best be:[^1][^6]
    - **Type‑indexical**: pointing to statistical types in training data (“this is what the model takes ‘CEO’ or ‘riot’ to look like”).
    - **Provenance‑indexical**: pointing to a particular *generation* event (model M, prompt P, seed S at time T).
They are not token‑indexical to the depicted scene because that scene never existed outside the model.
3. **Re‑anchor indexicality in practice (use and purpose)**
Peirceans who write on photography now stress that using his semiotics requires attention to how producers and viewers *use* photos as signs and for what purposes. An AI image of a hypothetical galaxy collision used in a simulation paper may be epistemically appropriate as a model illustration, even though it is non‑indexical with respect to the cosmos; what matters is whether viewers are *invited* to treat it as a trace or as a diagram.[^10]

Post‑photographic and rephotography work similarly shows that indexicality can be “performed” by aligning old photos with present spaces, making the here‑now and there‑then resonate. For AI images, you can think analogously: when an AI‑generated reconstruction is anchored to archival documents, eyewitness accounts, and explicit disclaimers about its hypothetical status, it participates in a **relational indexical practice** that can still support learning—though never as raw evidence.[^11]
4. **Treat “indexical crisis” as an opportunity for explicit semiotic governance**

The “indexical crisis” thesis is not just descriptive; it implies that we need to **govern** how images claim indexicality. That lines up neatly with provenance and labeling regimes:[^14][^2]
    - C2PA manifests and similar tools function, semiotically, as *secondary indexes* connecting an image to devices, software, and generation contexts.[^18][^17]
    - Content labels (“AI‑generated concept illustration”, “archival photograph”, “simulation output”) operate as *symbols* that regulate how viewers are entitled to read indexical claims.[^10]

In Peircean terms, we are engineering a new semiotic ecology in which AI images are explicitly classified as icons plus symbolic provenance claims, with no presumptive existential tie to what they depict.

***

## 5. How this helps you theorize AI images

Putting the pieces together, the semiotic/indexical tradition doesn’t just tell us, “AI images are fake, case closed.” It supports a more articulated position:

- **Ontologically**: AI images are computational icons whose generation process lacks direct existential contiguity with depicted scenes; they are “non‑indexical photographs” in Bazin’s sense but still embedded in wider semiotic chains.[^19][^16][^15][^4]
- **Epistemically**: they cannot, by themselves, underwrite “that‑has‑been” style claims about particular events, but they can legitimately support:
    - model‑world knowledge (what the model thinks a category looks like),
    - hypothetical or counterfactual visualization (diagrams with photographic surface),
    - relationally indexical knowledge about the socio‑technical system that produced them (which model, whose prompt, which platform).[^14][^1][^10]
- **Normatively**: the crisis is not that indexicality vanishes, but that it becomes **opaque** if we rely on surface appearance. The obvious semiotic response is to move indexicality into formally specified provenance layers and socially negotiated labeling and viewing practices.[^17][^18][^4][^14]

This opens space for a research agenda where you explicitly treat AI image pipelines as semiotic machines: tracing how icon, index, and symbol are redistributed across training sets, model architectures, prompts, provenance, captions, and institutional uses.

To shape that agenda: are you most interested in pushing this tradition toward (a) formalizing new “levels” of indexicality for AI images, (b) connecting indexical crisis to legal evidence and testimony, or (c) using it to redesign labeling/provenance schemes as semiotic interventions?
