Example: "A hyper-realistic, high-definition image of rippling water with reflections of the sunset, featuring dynamic lighting and detailed textures."

Names of Recognized Artists and Scientists from Ancient Times of Egypt to Current

Ancient Egypt

Imhotep (c. 2650–2600 BCE): Architect, engineer, and physician; designed the Step Pyramid of Djoser; considered one of the earliest known architects and polymaths.  
Thutmose (18th Dynasty): Royal sculptor known for creating the famous bust of Nefertiti.

Ancient Greece

Scientists and Philosophers

Thales of Miletus (c. 624–546 BCE): One of the Seven Sages of Greece; philosopher and mathematician.  
Pythagoras (c. 570–495 BCE): Mathematician; Pythagorean theorem.  
Hippocrates (c. 460–370 BCE): "Father of Medicine"; Hippocratic Oath.  
Socrates (c. 470–399 BCE): Philosopher known for the Socratic method.  
Plato (c. 428–348 BCE): Student of Socrates; founded the Academy in Athens.  
Aristotle (384–322 BCE): Student of Plato; tutor to Alexander the Great; wrote on diverse subjects.  
Archimedes (c. 287–212 BCE): Mathematician and inventor; principles of levers and buoyancy.  
Euclid (c. 300 BCE): Mathematician; "Father of Geometry."

Artists

Phidias (c. 480–430 BCE): Sculptor of Zeus at Olympia and the Parthenon statues.  
Polykleitos (5th century BCE): Sculptor known for his treatise "Canon" on ideal human proportions.  
Praxiteles (4th century BCE): Sculptor known for lifelike and graceful figures.

Ancient Rome

Vitruvius (1st century BCE): Architect and engineer; author of "De Architectura."  
Galen (c. 129–c. 200 CE): Physician whose writings influenced medical science for centuries.  
Pliny the Elder (23–79 CE): Author of "Natural History"; encyclopedic work on science and art.

Middle Ages

Scientists

Al-Khwarizmi (c. 780–850): Persian mathematician; "Father of Algebra."  
Avicenna (Ibn Sina) (980–1037): Persian polymath; wrote "The Book of Healing" and "The Canon of Medicine."  
Alhazen (Ibn al-Haytham) (965–1040): Arab mathematician and physicist; significant contributions to optics.  
Roger Bacon (c. 1219–1292): English philosopher and Franciscan friar; early advocate of the scientific method.

Artists

Giotto di Bondone (c. 1267–1337): Italian painter and architect; pivotal figure in the transition from medieval to Renaissance art.  
Renaissance

Scientists

Leonardo da Vinci (1452–1519): Italian polymath; artist, engineer, anatomist, and inventor.  
Nicolaus Copernicus (1473–1543): Polish astronomer; heliocentric model of the solar system.  
Galileo Galilei (1564–1642): Italian astronomer and physicist; improvements to the telescope; support of heliocentrism.  
Johannes Kepler (1571–1630): German astronomer; laws of planetary motion.

Artists

Leonardo da Vinci (also an artist).  
Michelangelo Buonarroti (1475–1564): Italian sculptor, painter, and architect; Sistine Chapel ceiling.  
Raphael Sanzio (1483–1520): Italian painter and architect; renowned for his Madonnas.  
Sandro Botticelli (c. 1445–1510): Italian painter; "The Birth of Venus" and "Primavera."

17th Century

Scientists

Isaac Newton (1643–1727): English mathematician and physicist; laws of motion and universal gravitation.  
Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (1646–1716): German polymath; co-inventor of calculus.

Artists

Rembrandt van Rijn (1606–1669): Dutch painter and etcher; master of light and shadow.  
Caravaggio (1571–1610): Italian painter known for dramatic use of chiaroscuro.  
Peter Paul Rubens (1577–1640): Flemish Baroque painter.

18th Century

Scientists

Benjamin Franklin (1706–1790): American polymath; electricity experiments; inventor.  
Carolus Linnaeus (1707–1778): Swedish botanist; developed system of taxonomy.  
James Watt (1736–1819): Scottish inventor; improvements to the steam engine; pivotal in the Industrial Revolution.  
Antoine Lavoisier (1743–1794): French chemist; "Father of Modern Chemistry."

Artists

Francisco Goya (1746–1828): Spanish romantic painter and printmaker.  
Thomas Gainsborough (1727–1788): English portrait and landscape painter.  
Jacques-Louis David (1748–1825): French painter; leader of the Neoclassical movement.

19th Century

Scientists

Charles Darwin (1809–1882): English naturalist; theory of evolution by natural selection.  
Louis Pasteur (1822–1895): French chemist and microbiologist; pasteurization; vaccines.  
Marie Curie (1867–1934): Polish-French physicist and chemist; research on radioactivity; first woman to win a Nobel Prize.  
Thomas Edison (1847–1931): American inventor; phonograph; electric light bulb.  
Gregor Mendel (1822–1884): Austrian monk; "Father of Genetics."

Artists

Vincent van Gogh (1853–1890): Dutch post-impressionist painter.  
Claude Monet (1840–1926): French founder of Impressionism.  
Auguste Rodin (1840–1917): French sculptor; "The Thinker."  
Edgar Degas (1834–1917): French artist famous for ballet dancers.

Scientists

Albert Einstein (1879–1955): German-born theoretical physicist; theory of relativity.  
Niels Bohr (1885–1962): Danish physicist; model of the atom.
