

# **The Prototype Pavilion: Founding the 21st Century Foundation as a Living Public Experiment**

## **Executive Summary**

This report details a new paradigm for institutional design within the philanthropic sector: the Prototype Pavilion. This model recasts the traditional foundation from a static, proclamation-based entity into a dynamic, experimental organization that treats its core mission as a "living hypothesis." The exhibition space, or "pavilion," serves as the public-facing laboratory where this hypothesis is continuously tested, critiqued, and iterated upon by the very community it aims to serve. Instead of issuing grants from a fixed position of authority, the Prototype Pavilion displays its foundational elements—governance structures, funding mechanisms, and community programs—as interactive prototypes. Visitors are transformed from passive beneficiaries into active participants, co-designers, and researchers in a process of "founding by exhibition."

The core argument of this report is that by strategically integrating methodologies from diverse fields, a foundation can achieve a new level of adaptability, legitimacy, and impact. The analysis demonstrates how the **Lean Startup** methodology provides an operational metabolism through its "Build-Measure-Learn" feedback loop, enabling the institution to evolve based on validated public learning. The **Living Lab** framework offers a model for creating an open innovation ecosystem in a real-world environment, positioning the public as co-creators. Principles from **Participatory Art** and **Relational Aesthetics** inform the design of the human interface, ensuring that engagement is meaningful and that the social interactions themselves become a primary outcome. Finally, models from **Open-Source Governance**, **Co-operatives**, and **Decentralized Autonomous Organizations (DAOs)** provide a rich toolkit for the transparent and experimental prototyping of the foundation's internal power structures.

The report is structured to guide strategic innovators from conceptual architecture to a practical implementation blueprint. Part I deconstructs the core metaphors of the "living hypothesis" and the "public laboratory." Part II analyzes the mechanics of engineering deep and equitable public participation. Part III provides a concrete catalogue of the institutional functions that can be prototyped as public "exhibits," including governance experiments, community-funded pilots, and new public services. Part IV presents a strategic framework for implementation, drawing on pioneering case studies from organizations like the Participatory City Foundation, RadicalxChange, Dark Matter Labs, and The GovLab. The report concludes by addressing the key challenges of this model—namely, balancing long-term stability with constant experimentation and navigating the ethics of public experimentation—and issues a call to action for the philanthropic sector to embrace this new mode of founding.

## **Introduction: A Paradigm Shift for Philanthropy**

### **The Problem with Proclamation**

The modern philanthropic foundation is largely a product of the late 19th and early 20th centuries, an era of industrial consolidation and top-down management philosophies.1 This historical context shaped its dominant operational model: "founding by proclamation." In this model, a founder, family, or board of trustees establishes a set of core principles, a mission, and a theory of change, which are then enshrined in legal documents and operationalized through a hierarchical structure. This institution, once proclaimed, is designed for permanence and stability, executing its mission by allocating resources to external recipients.

While this model has achieved significant successes, its structural rigidity makes it increasingly ill-suited for addressing the complex, interconnected, and rapidly evolving challenges of the 21st century. These "wicked problems"—from climate change and systemic inequality to social polarization—defy simple, linear solutions. The proclamation-based model often suffers from several critical flaws. It can create a significant disconnect between the foundation's stated mission and the lived realities of the communities it serves. Its inherent risk aversion, often a byproduct of a culture that rewards "safe" choices, can stifle the very innovation needed to generate breakthrough solutions.2 Furthermore, by positioning the public as passive beneficiaries or grant recipients, it fails to leverage the collective intelligence, creativity, and agency of the community itself, perpetuating a power dynamic that can undermine trust and long-term sustainability.

### **The Proposition of the Prototype Pavilion**

The Prototype Pavilion is proposed as a direct and radical response to these limitations. It is an institutional model that inverts the traditional logic of founding. Instead of beginning with a fixed proclamation, it begins with a "living hypothesis" \[User Query\]. The foundation's core claims—its mission, its values, its strategies—are not presented as immutable truths but as initial assumptions to be rigorously and publicly tested.

The physical and conceptual space for this testing is the "pavilion," an exhibition that functions as a public laboratory. This transforms the act of founding from a singular, historical event into a continuous, iterative process of "founding by exhibition" \[User Query\]. The exhibits within this pavilion are not static displays of past achievements but interactive prototypes of the foundation's core functions. These include not only the programs and services it might support but also its own internal mechanics: its governance rules, its decision-making processes, and its funding mechanisms. The public is invited not merely to observe, but to interact, critique, co-design, and validate these prototypes. Their engagement provides the data that drives the foundation's evolution, turning participatory research into the primary engine of institutional development.

### **Report Roadmap**

This report provides a comprehensive theoretical and practical guide to the Prototype Pavilion model. Its objective is to equip philanthropic leaders, social innovators, and institutional designers with the conceptual tools and strategic frameworks necessary to understand, adapt, and implement this new paradigm.

Part I, "The Conceptual Architecture," will establish the theoretical foundations of the model, deconstructing the core metaphors of the "living hypothesis" and the "public laboratory." Part II, "The Human Interface," will delve into the mechanics of public engagement, drawing on theories from participatory art and relational aesthetics to design interactions that are both meaningful and productive. Part III, "Prototyping the Institution," will detail the specific types of experiments the Pavilion can host, from governance models to funding mechanisms. Finally, Part IV, "A Strategic Blueprint," will translate theory into practice, analyzing relevant case studies from pioneering organizations and outlining a phased implementation plan. Through this structured exploration, the report aims to present a compelling and actionable vision for the future of founding.

## **Part I: The Conceptual Architecture**

This section establishes the two foundational metaphors that define the Prototype Pavilion: the concept of the foundation as a "living hypothesis" and the exhibition space as a "public laboratory." By grounding these ideas in robust theoretical frameworks from architecture, social innovation, and organizational design, this part builds the conceptual chassis upon which the entire model rests.

### **1.1 The Foundation as a "Living Hypothesis"**

The central innovation of the Prototype Pavilion is its reconceptualization of the foundation's identity. It moves from a static entity defined by a fixed charter to a dynamic organism defined by a "living hypothesis." This requires a synthesis of concepts from disparate fields: the biological metaphor of a "living" system and the scientific rigor of hypothesis-driven experimentation.

#### **Deconstructing the "Living" Metaphor**

The term "living" is not merely a poetic descriptor; it signals a fundamental paradigm shift from a mechanistic to an ecological worldview. In advanced architectural and engineering theory, a "living" system is one that moves beyond conventional, static design to become a regenerative, ecologically interconnected, and resilient system.4 Such systems are characterized by dynamic processes that we instinctively recognize as properties of life: "the balance between growth and decay, metabolic cycles, regenerative processes, adaptive responses, reproduction and evolution, and energy transformation".4 A foundation conceived as a living system is therefore not a machine built to perform a single function, but an adaptive environment that is "fluid, generative, and life-like," capable of evolving in response to ecological and social shifts.4

This dynamic, evolving quality stands in contrast to the traditional definition of a hypothesis in the social sciences, which is typically a static, falsifiable claim about the world that is tested to be proven or disproven.5 The Prototype Pavilion model merges these two ideas. The foundational claim—the "hypothesis"—is itself endowed with the properties of a living organism. It is not a fixed statement to be validated once, but a core identity that is expected to adapt, grow, and transform through continuous interaction with its environment.

#### **The Lean Startup as an Operational Metabolism**

If the "living" metaphor provides the conceptual framework, the Lean Startup methodology provides the practical engine—the operational metabolism—that allows the hypothesis to live and evolve. Developed for high-uncertainty environments, this methodology offers a rigorous process for navigating the path from idea to sustainable impact.7 It posits that every new venture is a grand experiment designed to answer two fundamental questions: "Should this product be built?" and "Can we build a sustainable business \[or organization\] around this set of products and services?".7

The core of this methodology is the **Build-Measure-Learn feedback loop**, a continuous cycle that drives the organization's evolution.7

* **Build:** The first step is to turn ideas into a **Minimum Viable Product (MVP)**. In the context of the Pavilion, the MVPs are the prototypes on display—the governance experiments, community-funded pilots, and service mock-ups. The purpose of the MVP is not to be a perfect, finished product, but to begin the process of learning as quickly and cheaply as possible.2  
* **Measure:** The next step is to measure how the public responds to the MVP. This requires a new form of "innovation accounting" that focuses on actionable metrics—data that reveals clear cause-and-effect relationships—rather than "vanity metrics" that sound impressive but do not inform future strategy.7 For the Pavilion, this means systematically collecting and analyzing data from visitor interactions, workshops, and feedback sessions.  
* **Learn:** This is the critical cognitive phase of the loop. The data gathered is used to achieve **validated learning**, a rigorous method for demonstrating progress when traditional key performance indicators are unavailable.7 This learning leads to a crucial decision point: whether to  
  **pivot** or **persevere**. A pivot is a "structural course correction to test a new fundamental hypothesis about the product, strategy, and engine of growth".7 Persevering means staying the current course while continuing to iterate and optimize. This disciplined process of learning and adapting is what allows the foundation's "living hypothesis" to evolve intelligently.

The foundation's primary function is thus transformed. It shifts from being a resource allocator to a knowledge creator. Its core activity is not grant-making but the systematic conversion of public interaction into validated learning about how to create positive social change. This process can be understood as a form of "information metabolism." The raw input of public engagement and feedback serves as the "nutrients" for the organization. The Measure and Learn phases are the metabolic processes that break down this input into actionable insights. The subsequent Build phase—the creation of new or iterated prototypes—is the tangible output, representing the growth and evolution of the institutional organism. This reframes the very definition of philanthropic impact, moving it from a focus on outputs to a focus on learning and adaptation.

#### **Adopting a Culture of "Failing Forward"**

A necessary precondition for the Lean Startup metabolism to function is a profound cultural shift toward embracing failure. In many traditional non-profits and foundations, change represents risk, and there is little incentive to innovate; employees are often rewarded for making the "safe" choice.2 This creates an organizational culture that is actively resistant to the kind of experimentation the Prototype Pavilion requires.

To succeed, the Pavilion must actively cultivate a culture of "failing forward".3 Within this framework, a quick failure that eliminates uncertainty and allows the organization to pivot is considered far more valuable than a prolonged, expensive, and ineffective strategy.2 Discovering that an initial hypothesis is wrong is not a setback but a cause for celebration, as it accelerates the journey toward a more effective solution.3 This requires explicit and sustained buy-in from all stakeholders, including the board, funders, staff, and the participating public, who must understand that the goal is not to execute a flawless plan but to learn as efficiently as possible.

### **1.2 The Pavilion as a Public Laboratory**

If the "living hypothesis" is the foundation's dynamic soul, the "pavilion" is its physical and social body—the public laboratory where the hypothesis is tested and brought to life. This space is intentionally designed to blur the lines between an art exhibition, a community center, and a research and development lab.

#### **From Gallery to Laboratory: The Exhibition as a Testing Ground**

The concept draws inspiration from a contemporary movement in the art world that redefines the purpose of the exhibition space. Rather than serving as a passive container for finished artworks, progressive galleries and project spaces are increasingly operating as sites of active experimentation.8 Spaces like Studio 12 of the Backwater Artists Group are explicitly conceived as a "public testing ground for new artworks and ideas," fostering discussion, peer support, and a flexible approach to creation.9 This model, which treats the exhibition as a process rather than a final product, provides a direct precedent for the Pavilion's mission of "founding by exhibition."

#### **The Living Lab Framework**

This approach can be formalized and scaled through the **Living Lab** framework. The European Network of Living Labs (ENoLL) defines a Living Lab as an "open innovation ecosystem in real-life environments based on a systematic user co-creation approach that integrates research and innovation activities in communities".10 This model is not a single method but a holistic environment for innovation, and its core principles align perfectly with the objectives of the Prototype Pavilion.11

* **Real-Life Environment:** A Living Lab is not a sterile, controlled environment separate from society. It is embedded within the real-life context of its users.10 The Pavilion must be physically and culturally accessible, situated within the community it serves, ensuring that the experiments it conducts are relevant and grounded in lived experience.  
* **Multi-Stakeholder Participation:** Living Labs operate on the "Quadruple Helix" model, fostering co-creation among four key actor groups: citizens (the public), government (public sector), industry (private sector), and academia (research).10 The Pavilion would act as the orchestrator of this multi-stakeholder collaboration, bringing diverse perspectives and resources to bear on shared challenges.12  
* **User-Centricity and Co-Creation:** This is the most critical principle. In a Living Lab, citizens are not treated as subjects to be studied or as consumers to be satisfied. They are elevated to the role of active co-creators and co-producers of knowledge.11 The innovation process is centered on their needs, experiences, and insights, ensuring that solutions are not only technically viable but also socially desirable and sustainable.

#### **Founding a Participatory Democracy**

The philosophical roots of this model can be traced to the American pragmatist philosopher John Dewey. Dewey argued that art and aesthetic experience are not luxuries to be confined to museums and galleries but are essential components of a vibrant democratic life.13 He believed that active participation in cultural practices, both as producers and receivers, is a vital form of social and political action. By breaking down the barriers between "elitist arts" and everyday life, society could educate citizens, making them "more aware and more able to contribute to common life".13

The Prototype Pavilion is a direct embodiment of Dewey's vision. It takes the "art" of institutional design—a practice typically reserved for a privileged few—and makes it a public, participatory experience. By inviting citizens to co-create the very structures that are meant to serve them, the Pavilion becomes more than just an innovative foundation; it becomes a training ground for participatory democracy, building the civic capacity, trust, and shared understanding necessary for collective action.

## **Part II: The Human Interface: Engineering Participation**

Moving from the conceptual architecture to the mechanics of public engagement, this section analyzes how the Prototype Pavilion can design interactions that are meaningful, productive, and equitable. The success of the model hinges on its ability to transform the public from a passive audience into active co-creators. This requires a sophisticated understanding of participation, drawing on theories from participatory art and relational aesthetics to engineer a truly collaborative social environment.

### **2.1 The Spectrum of Participation: From Interactive to Co-Creative**

The foundational principle of the Pavilion's human interface is its rejection of the traditional, passive role assigned to the public. In both the art world and the philanthropic sector, a dominant model has long persisted: a small class of professionals (artists or program officers) creates work that a much larger public observes or consumes.14 Participatory art directly challenges this dynamic by engaging the public in the creative process, making them "co-authors, editors, and observers of the work".14 The Pavilion applies this same challenge to the field of social innovation, recasting community members as co-designers of the foundation itself.

To navigate the complexities of this engagement, it is useful to employ an analytical framework like Sherry Arnstein's "Ladder of Citizen Participation".14 This model describes varying degrees of public involvement, ranging from non-participatory forms of "manipulation" and "therapy" at the bottom, through levels of "tokenism" such as informing and consultation, to the highest rungs of genuine "citizen power," including partnership, delegated power, and citizen control. The explicit goal of the Prototype Pavilion is to operate on these highest rungs, moving beyond simply gathering feedback to facilitating true co-authorship and shared decision-making power.

Achieving this level of participation has radical implications for the role of the foundation's staff and leadership. A central tenet of truly participatory art is that the artist must be willing to recede, either by being physically absent or by structuring the interaction in a way that they "become equal with the participants".14 This is the only way to offer participants genuine creative agency, free from the subjugating authority of the artist. For the Pavilion, this translates into a profound redefinition of professional roles. Founders, directors, and program staff must transition from being the primary authors of the foundation's strategy to becoming facilitators, conveners, and stewards of a participatory platform. Their expertise is redirected from dictating solutions to designing the processes through which the community can discover its own solutions.

### **2.2 Relational Aesthetics: The Social Interaction *is* the Artwork**

To fully grasp the nature of the Pavilion's work, it is necessary to look beyond traditional metrics of impact and engage with the theory of **Relational Aesthetics**. Coined by French curator Nicolas Bourriaud in the 1990s, this theory posits that the true subject and material of a certain strain of contemporary art is not the object itself, but "the whole of human relations and their social context".15 The artwork becomes a social event, a situation, or an environment that creates a "social environment in which people come together to participate in a shared activity".15 The meaning of the work is not found in a static object but is elaborated collectively through the encounters between people that the work facilitates.15

When this lens is applied to the Prototype Pavilion, it reveals the true nature of its "product." The prototypes on display—the governance models, the funding mechanisms, the service mock-ups—are not the end goal. They are catalysts. The real "work" of the foundation is the web of social interactions, the dialogues, the debates, the shared learning, and the new relationships that these prototypes generate. The success of the Pavilion is therefore measured not by the number of projects funded or services delivered, but by the quality and density of the social fabric it helps to weave.

This perspective represents an "institutional social turn," mirroring the shift that occurred in the art world where the focus moved from the object to the social process.16 The Prototype Pavilion is not merely a foundation that

*uses* participatory methods; it is a foundation whose very *ontology*—its fundamental nature of being—is participatory. Its primary outputs are the social bonds, collective intelligence, and capacity for collaborative action that emerge from its activities.19 This has significant implications for staffing and organizational culture. The core competencies required are not those of traditional grant evaluation and financial management, but rather skills in community organizing, facilitation, experience design, and the curation of high-quality human interaction.

However, this approach is not without its critical challenges. As art critic Claire Bishop has pointedly asked in her critique of relational aesthetics, if the art produces human relations, "then the next logical question to ask is what types of relations are being produced, for whom, and why?".16 This crucial normative question prevents a naive or uncritical celebration of all social interaction. The Pavilion must be rigorously self-aware about the power dynamics at play in its "public laboratory." It must actively design its processes to be inclusive, to mitigate the risk of exploitation, and to ensure that the relations being produced are equitable and empowering, rather than reinforcing existing inequalities.16 The foundation must constantly ask itself not just "Are we engaging people?" but "Who are we engaging, on what terms, and in whose interest?"

## **Part III: Prototyping the Institution: The Exhibits on Display**

This part of the report moves from the abstract conceptual framework to the concrete operational reality of the Prototype Pavilion. It details the specific types of experiments the Pavilion would host, framing them as interactive "exhibits" in a public gallery. Each exhibit represents a core function of a traditional foundation—its governance, its funding, and its programs—that is being opened up for public testing, critique, and co-creation.

### **3.1 Exhibit A: Governance Experiments**

This exhibit is perhaps the most radical, as it puts the foundation's own power structures and decision-making processes on public display for experimentation. Instead of being hidden in boardrooms and legal documents, the rules of the organization become a central object of public inquiry and design.

#### **The Social Contract as a Prototype**

The foundational documents of the organization—its constitution, bylaws, and policies—are presented not as immutable legal texts but as a living "social contract".21 This concept, rooted in political philosophy, describes the explicit or implicit agreement among members of a society to cooperate for social benefits.23 In a practical application, similar to how a teacher might create a social contract with a class, the Pavilion works with its community to co-create a set of shared expectations for behavior and interaction.24 All participants, including staff, would agree to this contract, sharing responsibility for upholding its principles. This transforms governance from a top-down imposition of rules into a bottom-up process of collective agreement.

#### **Open-Source Governance**

To operationalize this living social contract, the Pavilion would adopt principles from the world of open-source software development.25 The foundation's governing documents would be treated as open-source code:

* **Public Accessibility:** All rules, policies, and meeting minutes would be hosted on a public platform like GitHub, making them transparent and accessible to any interested party.25  
* **Contribution and Version Control:** Clear guidelines would be established for how community members can propose changes, debate amendments, and contribute to the evolution of the rules.26 This process of "forking" and "merging" allows the institutional code to adapt over time based on community input, creating a truly dynamic governance framework.

#### **Testing Alternative Decision-Making Models**

The Pavilion would serve as a real-world laboratory for testing a diverse array of governance models, allowing the community to experience and evaluate different ways of making collective decisions. This could involve running parallel experiments or applying different models to different types of decisions.

* **Co-operative Models:** Drawing from the long history of co-operative movements, these models are built on the principle of democratic member control.28 Experiments could include direct democracy mechanisms like "one member, one vote" for specific project decisions, or the election of representative member councils to oversee the board of directors for broader strategic choices.30  
* **Decentralized Autonomous Organizations (DAOs):** The Pavilion could leverage blockchain technology to experiment with DAOs, which use smart contracts to automate and enforce governance rules with high transparency.31 This would allow for direct public testing of competing mechanisms:  
  * *Token-Based vs. Reputation-Based Voting:* The foundation could run two parallel DAOs to govern different aspects of its work. One might use a token-based system, where voting power is proportional to a member's financial stake, to test how capital alignment influences decisions. The other could use a reputation-based system, where voting power is earned through active contribution and participation, to test a more meritocratic model.31  
  * *Novel Voting Mechanisms:* The Pavilion is an ideal environment to prototype emerging voting systems designed to capture more nuanced expressions of public will. A prime example is **Quadratic Voting (QV)**, where the cost of casting additional votes on an issue increases quadratically (cost=(votes)2). This system allows participants to express not just their preference but also the *intensity* of that preference, giving minority groups with strong feelings on a particular issue a more powerful voice and mitigating the "tyranny of the majority".31  
* **Open-Source Models:** The Pavilion could also experiment with governance structures common in successful open-source software projects.35 These include:  
  * The **"Benevolent Dictator for Life" (BDFL)** model, where a single, trusted leader holds final say, testing the efficiency of centralized vision.  
  * The **Meritocracy** model, where formal decision-making roles are granted to those who have demonstrated merit through consistent, high-quality contributions.35  
  * The **Liberal Contribution** model, where influence is based on current work and decisions are made through a consensus-seeking process that strives to include as many community perspectives as possible.35

To make these abstract concepts tangible for participants, the Pavilion could feature an interactive exhibit that allows visitors to use and compare these models directly. The following table provides a framework for such a comparison, serving as both an analytical tool for the foundation and an educational resource for the public.

| Model | Core Principle | Decision-Making Mechanism | Key Advantage | Key Challenge | Suitability for Pavilion |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Traditional Board** | Fiduciary Duty & Expertise | Majority vote by a small, appointed group of experts. | High efficiency in decision-making; clear lines of accountability. | Can be insular, slow to adapt, and lacks broad public legitimacy. | Serves as a baseline model for comparison and for decisions requiring specialized legal/financial oversight. |
| **Worker Co-operative** | Democratic Member Control | One Member, One Vote; or modified consensus processes.28 | High internal legitimacy and equality; fosters a sense of ownership. | Can be slow and inefficient at scale; may struggle with complex decisions. | Excellent for governing the internal operations of the Pavilion's staff or for small, member-funded projects. |
| **Token-Based DAO** | Capital-Weighted Control | Voting power is proportional to the number of governance tokens held (1 Token \= 1 Vote).31 | Aligns financial stake directly with governance power; efficient execution via smart contracts. | Prone to power concentration by large token holders ("whales"); can suffer from voter apathy.33 | Suitable for testing community-funded pilots where financial contribution is a key metric of commitment. |
| **Reputation-Based DAO** | Meritocratic Control | Voting power is earned through non-transferable reputation points gained via active contributions.33 | Incentivizes meaningful participation; resists plutocracy and financial manipulation. | Reputation can be subjective to measure; potential for social gaming or clique formation. | Ideal for governing community standards, content moderation, or awarding non-financial recognition. |

### **3.2 Exhibit B: Community-Funded Pilots**

This exhibit reimagines the foundation's core function of resource allocation, transforming it from a closed-door process into a transparent, public activity. It tests new ways of directing financial capital by empowering the community to act as co-investors and co-decision-makers.

* **Participatory Budgeting (PB) as a Model:** The Pavilion would dedicate a portion of its annual budget to a participatory budgeting process. This model, famously pioneered and scaled in Porto Alegre, Brazil, is a democratic process in which community members directly decide how to spend part of a public budget.37 The Pavilion would host a series of public assemblies where residents could propose ideas for local projects, deliberate on their merits, and vote on which initiatives receive funding.39 The Porto Alegre experience demonstrated that such a process can dramatically increase civic engagement, build trust between institutions and the public, and effectively redirect resources to address the most pressing community-identified needs.37  
* **Crowdfunding for Social Good:** The Pavilion would function as a dynamic platform for community-initiated projects, incorporating elements from established crowdfunding websites like Donorbox, GoFundMe, or Chuffed, which is specifically designed for social development organizations.40 Residents could launch campaigns for their ideas on the Pavilion's platform. The foundation could then play several roles: providing the digital and physical infrastructure for the campaigns, offering training and support to campaigners, and acting as a matching funder to amplify community contributions. This approach allows the foundation to use public interest as a real-time validation mechanism, identifying projects with genuine grassroots support before committing more substantial resources.  
* **Advanced Mechanisms: Plural Funding:** To push the democratic frontier of funding even further, the Pavilion could implement **Plural Funding**, also known as Quadratic Funding (QF). This mechanism, developed by researchers affiliated with RadicalxChange, is designed to fund public goods in a way that reflects broad community preference rather than the influence of wealthy donors.43 In a QF round, a central matching pool (provided by the foundation) matches individual contributions. However, the matching formula is based on the  
  *square of the sum of the square roots of each contribution*. In simpler terms, the number of individual contributors matters more than the total amount they donate. A project that receives 100 donations of $1 will receive significantly more in matching funds than a project that receives one donation of $100. This system powerfully incentivizes projects to build broad-based support and provides a robust defense against the plutocratic capture of philanthropic resources.

### **3.3 Exhibit C: Public Service Prototypes**

This exhibit showcases the tangible outputs of the foundation's work—the services, initiatives, and community programs it supports. However, in line with the Pavilion's ethos, these are not presented as finished products. Instead, they are introduced as early-stage prototypes that are explicitly designed for public testing and refinement before any decision is made to scale them.

* **Prototyping Public Services:** The Pavilion would champion an approach of low-cost, low-risk, iterative experimentation for developing new public services.45 This methodology, which grew out of design and engineering, allows ideas to be seen, felt, and experienced by potential users early in the development cycle, providing invaluable feedback when the cost of making changes is lowest.45 The report emphasizes the important distinction between prototyping and piloting: prototyping is about testing and refining the core  
  *idea*, usability, and desirability of a service with a small group of users. Piloting, in contrast, is a later-stage test of a relatively well-specified service to assess its operational viability and measure outcomes before a full-scale launch.45  
* **Methods of Prototyping:** The exhibits would employ a variety of quick and inexpensive prototyping techniques to make service ideas tangible for public interaction 46:  
  * **Storyboards:** A series of drawings or images that visualize the steps a citizen would take to use a new service. Visitors could walk through the storyboard and provide feedback on the clarity, logic, and desirability of the proposed experience.  
  * **Role-Playing:** This approach uses actors (who could be staff, volunteers, or other citizens) to bring a service interaction to life. For example, to test a new process for a youth program, visitors could role-play as young people signing up, allowing observers to note points of confusion, frustration, or delight.  
  * **Mock-ups:** This involves creating a physical, non-functional model of a tangible item. To test the design of a new community tool-lending library kiosk, the Pavilion could build a full-scale version out of cardboard and have visitors interact with it to test ergonomics, button placement, and clarity of instructions.46  
* **A Catalogue of Potential Prototypes:** The Pavilion would host a continuously rotating series of prototypes, reflecting the diverse needs and ideas of the community. This could include a wide range of community service projects, such as mock-ups for community gardens, workshops for skill-sharing programs, pop-up recycling drives, or interactive installations designed to raise mental health awareness.47 By making the development of these services a public and participatory process, the foundation not only improves the quality of the final product but also builds a ready-made group of advocates and users for the service once it launches.

## **Part IV: A Strategic Blueprint for the Prototype Pavilion**

This final part of the report translates the conceptual frameworks and operational models into an actionable strategy. It begins by analyzing the work of pioneering real-world organizations that embody key aspects of the Prototype Pavilion model, extracting critical lessons from their methodologies. It then synthesizes these lessons into a phased implementation plan, providing a step-by-step guide for bringing a Prototype Pavilion from concept to reality.

### **4.1 Learning from the Pioneers: Case Studies in Systemic Innovation**

While the Prototype Pavilion as a fully integrated concept is novel, its core components are being actively developed and tested by a new generation of innovative organizations. By examining their work, we can derive proven strategies and a deeper understanding of the model's potential.

* **Participatory City Foundation ("Every One Every Day"):**  
  * **Methodology:** This ambitious project, conducted in the London Borough of Barking and Dagenham, aimed to create a large-scale "practical participation ecosystem".50 Its core innovation was the creation of a  
    **support platform**—a network of accessible neighborhood shops and a large makerspace warehouse—that provided residents with the space, tools, training, and insurance needed to launch their own peer-to-peer projects.51 This model intentionally removed the significant bureaucratic burdens (forming a charity, applying for grants, managing finances) that typically prevent everyday citizens from taking collective action.52  
  * **Relevance:** The "Every One Every Day" project provides a powerful, tested model for the Pavilion's role as an **enabling infrastructure**. It demonstrates that by focusing on lowering the barriers to entry and providing practical support, an organization can unleash a tremendous amount of latent creativity and collaborative energy within a community. The foundation's primary role shifts from being a direct provider of services to being a cultivator of an ecosystem of resident-led activity.  
* **RadicalxChange:**  
  * **Methodology:** RadicalxChange is a global movement focused on designing, promoting, and implementing next-generation mechanisms for governance and economics.43 Their work is centered on core concepts like Quadratic Voting (QV), Quadratic Funding (QF), and Partial Common Ownership (a system for property that aims to increase both efficiency and equality).34 Crucially, RadicalxChange does not just theorize; it actively pilots these mechanisms in real-world contexts. A notable case study is their collaboration with the Colorado State Legislature, which has used QV to prioritize spending bills, and with community groups in the state to facilitate citizen assemblies on climate policy.34  
  * **Relevance:** RadicalxChange provides the specific, field-tested toolkit for the Pavilion's "Governance Experiments" and "Community-Funded Pilots" exhibits. Their work demonstrates that these seemingly abstract models can be successfully applied in real political and civic settings to produce more nuanced and democratic outcomes.  
* **Dark Matter Labs:**  
  * **Methodology:** Dark Matter Labs is a strategic design and development organization that focuses on what it calls "institutional dark matter"—the invisible systems of finance, law, governance, and policy that shape our world.56 Their work involves designing and building new institutional infrastructures for the 21st century. Projects like "Radicle Civics" explore alternative ways of organizing society based on distributed agency, while other initiatives develop new legal and financial instruments for stewarding common assets, such as trees or urban spaces.57 Their approach is deeply systemic, experimental, and focused on rewriting the fundamental "code" of our social operating systems.60  
  * **Relevance:** Dark Matter Labs offers a conceptual framework for the deepest and most ambitious level of prototyping the Pavilion could undertake. While other exhibits might test specific programs or voting systems, the principles guiding Dark Matter Labs suggest that the Pavilion could also become a space for prototyping the fundamental legal and financial structures upon which the foundation itself is built.  
* **The GovLab:**  
  * **Methodology:** The GovLab is an action-research center based at New York University that focuses on using advances in technology and science to create more open, collaborative, and legitimate governance.61 A key part of their work involves identifying successful governance innovations and translating them into actionable knowledge for others to use. Their "CrowdLaw" initiative, for instance, studied dozens of examples of public participation in lawmaking around the world and synthesized the findings into a "CrowdLaw Playbook" for legislatures, complete with case studies and practical strategies.62  
  * **Relevance:** The GovLab provides a model for a critical function of the Prototype Pavilion: knowledge dissemination. The Pavilion is not just a laboratory for its own benefit; it is a site for generating public knowledge. By adopting a GovLab-style approach, the Pavilion can systematically document its experiments, failures, and successes, and package these learnings into replicable playbooks, tools, and frameworks that can be adopted and adapted by other institutions globally.

The analysis of these pioneering organizations reveals a common, powerful thread. Traditional foundations typically fund other organizations that directly deliver services. These pioneers, however, operate at a different level. They build the infrastructures (Participatory City), protocols (RadicalxChange), institutional logics (Dark Matter Labs), and knowledge platforms (The GovLab) that enable *new forms of organizing* to emerge. This points to the rise of a new institutional category: the **"meta-institution."** The primary function of a meta-institution is not to execute a specific social mission itself, but to create the conditions, tools, and rules—the operating system—for a diverse ecosystem of actors to self-organize and pursue their own missions more effectively. The Prototype Pavilion is the ultimate expression of this concept. It is a foundation that prototypes the very operating system for civil society, making it an open platform upon which countless other community-built organizations can be launched and sustained.66

### **4.2 Phased Implementation Plan**

This section outlines a practical, three-phase plan for launching and developing a Prototype Pavilion. This blueprint is designed to be iterative, incorporating the model's own principles of experimentation and learning at each stage.

#### **Phase 1: Scaffolding the Experiment (Months 1-6)**

This initial phase is focused on establishing the minimum necessary structure to begin the public experiment.

* **Legal Structure:** The first step is to choose a legal framework that allows for both a clear social mission and significant operational flexibility. An unincorporated association is simple to start but offers no liability protection.68 A company limited by guarantee, particularly a  
  **Community Interest Company (CIC)** in the UK context, or a **Charitable Incorporated Organisation (CIO)**, provides the benefits of incorporation (the organization can hold contracts and property, and liability is limited) while being designed for social enterprise and community benefit.68  
* **Define Initial Hypothesis:** The founding group must articulate the first version of the foundation's mission as a clear, testable hypothesis. This should be specific and measurable. For example: "We hypothesize that by providing free, open access to a shared industrial kitchen and business development support (the prototype), we can increase the number of new, locally-owned food businesses in the community by 20% within two years."  
* **Minimum Viable Governance:** Instead of trying to design the perfect, permanent governance structure from the outset, the Pavilion should start with a simple, transparent, and explicitly temporary model. A small, **self-appointing council** or steering committee, composed of key initial stakeholders, can be formed with the clear, public mandate to guide the initial launch and to oversee the transition to a more participatory, community-driven governance model within a set timeframe (e.g., 18 months).36

#### **Phase 2: Launching the Public Laboratory (Months 7-18)**

This phase focuses on opening the physical space and deploying the first set of interactive prototypes.

* **Physical Space:** Secure and design the Pavilion space itself. Following the model of "Every One Every Day," the space should be highly visible, accessible (e.g., a high-street shop front), and welcoming.52 The interior design should be flexible and modular, with designated areas for interactive exhibits, workshops, project development, and informal social gatherings.51  
* **Deploy First Prototypes:** Launch the initial set of exhibits, each corresponding to a core institutional function. This could include:  
  * A "Governance Station" where visitors can use tablets to test and compare different voting mechanisms (e.g., one-person-one-vote vs. Quadratic Voting) on a sample issue.  
  * A "Participatory Budgeting Wall" where a small, real pilot fund is allocated based on visitors placing stickers or tokens on proposed community projects.  
  * A "Service Mock-up Corner" featuring a cardboard mock-up of a proposed new community resource, which visitors are invited to interact with and redesign.46  
* **Establish Feedback Loops:** Implement robust, multi-channel systems for capturing public feedback. This should include **active** methods, such as facilitated workshops, user-testing sessions, and surveys, as well as **passive** methods, such as systematically observing how visitors interact with the exhibits, which parts they ignore, and where they express confusion or delight.2

#### **Phase 3: Iteration and Ecosystem Growth (Months 19+)**

This ongoing phase is where the "living" nature of the institution becomes fully manifest.

* **Public Learning:** The foundation must commit to radical transparency. It should host regular public forums or "learning festivals" to share the data and insights gathered from the experiments. This includes being honest about what failed, what was learned from those failures, and how that learning is shaping the foundation's next steps. This public accounting is the "Learn" phase of the feedback loop at an institutional scale.  
* **Evolve the Institution:** The validated learning gathered must be used to make concrete, structural changes to the foundation. If a particular governance model proves more effective and legitimate in public tests, it should be formally adopted. If community-funded pilots consistently outperform top-down grant-making, the foundation's budget allocation should shift accordingly. This is the institutional-scale "pivot or persevere" decision, demonstrating that the foundation is truly responsive to its community.  
* **Spin-Off and Scale:** A key measure of the Pavilion's long-term success is its ability to cultivate a thriving ecosystem of independent, community-built organizations.66 The Pavilion should develop clear pathways for successful prototypes to graduate and become self-sustaining entities. A community garden project that gains significant traction, for example, could be provided with support to formally incorporate as its own co-operative or unincorporated association.68 This process, which mirrors the way successful art projects evolve into formal organizations 70, ensures that the Pavilion's resources are continually freed up to test new hypotheses, allowing it to remain a perpetual engine of innovation for the community.

## **Conclusion: The Future of Founding**

The Prototype Pavilion represents more than just an innovative model for a single foundation; it proposes a fundamental rethinking of how social institutions are created and sustained in the 21st century. By recasting the foundation as a living hypothesis and its public interface as an experimental laboratory, it offers a powerful pathway toward creating organizations that are inherently adaptive, deeply legitimate, and genuinely co-created with the communities they serve. This model marks a critical shift in the philosophy of philanthropy: a move away from the certainty of "knowing" what is best for a community and toward the humility and dynamism of "learning" alongside it; a transition from the role of a "provider" of solutions to that of an "enabler" of community agency.

However, embarking on this path requires confronting two significant challenges that are inherent to the model's design. The first is the **paradox of stability**: how can an institution built on the principle of perpetual experimentation maintain the long-term focus, commitment, and reliability necessary to achieve lasting impact? An organization that is constantly pivoting risks becoming incoherent and untrustworthy. The solution likely lies in a multi-layered structure where the deepest level—the meta-hypothesis or the core commitment to a participatory process of community betterment—remains stable, while the specific strategies, programs, and governance structures used to pursue that mission are subject to continuous iteration. The stability comes from the process, not the specific form.

The second, and more profound, challenge lies in the **ethics of public experimentation**. A public laboratory is not a sterile, controlled environment; it involves real people, real resources, and real community aspirations. The foundation bears an immense responsibility to conduct its experiments ethically. This requires a steadfast commitment to genuine inclusivity, ensuring that the voices of the most marginalized are not only heard but centered in the design process. It demands radical transparency about the experimental nature of the work, managing expectations and building trust through honesty about both successes and failures. It also necessitates robust protocols for protecting participant data and carefully navigating the complex power dynamics that exist between any institution and the public it engages.

Confronting these challenges is not a barrier to adopting the model, but rather an integral part of its work. The Prototype Pavilion is, at its core, an experiment in building trust. It is a call for philanthropic innovators, community leaders, and institutional designers to step out of the boardroom and into the public square. It is an invitation to move beyond the static blueprints of the past and to embrace the messy, unpredictable, but ultimately more resilient and democratic process of founding by exhibition. In doing so, we can begin to create the living, breathing institutions that our complex future demands.
