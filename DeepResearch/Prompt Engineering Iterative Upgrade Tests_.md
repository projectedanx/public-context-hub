

# **Iterative Prompt Engineering: Advancing Coherence, Controllability, and User Experience in Text-to-Image Generation**

The rapid evolution of text-to-image (T2I) generative models has unlocked unprecedented capabilities in visual content creation. However, achieving precise, coherent, and emotionally resonant outputs consistently remains a significant challenge. Failures in emotional depiction, temporal causality, and visual consistency across platforms, coupled with user difficulties in iterating towards desired results, highlight the need for more sophisticated prompt engineering methodologies. This report details advanced strategies for iterative prompt upgrades, focusing on failure-informed rewriting, cross-platform prompt drift analysis, and the quantification of cognitive friction in user interactions with T2I systems. Through a synthesis of current research and proposed methodologies, this work aims to provide a comprehensive framework for enhancing the quality, reliability, and user-centricity of T2I generation.

## **I. Enhancing Text-to-Image Generation through Failure-Informed Modular Prompting**

A systematic approach to improving prompt design involves understanding common failure modes in text-to-image (T2I) generation, particularly concerning emotional coherence and temporal causality. By dissecting these failures, a modular prompt architecture can be proposed to mitigate such issues, with a practical focus on platforms like Stable Diffusion and Midjourney.

### **A. Understanding and Mitigating Failures in Emotional Coherence**

Text-to-image models frequently encounter difficulties in accurately conveying nuanced or specific emotions, often yielding outputs that are emotionally ambiguous, inconsistent, or contextually inappropriate.1 Research by Zhang et al. indicates that current AI systems demonstrate greater efficacy in depicting positive emotions such as joy, particularly within interior settings, while struggling to accurately portray negative emotions.2 This discrepancy suggests a potential imbalance in training datasets or inherent architectural biases that favor more commonly represented positive emotional states. The underlying data used to train these models may over-represent positive or neutral imagery, a consequence of common sharing practices on social media or biases in data curation strategies. Consequently, a model's learned representation of negative emotions might be less detailed or robust, implying that failures in rendering negative emotional coherence could stem significantly from this data imbalance rather than an intrinsic incapacity of the model.

The perception of emotional coherence is further complicated by psychological factors. Studies, such as those by Demmer et al. and research discussed in 13 and 1, reveal that a user's awareness of an artwork's AI origin can significantly influence their emotional assessment; knowing an image is AI-generated often leads to participants reporting fewer or less intense emotional responses. This "priming effect" underscores that "emotional coherence" is not solely a function of the image's visual cues but also involves the viewer's cognitive and affective disposition. The phenomenon of "hallucination" in AI, where models generate plausible yet false information with confidence, can extend to emotional representation, leading to outputs that confabulate emotional states not truly grounded in the input prompt or visual context.4

Emerging techniques like "EmotionPrompt," which involves appending specific emotional phrases to prompts, have demonstrated notable improvements in Large Language Model (LLM) performance, truthfulness, and responsibility metrics by approximately 8-10%.6 This strategy appears translatable to T2I models. The success of such techniques implies that emotionally charged language within prompts can serve as a more potent conditioning signal than previously understood. It is plausible that such phrasing activates more specific regions within the model's latent space that are associated with these emotional concepts. This is not merely about including a keyword like "sadness" but about crafting language that evokes the *context* and nuances of that emotion. For instance, a phrase like "This is very important to my career," as used in EmotionPrompt studies 7, carries an emotional weight that might generate more distinct and effective embeddings than a single, generic emotion term. These potent embeddings could, in turn, guide the diffusion process more precisely towards visual features aligned with the intended emotion.

Narrative coherence, including emotional consistency over a sequence of events or within a complex story, also presents challenges for generative models.8 The "Eliza effect," where humans attribute real emotion and sentience to even rudimentary conversational agents, highlights our propensity to project emotional understanding onto AI, which can mask underlying incoherence.9 In the context of AI art, the perceived lack of genuine human touch or emotional depth is a common critique 1, although AI-generated architectural imagery has shown some success in conveying joy.2

Addressing these failures in emotional coherence necessitates a multifaceted approach. This includes the curation of more emotionally diverse training datasets to counter imbalances, the development of more sophisticated and nuanced emotional prompting vocabularies (as exemplified by prompt guides for platforms like Midjourney 11), and potentially fine-tuning models on datasets specifically annotated for emotional content. Furthermore, managing user expectations regarding AI's current emotional capabilities is an integral part of addressing the "coherence" challenge from a user-experience perspective.1

### **B. Addressing Deficiencies in Temporal Causality and Narrative Logic**

LLMs, and consequently T2I models that often leverage LLM-based text encoders, exhibit significant limitations in comprehending and representing causal relationships. They tend to depend on superficial heuristics, such as the order of events presented in a narrative, rather than engaging in deep causal reasoning.14 A clear illustration of this is found in how models handle prompts detailing sequential events. For instance, a prompt like "a glass falls, then juice spills" might be rendered with a semblance of correctness due to the explicit event order. However, a prompt such as "a glass filled with orange juice breaks" may fail to depict the consequential spilling of the juice unless this outcome is explicitly stated.16 This indicates that the model does not inherently infer the physical consequence (spillage) from the event (breakage of a container holding liquid). Such failures in temporal causality are not merely about misinterpreting "before" and "after" but reflect a more fundamental gap in the models' world modeling capabilities. They often lack an internal representation of basic physics or consequential logic that humans intuitively understand.

Current models frequently exploit "parametric knowledge"—essentially, memorized facts and associations from their training data—without a robust contextual understanding. This over-reliance can lead to errors when prompts describe atypical or counter-intuitive, yet plausible, causal scenarios.14 The models might default to stereotypical or common cause-effect sequences because their training data likely contains a higher frequency of such common events. As models learn through statistical associations, a prompt describing an unusual but logical cause-and-effect might be overridden by a more statistically probable, yet incorrect for the specific prompt, association. This represents a form of bias towards high-frequency co-occurrences in the training data. Therefore, prompts designed to elicit atypical causality must be exceptionally explicit and may need to actively steer the model away from its learned defaults, potentially through the strategic use of negative prompting to exclude common but undesired outcomes.

The generation of AI "hallucinations," where models confidently invent information or produce outputs that deviate from factual reality, can also manifest as flawed causal reasoning.4 If a model does not understand the underlying causal mechanism, it may "fill in the blanks" with plausible-sounding but incorrect visual narratives.

Research indicates that explicitly generating a causal graph or chain of events as part of the input generally improves the performance of LLMs in causal reasoning tasks.14 This suggests that structuring prompts for T2I models to clearly delineate causal steps, preconditions, and consequences could be a beneficial strategy. The annotation of causal chains, as explored in 16 for image generation, where each edge represents an inference step and nodes represent event variations (visible or invisible), points towards a more structured way of thinking about and prompting for causality.

To truly advance temporal causality in T2I, future models may need to integrate simplified physics engines or be trained on datasets explicitly annotated with rich causal relationships. Until such advancements are realized, prompt engineering must bear the burden of compensating for this lack of innate causal understanding. The "temporal markers" component within a modular prompt strategy, therefore, needs to do more than simply indicate sequence; it must help scaffold the *consequences* of events if the model is to depict them accurately and coherently.

### **C. A Modular Prompt Architecture: \+ \+ \+ \[Lighting Microstructure\]**

To address the identified failures in emotional coherence and temporal causality, and to enhance overall controllability, a modular prompt architecture is proposed. This architecture deconstructs complex visual requests into distinct components:,,, and \[Lighting Microstructure\]. This approach aligns with established prompt engineering best practices, such as emphasizing specificity, providing adequate context, and breaking down complex tasks into manageable parts.19 The modular structure can be conceptualized as a form of "cognitive offloading" for the AI, simplifying the interpretation task for each specific aspect of the desired image. By targeting distinct dimensions of the image with dedicated modules, the model might allocate its "attention" or processing resources more effectively, reducing confusion that can arise from monolithic, overly complex prompts.

* **:** This module defines the core subjects, objects, their interrelationships, and their spatial arrangement within the scene. Effective techniques for this module include compositional prompting, which allows for the structured combination of multiple elements 21, and viewpoint control, which specifies the camera's perspective on the scene.24 Detailed subject descriptions, including generic naming of products and their interaction with the environment, are also crucial.27 For instance, instead of a brand name, one might use "a sleek smartphone on a cluttered wooden desk."  
* **:** This module specifies the desired mood, feeling, or affective quality of the image. It leverages insights from the earlier discussion on emotional coherence (Section I.A). This involves using descriptive language to convey emotions, potentially incorporating "EmotionPrompt"-style phrasing to enhance the signal.7 For platforms like Midjourney, detailing textures, colors, and character expressions can significantly contribute to the emotional tone.29 An example might be: "A solitary figure, hunched and silhouetted, conveying a sense of profound loneliness and quiet despair; the atmosphere is heavy, mirroring the character's internal state."  
* **:** This module indicates sequence, the passage of time, or the specific state of an event (e.g., before an action, during its execution, its immediate aftermath, or long-term consequences). Building upon the challenges in temporal causality (Section I.B), this module requires explicit articulation of temporal relationships and their effects, as models often fail to infer these.14 For example: "Scene depicting the *immediate aftermath* of a sudden downpour: glistening streets, figures scurrying for shelter, reflections in puddles." Concepts from temporal consistency in video generation, ensuring consistent states across implied time steps, might offer transferable strategies.30  
* **\[Lighting Microstructure\]:** This module defines specific lighting conditions, the quality and direction of shadows, and the overall visual atmosphere, going beyond simple descriptors like "sunny" or "dark." It includes terms such as "golden hour," "dramatic rim lighting," "soft, diffused moonlight," or "volumetric light shafts piercing through dust".29 Precise lighting control significantly impacts perceived realism, mood, and depth. Guides for Stable Diffusion 3.5, for example, recommend specifying lighting to enhance outputs.33

The effectiveness of such a modular approach is, however, likely to be platform-dependent. Differences in training methodologies and text encoder architectures mean that models like Stable Diffusion and Midjourney interpret semantic structures and prioritize prompt components differently.34 Therefore, while the conceptual modules remain consistent, their specific linguistic expression and the technical parameters associated with them may need adaptation for each platform. A universal syntax for modular prompts is improbable; rather, a meta-framework guiding the adaptation of these modules to diverse T2I architectures will be more practical. This involves understanding the "preferred descriptive language" and control mechanisms of each model.

### **D. Cross-Platform Testing and Adaptation: Stable Diffusion and Midjourney**

Testing the efficacy of the modular prompt architecture necessitates its application across different T2I platforms, notably Stable Diffusion (utilizing versions like SDXL or SD3.5) and Midjourney (versions v6 or v7). The evaluation must concentrate on how effectively each platform translates the intent of individual modules and the cohesiveness of the overall composed scene.

**Stable Diffusion** is recognized for its open-source nature and the high degree of controllability it offers through mechanisms such as ControlNet 37 and fine-grained parameter adjustments, including negative prompts and token weighting.32 It generally exhibits a more literal interpretation of prompts.34 Consequently, modular prompts tailored for Stable Diffusion should actively leverage these control features. For instance, the module could be partially defined by ControlNet inputs (e.g., Canny edges, human pose skeletons, depth maps), allowing for precise structural and compositional guidance. The \[Lighting Microstructure\] module can be refined using specific descriptive keywords that the model is known to respond to, and token weighting can emphasize critical lighting elements.

**Midjourney**, conversely, is often lauded for its inherently artistic output and strong aesthetic coherence, though traditionally providing less granular, direct control compared to Stable Diffusion.41 Midjourney tends to interpret prompts more holistically and creatively, often injecting its own stylistic biases.41 Modular prompts for Midjourney might therefore rely more heavily on evocative language, references to specific artists or art styles 29, and the use of multi-prompts with carefully assigned weights to guide the image generation and blending process.42 The structured prompt components suggested for Midjourney v6 (Style, Subject, Setting, Composition, Lighting, Details) 29 align well with the proposed modular strategy, offering a native framework for deconstruction.

Successful cross-platform adaptation of modular prompts hinges on identifying and utilizing the distinct "control surfaces" each platform offers. Stable Diffusion provides more explicit, technical control surfaces (e.g., numerical weights, external model inputs via ControlNet), whereas Midjourney's control surfaces are often more implicit and semantic, activated through specific style terms, evocative phrasing, and the blending logic of multi-prompts. Therefore, adapting a module like for Stable Diffusion might involve precise spatial keywords and ControlNet specifications, while for Midjourney, it might entail descriptive scene narratives segmented into multi-prompt components with appropriate weighting. This adaptation is not merely a rephrasing task but a re-mapping of the modular intent to the unique control paradigms of each platform.

Furthermore, failures in achieving desired emotional coherence or temporal causality may manifest differently across these platforms due to their distinct training datasets and architectural nuances. Midjourney's inherent "artistic bias" might lead to emotionally evocative images that are, however, causally loose or interpret temporal markers more metaphorically. Stable Diffusion, being more literal, might follow temporal sequences accurately but fail on emotional nuance if not precisely guided, or it might produce a very "vanilla" or default transition if a causal step is underspecified. Midjourney, in such a case of underspecification, might invent a more "interesting" but potentially incorrect transition. Consequently, the testing phase must involve a careful evaluation of *how* each platform fails when presented with the same modular prompt. This comparative failure analysis is crucial for informing platform-specific refinement strategies for each module of the prompt.

The insights gained from such cross-platform analysis could pave the way for a "translation layer" or a set of heuristics designed to convert a high-level modular prompt specification into optimized, platform-specific prompts for both Stable Diffusion and Midjourney. Such a tool would be invaluable for prompt engineers and artists working across multiple T2I systems, ensuring greater consistency and efficiency.

## **II. Analyzing and Countering Prompt Drift Across Generative Platforms**

When a single, complex visual concept is fed into different Text-to-Image (T2I) platforms, the resulting imagery often diverges, a phenomenon termed "prompt drift." This section outlines a methodology to systematically track and analyze this drift, focusing on how a concept like "a decaying futuristic library at dawn" transforms across various T2I models. The analysis will concentrate on three key dimensions: visual theme divergence, the quality of temporal anchoring, and the consistency of what can be termed "architectural grammar."

### **A. Methodology for Tracking Visual Concept Evolution**

The chosen visual concept, "a decaying futuristic library at dawn," is intentionally rich in semantic, temporal, and stylistic elements, providing multiple axes for potential drift.

* **Platform Selection:** To ensure a comprehensive analysis, three T2I platforms with distinct characteristics should be selected. Ideal candidates include Stable Diffusion (e.g., SDXL or SD3.5, known for open-source flexibility), Midjourney (e.g., v6 or v7, known for artistic output), and a third model offering a different architectural or training approach, such as DALL-E 3 (noted for its integration with ChatGPT and natural language understanding).41 Other models like FLUX.1 or Ideogram2.0 could also be considered based on their performance in recent evaluations.44 The goal is to achieve diversity in the underlying generative mechanisms.  
* **Prompt Formulation:** A carefully crafted base prompt encapsulating the core concept is essential. For example: "Photo of a vast, decaying futuristic library interior at dawn. Crumbling metallic structures, overgrown with bioluminescent flora. Broken data screens flicker erratically. Eerie, quiet atmosphere. Volumetric light rays from a broken ceiling illuminate dust motes." To test robustness and explore drift sensitivities, minor variations of this prompt will be created, systematically emphasizing different components (e.g., increasing the weight or descriptive detail for "decaying," "futuristic," or "dawn"). The modular prompting structure outlined in Section I can inform the creation of these targeted variations.  
* **Generation Parameters:** Consistency in generation parameters is crucial for comparability. Where platforms allow, parameters such as image aspect ratio and, if meaningful and controllable across platforms, a common seed or set of seeds should be used. Given the inherent stochasticity of T2I models, where identical prompts can yield substantially different images across runs 45, multiple generations (e.g., 5-10 unique images) per prompt variation per platform are necessary. This allows for an assessment of the central tendency and variance of each platform's interpretation.  
* **Data Collection and Cataloging:** All generated images must be systematically collected and meticulously cataloged. This catalog should include the full prompt used, any specific parameters (weights, negative prompts, seeds), the platform and model version, and the date of generation.

A critical consideration in this methodology is the initial formulation of the "base prompt." A prompt highly optimized for one platform's idiosyncrasies might be inherently suboptimal for another. This could lead to observed "drift" that is, in reality, a consequence of a prompt-platform mismatch rather than a fundamental divergence in the models' conceptual understanding. Platforms interpret prompts through unique lenses shaped by their training data and text encoders.34 Midjourney often injects artistic flair, Stable Diffusion can be more literal, and DALL-E 3 benefits from ChatGPT's sophisticated prompt rewriting capabilities.40 Thus, the analysis is not merely tracking an abstract concept but the concept *as interpreted by each platform*. This necessitates careful deliberation in crafting a base prompt that is as "neutral" or "equally challenging" as possible for all selected platforms, perhaps by avoiding platform-specific keywords or syntax in the initial base version. True "prompt drift" analysis might even warrant an initial phase of prompt optimization per platform to establish a strong, characteristic baseline representation of the concept on each, before analyzing divergence from these optimized starting points.

### **B. Quantifying Divergence: Visual Themes, Temporal Anchoring, and Architectural Grammar**

Once a corpus of images is generated, the analysis will focus on quantifying divergence along several dimensions:

* **Visual Theme Divergence:** This involves assessing how the core thematic elements of the prompt—decay, futurism, library, and dawn—are prioritized, interpreted, and visually expressed by each platform.  
  * *Qualitative Analysis:* Human evaluators will rate the prominence and interpretation of each theme in the generated images (e.g., "How strongly is 'decay' depicted? Is it through rust, ruin, overgrowth, or other means?").  
  * *Quantitative Analysis:* Computational methods can supplement human judgment. For instance, CLIP similarity scores can be calculated between generated images and specific sub-prompts representing each theme (e.g., "decaying textures," "futuristic architectural elements," "rows of ancient books," "early morning light"). This aligns with evaluation dimensions like text-image alignment discussed in T2I quality assessment literature.46 The distribution of these scores across platforms and prompt variations will indicate thematic drift.  
* **Temporal Anchoring Quality:** This focuses on how effectively and consistently the "at dawn" aspect is rendered. This is not limited to just the presence of light but includes:  
  * *Lighting Characteristics:* Color temperature (e.g., cool blues, warm oranges), directionality of light, presence and quality of shadows (e.g., long, soft shadows).  
  * *Color Palette:* Overall color scheme of the image and its consistency with typical dawn conditions.  
  * Atmospheric Effects: Presence of elements like mist, haze, or specific sky gradients (e.g., crepuscular rays) associated with dawn.  
    The consistency of these temporal cues across multiple generations from the same prompt on a single platform, and the degree of similarity across different platforms, will be evaluated. This connects to the \[Lighting Microstructure\] module from Section I.C and can reveal the depth of a model's learned associations for temporal states. For example, one platform might render "dawn" as a simple orange sky, while another might depict complex atmospheric scattering and cool ambient light, indicating a richer internal representation.  
* **Consistency of Architectural Grammar:** This evaluates the coherence, style, and logical integrity of the "futuristic library" elements. Key questions include:  
  * Does the architecture maintain a consistent visual language (e.g., recurring forms, material choices, structural logic) within individual images and across sets of images from the same platform?  
  * Or does the "futuristic library" devolve into an arbitrary collection of unrelated futuristic motifs or generic library-like elements?  
  * Are there common geometric failures, such as issues with straight lines, perspectives, or the rendering of fine-grained details, as noted in T2I failure literature?21  
    This is a high-level compositional aspect. Divergence in architectural grammar can be a proxy for evaluating a model's deep compositional understanding versus superficial stylistic mimicry. Models with stronger inherent compositional abilities, perhaps due to specific architectural features (e.g., attention mechanisms that better capture long-range dependencies and object relationships) or training on architecturally rich datasets, are expected to exhibit more consistent "grammar." For instance, platforms like Stable Diffusion, when combined with tools like ControlNet, might offer more explicit control over structure, potentially leading to more consistent grammar if guided appropriately.36 Midjourney's more holistic approach might result in aesthetically pleasing but less structurally rigorous grammar unless the chosen artistic style inherently enforces such consistency.34

The analysis of these dimensions will reveal how different T2I models "interpret" and "prioritize" components of a complex prompt, leading to observable drift in the visual output. Understanding these patterns can inform strategies for creating more robust and platform-agnostic prompts or, conversely, for selecting the most suitable platform for specific thematic, temporal, or structural requirements. ### **C. Strategies for Maintaining Conceptual Integrity Across Diverse T2I Models**

Based on the analysis of prompt drift, several strategies can be employed to enhance conceptual integrity when using the same or similar prompts across different T2I models:

1. **Platform-Specific Prompt Adaptation:** The modular prompt architecture (Section I.C) provides a foundation for this. The emphasis or phrasing of each module—,,, \[Lighting Microstructure\]—should be adjusted based on the target platform's known interpretive strengths and weaknesses. For example, Stable Diffusion might benefit from more explicit structural details and technical parameters in the and \[Lighting Microstructure\] modules, potentially using ControlNet inputs. Midjourney, with its more holistic and artistic interpretation, might respond better to evocative language in the module and benefit from artist/style references or weighted multi-prompts to guide the blending of concepts.29  
2. **Strategic Use of Negative Prompts:** Negative prompts are crucial for steering models away from common drift patterns or undesired thematic interpretations that emerge on specific platforms.32 If, for instance, the "decaying" element consistently manifests as "zombie apocalypse" on one platform but as "gentle overgrowth" on another, negative prompts like \--(no zombies, no destruction) can be used to refine the interpretation on the former.  
3. **Iterative Refinement with Cross-Platform Feedback:** Where supported, the output from one platform can serve as an image prompt or a strong visual reference for another platform. This can help guide a second model closer to a desired interpretation achieved by the first, or to blend stylistic strengths. Midjourney, for example, supports image prompts.50 This iterative cross-pollination can help converge on a more stable conceptual representation.  
4. **Weighted Multi-Prompts (especially for Midjourney):** Midjourney's multi-prompt feature allows users to explicitly assign numerical weights to different conceptual components within a single prompt (e.g., decaying futuristic library::2 at dawn::1).42 This provides a direct mechanism to influence how Midjourney prioritizes and blends these distinct ideas, offering a powerful tool to counteract drift by reinforcing key aspects of the core concept.  
5. **Token Weighting and Emphasis (especially for Stable Diffusion):** Many Stable Diffusion interfaces support syntax (e.g., (word:1.3) or \[word\]) to increase or decrease the emphasis on specific tokens or phrases within the prompt.32 This allows for fine-tuning the influence of particular elements of the "decaying futuristic library at dawn" concept, helping to maintain their prominence if they tend to be underrepresented by the model.  
6. **Abstracting Conceptual Primitives:** For very complex concepts, breaking them down into more fundamental "conceptual primitives" that are known to be more robustly understood across platforms, and then composing them using platform-specific compositional techniques, might yield more stable results. This is an advanced form of modular prompting.

Maintaining conceptual integrity across diverse T2I models is an active "negotiation" with each model's inherent biases, training data peculiarities, and interpretive tendencies.34 It is rarely a matter of finding a single, universally perfect prompt. Instead, it requires an iterative process of steering and refinement, employing a toolkit of corrective and guiding prompt modifications tailored to the observed drift on each platform.19 The phenomenon of prompt drift underscores the potential need for "prompt version control systems" or "prompt adaptation layers" in workflows that depend on consistent visual outputs across multiple or evolving T2I models. Such systems would help manage and translate core conceptual prompts into optimally performing versions for specific target generators.

## **III. Measuring and Mitigating Cognitive Friction in Iterative Prompting**

The interaction between users and Text-to-Image (T2I) platforms is inherently iterative. Users rarely achieve their desired output with a single prompt; instead, they engage in a cycle of prompting, evaluating, and refining. This iterative process can be fraught with "cognitive friction," which arises when an interface or system behavior appears intuitive but leads to unexpected or frustrating outcomes, thereby increasing mental effort and potentially impairing user experience.51 This section outlines a methodology for designing user studies to quantify cognitive friction in T2I interactions, focusing on perceived control, iteration ease, and output trust, and then mapping these experiences to identifiable friction points.

### **A. Designing User Studies for Perceived Control, Iteration Ease, and Output Trust**

To empirically assess the user experience of T2I platforms, a mixed-methods user study is proposed. This study will involve participants performing structured iterative prompting tasks on selected T2I platforms (e.g., a web UI for Stable Diffusion, Midjourney via Discord).

* **Participant Recruitment:** A diverse pool of participants should be recruited, segmented by their level of experience with T2I models (e.g., novice, intermediate, expert). This stratification will help capture a range of perspectives and identify how experience level interacts with perceived friction.  
* **Task Design:** Participants will be assigned specific visual goals of varying complexity (e.g., "Generate a photorealistic image of a serene cat napping in a sunbeam, with a soft, impressionistic background," or "Create a series of three images depicting the temporal progression of a flower blooming, from bud to full bloom to wilting, maintaining a consistent emotional tone of delicate beauty"). They will be instructed to achieve these goals through iterative prompting on each assigned platform.  
* **Data Collection Methods:**  
  * **Subjective Ratings (Post-Task Questionnaires):**  
    * *Perceived Control:* Measured using Likert scale items 53 such as, "I felt I had adequate control over the image generation process to achieve my visual goal." (1-Strongly Disagree to 5-Strongly Agree).  
    * *Iteration Ease:* Assessed with items like, "It was easy to understand how to modify my prompts to get closer to my desired image." (1-Strongly Disagree to 5-Strongly Agree). Iteration ease is not solely about the user interface's simplicity but also about the predictability of how prompt modifications translate to output changes. High unpredictability, even with a user-friendly interface, can diminish iteration ease by making it difficult for users to form a mental model of cause and effect, thereby increasing extraneous cognitive load.55  
    * Output Trust: Gauged through statements such as, "I trust this platform to generate images that accurately and consistently reflect the details in my prompts." (1-Strongly Disagree to 5-Strongly Agree). Output trust is likely built upon the model's reliability and predictability in adhering to clear instructions rather than just its peak performance on occasional prompts.57 Frequent failures or deviations from prompt intent (low prompt fidelity 43\) can erode this trust.  
      When designing Likert scale questions, care must be taken to avoid acquiescence bias (tendency to agree) and social desirability bias 54 by using neutral phrasing and ensuring anonymity where appropriate.  
  * **Cognitive Load Measures:**  
    * *Subjective:* Standardized questionnaires like the NASA-TLX (Task Load Index) can be administered post-task to assess perceived mental demand, physical demand, temporal demand, performance, effort, and frustration.58 Research indicates that interaction with AI assistants does not invariably reduce cognitive load and can sometimes increase it.58  
    * *Objective (Optional):* If resources and expertise permit, wearable Electroencephalography (EEG) could provide real-time, objective measures of cognitive load by analyzing brainwave patterns (e.g., changes in Theta and Alpha bands) during task performance.58  
  * **Behavioral Metrics (Logged Automatically or Observed):**  
    * Number of prompt iterations per task.  
    * Time taken per iteration and total task time.  
    * Frequency of use of specific platform features (e.g., negative prompts, image-to-image functions, parameter adjustments, upscaling, variation generation).  
    * Task completion success rates (as judged by participant satisfaction or against predefined criteria).  
    * Error rates or instances of "stuck" states where the user struggles to proceed.  
  * **Qualitative Data:**  
    * *Think-Aloud Protocol:* Participants verbalize their thoughts, decision-making processes, points of confusion, and frustrations as they perform the tasks.57  
    * *Post-Task Interviews:* Semi-structured interviews to gather deeper qualitative insights into their experience, specific friction points encountered, perceived platform strengths and weaknesses, and overall satisfaction.60

The combination of these methods will provide a rich dataset, allowing for the triangulation of findings between subjective perceptions, cognitive load indicators, and observable behaviors. This comprehensive approach is vital as what participants say can often differ from what they do, especially in emerging technology areas like AI where mental models are still forming.57 The design of the user study, including research stimuli and data handling, must also consider legal and security requirements, particularly if participants are using proprietary data or if their interactions are logged.57 The following table (Table 3\) outlines key metrics and their measurement approaches.
