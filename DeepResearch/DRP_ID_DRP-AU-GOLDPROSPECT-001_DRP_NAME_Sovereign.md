## **ORIENTATION TO AUSTRALIAN GOLDFIELDS — MODULE 1**

### **Definition \& Conceptual Framework**

Gold prospecting in Australia encompasses legal search for gold on public and private land using hand tools, metal detectors, and gravity concentration methods. The term encompasses both "fossicking" (recreational, non-commercial) and "prospecting" (potentially income-earning, formal permits). A "goldfield" is both a **geographical region** historically productive in gold and a **geological setting** hosting economically viable ore. Australia's primary gold provinces formed during Archean (>2.5 billion years ago) and Proterozoic periods; alluvial deposits derive from weathering and transport of primary gold into river systems over the past 65–100 million years.[^1]

### **Why Gold Prospecting Matters in Australia**

Victoria, Western Australia, and Queensland host the world's most accessible alluvial goldfields. Geoscience Australia estimates Victoria alone contains >75 million ounces of undiscovered gold—approximately equal to all gold mined there since 1850. This represents a genuine opportunity for beginner prospectors: unlike some global regions, Australian goldfields are geologically well-mapped, legally accessible under miner's rights, and contain frequent small nuggets and flakes recoverable with modest equipment and skill.[^2]

### **Core Terminology (Defined \& Anchored)**

- **Miner's Right**: A state-issued permit transferring ownership of found minerals to the holder; typically valid 2–10 years; cost \$25–\$50. Required in VIC, NSW, WA, TAS.
- **Fossicking**: Small-scale, hand-held, non-commercial mineral search on Crown land or private land with permission. Often allowed in designated "Fossicking Districts."
- **Crown Land**: Government-owned land (state forests, unallocated land); access rules vary by state.
- **Declared Fossicking Area**: Designated zone where simplified rules apply; minimal permits needed.
- **Alluvial Gold**: Fine gold particles (flakes, "flour") and nuggets in gravels and sediments deposited by water. Source: weathered primary gold deposits upstream.
- **Lead (Historical Mining Term)**: Ancient buried river channel, often containing concentrated alluvial gold. Example: Kanowna Deep Lead (WA) buried under laterite; recoverable by deep mining or drill sampling.
- **Paydir/Paydirt**: Sediment (gravel, clay, sand) containing economically significant gold concentrations; separated using pan, sluice, or detector.
- **Wash**: Accumulated lighter material (clay, silt) winnowed away during panning; residual heavy minerals (gold + black sand) remain.
- **Hot Rocks**: Naturally magnetic or mineralized stones causing false detector signals; distinguished from gold by strength of signal and visual inspection.
- **Tailings/Mullock Heaps**: Piled spoil from historical mining; often reworked by modern prospectors for missed fine gold.
- **Eluvial/Colluvial Deposits**: Gold on slopes near primary source, moved by gravity/weather rather than water; coarser, angular particles.
- **Reef**: Primary hard-rock gold mineralization in quartz veins or host rock; requires large-scale mining; not accessible to hand prospectors.

Reading maps and history reveals that successful prospectors exploit patterns (e.g., gold concentrates along fault zones; old mining concentrations indicate undiscovered gold nearby).

### **Top 10 Beginner Mistakes – Module 1**

1. Assuming "old gold rushes = easy gold today." Truth: Easy alluvial gold was mostly worked out; remaining gold requires systematic technique.
2. Skipping legal research. Truth: Trespassing leads to fines or prosecution; always verify permits and access first.
3. Ignoring geological maps. Truth: Gold is not random; it follows geological structures (faults, lithology, weathering patterns).
4. Picking "scenic" sites instead of productive ones. Truth: Beautiful creeks often have no gold; productive sites often look unpromising.
5. Believing locals' tall tales uncritically. Truth: Seek verification; folk knowledge contains both gems and myths.
6. Thinking one state's rules apply to all. Truth: Each state has unique permit, equipment, and access rules.
7. Underestimating time cost. Truth: Learning 100+ hours of detector work; prospecting 10+ field trips before confidence builds.
8. Buying expensive detector without training. Truth: Mastery matters more than equipment cost; a \$1,000 detector in expert hands beats a \$5,000 detector in novice hands.
9. Ignoring heritage/Indigenous considerations. Truth: Many sites are sacred; disrespect = legal liability + community harm.
10. Starting in winter without seasonal knowledge. Truth: High water = inaccessible creeks; fire bans restrict access; seasons matter.

### **Stop / Don't – Safety \& Legality Callouts**

🛑 **NEVER** trespass on mining leases, exploration licences, or national parks without formal consent.
🛑 **NEVER** ignore fire ban days; Australian bush fires move fast; prospecting in remote areas during bans is dangerous.
🛑 **NEVER** disturb Aboriginal rock art, burial sites, or heritage places; this is both illegal and deeply disrespectful.
🛑 **NEVER** assume "public land = open access." Crown forests, state parks, waterways have specific rules per state.

## **RULES, PERMITS, ACCESS \& WHERE YOU CAN ACTUALLY PROSPECT — MODULE 2**

### **State-by-State Permit Matrix (Current as of January 2026)**[^4][^5][^6][^7][^8]

#### **Victoria**

- **Permit Required**: Miner's Right (recreational prospecting)
- **Cost**: \$25.70–\$26.20 per 2 years
- **Where Allowed**: State forests, regional parks, some national parks (Castlemaine Diggings, Warrandyte, Chiltern-Mt Pilot, Greater Bendigo, Heathcote-Graytown), waterways (specific list; check earthresources.vic.gov.au)
- **Equipment Allowed**: Hand tools, pans, sieves, hand-held sluices, metal detectors
- **Equipment Banned**: Power-operated equipment, explosives, motorized pumps
- **Private Land**: Written permission from landholder required
- **Special Rules**: Must carry permit at all times; inspectors conduct spot checks; no disturbance of Aboriginal/heritage places
- **Heritage Overlay**: Crown minerals remain state property; Miner's Right transfers found minerals to finder
- **Application**: Online at earthresources.vic.gov.au (~3 minutes)


#### **New South Wales**

- **Permit Type**: Fossicking (no "Miner's Right" but similar concept)
- **Cost**: Free in fossicking districts; permits required for State forests (12 months, nominal fee)
- **Designated Fossicking Districts**: Multiple across state (check minview.geoscience.nsw.gov.au); no exploration licence holder consent needed within districts
- **State Forests**: 12-month permits via Forestry Corporation (forestrycorporation.com.au)
- **Equipment Allowed**: Hand-held tools, pans, sieves, metal detectors
- **Equipment Banned**: Power equipment, explosives, mechanized dredges, jack hammers
- **Restrictions**: Max 1 m³ disturbance per 48-hour period; max 50g gold recovery (except nuggets ≥10g); all refuse removed; holes filled
- **Private Land**: Written landholder consent required
- **Waterways**: Must protect fish habitat; no removal of snags >3m, cobbles >500mm, aquatic vegetation
- **Native Title**: No fossicking without registered Native Title Body Corporate consent
- **Heritage**: Consult NSW National Parks \& Wildlife Service re: archaeological sites


#### **Queensland**

- **Permit Type**: Fossicking Licence
- **Cost**: Nominal annual fee (purchased online or from mines assessment hubs)
- **Where Allowed**: Designated fossicking areas, unallocated state land (unless Native Title/vested), private land with permission
- **Equipment Allowed**: Hand tools; metal detectors (status varies; check qld.gov.au/fossicking)
- **Equipment Banned**: Power equipment, explosives
- **Private Land**: Written permission required
- **Unallocated State Land**: No permission needed *unless* Native Title determination or land vested to another agency
- **Camping**: May require separate fossicking camping permit in some areas
- **Equipment Limits**: No undermining of banks, no pits creating tunnels/overhangs
- **Contact**: Department of Resources (qld.gov.au/fossicking)


#### **Western Australia**

- **Permit Type**: Miner's Right (Crown land); Section 40E Permit (exploration licences)
- **Miner's Right Cost**: Nominal fee; enables prospecting on Crown land, pastoral leases
- **Section 40E Permit**: Required to prospect within existing exploration licence; 21-day notification period to licence holder
- **Where Allowed**: Crown land (all unencumbered public land except reserved/protected areas), pastoral leases with written occupier consent
- **Equipment Allowed**: Hand tools, pans, metal detectors, samples up to 20 kg
- **Equipment Banned**: Power equipment, strip mining (bulk surface disturbance)
- **Restricted Areas**: Class A reserves, certain Crown lands; written consent required
- **Private Land**: Written consent required
- **Rehabilitation**: Fill disturbances, prevent fire/erosion, do not obstruct thoroughfares, no undermining of infrastructure
- **Contact**: Department of Mines, Petroleum \& Exploration (dmp.wa.gov.au)


#### **South Australia**

- **Permit Type**: Fossicking (no formal licence for designated areas; permit required for others)
- **Cost**: No charge for designated fossicking areas; permit required elsewhere
- **Where Allowed**: Crown land (outside national parks, conservation parks, forest reserves); private land with permission
- **Equipment Allowed**: Hand tools, pans, metal detectors
- **Equipment Banned**: Power equipment, mechanical dredges
- **Private Land**: Owner consent required
- **Heritage**: South Australian Heritage Council permit required for excavation in heritage places (section 25–26, Heritage Places Act 1993)
- **Contact**: Department for Environment and Water; Energy \& Mining
- **Restricted Areas**: National Parks, Conservation Parks, Forest Reserves, Aboriginal heritage sites


#### **Tasmania**

- **Permit Type**: Prospecting Licence (outside declared fossicking areas); Fossicking (inside declared fossicking areas)
- **Prospecting Licence Cost**: Modest fee; valid 5 years
- **Where Allowed**: Declared fossicking areas (no licence); outside these areas with Prospecting Licence
- **Equipment Allowed**: Hand-held tools, hand prospecting, metal detectors (outside fossicking areas only)
- **Equipment Banned**: Mechanical machinery, explosives, sluices, motorized pumps
- **Depth Limit**: <2 m below surface
- **Private Land \& Mineral Tenements**: Written consent required from landowner or tenement holder
- **Heritage**: Check with Mineral Resources Tasmania re: Aboriginal cultural heritage
- **Group Prospecting**: Available; group members must hold individual licences
- **Contact**: Mineral Resources Tasmania (mrt.tas.gov.au)


#### **Northern Territory**

- **Permit Type**: Section 40E Permit (primarily for tourist activities)
- **Where Allowed**: Designated fossicking areas, Crown land (varies)
- **Equipment Allowed**: Hand tools, metal detectors (varies by area)
- **Contact**: Department of Industry, Business \& Innovation; fossicking.nt.gov.au
- **Heritage**: Aboriginal Land Rights (Northern Territory) Act 1976; Aboriginal land access requires consent; check NTA (NT Aboriginal Corporation)

### **Detailed Approach Profiles**

#### **1. Gold Panning (Best for Beginners)**

**What it is**: Gravity concentration of sediment in a shallow pan. Heavy gold sinks; light material is washed away in concentric spirals.

**Advantages**:

- Cheapest entry (\$20–\$100 for pan + classifier + snuffer bottle)
- No electricity, battery, or complex setup
- Works anywhere with water
- Teaches gold identification and handling
- Excellent for sampling ("Is this creek worth a day's detecting?")

**Disadvantages**:

- Very slow (processes handful of sediment per 5 minutes)
- Fine gold easily lost if technique poor
- Waterway access required; seasonal flow variations
- Wet, cold work in cool climates

**Ideal Conditions**:

- Spring/summer (water flow, temperature)
- Creeks with visible "black sand" (magnetite, ilmenite) = gold likely
- Areas with previous alluvial workings
- Gentle slopes (easier water access than deep gorges)

**Beginner Skill Path**:

- Week 1: Dry practice (sand + lead BB's simulating gold)
- Week 2–3: First creek visits; sample 10–20 pans to identify hot spots
- Month 2+: Can reliably distinguish gold from "fool's gold" (pyrite); predict which stretches will yield gold


#### **2. Metal Detecting (Most Popular; Best for Nuggets)**

**What it is**: Electromagnetic scanning of ground using VLF (small gold, clear signals) or PI (large gold, mineralized ground) technology.

**Advantages**:

- Covers large areas quickly (1–2 hectares in 4–6 hours)
- Finds nuggets before they're eroded/lost
- Works on dry ground (creeks, flats, slopes)
- Provides immediate feedback (beep = target)
- Data-driven (gridding ensures systematic coverage)

**Disadvantages**:

- Expensive (\$700–\$10,000 for quality detector)
- Steep learning curve (100+ hours to proficiency)
- Requires ground balance adjustment for soil mineralization
- False positives from "hot rocks" = frustration
- Battery runtime 8–15 hours; carry spares

**Ideal Conditions**:

- Old mine workings, crumbled mullock heaps
- Shallow ground (topsoil <1 m)
- Low-to-moderate mineralization (VLF) OR high mineralization (PI)
- Cooler months (less heat stress; better for deep work)

**Beginner Skill Path**:

- Week 1: Detector training (factory settings, ground balance, sensitivity tuning)
- Weeks 2–4: 20–30 hours practice in "clean" (non-productive) ground; learn signal recognition
- Weeks 5–12: 100+ hours in target areas; first small nuggets expected
- Month 4+: Confident in detector interpretation; troubleshooting basic issues; targeting strategies


#### **3. Sluicing (High-Volume Recovery)**

**What it is**: Water-driven concentration using gravity riffles; sediment flows through; gold settles in riffles.

**Advantages**:

- Processes large volumes (buckets to yards of material per day)
- Efficient if you've found good ground (high concentration zone)
- Team-friendly (some work, some pan concentrates)
- Relatively cheap equipment (\$100–\$500)

**Disadvantages**:

- Requires reliable water source (creek, pump)
- High physical labor
- Setup/breakdown time
- Can only work in-season (water flow)
- Needs multiple team members for efficiency

**Ideal Conditions**:

- Creeks with year-round or seasonal flow
- Already-identified productive zones
- Group/family outings
- Summer months (water warm, comfortable)

**Beginner Skill Path**:

- Week 1: Learn sluice operation (water velocity, riffle design, concentrate panning)
- Weeks 2–3: Run sluice on known productive ground with mentor
- Month 2+: Independently operate, interpret concentrates, identify recovery rates


#### **4. Crevicing (Specialized; High Reward)**

**What it is**: Excavating cracks/joints in bedrock using screwdriver, chisel, brush; ancient water-flow concentrated gold in these cracks.

**Advantages**:

- High success rate in right conditions (gold-rich cracks; often 1–5g per crevice)
- Low cost (\$10–\$50 for tools)
- Doesn't require flowing water
- Teaches bedrock gold behavior

**Disadvantages**:

- Very localized (only works where bedrock exposed)
- Requires rocky creek with suitable structure
- Physically demanding (hammering, scraping)
- Slow (one crack = 30 minutes to an hour)
- Seasonal (low water needed to access bedrock)

**Ideal Conditions**:

- Rocky creeks with exposed bedrock
- Historical gold-bearing areas
- Low-water seasons (winter, dry summer)
- Sites showing iron oxidation/weathering (indicates gold presence)

**Beginner Skill Path**:

- Day 1: Learn crevice identification and safe tool use
- Weeks 1–2: 3–4 weekend outings; practice safe excavation; identify productive cracks
- Month 2+: Can locate potential cracks, excavate safely, retrieve gold


#### **5. Tailings/Mullock Picking (Lowest Barrier to Entry)**

**What it is**: Hand-sorting piled spoil from old mines; picking out visible gold-bearing material or processing it separately.

**Advantages**:

- Zero permit issues (old, abandoned heaps on public land; check local rules)
- No equipment needed
- Gold often visible or easily concentrated
- Good for families, low fitness
- Teaches gold recognition

**Disadvantages**:

- Gold is "low-hanging fruit" (best pieces already taken historically)
- Physical sorting tedious
- Asbestos risk in some old tailings (do NOT disturb if present)
- Quality of tailings varies
- Limited volume of recoverable gold

**Ideal Conditions**:

- Historic goldfield towns (Bendigo VIC, Kalgoorlie WA, etc.)
- Heaps on public land or private land with permission
- Recent heavy rain (loosens material; makes sorting easier)

**Beginner Skill Path**:

- Day 1: Identify suitable heaps; learn safe sorting technique; asbestos awareness
- Week 1: Sort/pan 5–10 buckets; recovery rate establishes whether heap is worth more work
- Month 1+: Can rapidly assess heap quality; decide on efficiency

## **GOLD GEOLOGY FOR PRACTICAL PROSPECTORS — MODULE 6**

### **Alluvial Systems \& Where Gold Settles**

#### **How Gold Moves: Source to Deposit**

**Stage 1: Erosion**

- Weathering and tectonic uplift expose primary gold in quartz veins or mineralized host rock
- Water and gravity mobilize gold particles (fine dust to coarse flakes/nuggets)
- Transport distance: 1–100+ km depending on river strength and gold particle size

**Stage 2: Fluvial Transport**

- Rivers and creeks carry sediment (and gold) downstream
- Larger particles (nuggets, coarse flakes) settle in low-energy zones (inside bends, behind obstacles)
- Smaller particles (flour gold) travel farther; some enter ocean

**Stage 3: Settling (Concentration)**

- **Inside creek bends**: Current velocity drops; heavy gold sinks; light material washes away
- **Behind large boulders**: Flow shadow; turbulence = settling zone
- **Bedrock potholes/cracks**: Water spins; gold accumulates in depression
- **Base of waterfalls/rapids**: High-energy zone; gold dropped as velocity crashes
- **Riffles (natural or artificial sluice)**: Water accelerates, lifts light material; gold trapped

**Stage 4: Burial (Deep Leads)**

- River channel migrates; upstream catchment erosion buries old channel under sediment/volcanic rock
- Gold-bearing gravels encased in clay, sand, lateral deposit
- Can remain undisturbed for millions of years; Cretaceous/Tertiary age leads in Australia still rich with gold


### **Alluvial Deposit Types**

| Type | Formation | Characteristics | How to Target |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **Stream Channel** | Active/recent river migration | Coarse gold; nuggets + flakes; concentrated in riffles/bends | Sample actively flowing creeks; focus on inside bends, bedrock |
| **Bench Deposits** | Ancient river (now elevated due to erosion/tectonics) | Gold in old gravels; 10–100 m above current creek | Look for elevated flat areas with gravels; detect flanks of old terraces |
| **Deep Lead** | Buried ancient river under volcanic/sedimentary cover | Very rich; 10–50 m depth; concentrated in narrow paleochannel | Use geological maps, magnetic surveys, historical records to locate paleochannel; deep digging/drilling needed |
| **Eluvial** | Gold weathered from primary on hillside; moved downslope by gravity | Coarse, angular gold; minimal transport distance | Prospect slopes near quartz outcrops; gold-bearing rock float indicates upslope source |
| **Colluvial** | Gold transported short distance in talus/alluvial fan | Mix of sizes; less sorted than stream deposits | Common in foothills; sample fans near mineralized ridges |

### **Gold-Bearing Rock Indicators**

#### **Primary Gold Indicators** (where gold ore originally formed):

- **Quartz veins** (white, hard, resistant to erosion)
- **Pyrite-bearing rocks** (iron sulfide; metallic luster; often associated with gold)
- **Iron-stained zones** (rust-colored; indicates oxidation = past sulfide minerals = possible gold)
- **Shear zones** (folded, fractured rock; common locus for hydrothermal gold)
- **Greenstone belts** (metamorphosed volcanic rock; hosts major Australian gold deposits, especially Archean age)


#### **Secondary (Weathered) Indicators**:

- **Limonite/Gossan** (iron oxide; yellow-brown staining; indicates buried ore)
- **Black sand** (magnetite, ilmenite; heavy minerals; often co-concentrates with gold)
- **Laterite caps** (hardened iron-rich soil; can seal paleochannels; dig beneath)


### **Practical Geology: Reading a Geological Map**

**What to look for**:

1. **Fault lines** (bold lines on map): Tectonic breaks; gold often mineralized along faults
2. **Lithological boundaries** (colored regions): Transition zones between rock types; mineralization often at boundaries
3. **Historical mine symbols** (small cross/circle): Mark past production; nearby unworked ground likely productive
4. **Age symbols**: Archean/Proterozoic = higher gold potential; Cenozoic = typically alluvial only (no primary)
5. **Greenstone or metasedimentary designations**: Common gold hosts

**How to use it in field**:

- Identify your target creek on map
- Note upstream rock types; if you see quartz or greenstone upslope = gold likely
- Follow fault lines; prospect ground parallel to faults

## **TECHNIQUE PLAYBOOKS: STEP-BY-STEP FIELD ROUTINES — MODULE 8**

### **Playbook 1: Gold Panning (Core Technique)**

#### **Setup**

- Location: Creek or water source with sediment to pan
- Equipment: 14–16" pan, classifier, snuffer bottle, water
- Time: 5 minutes per pan (beginner); 3 minutes (experienced)


#### **Detailed Steps**

**Step 1: Classify \& Prepare Paydirt**

- Fill classifier with sediment/gravel from promising location (under water)
- Shake gently; larger rocks remain on top, fines fall through
- Transfer fines to your gold pan
- Load pan ~80% full (leaving room for water and movement)

**Step 2: Submerge \& Stratify**

- Submerge pan fully in water
- Shake the pan side-to-side in circular motions for 30 seconds
- **Why**: Gold is heaviest; settles to bottom. Light clay/silt rises to top
- You should hear/feel the sediment moving freely

**Step 3: Wash Off Lights (First Pass)**

- Tilt pan slightly forward (toward you)
- Insert pan 2 inches into water
- Lift straight up and down 5 times, fast (pulsing motion)
- **Effect**: Light material washes off the upper slope; gold stays at bottom
- Repeat: tip forward, pulse-lift 5 times, restratify
- **Continue**: Repeat until you have ~1/4 of original material

**Step 4: Aggressive Wash (Second Pass)**

- Now tilt more aggressively (45 degrees)
- Swirl the water in the pan; let heavy materials slide toward the tilted end
- Slowly rinse away light material over the edge (gentle pour, not violent)
- **Watch**: Black sand and gold should concentrate at the leading edge
- Restratify every few swirls; continue until mostly black sand + visible gold

**Step 5: Fine Concentration (Third Pass)**

- Remaining material is black sand + gold (heavy + heavy)
- Swirl gently; add small amount of water
- Let current separate black sand from gold (gold denser; sinks to center)
- Tilt pan slightly; gold should form small trail
- Continue fine tilting until only handful of black sand + gold visible

**Step 6: Final Recovery (Snuffer Bottle)**

- Most pans now have mixture of fine gold + black sand
- Use snuffer bottle (gentle suction) to pick up gold pieces
- **Technique**: Position snuffer mouth just above target; suck gently; release into vial
- Continue until nearly all gold removed (tiny flour gold may remain in black sand)
- **Optional**: Further pan the black sand concentrate to recover fine gold


#### **Common Mistakes \& Fixes**

| Mistake | Cause | Fix |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Gold disappears partway through | Too aggressive washing; gold washed away | Slow down; smaller pulses; watch gold location |
| Black sand and gold won't separate | Insufficient water movement | Swirl more; tilt at 45 degrees; ensure water flow |
| Takes 20+ minutes per pan | Rushing; not stratifying between washes | Stratify every 3 swirls; let gravity work |
| No gold found despite heavy black sand present | Black sand indicates right place, but no gold | Try different location 5–10 m away; test multiple spots |

#### **Efficiency Metrics**

- **Beginner**: 10–15 pans/hour; occasional gold recovery
- **Intermediate**: 20–30 pans/hour; consistent small finds
- **Advanced**: 40+ pans/hour; rapid assessment of ground


#### **Extension: Sluicing (Gravity Upgrade)**

Once you've identified a productive zone via panning:

- Deploy a hand sluice (1–2 person operation)
- Run buckets of sediment through sluice (water carries fines; gold settles in riffles)
- Recover concentrates from riffles; pan to final recovery
- **Efficiency gain**: 10–20 buckets/hour; much faster than panning

### **Playbook 2: Metal Detecting (Nugget Finding)**

#### **Pre-Field Checks**

- Batteries fully charged; spare set in vehicle
- Detector coil clean; no debris lodged
- Headphones working; volume comfortable
- Ground balance method reviewed (manual vs. automatic for your model)


#### **Field Deployment: Standard Gridding Pattern**

**Step 1: Mark Your Area**

- Choose a 20 m × 20 m patch (or adjust per your location size)
- Mark corners with sticks, rocks, or flagging tape
- Establish consistent lane width (~75% of your coil diameter; e.g., 45 cm for 60 cm coil)

**Step 2: Ground Balance (Soil Calibration)**

- Find a clean area (away from detected metal, old workings)
- Power on detector; select all-metal mode
- Hold coil 6–8 inches above ground
- Slowly lower and raise coil several times
- **Adjust ground balance knob until**: Threshold tone (background hum) remains consistent whether coil is raised or lowered
- **Why**: Compensates for soil mineralization (hot rocks, magnetite) that otherwise causes false signals

**Step 3: Set Sensitivity to Stable Maximum**

- In your ground-balanced area, slowly increase sensitivity setting until you hear constant chatter/noise
- Back off slightly until noise stops
- This is your **"threshold of stability"** for current conditions
- **Typical range**: 70–90% of maximum (depends on soil, weather, location)

**Step 4: Begin Gridding**

- Start at one corner; walk first lane maintaining coil 1–2 inches above ground
- **Sweep pattern**: Side-to-side (left-right-left) every step; overlap sweeps by ~25%
- **Speed**: Slow (1 step per 2 seconds); rushing misses targets
- **Coil angle**: Keep level to ground; tilting reduces depth
- **If ground uneven**: Follow contours to keep consistent height

**Step 5: Mark \& Investigate Signals**

- When you hear a strong, sharp signal: **STOP**
- Sweep coil over area 2–3 more times to confirm signal repeats in same location
- **Mark spot** with small flag or stone
- **Pinpoint target depth**: Lower coil slowly until signal strength peaks; that's approximate target depth
- **Decide**: Dig now or mark for later? (beginners often dig everything; experienced prospectors defer marginal signals)

**Step 6: Dig \& Confirm Target**

- Use trowel to remove 5 cm of soil near signal location
- Sweep coil over exposed soil; signal should be stronger
- Continue removing soil in 5 cm layers until signal source found
- **Most targets are junk** (nails, wire, can lids); gold nuggets are rarer
- **Keep all holes small** (pencil-sized start, expand only if necessary)

**Step 7: Backfill \& Continue**

- Return soil to hole; press down
- Move to next lane; resume gridding from where you left off


#### **Grid Completion \& Productivity Assessment**

- **After first grid pass**: You've covered area once
- **If productive** (multiple targets found): Run second grid perpendicular to first (cross-grid)
- **If unproductive**: Move to adjacent 20 × 20 m patch; repeat grid
- **Document**: Note coverage area (e.g., "0.4 hectare gridded"), \# targets found, \# gold, estimated time


#### **Common Mistakes \& Fixes**

| Mistake | Cause | Fix |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| False signals; constant noise | Sensitivity too high; ground not properly balanced | Rebalance; lower sensitivity; move away from old scrap metal |
| Miss faint signals | Sensitivity too low; not listening carefully | Increase sensitivity gradually; use quality headphones; focus |
| Inconsistent coil height | Uneven terrain; fatigue | Use contour sweep pattern; take frequent short breaks |
| Same area detected multiple times; inefficiency | No lane marking; haphazard path | Mark lanes with sticks; count passes; force yourself through full grid |
| Dig many holes but find no gold | Wrong ground selection; technique poor | Choose area with old workings, black sand, or known gold nearby; review ground balance |

#### **Advanced: Coil Swapping**

- Once you've gridded area with standard coil and found nothing: try **smaller coil** (more sensitive to fine gold)
- Regrid same area; often find small gold missed by large coil

### **Playbook 4: Crevicing (Rocky Creeks)**

#### **Site Selection**

- Rocky, shallow creek with exposed bedrock
- Recent water erosion visible (undercut banks, bedrock channels)
- Evidence of historical work nearby (old pits, tailings, iron staining)


#### **Process**

1. **Identify target cracks**: Look for bedrock joints, undercuts, or places where water swirled
2. **Excavate carefully**: Use small screwdriver or chisel; tap gently (don't hammer hard; destroys targets)
3. **Remove material**: Brush cracks clean; collect loose gravel
4. **Pan extracted material**: Test for gold presence
5. **Mark productive cracks**: Return with specialized tools to extract remaining gold

#### **Typical Recovery**

- Unproductive cracks: Nothing
- Average cracks: 0.1–1 gram
- Rich cracks: 1–10 grams (rare, but memorable)

## **TROUBLESHOOTING \& INTERMEDIATE COMPETENCY — MODULE 9**

### **Common Field Problems \& Diagnosis**

#### **Detector Issues**

| Symptom | Likely Cause | Solution |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |

| Symptom | Cause | Solution |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **Gold disappears midway** | Washing too aggressively; pan tilted wrong | Slow wash; smaller pulses; keep gold in view; tilt less severely |
| **Black sand won't separate from gold** | Insufficient water flow; not stratifying | Add more water; swirl harder; restratify every 2–3 swirls |
| **No gold found despite sampling many pans** | Wrong location; no source gold upstream; technique issue | Move 50–100 m uphill or downhill; test different creek stretch; practice panning at home first |

| Symptom | Assessment |
| :-- | :-- |
| **Productive ground first trip; unproductive second trip** | Different seasonal conditions (water level, accessibility); different microspot; normal variance |
| **All ground tested yields zero gold** | Wrong region geologically; skipped research phase; misidentified rock types |
| **Finding mostly flour gold, no nuggets** | Ground is low-grade alluvial; correct location but low concentration; try different spotwithin area |
| **Hot rocks dominate detections** | High-mineralization soil; need PI detector (not VLF); use ground balance more carefully |

1. Giving up after one unproductive day. Truth: Variance is normal; gold prospecting is probabilistic; retry.
2. Blaming equipment instead of technique. Truth: 80% of failures are technique; master basics first.
3. Not analyzing why you succeeded/failed. Truth: Logbook data reveals patterns; ignore it, repeat mistakes.
4. Moving on too quickly from an area. Truth: Spending 20 hours on one site beats sampling 10 sites for 2 hours each.
5. Ignoring seasonal changes. Truth: High water restricts access; same area different season = different results.
6. Not asking experienced prospectors for feedback. Truth: 5 minutes with an expert saves 10 hours of trial-and-error.
7. Changing methods too often. Truth: Stick with one method for 100 hours; master it before switching.
8. Not maintaining detector/equipment properly. Truth: Poorly maintained detector = false signals = wasted days.
9. Prospecting when fatigued or stressed. Truth: Poor decisions, safety risks; prospecting requires focus; do it fresh.
10. Setting unrealistic expectations. Truth: First month = learning, not big finds; Month 4–6 = consistent small finds likely.

### **Stop / Don't – Safety \& Legality Callouts**

🛑 **NEVER** continue prospecting if you feel unwell (heat illness, dehydration, injury); call it a day.
🛑 **NEVER** ignore signs of hot rocks or false signals; they consume time and reduce morale.

### **Practice Drill – Module 9**

*"Analyze Your Progress: 6-Month Review" (2 hours office work)*

1. Review all logbooks from past 6 months.
2. Create summary table: date, location, method, hours, gold found, assessment.
3. Calculate: total gold recovered, total hours, gold per hour, most productive location, best method.
4. Identify 3 patterns (e.g., "Spring creeks more productive than summer" or "Detecting near old workings yields 10x more gold").
5. Write 1-page summary: strengths, weaknesses, plan for next 6 months.
6. Share with club; gather feedback on your trajectory.

## **ETHICS, COMMUNITY \& LONG-TERM IMPROVEMENT — MODULE 10**

### **Prospecting Ethics \& Environmental Stewardship**

#### **Restoration Obligations**

After every field visit, leave the site **in better or equal condition**:

- **Fill all holes**: Use removed soil; compact lightly
- **Replace vegetation**: Return rocks, branches, leaf litter to original placement
- **Pack out trash**: Take more out than you bring in (including any old garbage you find)
- **Minimize disturbance footprint**: Work in concentrated zones; avoid unnecessary digging across wide areas
- **Respect waterways**: Do not remove woody debris >3 m, large cobbles, aquatic plants; avoid siltation


#### **Cultural \& Heritage Respect**

- **Know if site is on Indigenous land**: Check Native Title status; respect cultural protocols
- **Do NOT disturb rock art, burial grounds, or sacred sites**: Contact local Aboriginal land council if uncertain
- **Ask permission**: Even on "public" land, proximity to sacred sites requires consent
- **Document \& report**: If you discover previously unknown heritage sites, contact your state Aboriginal heritage authority


#### **Community Etiquette**

- **Share knowledge**: If you discover a productive area, consider inviting club members
- **Respect other prospectors' claims**: If someone is actively working a zone, don't set up next to them without asking
- **Report hazards**: If you find abandoned mine adits, hazardous old workings, or environmental issues, report to relevant authority
- **Support prospecting rights**: Join a club; attend meetings; support legislative advocacy protecting prospector access

### **Final Cautionary Note: Temper Expectations**

Gold prospecting is **rewarding and learnable, but not a shortcut to wealth**. Most hobbyists recover 5–20 grams per year in Australia, worth \$200–\$800 at current prices (~\$60–\$80/gram). The value is in:

- **Outdoor activity** (exercise, fresh air, exploration)
- **Skill mastery** (geology, fieldcraft, data analysis)
- **Community** (friendships, knowledge-sharing)
- **Discovery** (the thrill of finding your first nugget)

Expect to invest **\$1,000–\$3,000 initial equipment cost** and **100+ hours of learning** before you find meaningful amounts of gold. If your expectation is faster or bigger, reassess; this guide is for genuine interest, not get-rich schemes.
