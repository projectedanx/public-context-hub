# Asymmetric Entropy in Bidirectional LLM Evaluation: A Full Empirical Design

## Foundational Architecture of the Problem

The Jekyll \& Hyde framework (arXiv:2408.08631) operationalizes ensemble reasoning by running a **Persona Solver** and a **Neutral Solver** in parallel and passing both solutions to an LLM Evaluator for pairwise selection. The framework's known failure mode is that "LLM-based evaluators tend to be affected by the order of those potential solutions within the prompt when selecting the proper solution". The root cause, however, is far more structured than a simple surface-level ordering preference. It is a **neural-level entropy asymmetry**: the token-probability distributions generated by the evaluator are not symmetric under permutation of the two inputs, and this asymmetry is geometrically traceable to specific attention head subgroups. A 2025 study involving 15 LLM judges across MTBench and DevBench with over 150,000 evaluation instances confirmed that "position bias is not due to random chance and varies significantly across judges and tasks," with position bias being "strongly affected by the quality gap between solutions".[^1][^2]

***

## Formalizing Asymmetric Entropy Delta (AED)

The first task is to construct a mathematically rigorous measurement of how the evaluator's token-probability distribution changes under input reversal. Let $\mathcal{E}$ denote the LLM Evaluator, $S_J$ the Jekyll (persona) solution, and $S_H$ the Hyde (neutral) solution.

For a forward ordering prompt $\mathcal{P}^+ = (S_J, S_H)$ and reverse ordering prompt $\mathcal{P}^- = (S_H, S_J)$, the evaluator generates token sequences $\mathbf{t}^+ = (t_1^+, \ldots, t_T^+)$ and $\mathbf{t}^- = (t_1^-, \ldots, t_T^-)$ with corresponding per-token probability distributions $p^+(t | \mathbf{t}^+_{<i})$ and $p^-(t | \mathbf{t}^-_{<i})$.

**Definition 1 — Per-Token Asymmetric Entropy Delta (AED):**

$$
\text{AED}(i) = H\!\left(p^+(t | \mathbf{t}^+_{<i})\right) - H\!\left(p^-(t | \mathbf{t}^-_{<i})\right)
$$

where Shannon entropy is computed as $H(p) = -\sum_{v \in \mathcal{V}} p(v) \log p(v)$ [^3]. Tokens with $|\text{AED}(i)| > \tau$ (a calibrated threshold) are designated **Entropy-Divergent Tokens (EDTs)** — the exact positions where ordering rewires the evaluator's uncertainty geometry.

**Definition 2 — Global Asymmetric Entropy Score (GAES):**

$$
\text{GAES} = \frac{1}{T}\sum_{i=1}^{T} |\text{AED}(i)|
$$

A GAES near zero indicates robust, order-invariant evaluation. A high GAES indicates that the evaluator is structurally dependent on positional priors. Critically, token log-probability proxies for evaluator confidence "produce identical Spearman correlations with correctness ($\rho = +0.166$)" but "confidence ranges collapse due to normalization", which is why AED must be computed at the raw logit level, before any normalization artifacts compress the signal.[^4]

***

## Empirical Study Design

### Phase 1: Dataset Construction and Instrumentation

The study requires a controlled corpus of $N$ reasoning problems where ground truth is unambiguous. Recommended: 2,000 instances drawn from GSM8K (mathematical reasoning), ARC-Challenge (scientific reasoning), and a custom commonsense set, mirroring the Jekyll \& Hyde evaluation protocol.[^1]

For each problem, generate:

- $S_J$: A persona-prompted solution (role-playing solver)
- $S_H$: A neutral-prompted solution

Four conditions per problem must be recorded:

1. **Forward** evaluation: evaluator receives $(S_J, S_H)$
2. **Reverse** evaluation: evaluator receives $(S_H, S_J)$
3. **Ground-truth label**: which solution is objectively correct (verified independently)
4. **Full token logit streams** for both orderings (requires white-box access to the evaluator)

The instrumentation layer must log, for every generation step $i$, the full vocabulary probability vector $p_i \in \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{V}|}$ and all attention head activation matrices $A^{(l,h)}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ across layers $l$ and heads $h$. A 2026 study on entropy dynamics and LLM reasoning (arXiv:2604.06192) confirms that entropy trajectories — the step-by-step evolution of $H_k$ — carry predictive signal that scalar confidence proxies cannot capture [^5].

### Phase 2: Statistical Characterization of AED

The empirical study must test three null hypotheses:

- **H₀¹**: The distribution of GAES scores is symmetric around zero (no systematic directional bias)
- **H₀²**: GAES is uncorrelated with correctness outcome (bias does not predict evaluation failure)
- **H₀³**: GAES is constant across task type and quality gap size

Based on existing position bias research, H₀¹ and H₀³ should both be rejected. The quality gap effect (confirmed empirically at $p < 0.05$ by ) predicts that GAES will be significantly modulated by how similar the two competing solutions are. When $S_J \approx S_H$ in quality, the AED should spike — the evaluator is maximally uncertain at the semantic level and falls back on positional priors.[^6][^2]

The entropy trajectory analysis from arXiv:2603.18940 provides the key methodological precedent: "token log-probability confidence worsens in calibration with step depth (ECE: 0.186 → 0.312 from step 0 to step 7)". This means AED should be reported not as a single scalar but as a time-series, revealing at which token depth the positional divergence becomes non-recoverable.[^4]

## Isolating Attention-Head Mechanisms for Position Bias

### Head Entropy as a Bias Localization Tool

The most direct mechanistic tool available is **2-Rényi entropy** applied per attention head. For each head $(l,h)$ and input ordering $o \in \{+, -\}$, the Rényi attention entropy is:[^7]

$$
H_2^{(l,h,o)} = -\log \sum_{j=1}^{n} \left(A^{(l,h,o)}_{ij}\right)^2
$$

The **Bias Head Score (BHS)** for head $(l,h)$ is then:

$$
\text{BHS}(l,h) = \mathbb{E}_{i \sim \text{EDT}} \left| H_2^{(l,h,+)} - H_2^{(l,h,-)} \right|
$$

Heads with high BHS are the mechanistic substrate of position bias. This method builds directly on "Head Entropy" (arXiv:2602.13699), which "predicts answer correctness from attention entropy patterns" with "0.88 AUROC on average". Crucially, the 2-Rényi metric is "symmetric and does not depend on the order of inputs", meaning any BHS asymmetry detected is attributable purely to how the positional context alters the attention distribution — not to a mathematical artifact of the metric.[^7]

### Empirical Isolation Protocol

This three-stage protocol directly identifies and validates position-bias heads:

1. **Ranking**: Compute BHS for all $L \times H$ heads. Rank by descending BHS. The top-$k_{\text{head}}$ heads (typically 5–15% of the total) are designated **Positional Bias Heads (PBHs)**.[^8]
2. **Causal Intervention (Activation Patching)**: Run counterfactual experiments — patch the activations of each PBH from the forward ordering run into the reverse ordering run. If the evaluator's output distribution shifts toward the forward-ordering decision, that head causally encodes the positional prior. This leverages **Generative Causal Mediation** (arXiv:2602.16080), which uses "linear probes to locate where to steer a desired concept".[^9]
3. **Ablation Validation**: Mask the top-k PBHs (zero out their contribution to the residual stream) during evaluation. The expected result, confirmed by analogous work on fairness (arXiv:2510.10142), is a significant reduction in positional preference: "editing (masking) only those heads — the DiffHeads approach — cuts unfairness by a further 49.4%, 40.3% for DA and CoT while leaving accuracy on representative tasks unchanged".[^8]

A critical nuance: attention patterns show a **dissociation between attention and uncertainty** — "attention is consistently label-dominant... while decision uncertainty is highest under AI labels," indicating that "label effects in LLM-as-a-Judge manifest in both label-focused attention patterns and label-dependent shifts in uncertainty". This means the BHS metric must be computed jointly — attention spread alone does not capture the full picture; it must be triangulated with the token-level entropy signal.[^10]

## The Dynamic-k Protocol: Formal Design

### Core Motivation Against Static k

The Jekyll \& Hyde paper shows that "performance of the framework increases as the number of attempts increases" and outperforms single-perspective LLM "when the max attempt k is bigger than 1". However, a static ceiling (k=5) is epistemically unjustified: it simultaneously wastes compute on problems the evaluator resolves at k=2, and permits continued inference on problems where the evaluator entered an entropic attractor at k=1 — neither of which is addressable by a fixed limit. The MiCP framework (arXiv:2604.01413) establishes the principle that "some questions can be answered after one retrieval step, while others require multiple hops. Using the same budget everywhere wastes it on easy, early cases".[^11][^1]

### Log-Probability Variance as the Control Signal

At each attempt $k$, the evaluator produces a decision log-probability $\ell_k = \log p_{\mathcal{E}}(\text{choose } S_J | \mathcal{P}^+_k)$. Define the rolling variance over attempts $1, \ldots, k$:

$$
\sigma^2_k = \frac{1}{k-1} \sum_{j=1}^{k} \left(\ell_j - \bar{\ell}_k\right)^2
$$

and its second derivative (acceleration):

$$
\Delta^2\sigma^2_k = \sigma^2_k - 2\sigma^2_{k-1} + \sigma^2_{k-2}
$$

The three regimes that govern Dynamic-k termination are:


| Regime | Signal Condition | Interpretation | Action |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **Convergent** | $\sigma^2_k < \epsilon_{\text{low}}$ | Evaluator locked on a stable, order-invariant decision | **Halt: return answer** |
| **Divergent** | $\sigma^2_k > \epsilon_{\text{high}}$ | Evaluator oscillating with no attractor | **Continue to next attempt** |
| **Attractor-Locked (Biased)** | $\Delta^2\sigma^2_k < 0$ and $\sigma^2_k > \epsilon_{\text{high}}$ | Variance compressing onto a non-zero bias value | **Halt: return 'Can't answer'** |

The attractor-locked condition is the key innovation. It detects that variance is *decreasing while still high* — the evaluator is converging, but onto a biased position-dependent fixed point rather than a semantically grounded one. This mirrors the entropy-slope monitoring used in Sequential-EDFL (arXiv:2510.06478): "detect drift through entropy-slope monitoring and trigger resets with fresh error budgets".[^12]

### Full Algorithm: Dynamic-k Protocol

```
Algorithm: Dynamic-k Evaluator with Positional Bias Abort

Input:  S_J, S_H, ε_low, ε_high, k_max_absolute (safety ceiling)
Output: Selected solution OR "Can't answer"

1.  Initialize: k=1, log_probs = []
2.  LOOP while k ≤ k_max_absolute:
    a.  Run forward eval: ℓ_k^+ = log p_E(choose S_J | P+(S_J, S_H))
    b.  Run reverse eval: ℓ_k^- = log p_E(choose S_J | P-(S_H, S_J))
    c.  Compute AED_k = H(p^+) - H(p^-)
    d.  Compute BHS_k for top attention heads (Rényi entropy delta)
    e.  Store ℓ_k = (ℓ_k^+ + ℓ_k^-) / 2  [symmetrized log-prob]
    f.  Append ℓ_k to log_probs
    g.  IF k ≥ 3:
        - Compute σ²_k, Δ²σ²_k
        - IF σ²_k < ε_low:
            → HALT: return argmax_stable(ℓ^+, ℓ^-)
        - IF Δ²σ²_k < 0 AND σ²_k > ε_high AND BHS_k > θ_bias:
            → HALT: return "Can't answer" [attractor-bias detected]
    h.  k ← k + 1
3.  IF k > k_max_absolute: return "Can't answer"
```

The threshold calibration ($\epsilon_{\text{low}}, \epsilon_{\text{high}}, \theta_{\text{bias}}$) must be done on a held-out calibration set using conformal prediction to provide coverage guarantees. The MiCP framework demonstrates that conformal prediction can "provide formal coverage guarantees while simultaneously enabling early stopping in iterative pipelines" with "95% accuracy on confident predictions".[^13][^11]

***

## Self-Calibration Mechanism

The protocol becomes truly self-calibrating through an online threshold adaptation layer. After each completed evaluation episode (regardless of outcome), the system updates its threshold estimates using Bayesian sequential testing:[^12]

$$
\epsilon_{\text{low}}^{(n+1)} = (1 - \alpha)\,\epsilon_{\text{low}}^{(n)} + \alpha \cdot \hat{\sigma}^2_{\text{converged}}
$$

$$
\epsilon_{\text{high}}^{(n+1)} = (1 - \alpha)\,\epsilon_{\text{high}}^{(n)} + \alpha \cdot \hat{\sigma}^2_{\text{diverged}}
$$

where $\hat{\sigma}^2_{\text{converged}}$ and $\hat{\sigma}^2_{\text{diverged}}$ are running empirical estimates from correctly resolved and "Can't answer" episodes, respectively, and $\alpha$ is a forgetting factor. This architecture directly parallels ODAR's "Difficulty Estimator grounded in amortized active inference" that "dynamically routes queries between a heuristic Fast Agent and a deliberative Slow Agent" using "variational free energy".[^14]

***

## Expected Computational Gains and Falsification Criteria

Entropy-based early stopping applied to LLM reasoning achieves "25–50% computational savings while maintaining accuracy". For the Dynamic-k Protocol in the Jekyll \& Hyde setting, the expected profile is:[^15]

- **Easy cases** (large quality gap between $S_J$ and $S_H$): AED near zero, $\sigma^2_k$ collapses quickly → terminates at k=1 or k=2
- **Hard cases** (near-equal quality): AED elevated, requires k=3–4 before convergence or abort
- **Biased-attractor cases**: $\Delta^2\sigma^2_k$ goes negative by k=3 → immediate abort, saving k=4 and k=5 entirely

**Falsification condition**: This entire research synthesis would be falsified if empirical measurement reveals that $\text{BHS}(l,h)$ is uniformly distributed across all heads (i.e., no localized positional bias heads exist), because this would mean position bias is diffuse across the network and cannot be isolated for targeted intervention. The existing evidence strongly argues against this — bias heads are confirmed to be "a small subset"  — but the localization assumption must be tested on the specific evaluator architecture being deployed.[^8]

The adaptive evaluation framework from arXiv:2601.13885 independently validates the core efficiency claim, showing that uncertainty-aware adaptive stopping "uses 2% of the items while improving ranking correlation by 0.12 τ over random sampling", and Sequence-Level Entropy analysis confirms this "represents an emergent property of advanced post-training optimization present in modern reasoning models"  — meaning the Dynamic-k Protocol's entropy signals will be most reliable on RLHF-tuned evaluator models rather than base-pretrained ones, a critical practical constraint for deployment.[^13][^15]
