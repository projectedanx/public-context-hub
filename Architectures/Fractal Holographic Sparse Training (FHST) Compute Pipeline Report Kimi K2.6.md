### 2.1 HRR Circular Convolution: The O(d log d) Basis

Holographic Reduced Representations (HRR) bind two d-dimensional vectors **x** and **y** via circular convolution:

$$
(x \circledast y)_k = \sum_{j=0}^{d-1} x_j \cdot y_{(k-j) \mod d}
$$

This operation, performed naively, is O(d²). The FHST pipeline's core computational claim exploits the Fourier convolution theorem:[^1][^2]

$$
\mathcal{F}(x \circledast y) = \mathcal{F}(x) \odot \mathcal{F}(y)
$$

Where $\mathcal{F}$ is the Fast Fourier Transform and $\odot$ is element-wise multiplication. This converts the O(d²) binding operation to:
            - FFT: **O(d log d)**
            - Element-wise multiply: **O(d)**
            - Inverse FFT: **O(d log d)**
            - **Total: O(d log d)** — confirmed by HyperSpace framework benchmarks[^2]

The critical property preserved by this transform: **dimensionality is fixed**. Unlike concatenation-based fusion (which expands d proportionally with the number of bound attributes), HRR circular convolution keeps the output vector in the same d-dimensional space. This is the mathematical core of the FHST dimensionality claim: you can bind arbitrarily many semantic relationships into a fixed-width vector without parameter count inflation.[^1]

The FHRR variant (Fourier Holographic Reduced Representations) operates *natively* in the complex frequency domain via element-wise complex multiplication at **O(d)**, eliminating FFT transform overhead entirely. For hardware capable of complex-valued tensor operations (e.g., H100/B200 with complex CUDA kernels), FHRR offers a strict improvement over HRR as the base kernel.[^2]

### 2.2 The Superposition Bound: Hard Interference Constraint

HRR achieves superposition — multiple bound pairs can coexist in one vector — but at a cost. The noise-to-signal ratio during unbinding follows:

$$
\text{SNR} \approx \frac{1}{\sqrt{k/d}}
$$

where **k** is the number of superimposed bindings and **d** is the vector dimension. This is the **Superposition Interference Bound** (SIB). It is not a soft engineering trade-off — it is an algebraic consequence of the fact that bundled vectors generate cross-talk terms during approximate inverse convolution. The SIB sets a hard ceiling on the semantic density the micro-kernel can sustain before retrieval fidelity collapses. For FHST's extrusion claim to hold, the micro-kernel must be trained with k << d — specifically, the LatentSparsityGuard(k=10) decorator in the FHST protocol is not arbitrary; it is a direct enforcement of this bound. With k=10 and d=4096 (a typical embedding dimension), the SNR is approximately 20.2, which is operationally stable. Increasing k beyond ~sqrt(d) ≈ 64 triggers SIB-tachycardia.[^3]

The S5 model (Simplified Structured State Space) achieves linear-time sequence processing via a diagonal state-space parameterization:[^4]

$$
\dot{x}(t) = Ax(t) + Bu(t), \quad y(t) = Cx(t) + Du(t)
$$

with **A** constrained to diagonal structure. This enables both recurrent and convolutional inference modes, achieving O(L) per step or O(L log L) globally via FFT-based convolution. The "Modal" qualifier in FHST's S5-Modal Attention applies the **S5 Kripke frame** — specifically, the S5 modal logic axioms: reflexivity (T: □φ → φ), symmetry (B: φ → □◇φ), and transitivity (4: □φ → □□φ).[^5][^6][^7][^4]

In attention terms, enforcing S5-Kripke reflexivity means every token attends to itself with weight ≥ 1.0 (the accessibility relation is reflexive). Symmetry means if token i attends to token j, j attends to i. Transitivity means attention chains propagate without decay across the accessibility graph. These constraints, when applied to the attention matrix, prevent non-separable conjunctions (PNS5) — where two propositions appear jointly true but neither is individually inferable — from being reduced to simple additive operations.
