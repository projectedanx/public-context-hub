The Seminar of Echoes: Archaeoacoustics and Cultural Transmission

This research establishes ancient megalithic tombs and ritual chambers as physical metaphors for the transmission of ideas. These spaces are not passive containers but active media that filter, amplify, and reframe utterances, mirroring the ways cultural contexts transform meaning.

Core Concept

The Seminar of Echoes

A model suggesting that the survival of an idea depends less on the fidelity of its original transmission and more on its capacity to resonate within new interpretive spaces. Meaning is treated as an emergent property of resonance between a signal, a medium, and a receiver.

Core Analytical Frameworks

The research bridges the empirical data of sound physics with the theoretical frameworks of the humanities.

Megalith Frequency (110-112 Hz)

A consistent resonant frequency identified across diverse ancient sites. This frequency induces a neurological shift, deactivating language centers and shifting brain activity to right-hemisphere dominance, which favors emotional and intuitive processing over literal comprehension.

Acoustic Leveling and Sharpening

Physical analogues to the psychological mechanisms of rumor. The architecture 'levels' sound by filtering high-frequency consonants (semantic detail) and 'sharpens' it by amplifying specific resonant frequencies (emotional highlights).

The Fusion of Horizons

A hermeneutic principle where meaning is created by the meeting of the listener's personal context and the 'horizon' of the space. In the tomb, the massive stone and ancient function constitute an unassailable context that shapes every received sound.
