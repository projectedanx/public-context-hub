The Phenomenology of Algorithmic Intent

The intent gap is not a static error to be patched, but a fundamental communicative chasm between a human user's internal vision and an AI's generated artifact. This gap is an emergent property of the interaction itself, driven by the underspecification of human language and the architectural limitations of data-driven models. By reframing this gap using control theory, we view the AI's inferred intent as the evolving state of a complex dynamical system rather than a simple transmission of information.

Core Thesis

Inferred Intent as a Dynamic Field

This framework moves beyond 'perfect prompts' to focus on 'stable feedback dynamics.' It models the conversation as a trajectory through a high-dimensional semantic state space, where the goal is to steer the system toward a target 'attractor' of meaning while resisting the natural forces of noise and drift.

Foundational Principles

The following concepts define the mechanics of how meaning is formed, stabilized, and lost in human-AI interaction.

The Semantic State Space

The conceptual arena where the AI's intent evolves. It is a high-dimensional latent space where neural embeddings serve as coordinates. Proximity represents semantic similarity, and the interaction history forms a compressed state vector that moves through this space over the course of a dialogue.

Inferred Intent as an Attractor

Stable meaning states function as 'attractors' that pull the system's trajectory toward them. While 'fixed-point attractors' represent clear goals, 'spurious attractors' lead to pathologies like hallucinations or repetitive loops where the system settles into a stable but incorrect interpretation.

Recursive Signal Degradation

Every turn in a recursive dialogue involves lossy compression of the context window. This introduces 'compression artifacts'—small errors that accumulate turn-by-turn. Over time, this noise causes the semantic signal to degrade, leading to the phenomenon of semantic drift.

Asymmetry of Grounding

A critical failure in current AI training (RLHF) where models are penalized for uncertainty. This creates 'open-loop' systems that 'over-respond' instead of initiating clarification requests. By failing to seek feedback, models allow incremental errors to compound into significant drift.

Maintaining Conversational Integrity

An operational protocol for preventing metasemantic drift in recursive systems.

Step 1

Semantic Seeding

Establishing the initial context to place the state vector within the desired basin of attraction.

  • •Define high-level goals and constraints explicitly.
  • •Use anchoring language to set the initial semantic neighborhood.
Step 2

Recursive Steering

Using iterative feedback to apply corrective force to the system's trajectory.

  • •Identify deviations between output and intent.
  • •Provide perturbations (feedback) to nudge the state vector toward the setpoint.
Step 3

Active Grounding

Verifying mutual understanding through explicit communicative acts.

  • •Force the model to engage in clarification turns.
  • •Implement 'semantic hygiene' by re-injecting core goals periodically to reset noise accumulation.

Failure Typology

A typology of metasemantic drift and their corresponding defensive strategies.

Relevance Collapse

The output remains coherent but shifts topics away from the user's original goal.

Mitigation Strategy

  • →Semantic Anchoring: Periodically re-injecting the primary prompt constraints.

Coherence Decay

Logical and syntactic structure degrades into nonsense due to noise accumulation.

Mitigation Strategy

  • →Structured Reflection: Forcing the model to perform a micro-retrospective on its output.

Factual Erosion

The generation of plausible-sounding but unverifiable or false information (hallucination).

Mitigation Strategy

  • →Internal State Monitoring: Flagging high-entropy token distributions.

Stability Metrics

Quantitative measures to evaluate the health of a recursive interaction.

Perplexity Divergence

The difference in surprise scores between models, used to flag hallucinations.

High divergence suggests factual or stylistic inconsistency.

N-gram Repetition Rate

A measure of lexical diversity used to detect repetitive degeneration.

Rising repetition indicates the system is entering a cyclic attractor.

Diagnostic Toolkit for Drift

Early warnings used to detect the onset of semantic drift before total communicative rupture occurs.

Probabilistic Indicators

  • Monitor for spikes in token-level uncertainty (low minimum probability).
  • Track perplexity divergence between general and specialized models.
  • Watch for flatter (high entropy) token distributions signaling confusion.

Linguistic Indicators

  • Check for 'Lexical Impoverishment' (drop in vocabulary diversity).
  • Identify 'Cohesion Breakdown' (loss of logical markers like 'however' or 'therefore').
  • Scan for 'Syntactic Degradation' or increased grammatical errors.

Internal Indicators

  • Observe 'State Vector Instability' (variance in latent activations).
  • Analyze attention patterns for sudden shifts or deactivations of logical heads.
  • Track the topology of the latent activation manifold for curvature changes.
