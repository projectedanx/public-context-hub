A Cross-Disciplinary Blueprint for Agentic AI

This blueprint presents a cross-disciplinary approach to AI agent design, utilizing formalisms from economics and linguistics to solve challenges in deliberation, coordination, and systemic risk. By treating 'thought' as a scarce resource and 'memory' as a competitive marketplace, it provides a rigorous foundation for the next generation of autonomous systems.

Core Thesis

Computational Rationality

The framework posits that mature models of marginal utility, interlingual translation, and modern portfolio theory offer the most effective vocabulary for regulating complex agentic workflows and ensuring systemic resilience.

  • Efficiency
  • Resilience
  • Interoperability

Theoretical Foundations

The blueprint rests on these three pillars of economic and linguistic theory.

Marginal Utility of Thought

A formal calculation where an agent continues deliberating only if the expected benefit (solution quality) outweighs the computational cost (time, energy, opportunity cost).

Neural Interlingua

A dynamically learned, shared vector space that replaces rigid, hand-crafted ontologies for communication between heterogeneous agents.

Agent Beta (β)

A metric inspired by the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) to quantify an agent's contribution to systemic risk in a multi-agent environment.

Operational Blueprints

These workflows translate theoretical principles into executable agent architectures.

Step 1

Optimal Stopping Reasoning

Regulating cognitive depth through resource-aware stopping rules.

  • •Look Phase: Establish a quality baseline and calibrate expectations.
  • •Leap Phase: Iteratively refine solutions until marginal utility drops below cost.
Step 2

Mnemonic Marketplace

Managing long-term memory through market-based competition.

  • •Retrieval Market: Memory traces bid for space in the context window based on relevance.
  • •Retention Market: Memories pay 'rent' using accumulated utility capital or face eviction.
Step 3

Bisociative Generative-Evaluator

Generating creative breakthroughs by fusing incompatible frames of reference.

  • •Deconstruction: Breaking goals into semantic primitives.
  • •Analogy Mapping: Identifying distant but structurally similar domains.
  • •Fusion: Blending concepts to generate novel 'compound ideas'.

Systemic Resilience

Managing emergent failure modes in interconnected autonomous systems.

Monoculture Collapse

Simultaneous failure of all agents due to shared base models or training data.

Mitigation Strategy

  • →Deliberate model heterogeneity
  • →Portfolio diversification policies

Mnemonic Hyperinflation

Newly acquired but useless information outbids core knowledge traces.

Mitigation Strategy

  • →Utility subsidies for core identity knowledge
  • →Novelty taxes on recent data

Cascading Hallucination

A single agent's error propagates through the interlingua to the entire team.

Mitigation Strategy

  • →Symbolic verification layers
  • →Cross-domain 'look' phases
