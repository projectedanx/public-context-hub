Cognitive Scaffolding in Deep Time

This research program moves beyond the 'meaning' of Palaeolithic art to investigate the 'how' of its creation. By focusing on the cognitive operations necessary for symbolic behavior, it identifies double-scope blending as the pivotal mechanism of the Upper Palaeolithic creative explosion.

The Cognitive Turn

From Semiotics to Mental Architecture

Traditional archaeology struggles to interpret the specific beliefs of deep time. This framework shifts the focus to material traces of cognitive processes, treating artifacts like the Löwenmensch as fossils of specific mental operations like double-scope blending.

Core Cognitive Mechanisms

The following concepts form the theoretical toolkit for deconstructing the Palaeolithic mind.

Double-Scope Blending

A high-level cognitive operation where two clashing mental frames (e.g., Human vs. Lion) are integrated into a novel blend with its own emergent structure and organizing logic.

Embodied Cognition

The theory that abstract concepts are grounded in sensorimotor experiences and neural circuits linked to bodily interactions with the physical environment.

Cognitive Fluidity

The evolutionary integration of domain-specific intelligences (social, technical, natural history), allowing for cross-domain thinking and symbolic innovation.

Mythographic Compression

The ability of a single symbolic image, such as 'The Sorcerer,' to condense complex narratives and cosmological relationships into a potent, visually coherent unit.

Analytical Methodology

The research follows a four-stage process to move from qualitative description to formal cognitive deconstruction.

Step 1

Artifact Survey

Systematic documentation of composite or therianthropic imagery within the published corpus of Upper Palaeolithic European art.

Step 2

Cognitive Mapping

Identifying input spaces, mapping projected elements to the blended space, and determining the clashing organizing frames.

Step 3

Diachronic Comparison

Evaluating the complexity and frequency of conceptual blends across cultural periods from the Aurignacian to the Magdalenian.

Step 4

Threshold Testing

Comparing Neandertal (Châtelperronian) symbolic toolkits against Sapiens (Aurignacian) record to identify cognitive discontinuities.

Critical Resilience

Identifying and mitigating the epistemological risks inherent in cognitive archaeology.

Interpretive Subjectivity

The risk of cognitive 'just-so stories' where mappings are forced to fit pre-existing narratives.

Mitigation Strategy

  • →Use strict CIT structural rules
  • →Focus on minimum necessary competence

WEIRD Bias

Applying modern Western cognitive models to ancient hominins with different lived experiences.

Mitigation Strategy

  • →Ground analysis in embodied primary metaphors
  • →Acknowledge the evolutionary limits of convergence

Archaeological Sample Bias

Treating the material record as the full extent of past cognitive ability.

Mitigation Strategy

  • →Define the record as a 'cognitive floor' (minimum competence)
  • →Account for differential preservation rates

Validation Metrics

Success is measured by the framework's ability to provide a more constrained and testable account of symbolic behavior than traditional interpretive models.

Structural Replicability

The degree to which different researchers consistently identify the same input frames and mappings for a given artifact.

Inter-rater reliability in CIT mapping Transparency of mapping protocols

Explanatory Power

The ability to account for the 'impossible' nature of composite art without relying on unfalsifiable ethnographic analogies.

Reduction in speculative 'belief system' narratives Cognitive floor identification

Deconstruction Checklist

A rigorous guide for applying Conceptual Integration Theory to specific artifacts.

Input Identification

  • Identify constituent physical domains (e.g., HUMAN, STAG).
  • Define the organizing frame for each input.

Mapping Analysis

  • Track which features are projected into the blend.
  • Identify 'clashing' relations (agency, body plan, cause-effect).

Emergent Meaning

  • Articulate the new inferences generated in the blend.
  • Describe the 'impossible' reality constructed by the artifact.
