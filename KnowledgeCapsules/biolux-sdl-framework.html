The Biolux-SDL Framework: A Unified Architecture for Multimodal Therapeutics

Biolux-SDL (Sensory Domain-Specific Language) represents a paradigm shift in digital health, moving from isolated interventions to a unified, context-aware environment. It is built as an Ambient Intelligence (AmI) system that proactively supports well-being by modulating the physical environment based on the body's interaction with its surroundings.

Core Mission

Synchronized Sensory Well-being

To create a seamless interface between human physiology and environmental actuators, using symbolic control to guide users toward states of calm, focus, or recovery through non-pharmacological means.

Architectural Foundations

The framework rests on three pillars that manage intent, timing, and biological feedback.

Master Context Intent Layer (MCIL)

The symbolic control layer that abstracts device complexity. It allows administrators to issue high-level therapeutic 'Intents'—such as 'promote_calm'—which are then translated into specific sensory actions across the network via an Intent-DSL.

Sync.Clock

A hybrid synchronization engine combining Precision Time Protocol (PTP) and MIDI-inspired logic. It ensures that multimodal stimuli (pulses, flickers, beats) are delivered with sub-microsecond accuracy to facilitate sensory entrainment.

State.Feedback Channel

A closed-loop personalization engine that utilizes real-time biometric data (HRV, GSR, EEG) to continuously refine and adapt the sensory environment to the user's specific physiological profile.

Behavior Tree Execution

Instead of rigid scripts, Biolux-SDL uses modular Behavior Trees to manage scene transitions. This allows the system to remain reactive, switching strategies dynamically if a user's stress levels rise during a session.

Resilience & Risk Mitigation

Identifying and mitigating system and physiological risks is critical for long-term therapeutic safety.

Olfactory Habituation

The rapid loss of scent perception due to constant exposure.

Mitigation Strategy

  • →Implementation of mandatory Inter-Stimulus Intervals (ISI)
  • →Use of pulsed diffusion rather than continuous release

Alert Fatigue

Sensory overload from non-critical or repetitive system notifications.

Mitigation Strategy

  • →Use of color-coded, dynamic visual alarms
  • →Qualitative rather than quantitative data presentation

Biometric Data Breach

Compromise of permanent, immutable physiological identifiers.

Mitigation Strategy

  • →Edge-based local processing of raw data
  • →Granular encryption and anonymization protocols
