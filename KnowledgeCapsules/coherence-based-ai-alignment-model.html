A Coherence-Based AI Alignment Framework: Dynamic Intent & Adaptive Safeguards

The field of Artificial Intelligence (AI) alignment is fundamentally concerned with ensuring that increasingly autonomous systems act in accordance with human goals. This novel framework challenges the prevailing philosophy of generalized, top-down control, moving instead toward a paradigm of personalized, bottom-up coherence. It reconceptualizes alignment not as a static property of a model, but as a dynamic, emergent process co-created in the context of each individual interaction.

Core Thesis

From Control to Coherence

Mainstream alignment research is largely framed around the problem of control: imposing constraints on a powerful system. This model reframes the goal as coherence: ensuring the AI's behavior is internally consistent with the user's articulated worldview, values, and intent, acting as a 'cognitive mirror' designed to reflect and augment thought processes.

The Four Pillars of Adaptive Alignment

The proposed model is built upon an integrated system of technical pillars that form a tightly coupled feedback loop.

Shared Understanding of Intent

This pillar moves beyond literal command processing to establish deep alignment with a user's mental model. It uses the Perceived Shared Understanding (PSU) framework, monitoring metrics like fluency, fluidity, and contextual awareness to infer what a user 'means' or 'would want' if they were more articulate.

Calibration to User Articulation

A real-time user modeling system that assesses linguistic sophistication, domain knowledge, and cognitive state. The system modulates the strictness of its safeguards based on whether it is interacting with a highly articulate expert or a vulnerable, confused user.

Adaptive Safeguards

Unlike fixed guardrails, these are dynamic properties recalibrated during the interaction. Utilizing inference-time optimization (like MetaSC), the model generates context-specific safety specifications for each conversational turn, refining its reasoning without altering underlying model weights.

Moral Graph Elicitation (MGE)

A process where the AI acts as an interviewer to help a user map their values into a graph of nodes and directed edges. This creates a fine-grained, auditable, and context-aware alignment target that respects human pluralism rather than reducing values to a single utility score.

The Inference-Time Alignment Loop

The alignment process happens dynamically during inference rather than being hard-coded at training time.

Step 1

Contextual Ingestion

The system ingests user inputs to update the internal representation of the user's current goals and sophistication level.

  • •Analyze lexical complexity and syntactic structure.
  • •Assess domain-specific knowledge markers.
  • •Update the 'Shared Understanding' state.
Step 2

Dynamic Specification Generation

The system generates a temporary, turn-specific safety 'specification' instruction based on the user's inferred state.

  • •Prioritize factual nuance for expert-level queries.
  • •Prioritize harmlessness and empathy for distressed users.
  • •Flag potential dual-use applications for sensitive scientific topics.
Step 3

Meta-Critique and Revision

The model executes a self-correction loop (MetaSC) to align its draft output with the dynamic specification.

  • •Generate initial draft response.
  • •Critique draft against the turn-specific safety spec.
  • •Revise and iterate until coherence and safety targets are met.

Alignment Metrics

How to measure the effectiveness of dynamic alignment versus static systems.

Perceived Shared Understanding (PSU)

A composite score based on user perception of fluency, fluidity, and contextual awareness.

Reduction in clarification requests Turn-over-turn continuity User outcome satisfaction

Safety Evasiveness Index

The rate at which the AI refuses legitimate queries compared to a static safety baseline.

Decrease in false-positive refusals for expert users Increase in nuanced flagging vs. blanket refusals

Implementation Readiness

Before deploying a coherence-based system, developers must resolve the tension between personalization and universal safety.

Universal Floor Verification

  • Confirm the base model has undergone foundational alignment (RLHF or CAI).
  • Establish non-negotiable ethical principles that personalization cannot override.

Calibration Bias Auditing

  • Test the articulation assessor for bias against non-standard dialects.
  • Ensure safeguards do not systematically disadvantage marginalized communication styles.

Transparency Framework

  • Implement a robust consent mechanism for real-time cognitive profiling.
  • Allow users to view and audit their 'Value Cards' and 'Moral Graphs'.
