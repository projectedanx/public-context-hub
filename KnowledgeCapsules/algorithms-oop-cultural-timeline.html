Algorithms and Object-Oriented Programming: A Socio-Technical Genealogy

This capsule synthesizes the history of computation from early 20th-century mathematical logic to the contemporary era of AI readiness. It reframes programming not merely as a technical exercise but as a profound cultural and political act that shapes literacy, social patterns, and power dynamics.

Core Thesis

Code as Performative Literacy

Programming has evolved from manual telegraphy and Turing machines into a complex layer of 'excitable speech.' The transition to Object-Oriented Programming (OOP) and standardized design patterns represents an attempt to manage the 'entropic tangle' of software through encapsulation and socialized architecture.

Foundational Paradigms

These concepts represent the intellectual pillars upon which modern software architecture and its critique are built.

Literate Programming

Originated by Donald Knuth, this concept advocates for code written to be readable by humans as much as machines, framing software as a narrative or literature rather than just execution.

Design Patterns

Drawing from Christopher Alexander's architectural theories, these are reusable solutions to recurring problems in software design, famously cataloged by the 'Gang of Four' in 1995 to standardize software behavior.

Algorithmic Performativity

The theory that code does not just describe a process but performs it, often with political and social consequences, as seen in surveillance capitalism and algorithmic bias research.

Object-Oriented Encapsulation

A method of hiding internal states and requiring all interaction to occur through a well-defined interface, which reduces side effects but creates new 'obscure objects' of study.

Step 2

Pattern Application

Implementing established design patterns to ensure reusability and maintainability.

  • •Selecting Creational, Structural, or Behavioral patterns.

  • •Assessing the environmental cost of computational execution.

Implementation & Critique Checklist

A guide for developers and researchers to evaluate both the technical and social quality of software.

Architectural Integrity

  • Is the code DRY (Don't Repeat Yourself) or intentionally WET (Write Everything Twice) for clarity?
  • Are SOLID principles being applied to class designs?
  • Is the complexity managed via appropriate design patterns?

Ethical & Aesthetic Audit

  • Does the algorithm reinforce existing social oppressions?
  • Is the software artful or purely utilitarian in its 'performativity'?
  • How does the code handle 'untimeliness' or endless loops?

Literacy & Maintenance

  • Is the documentation 'literate' enough for human comprehension?
  • Are there clear migrator paths for legacy systems?
  • Does the AI readiness align with actual data analytics prioritization?
